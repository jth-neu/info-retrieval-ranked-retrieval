<p><b>Inktomi Corporation</b> was a company that provided <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> for <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service providers</a> (ISPs). It was organized in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> and headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Foster_City,_California" title="Foster City, California">Foster City, California</a>. Customers included <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, <a href="/wiki/HotBot" title="HotBot">HotBot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/WalMart" class="mw-redirect" title="WalMart">WalMart</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10K_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10K-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company developed <a href="/wiki/Traffic_Server" class="mw-redirect" title="Traffic Server">Traffic Server</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Proxy_server" title="Proxy server">proxy server</a> <a href="/wiki/Web_cache" title="Web cache">web cache</a> for <a href="/wiki/World_wide_web" class="mw-redirect" title="World wide web">world wide web</a> traffic and on-demand <a href="/wiki/Streaming_media" title="Streaming media">streaming media</a> which transcoded images down to a smaller size for users of <a href="/wiki/Dial-up_Internet_access" title="Dial-up Internet access">dial-up Internet access</a>. Traffic Server was deployed by several large ISPs including <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, after the bursting of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>, the company was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> for $241 million.
</p><p>The company's name, pronounced 'INK-tuh-me', was derived from a legend of the <a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota people</a> about a trickster spider character, <a href="/wiki/Iktomi" title="Iktomi">Iktomi</a> which was known for his ability to defeat larger adversaries through wit and cunning.<sup id="cite_ref-formed_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-formed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The tri-color nested cube logo was created by Tom Lamar in 1996.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inktomi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Inktomi was founded in January 1996 by <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> professor <a href="/wiki/Eric_Brewer_(computer_scientist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Eric Brewer (computer scientist)">Eric Brewer</a> and graduate student Paul Gauthier. The company was initially founded based the <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">web search engine</a> that Eric Brewer and Paul Gauthier developed at the university.<sup id="cite_ref-formed_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-formed-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 1998, the company raised $36 million in an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 1998, the company acquired C2B Technologies for $95 million in stock, adding shopping engine technology to its portfolio.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1998, the company raised additional capital at a 688% premium to its IPO price 5 months earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 1999, CEO David Peterschmidt said that Inktomi would become an "arms merchant" to a growing number of <a href="/wiki/Content_delivery_network" title="Content delivery network">content delivery network</a> service providers.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Inktomi received revenue based on a  percentage of sales and/or a <a href="/wiki/Pay_per_click" class="mw-redirect" title="Pay per click">pay per click</a> model.
</p><p>In April 1999, the company acquired Impulse Buy Network, adding 400 merchants to its shopping engine.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>in November 1999, the company acquired Webspective, which developed technology for <a href="/wiki/Content_management" title="Content management">content management</a> across a host of distributed servers to be used in <a href="/wiki/Load_balancing_(computing)" title="Load balancing (computing)">load balancing</a>, for $106 million in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>in August 2000, the company acquired Ultraseek Server from <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Go.com" title="Go.com">Go.com</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>in September 2000, the company acquired FastForward Networks, which developed software for the distribution of live streaming media over the Internet using "app-level" multicast technology, for $1.3 billion in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>in December 2000, the company acquired the Content Bridge Business Unit from Adero, a content delivery network, which had formed the Content Bridge Alliance with Inktomi and other ISPs, hosting providers and IP transport providers in August 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>in July 2001, the company acquired eScene Networks, which developed software that provided an integrated workflow for the management and publishing of video content.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2000, the company's stock peaked at a price of $241 per share.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, after the burst of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>, the company restructured and Keyur Patel joined Inktomi as senior vice president, strategy, marketing and technology.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The restructuring led to the sale of the Ultraseek Server product (renamed Inktomi Enterprise Search) to Verity in late 2002 and the sale of the rest of the company to <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search" title="Yahoo! Search">Yahoo! Search</a> for $1.63 per share, or $241 million, completed on March 19, 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, the technology behind the Inktomi Proxy Server was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Websense" class="mw-redirect" title="Websense">Websense</a>, which was modified and included in the Websense Security Gateway.
</p><p>In 2009, Yahoo! donated the Traffic Server technology to the <a href="/wiki/Apache_Software_Foundation" title="Apache Software Foundation">Apache Software Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
