<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hull_RJD_37.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hull_RJD_37.JPG/220px-Hull_RJD_37.JPG" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hull_RJD_37.JPG/330px-Hull_RJD_37.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Hull_RJD_37.JPG/440px-Hull_RJD_37.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2735" data-file-height="1802" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hull_RJD_37.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historical railway map of Hull showing the ferry route and the layout of docks</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Humber</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/ʌ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cut&#39;">ʌ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a large <a href="/wiki/Tidal_estuary" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal estuary">tidal estuary</a> on the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a>. It is formed at <a href="/wiki/Trent_Falls" title="Trent Falls">Trent Falls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faxfleet" title="Faxfleet">Faxfleet</a>, by the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/Tidal_river" title="Tidal river">tidal rivers</a> <a href="/wiki/River_Ouse,_Yorkshire" title="River Ouse, Yorkshire">Ouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Trent" title="River Trent">Trent</a>. From there to the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, it forms part of the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding of Yorkshire</a> on the north bank and <a href="/wiki/North_Lincolnshire" title="North Lincolnshire">North Lincolnshire</a> on the south bank. Although the Humber is an <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> from the point at which it is formed, many maps show it as the <b>River Humber</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Below Trent Falls, the Humber passes the junction with the <a href="/wiki/Market_Weighton_Canal" title="Market Weighton Canal">Market Weighton Canal</a> on the north shore, the confluence of the <a href="/wiki/River_Ancholme" title="River Ancholme">River Ancholme</a> on the south shore; between <a href="/wiki/North_Ferriby" title="North Ferriby">North Ferriby</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Ferriby" title="South Ferriby">South Ferriby</a> and under the <a href="/wiki/Humber_Bridge" title="Humber Bridge">Humber Bridge</a>; between <a href="/wiki/Barton-upon-Humber" title="Barton-upon-Humber">Barton-upon-Humber</a> on the south bank and <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Kingston upon Hull</a> on the north bank (where the <a href="/wiki/River_Hull" title="River Hull">River Hull</a> joins), then meets the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> between <a href="/wiki/Cleethorpes" title="Cleethorpes">Cleethorpes</a> on the Lincolnshire side and the long and thin headland of <a href="/wiki/Spurn" title="Spurn">Spurn Head</a> to the north.
</p><p>Ports on the Humber include the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Hull" title="Port of Hull">Port of Hull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Grimsby" title="Port of Grimsby">Port of Grimsby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Immingham" title="Port of Immingham">Port of Immingham</a>, as well as lesser ports at <a href="/wiki/New_Holland,_Lincolnshire" title="New Holland, Lincolnshire">New Holland</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Killingholme_Haven" title="North Killingholme Haven">North Killingholme Haven</a>. The estuary is navigable for the largest of deep-sea vessels. Inland connections for smaller craft are extensive but handle only a quarter of the goods traffic handled in the <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Humber is now an <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a>. When the world sea level was lower during the <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Ages</a>, the Humber had a long freshwater course across what was then the dry bed of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Classical authors knew the Humber as the <i>Abus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">Ἄβος</span>),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> which was one of the principal rivers, or rather estuaries in the Roman province of <a href="/wiki/Maxima_Caesariensis" title="Maxima Caesariensis">Maxima Caesariensis</a> in Britain. It was reported as receiving many tributaries, and discharging itself into the German Ocean (the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>) south of <a href="/wiki/Ocelum_Promontorium" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocelum Promontorium">Ocelum Promontorium</a> (Spurn Head). Its left bank was inhabited by the Celtic tribe, whom the Romans entitled <a href="/wiki/Parisi_(Yorkshire)" title="Parisi (Yorkshire)">Parisi</a>, but according to a medieval poet, no great town or city anciently stood on its banks.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> period, the Humber was a major boundary, separating <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a> from the southern kingdom, though at its height Northumbria did cover areas south of the Humber. The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lindsey" title="Kingdom of Lindsey">Kingdom of Lindsey</a>, which today is Northern Lincolnshire, was part of Northumbria before being lost to <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>. The name <i>Northumbria</i> came from <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon language">Anglo-Saxon</a> <i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">Norðhymbre</i> (plural) = "the people north of the Humber".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Humber currently forms the boundary between the <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding of Yorkshire</a>, to the north and <a href="/wiki/North_Lincolnshire" title="North Lincolnshire">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_East_Lincolnshire" title="North East Lincolnshire">North East Lincolnshire</a>, to the south.
</p><p>From 1974 to 1996, the areas now known as the East Riding of Yorkshire, North Lincolnshire and North East Lincolnshire constituted the county of <a href="/wiki/Humberside" title="Humberside">Humberside</a>.
</p><p>On 23 August 1921, the British <a href="/wiki/Airship" title="Airship">airship</a> <a href="/wiki/R38-class_airship" title="R38-class airship">R38</a> crashed into the estuary near Hull, killing 44 of the 49 crew on board.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossings">Crossings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Crossings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The estuary's only modern crossing is the <a href="/wiki/Humber_Bridge" title="Humber Bridge">Humber Bridge</a>, which was the longest single-span <a href="/wiki/Suspension_bridge" title="Suspension bridge">suspension bridge</a> in the world from its construction in 1981 until 1998. It is now the <a href="/wiki/List_of_longest_suspension_bridge_spans" title="List of longest suspension bridge spans">ninth longest</a>.
</p><p>Before the bridge was built, a series of <a href="/wiki/Paddle_steamer" title="Paddle steamer">paddle steamers</a> operated from the  <a href="/wiki/Corporation_Pier_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporation Pier railway station">Corporation Pier railway station</a><sup id="cite_ref-cp_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cp-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Pier,_Kingston_upon_Hull" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria Pier, Kingston upon Hull">Victoria Pier</a> in Hull to the <a href="/wiki/New_Holland_Pier_railway_station" title="New Holland Pier railway station">railway pier in New Holland</a>. Steam ferries started in 1841, and in 1848 were purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Manchester,_Sheffield_and_Lincolnshire_Railway" title="Manchester, Sheffield and Lincolnshire Railway">Manchester, Sheffield and Lincolnshire Railway</a>. They, and their successors, ran the ferry until the bridge opened in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-cp_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cp-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Railway passenger and car traffic continued to use the pier until the end of ferry operations.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The line of the bridge is similar to an ancient ferry route from Hessle to <a href="/wiki/Barton_upon_Humber" class="mw-redirect" title="Barton upon Humber">Barton upon Humber</a>, which is noted in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> and in a charter of 1281. The ferry was recorded as still operating in 1856, into the railway era.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Humber was then one mile (1.6&#160;km) across.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defences">Defences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Defences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Humber_Forts" title="Humber Forts">Humber Forts</a> were built in the mouth of the river for the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. Planned in 1914, their construction started in 1915 and they were not completed until 1919.<sup id="cite_ref-ps1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ps1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ps2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ps2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A coastal battery at Easington, <i>Fort Goodwin</i> or <i>Kilnsea Battery</i>, faced the Bull Sands Fort.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> They were also garrisoned during the Second World War, and were finally abandoned for military use in 1956.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fort_Paull" title="Fort Paull">Fort Paull</a> is further upstream, a Napoleonic-era emplacement replaced in the early 20th century by <i>Stallinborough Battery</i> opposite <a href="/wiki/Sunk_Island" title="Sunk Island">Sunk Island</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossing_on_foot">Crossing on foot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crossing on foot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Graham_Boanas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Graham Boanas (page does not exist)">Graham Boanas</a>, a Hull man, is believed to be the first man to succeed in wading across the Humber since <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">ancient Roman</a> times. The feat, in August 2005, was attempted to raise cash and awareness for the medical research charity, <a href="/wiki/DebRA" class="mw-redirect" title="DebRA">DebRA</a>. He started his trek on the north bank at <a href="/wiki/Brough,_East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="Brough, East Riding of Yorkshire">Brough</a>; four hours later, he emerged on the south bank at <a href="/wiki/Whitton,_North_Lincolnshire" title="Whitton, North Lincolnshire">Whitton</a>. He is 6&#160;feet 9&#160;inches (2.06&#160;m) tall and took advantage of a very low tide.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He replicated this achievement on the television programme <i><a href="/wiki/Top_Gear_(current_format)" class="mw-redirect" title="Top Gear (current format)">Top Gear</a></i> (Series 10 Episode 6) when he raced <a href="/wiki/James_May" title="James May">James May</a> who drove an <a href="/wiki/Alfa_Romeo_159" title="Alfa Romeo 159">Alfa Romeo 159</a> around the inland part of the estuary without using the Humber Bridge.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossing_by_swimming">Crossing by swimming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crossing by swimming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Saturday 26 August 1911, Alice Maud Boyall became the first woman to swim the Humber. Boyall, then aged 19 and living in Hull, was the Yorkshire swimming champion. She crossed the Humber from Hull to New Holland Pier swimming the distance in 50 minutes, 6 minutes slower than the men's record.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 2011 Warners Health have organised the 'Warners Health Humber Charity Business Swim'. Twelve swimmers from companies across the Yorkshire region train and swim in an ellipse from the south bank to the north bank of the river under the Humber Bridge over a total distance of approximately <span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> miles (2.4&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg/250px-Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg" width="250" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg/375px-Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg/500px-Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="679" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hull_and_Humber_Estuary_2013-03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Humber from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a></div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humber&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most European <a href="/wiki/Hydronym" title="Hydronym">hydronyms</a> are <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a> in origin and numerous Celtic or <a href="/wiki/Pre-Celtic" title="Pre-Celtic">Pre-Celtic</a> derivations for <i>Humber</i> have been suggested. The Celtic root &#42;<i lang="cel" title="Celtic languages collective text">com</i>-&#42;<i lang="cel" title="Celtic languages collective text">bero</i> (coming together), gives the modern <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> toponym <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">cymer</i> which is found typically at the confluence of rivers. A derivation of Humber from &#42;<i lang="cel" title="Celtic languages collective text">cym(b)er</i> is supported by the proven Celtic/Germanic sound change of K to H, and the geographic fact of the Humber being the confluence of a number of important rivers.
</p><p>The estuary appears in some <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> sources as <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Abus</i> (A name used by <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Spenser" title="Edmund Spenser">Edmund Spenser</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Faerie_Queene" title="The Faerie Queene">The Faerie Queene</a></i>). This is possibly a Latinisation of the Celtic form <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Aber</i> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Language">Welsh</a> for river mouth or estuary), but is erroneously given as a name for both the Humber and <a href="/wiki/River_Ouse,_Yorkshire" title="River Ouse, Yorkshire">The Ouse</a> as one continuous watercourse.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Both <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Abus</i> and <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Aber</i> may record an older <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> word for water or river, (as in the ‘Five Rivers’ of the <a href="/wiki/Punjab" title="Punjab">Punjab</a>). An alternative derivation may be from the Latin verb <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">abdo</i> meaning black or dark river. The successive name <i>Humbre</i>/<i>Humbri</i>/<i>Umbri</i> may continue the meaning via the Latin verb <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">umbro</i> also meaning "to cover with shadows".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>By <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">Medieval</a> times, the river is recorded as <i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">Humbre</i> (Anglo-Saxon) <i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">Hwmyr</i> (Welsh) and <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Humbri</i> (Vulgar Latin <a href="/wiki/Dative" class="mw-redirect" title="Dative">dative</a>) / <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Umbri</i> (Classical Latin <a href="/wiki/Dative" class="mw-redirect" title="Dative">dative</a>). Since the name appears to reoccur in "Humber Brook" near "Humber Court" in <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> or <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>, it has been suggested that a root word &#42;<i lang="cel" title="Celtic languages collective text">humbr-</i> may have existed meaning "river", or something similar, in hypothetical Pre-Celtic language spoken <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Britain" title="Prehistoric Britain">before the Celtic Migration</a> (compare <a href="/wiki/Tardebigge" title="Tardebigge">Tardebigge</a>). An element &#42;<i lang="cel" title="Celtic languages collective text">ambri-</i> 'channel, river' is also reconstructible for <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Celtic">proto-Celtic</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Celtic">Ancient Celtic</a> <a href="/wiki/Prefix_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prefix (linguistics)">prefix</a> &#42;<i lang="cel" title="Celtic languages collective text">su</i>- 'good' routinely developed into <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> element &#42;<i lang="cy" title="Welsh language text">hy-</i>.
</p><p>The Humber features regularly in <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_of_Monmouth" title="Geoffrey of Monmouth">Geoffrey of Monmouth</a>'s 12th-century fictional chronicle <i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a href="/wiki/Historia_Regum_Britanniae" title="Historia Regum Britanniae">Historia Regum Britanniae</a></i>. According to Geoffrey, the Humber, invariably referred to by the Latin word for river, was named after "<a href="/wiki/Humber_the_Hun" title="Humber the Hun">Humber the Hun</a>", who drowned there while trying to invade in the earliest days of Britain's settlement.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Andrew_Marvell" title="Andrew Marvell">Andrew Marvell</a>'s poem "<a href="/wiki/To_His_Coy_Mistress" title="To His Coy Mistress">To His Coy Mistress</a>" mentions "the tide of Humber".
</p>
