<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Insular Celts</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Celtic_dagger,_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Celtic_dagger%2C_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG/220px-Celtic_dagger%2C_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG" width="220" height="106" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Celtic_dagger%2C_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG/330px-Celtic_dagger%2C_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Celtic_dagger%2C_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG/440px-Celtic_dagger%2C_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="576" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Celtic_dagger,_scabbard_and_buckle.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Celtic dagger found in Britain.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Insular Celts</b> are the speakers of the <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic languages</a>, which comprise all the living Celtic languages as well as their precursors, but the term is mostly used in reference to the peoples of the <a href="/wiki/British_Iron_Age" title="British Iron Age">British Iron Age</a> prior to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman conquest</a>, and their contemporaries in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>.
</p><p>According to older theories, the Insular Celtic languages spread throughout the islands in the course of the insular Iron Age.  But this is now doubted by most scholars, who see the languages as already present, and possibly dominant, in the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, and perhaps earlier.  At some point the languages split into the two major groups, <a href="/wiki/Goidelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Goidelic">Goidelic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brittonic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, corresponding to the population groups of the <a href="/wiki/Goidels" class="mw-redirect" title="Goidels">Goidels</a> (Gaels) on one hand and the <a href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (Celtic people)">Britons</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> on the other. The extent to which these peoples ever formed a <a href="/wiki/Genetic_history_of_the_British_Isles" title="Genetic history of the British Isles">distinct ethnic group</a> remains unclear. While there are early records of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Celtic_languages" title="Continental Celtic languages">Continental Celtic languages</a>, allowing a comparatively confident reconstruction of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Proto-Celtic">Proto-Celtic</a>, Insular Celtic languages become attested in connected texts only at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Age_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Dark Age Britain">Dark Ages</a>, from around the 7th century AD, by which time they had become mutually incomprehensible.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Celtic_settlement_of_Britain_and_Ireland">Celtic settlement of Britain and Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_Celts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Celtic settlement of Britain and Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archaeology">Archaeology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_Celts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archaeology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_Britain" title="Bronze Age Britain">Bronze Age Britain</a></div>
<p>In older theories, the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celts</a>, defined as speakers of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a>, which derive from a <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language" title="Proto-Celtic language">Proto-Celtic language</a>, roughly coincided with the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/European_Iron_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="European Iron Age">European Iron Age</a>. In 1946 the Celtic scholar <a href="/wiki/T._F._O%27Rahilly" title="T. F. O&#39;Rahilly">T. F. O'Rahilly</a> published his <a href="/wiki/O%27Rahilly%27s_historical_model" title="O&#39;Rahilly&#39;s historical model">influential model</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Early_history_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Early history of Ireland">early history of Ireland</a>, which postulated four separate waves of Celtic invaders, spanning most of the Iron Age (700 to 100 BCE). However the archaeological evidence for these waves of invaders proved elusive.  Later research indicated that the culture may have developed gradually and continuously between the Celts and the indigenous populations. Similarly in Ireland little archaeological evidence was found for large intrusive groups of Celtic immigrants, suggesting to archaeologists such as <a href="/wiki/Colin_Renfrew,_Baron_Renfrew_of_Kaimsthorn" class="mw-redirect" title="Colin Renfrew, Baron Renfrew of Kaimsthorn">Colin Renfrew</a> that the native late Bronze Age inhabitants gradually absorbed European Celtic influences and language.
</p><p>In the 1970s a "continuity model" was popularized by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colin_Burgess_(archaeologist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Colin Burgess (archaeologist) (page does not exist)">Colin Burgess</a> in his book <i>The Age of <a href="/wiki/Stonehenge" title="Stonehenge">Stonehenge</a></i> which theorised that Celtic culture in Great Britain "emerged" rather than resulted from invasion and that the Celts were not invading aliens, but the descendants of, or culturally influenced by, figures such as the <a href="/wiki/Amesbury_Archer" title="Amesbury Archer">Amesbury Archer</a>, whose burial included clear Continental connections.
</p><p>The archaeological evidence is of substantial cultural continuity through the 1st millennium BCE,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> although with a significant overlay of selectively adopted elements of the "Celtic" <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène culture</a> from the 4th century BCE onwards. There are claims of continental-style states appearing in <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">southern England</a> close to the end of the period, possibly reflecting in part immigration by élites from various Gallic states such as those of the <a href="/wiki/Belgae" title="Belgae">Belgae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Evidence of <a href="/wiki/Chariot_burial" title="Chariot burial">chariot burials</a> in England begins about 300 BC and is mostly confined to the <a href="/wiki/Arras_culture" title="Arras culture">Arras culture</a> associated with the  <a href="/wiki/Parisi_(Yorkshire)" title="Parisi (Yorkshire)">Parisii</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistics">Linguistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_Celts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linguistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Goidelic_substrate_hypothesis" title="Goidelic substrate hypothesis">Goidelic substrate hypothesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pre-Celtic" title="Pre-Celtic">Pre-Celtic</a></div>
<p>Remnants of pre-Celtic languages may remain in the names of some geographical features, such as the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">Clyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Tamar" title="River Tamar">Tamar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a>, whose etymology is unclear but possibly derive from a <a href="/wiki/Pre-Celtic" title="Pre-Celtic">pre-Celtic</a> <a href="/wiki/Substratum" class="mw-redirect" title="Substratum">substrate</a> (Gelling<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>).
</p><p>It is thought that by about the 6th century BCE most of the inhabitants of the isles of Ireland and Britain were speaking Celtic languages. A  controversial <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">phylogenetic</a> linguistic analysis of 2003 puts the age of Insular Celtic a few centuries earlier, at 2,900 years before present, or slightly earlier than the European Iron Age.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is not entirely clear if there was ever a "Common Insular Celtic" language, the alternative being that the Celtic settlement of Ireland and Great Britain was undertaken by separate populations speaking separate Celtic dialects from the beginning. 
However, the "Insular Celtic hypothesis" has been favoured as the most probable scenario in Celtic historical linguistics since the later 20th century (supported by e.g. Cowgill 1975; McCone 1991, 1992; and Schrijver 1995).
This would point to a single wave of immigration of early Celts (<a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt D</a>) to both Great Britain and Ireland, which however divided into two isolated groups (one in Ireland and one in Great Britain) soon after their arrival, placing the split of Insular Celtic into Goidelic and Brythonic close to 500 BCE. However, this is not the only possible interpretation. In an alternative scenario, the migration could have brought early Celts first to Britain (where a largely undifferentiated Insular Celtic was spoken initially), from whence Ireland was colonised only later. Schrijver has pointed out that according to the absolute chronology of sound changes found in Kenneth Jackson's "Language and History in Early Britain", British and Goidelic were still essentially identical as late as the mid-1st century CE apart from the P/Q isogloss, and that there is no archaeological evidence pointing to Celtic presence in Ireland prior to about 100 BCE.
</p><p>The Goidelic branch would develop into <a href="/wiki/Primitive_Irish" title="Primitive Irish">Primitive Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old Irish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Irish" title="Middle Irish">Middle Irish</a>, and only with the historical (medieval) expansion of the Gaels would it split into the modern Gaelic languages (<a href="/wiki/Modern_Irish" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Irish">Modern Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a>). 
Common Brythonic, on the other hand, split into two branches, <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">British</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pritenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pritenic">Pritenic</a> as a consequence of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_invasion_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman invasion of Britain">Roman invasion of Britain</a> in the 1st century. 
By the 8th century, Pritenic had developed into <a href="/wiki/Pictish_language" title="Pictish language">Pictish</a> (which would be extinct during the 9th century or so), and British had split into <a href="/wiki/Old_Welsh" title="Old Welsh">Old Welsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Old_Cornish" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Cornish">Old Cornish</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_genetics">Population genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_Celts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Population genetics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Genetic studies have supported the prevalence of native populations. 
A study in 2003 by Christian Capelli, David Goldstein and others at <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a> showed that <a href="/wiki/Genetic_marker" title="Genetic marker">genetic markers</a> associated with Gaelic names in Ireland and Scotland are also common in certain parts of Wales and England (in most cases, the Southeast of England with the lowest counts of these markers), and are similar to the genetic markers of the Basque people and most different from Danish and North German people.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This similarity supported earlier findings in suggesting a large pre-Celtic genetic ancestry, likely going back to the original settlement of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Paleolithic" title="Upper Paleolithic">Upper Paleolithic</a>. The authors suggest, therefore, that Celtic culture and the Celtic language may have been imported to Britain  at the beginning of the Iron Age by <a href="/wiki/Diffusionism" class="mw-redirect" title="Diffusionism">cultural contact</a>, not "mass invasions". 
In 2006, two  popular books, <i>The Blood of the Isles</i> by <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Sykes" title="Bryan Sykes">Bryan Sykes</a>  and <i>The Origins of the British: a Genetic Detective Story</i> by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Oppenheimer" title="Stephen Oppenheimer">Stephen Oppenheimer</a> discuss genetic evidence for the <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_settlement_of_the_British_Isles_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric settlement of the British Isles (disambiguation)">prehistoric settlement of the British Isles</a>, concluding that while there is evidence for a series of migrations from the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>  during the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> and, to a lesser extent, the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> eras,  there is comparatively little trace of any Iron Age migration.
</p><p>Later genetic studies regarding Y-DNA Haplogroup I-M284 did find evidence for some Late Iron Age migration of Celtic (<a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne" class="mw-redirect" title="La Tène">La Tène</a>) people  to Britain and onto north-east Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_Age_Britain">Iron Age Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_Celts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Iron Age Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Age Britain">Iron Age Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_tribes_in_Britain" title="Iron Age tribes in Britain">Iron Age tribes in Britain</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Brittonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallo-Brittonic">Gallo-Brittonic</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg/220px-South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg/330px-South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg/440px-South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3142" data-file-height="3020" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:South.Britain.Late.Iron.Age.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of southern Britain in the 1st century BC</div></div></div>
<p>The <i>British Iron Age</i> is a conventional name in the <a href="/wiki/Archaeology_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeology of Great Britain">archaeology of Great Britain</a>,  typically excluding <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_Ireland" title="Prehistoric Ireland">prehistoric Ireland</a>, which had an independent Iron Age culture of its own.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The parallel phase of Irish archaeology is termed the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron Age Ireland">Irish Iron Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The British Iron Age lasted in theory from the first significant use of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> for tools and weapons in Britain to the Romanisation of the southern half of the island. The Romanised culture is termed <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> and is considered to supplant the British Iron Age.
</p><p>The only surviving description of the Iron Age populations of the British Isles is that of <a href="/wiki/Pytheas" title="Pytheas">Pytheas</a>, who travelled to the region in about 325 BC.
The earliest <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_tribes_in_Britain" title="Iron Age tribes in Britain">tribal names</a> on record date to the 1st century AD (<a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caesar" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesar">Caesar</a>; to some extent<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Celtic_coinage" title="Celtic coinage">coinage</a>), representing the situation at the moment of Roman conquest.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_era_and_Dark_Ages">Roman era and Dark Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Insular_Celts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Roman era and Dark Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romano-British culture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotland_during_the_Roman_Empire" title="Scotland during the Roman Empire">Scotland during the Roman Empire</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sub-Roman_Britain" title="Sub-Roman Britain">Sub-Roman Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales_in_the_Early_Middle_Ages" title="Wales in the Early Middle Ages">Wales in the Early Middle Ages</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ireland_(400%E2%80%93800)" title="History of Ireland (400–800)">History of Ireland (400–800)</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">Britons (historical)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg/220px-All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg" width="220" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg/330px-All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg/440px-All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2036" data-file-height="2512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Roman_coins_1997_-_2010_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plot of all locations of <a href="/wiki/Roman_coins" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman coins">Roman coins</a> in Britain found between 1997 and 2010, intended as an illustration of the measure of "Romanization" (<a href="/wiki/Portable_Antiquities_Scheme" title="Portable Antiquities Scheme">Portable Antiquities Scheme</a>)</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> existed for about four centuries, from the mid 1st to the mid 5th century. 
This led to the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Syncretism" title="Syncretism">syncretized</a> <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">Romano-British culture</a> in the southern part of Great Britain, comparable in some aspects to <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Roman_culture" title="Gallo-Roman culture">Gallo-Roman culture</a> on the continent. However, while in Gaul Roman influence was sufficient to almost wholly replace the Gaulish language with <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">Vulgar Latin</a>, this was nowhere near the case in Roman Britain. Although a <a href="/wiki/British_Latin" title="British Latin">British Latin</a> dialect was presumably spoken in the population centres of Roman Britain, it did not become influential enough to displace British dialects spoken throughout the country. 
There did presumably remain pockets of Romance-speaking populations in Britain as late as the 8th century.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Northern Britain (north of the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Wall" title="Antonine Wall">Antonine Wall</a>) and Ireland would essentially remain in the prehistoric period until after the end of the Roman period.  The "protohistoric" period of Ireland can be argued to begin around 400 AD, due to cultural diffusion from Roman Britain, importing writing (<a href="/wiki/Ogham" title="Ogham">ogham</a>, reflecting the earliest records of <a href="/wiki/Primitive_Irish" title="Primitive Irish">Primitive Irish</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Early_history_of_Ireland#Early_Christian_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Early history of Ireland">Christianity</a>. 
The populations north of Roman Britain are summarized under the term <a href="/wiki/Caledonians" title="Caledonians">Caledonians</a> (the ancestors of the <a href="/wiki/Picts" title="Picts">Picts</a> of later centuries). Very little is known about them other than they posed a constant military threat to the Roman border.
</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_invasion_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon invasion of Great Britain">Anglo-Saxon invasion and settlement of Great Britain</a> in the 5th and 6th centuries, the British languages were gradually marginalised to the western parts of the island, to what is now <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>.
The transition may not necessarily present itself as a mass immigration with a substantial replacement of population, but rather could involve the arrival of a new elite installing their culture and language as a <a href="/wiki/Superstrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Superstrate">superstrate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A similar process happened as the <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaels</a> installed themselves over the formerly Pictish-speaking populations in Northern Britain. There seems to have been a period of British-Saxon syncretism during the 6th century, with British rulers bearing Saxon names (as in <a href="/wiki/Tewdrig" title="Tewdrig">Tewdrig</a>) and Saxon rulers bearing British names (as in <a href="/wiki/Cerdic_of_Wessex" title="Cerdic of Wessex">Cerdic</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the end of the Dark Ages, around the 8th century, the Insular Celtic peoples had become the bearers of the <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a> cultures of the historical <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales_in_the_High_Middle_Ages" title="Wales in the High Middle Ages">Medieval Wales</a>.
</p><p><br />
</p>
