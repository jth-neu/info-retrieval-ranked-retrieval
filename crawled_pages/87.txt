<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Dot-com bubble</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/260px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png" width="260" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/390px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg/520px-Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nasdaq_Composite_dot-com_bubble.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ_Composite" title="NASDAQ Composite">NASDAQ Composite</a> index spiked in the late 1990s and then fell sharply as a result of the dot-com bubble.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:US_VC_funding.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/US_VC_funding.png/260px-US_VC_funding.png" width="260" height="94" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/US_VC_funding.png/390px-US_VC_funding.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/US_VC_funding.png/520px-US_VC_funding.png 2x" data-file-width="4865" data-file-height="1762" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:US_VC_funding.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Quarterly U.S. Venture Capital Investments 1995-2017</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>dot-com bubble</b> (also known as the <b>dot-com boom</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the <b>dot-com crash</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the <b>tech bubble</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the <b>Internet bubble</b>, and the <b>information technology bubble</b>) was a historic <a href="/wiki/Economic_bubble" title="Economic bubble">economic bubble</a> and period of excessive <a href="/wiki/Speculation" title="Speculation">speculation</a> that occurred roughly from 1995 to 2000, a period of extreme growth in the usage and adaptation of the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Nasdaq_Composite" class="mw-redirect" title="Nasdaq Composite">Nasdaq Composite</a> <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_index" title="Stock market index">stock market index</a>, which included many Internet-based companies, peaked in value on March 10, 2000 before crashing. The burst of the bubble lasted from March 11, 2000 to October 9, 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> After the bubble burst, <a href="/wiki/Online_retail" class="mw-redirect" title="Online retail">online retail</a> companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Pets.com" title="Pets.com">Pets.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/Webvan" title="Webvan">Webvan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boo.com" title="Boo.com">Boo.com</a>, failed completely and shut down along with communication companies, such as <a href="/wiki/Worldcom" class="mw-redirect" title="Worldcom">WorldCom</a>, <a href="/wiki/NorthPoint_Communications" title="NorthPoint Communications">NorthPoint Communications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_Crossing" title="Global Crossing">Global Crossing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Cisco" class="mw-redirect" title="Cisco">Cisco</a>, whose stock declined by 86%,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Qualcomm" title="Qualcomm">Qualcomm</a>, lost a large portion of their market capitalization but survived, and some companies, such as <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, declined in value but recovered quickly.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prelude_to_the_bubble">Prelude to the bubble</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prelude to the bubble">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1993, the release of the <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(web_browser)" title="Mosaic (web browser)">Mosaic web browser</a> made access to the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> easier.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Internet usage increased as a result of the reduction of the "<a href="/wiki/Digital_divide" title="Digital divide">digital divide</a>" and advances in connectivity, uses of the Internet, and computer education. Between 1990 and 1997, the percentage of households in the United States owning computers increased from 15% to 35% as computer ownership progressed from a luxury to a necessity.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This marked the shift to the <a href="/wiki/Information_Age" title="Information Age">Information Age</a>, an economy based on <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">information technology</a>, and many new companies were founded.
</p><p>At the same time, low interest rates increased the availability of capital.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Taxpayer_Relief_Act_of_1997" title="Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997">Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997</a>, which lowered the top marginal <a href="/wiki/Capital_gains_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Capital gains tax in the United States">capital gains tax in the United States</a>, also made people more willing to make more speculative investments.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Chair_of_the_Federal_Reserve" title="Chair of the Federal Reserve">Chair of the Federal Reserve</a>, allegedly fueled investments in the stock market by putting a positive spin on stock valuations.<sup id="cite_ref-enigma_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enigma-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Act_of_1996" title="Telecommunications Act of 1996">Telecommunications Act of 1996</a> was expected to result in many new technologies, and people wanted to profit from them.<sup id="cite_ref-now_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-now-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_bubble">The bubble</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The bubble">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a result of these factors, many investors were eager to invest, at any valuation, in any <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_company" title="Dot-com company">dot-com company</a>, especially if it had one of the <a href="/wiki/Internet-related_prefixes" title="Internet-related prefixes">Internet-related prefixes</a> or a "<a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a>" <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> in its name.<sup id="cite_ref-bubble_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bubble-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">Venture capital</a> was easy to raise. <a href="/wiki/Investment_bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Investment bank">Investment banks</a>, which profited significantly from <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offerings</a> (IPO), fueled speculation and encouraged investment in technology.<sup id="cite_ref-swindle_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swindle-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A combination of rapidly increasing stock prices in the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_sector_of_the_economy" title="Quaternary sector of the economy">quaternary sector of the economy</a> and confidence that the companies would turn future profits created an environment in which many investors were willing to overlook traditional metrics, such as the <a href="/wiki/Price%E2%80%93earnings_ratio" title="Price–earnings ratio">price–earnings ratio</a>, and base confidence on technological advancements, leading to a <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_bubble" title="Stock market bubble">stock market bubble</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-enigma_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enigma-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Between 1995 and 2000, the Nasdaq Composite stock market index rose 400%.<sup id="cite_ref-bubble_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bubble-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It reached a price–earnings ratio of 200, dwarfing the peak price–earnings ratio of 80 for the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Nikkei_225" title="Nikkei 225">Nikkei 225</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_asset_price_bubble" title="Japanese asset price bubble">Japanese asset price bubble</a> of 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-enigma_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enigma-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 1999, shares of <a href="/wiki/Qualcomm" title="Qualcomm">Qualcomm</a> rose in value by 2,619%, 12 other large-cap stocks each rose over 1,000%  value, and 7 additional large-cap stocks each rose over 900% in value. Even though the Nasdaq Composite rose 85.6% and the <a href="/wiki/S%26P_500_Index" title="S&amp;P 500 Index">S&amp;P 500 Index</a> rose 19.5% in 1999, more stocks fell in value than rose in value as investors sold stocks in slower growing companies to invest in Internet stocks.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An unprecedented amount of personal investing occurred during the boom and stories of people quitting their jobs to engage in full-time <a href="/wiki/Day_trading" title="Day trading">day trading</a> were common.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/News_media" title="News media">news media</a> took advantage of the public's desire to invest in the stock market; an article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> suggested that investors "re-think" the "quaint idea" of profits, and <a href="/wiki/CNBC" title="CNBC">CNBC</a> reported on the stock market with the same level of suspense as many networks provided to the <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting_of_sports_events" title="Broadcasting of sports events">broadcasting of sports events</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-enigma_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enigma-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the height of the boom, it was possible for a promising dot-com company to become a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> via an IPO and raise a substantial amount of money even if it had never made a profit—or, in some cases, realized any material revenue. People who received <a href="/wiki/Employee_stock_option" title="Employee stock option">employee stock options</a> became instant paper millionaires when their companies executed IPOs; however, most employees were barred from selling shares immediately due to <a href="/wiki/Lock-up_period" title="Lock-up period">lock-up periods</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-swindle_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-swindle-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The most successful entrepreneurs, such as <a href="/wiki/Mark_Cuban" title="Mark Cuban">Mark Cuban</a>, sold their shares or entered into <a href="/wiki/Hedge_(finance)" title="Hedge (finance)">hedges</a> to protect their gains.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spending_tendencies_of_dot-com_companies">Spending tendencies of dot-com companies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spending tendencies of dot-com companies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most dot-com companies incurred <a href="/wiki/Net_operating_loss" title="Net operating loss">net operating losses</a> as they spent heavily on advertising and promotions to harness <a href="/wiki/Network_effect" title="Network effect">network effects</a> to build <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mind_share" title="Mind share">mind share</a> as fast as possible, using the mottos "get big fast" and "get large or get lost". These companies offered their services or products for free or at a discount with the expectation that they could build enough brand awareness to charge profitable rates for their services in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lessons_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lessons-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In January 2000, there were 16 <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_commercials_during_Super_Bowl_XXXIV" title="Dot-com commercials during Super Bowl XXXIV">dot-com commercials during Super Bowl XXXIV</a>, each costing $2 million for a 30-second spot.
</p><p>The "growth over profits" mentality and the aura of "<a href="/wiki/New_economy" title="New economy">new economy</a>" invincibility led some companies to engage in lavish spending on elaborate business facilities and luxury vacations for employees. Upon the launch of a new product or website, a company would organize an expensive event called a <a href="/wiki/Dot_com_party" title="Dot com party">dot com party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bubble_in_telecom">Bubble in telecom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bubble in telecom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_equipment" title="Telecommunications equipment">Telecommunications equipment</a> providers, convinced that the future economy would require ubiquitous <a href="/wiki/Broadband_access" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadband access">broadband access</a>, went deeply into debt to improve their networks with high-speed equipment and <a href="/wiki/Fiber_optic" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiber optic">fiber optic</a> cables. In many areas, such as the <a href="/wiki/Dulles_Technology_Corridor" title="Dulles Technology Corridor">Dulles Technology Corridor</a> in Virginia, governments funded technology infrastructure and created favorable business and tax law to encourage companies to expand.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In Europe, <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> companies overspent on <a href="/wiki/3G" title="3G">3G</a> licences, which led them deep into debt. The investments in infrastructure were far out of proportion to <a href="/wiki/Cash_flow" title="Cash flow">cash flow</a>. These were major factors that led to the <a href="/wiki/Telecoms_crash" title="Telecoms crash">telecoms crash</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-now_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-now-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bursting_of_the_bubble">Bursting of the bubble</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bursting of the bubble">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Early_2000s_recession" title="Early 2000s recession">Early 2000s recession</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:U.S._Treasuries.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/U.S._Treasuries.png/400px-U.S._Treasuries.png" width="400" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/U.S._Treasuries.png/600px-U.S._Treasuries.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/U.S._Treasuries.png/800px-U.S._Treasuries.png 2x" data-file-width="1889" data-file-height="830" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:U.S._Treasuries.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>United States Treasuries history compared to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Funds_Rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Funds Rate">Federal Funds Rate</a></div></div></div>
<p>Around the turn of the millennium, spending on technology was volatile as companies prepared for the <a href="/wiki/Year_2000_problem" title="Year 2000 problem">Year 2000 problem</a>, which, when the clocks changed to the year 2000, actually had minimal impact.
</p><p>On January 10, 2000, <a href="/wiki/America_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="America Online">America Online</a> announced a <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">merger</a> with <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a>, the largest to date and a move that was questioned by many analysts.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2000, with the Year 2000 problem no longer a worry, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a> announced plans to aggressively raise interest rates, which led to significant stock market volatility as analysts disagreed as to whether or not technology companies would be affected by higher borrowing costs.
</p><p>On March 10, 2000, the NASDAQ Composite stock market index peaked at 5,048.62.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 13, 2000, news that <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> had once again entered a <a href="/wiki/Recession" title="Recession">recession</a> triggered a global sell off that disproportionately affected technology stocks.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 15, 2000, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> and <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> ended merger talks and the Nasdaq fell 2.6% but the S&amp;P 500 Index rose 2.4% as investors shifted from strong performing technology stocks to poor performing established stocks.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 20, 2000, <i><a href="/wiki/Barron%27s_(newspaper)" title="Barron&#39;s (newspaper)">Barron's</a></i> featured a cover article titled "Burning Up; Warning: Internet companies are running out of cash -- fast", which predicted the imminent bankruptcy of many internet companies.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> This led many people to rethink their investments. That same day, <a href="/wiki/Microstrategy" class="mw-redirect" title="Microstrategy">Microstrategy</a> announced a revenue restatement due to aggressive accounting practices. Its stock price, which had risen from $7 per share to as high as $333 per share in a year, fell $120 per share, or 62%, in a day.<sup id="cite_ref-micro_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-micro-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The next day, the Federal Reserve raised interest rates, leading to an <a href="/wiki/Inverted_yield_curve" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverted yield curve">inverted yield curve</a>, although stocks rallied temporarily.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 3, 2000, judge <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Penfield_Jackson" title="Thomas Penfield Jackson">Thomas Penfield Jackson</a> issued his <i>conclusions of law</i> in the case of <a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Microsoft_Corp._(2001)" class="mw-redirect" title="United States v. Microsoft Corp. (2001)">United States v. Microsoft Corp. (2001)</a> and ruled that Microsoft was guilty of <a href="/wiki/Monopolization" title="Monopolization">monopolization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tying_(commerce)" title="Tying (commerce)">tying</a> in violation of the <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Antitrust_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Sherman Antitrust Act">Sherman Antitrust Act</a>. This led to a one-day 15% decline in the value of shares in Microsoft and a 350-point, or 8%, drop in the value of the Nasdaq. Many people saw the legal actions as bad for technology in general.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> That same day, Bloomberg published a widely read article that stated: "It's time, at last, to pay attention to the numbers".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On Friday, April 14, 2000, the Nasdaq Composite index fell 9%, ending a week in which it fell 25%. Investors were forced to sell stocks ahead of <a href="/wiki/Tax_Day_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax Day (United States)">Tax Day</a>, the due date to pay taxes on gains realized in the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By June 2000, dot-com companies were forced to rethink their advertising campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 9, 2000, Pets.com, a much-hyped company that had backing from Amazon.com, went out of business only nine months after completing its IPO.<sup id="cite_ref-pets_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pets-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> At that time, most internet stocks had declined in value by 75% from their highs, wiping out $1.755 trillion in value.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2001, just three dot-com companies bought advertising spots during <a href="/wiki/Super_Bowl_XXXV" title="Super Bowl XXXV">Super Bowl XXXV</a>: <a href="/wiki/E-Trade" title="E-Trade">E-Trade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monster.com" title="Monster.com">Monster.com</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_HotJobs" title="Yahoo! HotJobs">Yahoo! HotJobs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> accelerated the stock-market drop later that year.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Investor confidence was further eroded by several <a href="/wiki/Accounting_scandal" class="mw-redirect" title="Accounting scandal">accounting scandals</a> and the resulting bankruptcies, including the <a href="/wiki/Enron_scandal" title="Enron scandal">Enron scandal</a> in October 2001, the <a href="/wiki/WorldCom_scandal" class="mw-redirect" title="WorldCom scandal">Worldcom scandal</a> in June 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Adelphia_Communications_Corporation" title="Adelphia Communications Corporation">Adelphia Communications Corporation</a> scandal in July 2002.
</p><p>By the end of the <a href="/wiki/Stock_market_downturn_of_2002" title="Stock market downturn of 2002">stock market downturn of 2002</a>, stocks had lost $5 trillion in <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a> since the peak.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> At its trough on October 9, 2002, the NASDAQ-100 had dropped to 1,114, down 78% from its peak.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After venture capital was no longer available, the operational mentality of executives and investors completely changed. A dot-com company's lifespan was measured by its <a href="/wiki/Burn_rate" title="Burn rate">burn rate</a>, the rate at which it spent its existing capital. Many dot-com companies ran out of capital and went through <a href="/wiki/Liquidation" title="Liquidation">liquidation</a>. Supporting industries, such as advertising and shipping, scaled back their operations as demand for services fell. However, many companies were able to endure the crash; 48% of dot-com companies survived through 2004, albeit at lower valuations.<sup id="cite_ref-lessons_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lessons-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several companies and their executives were accused or convicted of <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a> for misusing shareholders' money, and the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> levied large fines against investment firms including <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merrill_Lynch" title="Merrill Lynch">Merrill Lynch</a> for misleading investors.
</p><p>After suffering losses, retail investors transitioned their investment portfolios to more cautious positions.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Job_market_and_office_equipment_glut">Job market and office equipment glut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Job market and office equipment glut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Layoffs of <a href="/wiki/Programmer" title="Programmer">programmers</a> resulted in a <a href="/wiki/General_glut" title="General glut">general glut</a> in the job market. University enrollment for computer-related degrees dropped noticeably.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Anecdotes of unemployed programmers going back to school to become accountants or lawyers were common.
</p><p>Failed startups liquidated all of their computer equipment and office equipment such as <a href="/wiki/Herman_Miller_(manufacturer)" title="Herman Miller (manufacturer)">Herman Miller</a> <a href="/wiki/Aeron_chair" title="Aeron chair">Aeron chairs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dot-com_bubble&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As growth in the information technology sector stabilized, companies consolidated, some, such as <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> gained market share and came to dominate their respective fields. The information technology industry came to more closely resemble other traditional sectors of the economy, albeit with still a faster growth rate and higher valuations than other sectors. There are now many information technology companies ranked at the top of the <a href="/wiki/Fortune_500" title="Fortune 500">Fortune 500</a>.
</p><p>
In a 2015 book, venture capitalist <a href="/wiki/Fred_Wilson_(financier)" title="Fred Wilson (financier)">Fred Wilson</a>, who funded dot-com companies and lost 90% of his net worth when the bubble burst, said about the dot-com bubble: </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"A friend of mine has a great line. He says 'Nothing important has ever been built without <a href="/wiki/Irrational_exuberance" title="Irrational exuberance">irrational exuberance</a>'. Meaning that you need some of this mania to cause investors to open up their pocketbooks and finance the building of the railroads or the automobile or aerospace industry or whatever. And in this case, much of the capital invested was lost, but also much of it was invested in a very high <a href="/wiki/Throughput" title="Throughput">throughput</a> backbone for the Internet, and lots of software that works, and databases and server structure. All that stuff has allowed what we have today, which has changed all our lives... that's what all this speculative mania built".<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p><br />
</p>

It was acquired by <a href="/wiki/KB_Toys" title="KB Toys">KB Toys</a>, which later also filed bankruptcy.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Excite" title="Excite">Excite</a> – A <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">web portal</a> founded by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Kraus" title="Joe Kraus">Joe Kraus</a> and others that merged with <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">internet service provider</a> <a href="/wiki/@Home_Network" title="@Home Network">@Home Network</a> in 1999 to become Excite@Home, promising to be the "<a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a> of <a href="/wiki/Broadband" title="Broadband">broadband</a>". After trying unsuccessfully to sell the Excite portal during a sharp downturn in online advertising, the company filed bankruptcy in September 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> It was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Ask.com" title="Ask.com">Ask.com</a> in March 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flooz.com" title="Flooz.com">Flooz.com</a> – A <a href="/wiki/Digital_currency" title="Digital currency">digital currency</a> founded by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Levitan" title="Robert Levitan">Robert Levitan</a>; it folded in 2001 due to lack of consumer acceptance. It was famous for having <a href="/wiki/Whoopi_Goldberg" title="Whoopi Goldberg">Whoopi Goldberg</a> as its spokesperson.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Freei" title="Freei">Freei</a> – Filed bankruptcy in October 2000, soon after canceling its IPO. At the time, it was the 5th largest <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">internet service provider</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, with 3.2 million users.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Famous for its mascot, <a href="/wiki/Baby_Bob" title="Baby Bob">Baby Bob</a>, the company lost $19 million in 1999 on revenues of less than $1 million.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gadzoox" title="Gadzoox">Gadzoox</a> - A <a href="/wiki/Storage_area_network" title="Storage area network">storage area network</a> company, its shares tripled on its first day of trading giving it a market capitalization of $1.97 billion; the company was sold 4 years later for $5.3 million.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geeknet" title="Geeknet">Geeknet</a> (formerly VA Linux) – A provider of built-to-order <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a> systems based on <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and other open source projects, it set the record for largest first-day price gain upon its IPO on December 9, 1999; after the stock priced at $30/share, it ended the first day of trading at $239.25/share, a 698% gain.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/GeoCities" class="mw-redirect" title="GeoCities">GeoCities</a> – Acquired by <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> for $3.57 billion in January 1999<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> and was shut down in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Global_Crossing" title="Global Crossing">Global Crossing</a> – A telecommunications company founded in 1997; it reached a market capitalization of $47 billion in February 2000 before filing bankruptcy in January 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/TheGlobe.com" title="TheGlobe.com">theGlobe.com</a> – A <a href="/wiki/Social_networking_service" title="Social networking service">social networking service</a> that launched in April 1995 and made headlines when its November 1998 IPO resulted in the largest first day gain of any IPO to date. CEO Stephan Paternot became a visible symbol of the excesses of dot-com millionaires and is famous for saying "Got the girl. Got the money. Now I'm ready to live a disgusting, frivolous life".<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/GovWorks" title="GovWorks">govWorks</a> – Founded by fraudster <a href="/wiki/Kaleil_Isaza_Tuzman" title="Kaleil Isaza Tuzman">Kaleil Isaza Tuzman</a>; it was featured in the <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Startup.com" title="Startup.com">Startup.com</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Handspring_(company)" title="Handspring (company)">Handspring</a> - A PDA maker that was defunct by 2003, when it was purchased by Palm Inc.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Healtheon" title="Healtheon">Healtheon</a> - Founded by <a href="/wiki/James_H._Clark" title="James H. Clark">James H. Clark</a>, it was acquired by <a href="/wiki/WebMD" title="WebMD">WebMD</a> in 1999 for $7.2 billion in stock but eventually shut down.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/HomeGrocer" title="HomeGrocer">HomeGrocer</a> - A public online grocer that merged with Webvan.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Infoseek" title="Infoseek">Infoseek</a> - A web portal that was acquired by Disney in 1999; it was valued at $7 billion but eventually shut down.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Inktomi" title="Inktomi">inktomi</a> – Reached a valuation of $25 billion in March 2000; acquired by Yahoo! in 2003 for $241 million.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Interactive_Intelligence" title="Interactive Intelligence">Interactive Intelligence</a> - A telecommunications software company whose stock doubled on its first day of trading.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Internet_America" title="Internet America">Internet America</a> - Its stock price doubled in a day in December 1999 despite no specific news about the company.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/IVillage" title="IVillage">iVillage</a> - On its first day of trading in March 1999, its stock rose 255% to $84 per share.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> It was acquired by NBC for $8.50 per share in 2006 and shut down.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/IWon" title="IWon">iWon</a> - Backed by <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>, it gave away $1 million to a lucky contestant each month and $10 million in April 2000 on a half-hour special program that was broadcast on CBS.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kozmo.com" title="Kozmo.com">Kozmo.com</a> – Offered one-hour local delivery of several items with no delivery fees from March 1998 until it went bust in April 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lastminute.com" title="Lastminute.com">Lastminute.com</a> – Its IPO in the United Kingdom on March 14, 2000 coincided with the bursting of the bubble.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Learning_Company" title="The Learning Company">The Learning Company</a> – Bought by <a href="/wiki/Mattel" title="Mattel">Mattel</a> in 1999 for $3.5 billion; sold for $27.3 million in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_Audio" title="Liquid Audio">Liquid Audio</a> - Despite its stock doubling in value on its first day of trading in July 1999, its technology was obsolete by 2002.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Looksmart" class="mw-redirect" title="Looksmart">Looksmart</a> - A website whose value went from $23 million to $5 billion within months, but lost 99% of its value as the bubble burst.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lycos" title="Lycos">Lycos</a> – Acquired by <a href="/wiki/Terra_Networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Terra Networks">Terra Networks</a> for $12.5 billion in May 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> It was sold in 2004 to <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Daum_Communications_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Daum Communications Corporation">Daum Communications Corporation</a> for $95.4 million.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MarchFirst" title="MarchFirst">MarchFirst</a> - A <a href="/wiki/Web_development" title="Web development">web development</a> company formed on March 1, 2000 by the merger of <a href="/wiki/USWeb" title="USWeb">USWeb</a> and <a href="/wiki/CKS_Group" title="CKS Group">CKS Group</a>, it filed bankruptcy and liquidation just over a year after it was formed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MicroStrategy" title="MicroStrategy">MicroStrategy</a> – After rising from $7 to as high as $333 in a year, its shares lost $120, or 62%, on March 20, 2000 following the announcement of a financial restatement for the previous two years.<sup id="cite_ref-micro_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-micro-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Net2Phone" title="Net2Phone">Net2Phone</a> - A <a href="/wiki/VoIP" class="mw-redirect" title="VoIP">VoIP</a> provider whose stock price soared after its 1999 IPO.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Netscape" title="Netscape">Netscape</a> - After a popular IPO, it was acquired by AOL in 1999 for $4.2 billion in stock.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/NorthPoint_Communications" title="NorthPoint Communications">NorthPoint Communications</a> – Agreed to a significant investment by <a href="/wiki/Verizon" class="mw-redirect" title="Verizon">Verizon</a> and a merger of <a href="/wiki/DSL" class="mw-redirect" title="DSL">DSL</a> businesses in September 2000; however, Verizon backed out 2 months later after NorthPoint was forced to restate its financial statements, including a 20% reduction in revenue, after its customers failed to pay as the bubble burst. NorthPoint then filed bankruptcy. After lawsuits from both parties, Verizon and NorthPoint settled out of court.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Palm_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Palm Inc.">Palm Inc.</a> - Spun off from 3Com at the peak of the bubble, its shares plunged as the bubble burst.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pets.com" title="Pets.com">Pets.com</a> – Sold pet supplies to retail customers before filing for bankruptcy in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-pets_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pets-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/PFSweb" title="PFSweb">PFSweb</a> - A B2B company whose stock price doubled after its IPO.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pixelon" title="Pixelon">Pixelon</a> – Streaming video company that hosted a $16 million <a href="/wiki/Dot_com_party" title="Dot com party">dot com party</a> in October 1999 in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a> with celebrities including <a href="/wiki/Chely_Wright" title="Chely Wright">Chely Wright</a>, <a href="/wiki/LeAnn_Rimes" title="LeAnn Rimes">LeAnn Rimes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Faith_Hill" title="Faith Hill">Faith Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dixie_Chicks" title="Dixie Chicks">Dixie Chicks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Ray" title="Sugar Ray">Sugar Ray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Cole" title="Natalie Cole">Natalie Cole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiss_(band)" title="Kiss (band)">KISS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">Tony Bennett</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Brian_Setzer_Orchestra" title="The Brian Setzer Orchestra">The Brian Setzer Orchestra</a>, and a reunion of <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>. The company failed less than a year later when it became apparent that its technologies were fraudulent or misrepresented. Its founder had been a convicted felon who changed his name.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/PLX_Technology" title="PLX Technology">PLX Technology</a> - Shares rose fivefold within months of its IPO.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prodigy_(online_service)" title="Prodigy (online service)">Prodigy</a> - An ISP whose stock price doubled on its first day of trading.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pseudo.com" title="Pseudo.com">Pseudo.com</a> – One of the first live streaming video websites, it produced its own content in a studio in <a href="/wiki/SoHo,_Manhattan" title="SoHo, Manhattan">SoHo, Manhattan</a> and streamed up to 7 hours of live programming a day on its website.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radvision" title="Radvision">Radvision</a> - An online conferencing company, its stock price rose 150% on its first day of trading.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Razorfish_(company)" title="Razorfish (company)">Razorfish</a> - An internet advertising consultancy, its stock doubled on its first day of trading in April 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Redback_Networks" title="Redback Networks">Redback Networks</a> - A telecommunications equipment company, its stock soared 266% in its first day of trading, giving it a market capitalization of $1.77 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ritmoteca.com" title="Ritmoteca.com">Ritmoteca.com</a> – One of the first <a href="/wiki/Online_music_store" title="Online music store">online music stores</a> selling music on a pay-per-download basis and an early predecessor to the <a href="/wiki/ITunes" title="ITunes">iTunes</a> business model. It pioneered digital distribution deals as one of the first companies to sign agreements with major music publishers.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Startups.com" title="Startups.com">Startups.com</a> – "Ultimate dot-com startup" that went out of business in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-50worst_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50worst-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/ModusLink_Global_Solutions" title="ModusLink Global Solutions">Steel Connect</a> (formerly CMGI Inc.) – a company that invested in many internet startups; between 1995 and 1999, its stock appreciated 4,921%, but declined 99% when the bubble burst.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Savvis" title="Savvis">Savvis</a> - A fiber optic company.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Think_Tools" title="Think Tools">Think Tools</a> – One of the most extreme symptoms of the bubble in Europe, this company reached a market valuation of CHF 2.5 billion in March 2000 despite no prospects of having a product.<sup id="cite_ref-50worst_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50worst-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/TIBCO_Software" title="TIBCO Software">TIBCO Software</a> - Its stock price rose tenfold shortly after its 1999 IPO.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tradex_Technologies" title="Tradex Technologies">Tradex Technologies</a> - A <a href="/wiki/B2B_e-commerce" title="B2B e-commerce">B2B e-commerce</a> company, it was sold for $5.6 billion at the height of the bubble, making <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Aegerter" title="Daniel Aegerter">Daniel Aegerter</a> a billionaire on paper.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Transmeta" title="Transmeta">Transmeta</a> - A semiconductor designer that attempted to challenge Intel, its IPO in November 2000 was the last successful technology IPO until the IPO of <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> in 2004. The company shut down in 2009 after failing to execute.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/UBid" title="UBid">uBid</a> - An <a href="/wiki/Online_auction" title="Online auction">online auction</a> site founded in 1997 as a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/PCM,_Inc." title="PCM, Inc.">PCM, Inc.</a> that went public in December 1998 at $15 per share before its stock price soared to $186 per share, a market value of $1.5 billion.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_Online" title="United Online">United Online</a> - An ISP formed by the merger of <a href="/wiki/NetZero" title="NetZero">NetZero</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juno_Online_Services" title="Juno Online Services">Juno Online Services</a> as the bubble burst.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Usinternetworking_Inc" title="Usinternetworking Inc">Usinternetworking Inc</a> - Its stock price rose 174% on its first day of trading.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/VerticalNet" title="VerticalNet">VerticalNet</a> – A host of 43 <a href="/wiki/Business-to-business" title="Business-to-business">business-to-business</a> (B2B) procurement portals that was valued at $1.6 billion after its IPO, despite having only $3.6 million in quarterly revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vignette_Corporation" title="Vignette Corporation">Vignette Corporation</a> - Its stock rose 1,500% within months after its IPO.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Webvan" title="Webvan">Webvan</a> – An online grocer that promised delivery within 30 minutes; it went bankrupt in 2001 after $396 million of venture capital funding and an IPO that raised $375 million and was folded into Amazon.com.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
