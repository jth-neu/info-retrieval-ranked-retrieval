<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Logical connective</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, a <b>logical connective</b> (also called a <b>logical operator</b>, <b>sentential connective</b>, or <b>sentential operator</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Symbol_(formal)" title="Symbol (formal)">symbol</a> or <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">word</a> used to connect two or more <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">sentences</a> (of either a <a href="/wiki/Formal_language" title="Formal language">formal</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a>) in a <a href="/wiki/Syntax_(logic)" title="Syntax (logic)">grammatically valid</a> way, such that the value of the compound sentence produced depends only on that of the original sentences and on the meaning of the connective.
</p><p>The most common logical connectives are <b><a href="/wiki/Binary_relation" title="Binary relation">binary</a> connectives</b> (also called <b><a href="/wiki/Dyadics" title="Dyadics">dyadic</a> connectives</b>) which join two sentences which can be thought of as the function's <a href="/wiki/Operand" title="Operand">operands</a>. Also commonly, <a href="/wiki/Negation" title="Negation">negation</a> is considered to be a <b><a href="/wiki/Unary_function" title="Unary function">unary</a> connective</b>.
</p><p>Logical connectives along with <a href="/wiki/Quantifier_(logic)" title="Quantifier (logic)">quantifiers</a> are the two main types of <a href="/wiki/Logical_constant" title="Logical constant">logical constants</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Formal_system" title="Formal system">formal systems</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Propositional_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Propositional logic">propositional logic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Predicate_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Predicate logic">predicate logic</a>. Semantics of a logical connective is often, but not always, presented as a <a href="/wiki/Truth_function" title="Truth function">truth function</a>.
</p><p>A logical connective is similar to but not equivalent to a <a href="/wiki/Conditional_operator" title="Conditional operator">conditional operator</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_language">In language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_language">Natural language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the grammar of natural languages two sentences may be joined by a <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical conjunction">grammatical conjunction</a> to form a <i>grammatically</i> <a href="/wiki/Compound_sentence_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Compound sentence (linguistics)">compound sentence</a>. Some but not all such grammatical conjunctions are <a href="/wiki/Truth_function" title="Truth function">truth functional</a>. For example, consider the following sentences:
</p>
<div><ol style="list-style-type:upper-alpha"><li>Jack went up the hill.</li><li>Jill went up the hill.</li><li>Jack went up the hill <i>and</i> Jill went up the hill.</li><li>Jack went up the hill <i>so</i> Jill went up the hill.</li></ol></div>
<p>The words <i>and</i> and <i>so</i> are <i>grammatical</i> conjunctions joining the sentences (A) and (B)  to form the compound sentences (C) and (D). The <i>and</i> in (C) is a <i>logical</i> connective, since the truth of (C) is completely determined by (A) and (B): it would make no sense to affirm (A) and (B) but deny (C). However, <i>so</i> in (D) is not a logical connective, since it would be quite reasonable to affirm (A) and (B) but deny (D): perhaps, after all, Jill went up the hill to fetch a pail of water, not because Jack had gone up the hill at all.
</p><p>Various English words and word pairs express logical connectives, and some of them are synonymous. Examples are:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_languages">Formal languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formal languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In formal languages, truth functions are represented by unambiguous symbols. These symbols are called <i>logical connectives</i>, <i>logical operators</i>, <i>propositional operators</i>, or, in <a href="/wiki/Classical_logic" title="Classical logic">classical logic</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Truth_function" title="Truth function">truth-functional</a> connectives</i>. See <a href="/wiki/Well-formed_formula" title="Well-formed formula">well-formed formula</a> for the rules which allow new well-formed formulas to be constructed by joining other well-formed formulas using truth-functional connectives.
</p><p>Logical connectives can be used to link more than two statements, so one can speak about <i><a href="/wiki/Arity" title="Arity"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">n</span>-ary</a> logical connective</i>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_common_logical_connectives">List of common logical connectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: List of common logical connectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commonly used logical connectives include
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Negation" title="Negation">Negation (not)</a>: ¬ , N (prefix), ~</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Logical_conjunction" title="Logical conjunction">Conjunction (and)</a>: &#8743; , K (prefix), &amp; , ∙</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Logical_disjunction" title="Logical disjunction">Disjunction (or)</a>: &#8744;, A (prefix)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Material_conditional" title="Material conditional">Material implication (if...then)</a>: &#8594; , C (prefix), &#8658; , &#8835;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Logical_biconditional" title="Logical biconditional">Biconditional (if and only if)</a>: &#8596; , E (prefix), &#8801; , =</li></ul>
<p>Alternative names for biconditional are <i>iff</i>, <i>xnor</i>, and <i>bi-implication</i>.

<p>Some authors used letters for connectives at some time of the history: <b>u.</b> for conjunction (German's "und" for "and") and <b>o.</b> for disjunction (German's "oder" for "or") in earlier works by Hilbert (1904); <b>N<i>p</i></b> for negation, <b>K<i>pq</i></b> for conjunction, <b>D<i>pq</i></b> for alternative denial, <b>A<i>pq</i></b> for disjunction, <b>X<i>pq</i></b> for joint denial, <b>C<i>pq</i></b> for implication, <b>E<i>pq</i></b> for biconditional in <a href="/wiki/Jan_%C5%81ukasiewicz" title="Jan Łukasiewicz">Łukasiewicz</a> (1929);<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> cf. <a href="/wiki/Polish_notation#Polish_notation_for_logic" title="Polish notation">Polish notation</a>.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Redundancy">Redundancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Redundancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Such a logical connective as <a href="/wiki/Converse_implication" title="Converse implication">converse implication</a> "←" is actually the same as <a href="/wiki/Material_conditional" title="Material conditional">material conditional</a> with swapped arguments; thus, the symbol for converse implication is redundant. In some logical calculi (notably, in <a href="/wiki/Classical_logic" title="Classical logic">classical logic</a>) certain essentially different compound statements are <a href="/wiki/Logical_equivalence" title="Logical equivalence">logically equivalent</a>. A less <a href="/wiki/Triviality_(mathematics)" title="Triviality (mathematics)">trivial</a> example of a redundancy is the classical equivalence between <span class="texhtml">¬<i>P</i> ∨ <i>Q</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i> → <i>Q</i></span>. Therefore, a classical-based logical system does not need the conditional operator "→" if "¬" (not) and "∨" (or) are already in use, or may use the "→" only as a <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_sugar" title="Syntactic sugar">syntactic sugar</a> for a compound having one negation and one disjunction.
</p><p>There are sixteen <a href="/wiki/Boolean_function" title="Boolean function">Boolean functions</a> associating the input <a href="/wiki/Truth_value" title="Truth value">truth values</a> <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">P</span> and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">Q</span> with four-digit <a href="/wiki/Binary_numeral_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Binary numeral system">binary</a> outputs.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> These correspond to possible choices of binary logical connectives for <a href="/wiki/Classical_logic" title="Classical logic">classical logic</a>. Different implementations of classical logic can choose different <a href="/wiki/Functionally_complete" class="mw-redirect" title="Functionally complete">functionally complete</a> subsets of connectives.
</p><p>One approach is to choose a <i>minimal</i> set, and define other connectives by some logical form, as in the example with the material conditional above.
The following are the minimal functionally complete sets of operators in classical logic whose arities do not exceed 2:

<p>See more details about functional completeness in classical logic at <a href="/wiki/Truth_function#Functional_completeness" title="Truth function">Functional completeness in truth function</a>.
</p><p>Another approach is to use with equal rights connectives of a certain convenient and functionally complete, but <i>not minimal</i> set. This approach requires more propositional <a href="/wiki/Axiom" title="Axiom">axioms</a>, and each equivalence between logical forms must be either an axiom or provable as a theorem.
</p><p>The situation, however, is more complicated in <a href="/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic" title="Intuitionistic logic">intuitionistic logic</a>.   Of its five connectives, {∧, ∨, →, ¬, ⊥}, only negation "¬" can be reduced to other connectives (see <a href="/wiki/False_(logic)#False,_negation_and_contradiction" title="False (logic)">details</a>). Neither conjunction, disjunction, nor material conditional has an equivalent form constructed of the other four logical connectives.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some logical connectives possess properties which may be expressed in the theorems containing the connective. Some of those properties that a logical connective may have are:
</p>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Associativity" class="mw-redirect" title="Associativity">Associativity</a></dt>
<dd>Within an expression containing two or more of the same associative connectives in a row, the order of the operations does not matter as long as the sequence of the operands is not changed.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Commutativity" class="mw-redirect" title="Commutativity">Commutativity</a></dt>
<dd>The operands of the connective may be swapped preserving logical equivalence to the original expression.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Distributivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Distributivity">Distributivity</a></dt>
<dd>A connective denoted by · distributes over another connective denoted by +, if <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i> · (<i>b</i> + <i>c</i>) = (<i>a</i> · <i>b</i>) + (<i>a</i> · <i>c</i>)</span> for all operands <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">c</span>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Idempotence" title="Idempotence">Idempotence</a></dt>
<dd>Whenever the operands of the operation are the same, the compound is logically equivalent to the operand.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Absorption_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Absorption Law">Absorption</a></dt>
<dd>A pair of connectives &#8743;, &#8744; satisfies the absorption law if <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\displaystyle a\land (a\lor b)=a}">
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <mi>a</mi>
        <mo>&#x2227;<!-- ∧ --></mo>
        <mo stretchy="false">(</mo>
        <mi>a</mi>
        <mo>&#x2228;<!-- ∨ --></mo>
        <mi>b</mi>
        <mo stretchy="false">)</mo>
        <mo>=</mo>
        <mi>a</mi>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle a\land (a\lor b)=a}</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9aabf1bc4aa530b4243da83d1676e9c6fbb83b46" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:14.76ex; height:2.843ex;" alt="a\land (a\lor b)=a"/></span> for all operands <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">a</span>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">b</span>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Monotonicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Monotonicity">Monotonicity</a></dt>
<dd>If <i>f</i>(<i>a</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., <i>a</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>) ≤ <i>f</i>(<i>b</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., <i>b</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>) for all <i>a</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., <i>a</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>, <i>b</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., <i>b</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> ∈ {0,1} such that <i>a</i><sub>1</sub> ≤ <i>b</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>a</i><sub>2</sub> ≤ <i>b</i><sub>2</sub>, ..., <i>a</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> ≤ <i>b</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>. E.g., &#8744;, &#8743;, ⊤, ⊥.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Affine_transformation" title="Affine transformation">Affinity</a></dt>
<dd>Each variable always makes a difference in the truth-value of the operation or it never makes a difference. E.g., &#172;, &#8596;,  <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\displaystyle \nleftrightarrow }">
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <mo>&#x21AE;<!-- ↮ --></mo>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \nleftrightarrow }</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dce85ed756bc5a6cdf0f62892f57a6a1f96803ac" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: 0.137ex; margin-bottom: -0.308ex; width:2.324ex; height:1.509ex;" alt="\nleftrightarrow"/></span>, ⊤, ⊥.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Duality_(mathematics)" title="Duality (mathematics)">Duality</a></dt>
<dd>To read the truth-value assignments for the operation from top to bottom on its <a href="/wiki/Truth_table" title="Truth table">truth table</a> is the same as taking the complement of reading the table of the same or another connective from bottom to top. Without resorting to truth tables it may be formulated as <span class="texhtml"><i>g&#771;</i>(¬<i>a</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., ¬<i>a</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>) = ¬<i>g</i>(<i>a</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., <i>a</i><sub><i>n</i></sub>)</span>. E.g., &#172;.</dd>
<dt>Truth-preserving</dt>
<dd>The compound all those argument are tautologies is a tautology itself. E.g., &#8744;, &#8743;, ⊤, &#8594;, &#8596;, ⊂ (see <a href="/wiki/Validity_(logic)" title="Validity (logic)">validity</a>).</dd>
<dt>Falsehood-preserving</dt>
<dd>The compound all those argument are <a href="/wiki/Contradiction" title="Contradiction">contradictions</a> is a contradiction itself. E.g., &#8744;, &#8743;, <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\displaystyle \nleftrightarrow }">
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <mo>&#x21AE;<!-- ↮ --></mo>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \nleftrightarrow }</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dce85ed756bc5a6cdf0f62892f57a6a1f96803ac" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: 0.137ex; margin-bottom: -0.308ex; width:2.324ex; height:1.509ex;" alt="\nleftrightarrow"/></span>, ⊥, ⊄, ⊅ (see <a href="/wiki/Validity_(logic)" title="Validity (logic)">validity</a>).</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Involution_(mathematics)" title="Involution (mathematics)">Involutivity</a> (for unary connectives)</dt>
<dd><span class="texhtml"><i>f</i>(<i>f</i>(<i>a</i>)) = <i>a</i></span>. E.g. negation in classical logic.</dd></dl>
<p>For classical and intuitionistic logic, the "=" symbol means that corresponding implications "…→…" and "…←…" for logical compounds can be both proved as theorems, and the "≤" symbol means that "…→…" for logical compounds is a consequence of corresponding "…→…" connectives for propositional variables. Some <a href="/wiki/Many-valued_logic" title="Many-valued logic">many-valued logics</a> may have incompatible definitions of equivalence and order (entailment).
</p><p>Both conjunction and disjunction are associative, commutative and idempotent in classical logic, most varieties of many-valued logic and intuitionistic logic. The same is true about distributivity of conjunction over disjunction and disjunction over conjunction, as well as for the absorption law.
</p><p>In classical logic and some varieties of many-valued logic, conjunction and disjunction are dual, and negation is self-dual, the latter is also self-dual in intuitionistic logic.

<p>However, not all compilers use the same order; for instance, an ordering in which disjunction is lower precedence than implication or bi-implication has also been used.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes precedence between conjunction and disjunction is unspecified requiring to provide it explicitly in given formula with parentheses. The order of precedence determines which connective is the "main connective" when interpreting a non-atomic formula.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_science">Computer science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Computer science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Logical_connective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small><i>(March 2012)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>A truth-functional approach to logical operators is implemented as <a href="/wiki/Logic_gate" title="Logic gate">logic gates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Digital_circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital circuit">digital circuits</a>. Practically all digital circuits (the major exception is <a href="/wiki/DRAM" class="mw-redirect" title="DRAM">DRAM</a>) are built up from <a href="/wiki/Logical_nand" class="mw-redirect" title="Logical nand">NAND</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logical_nor" class="mw-redirect" title="Logical nor">NOR</a>, <a href="/wiki/Negation" title="Negation">NOT</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Logic_gate" title="Logic gate">transmission gates</a>; see more details in <a href="/wiki/Truth_function#Computer_science" title="Truth function">Truth function in computer science</a>. Logical operators over <a href="/wiki/Bit_array" title="Bit array">bit vectors</a> (corresponding to finite <a href="/wiki/Boolean_algebra_(structure)" title="Boolean algebra (structure)">Boolean algebras</a>) are <a href="/wiki/Bitwise_operation" title="Bitwise operation">bitwise operations</a>.
</p><p>But not every usage of a logical connective in <a href="/wiki/Computer_programming" title="Computer programming">computer programming</a> has a Boolean semantic. For example, <a href="/wiki/Lazy_evaluation" title="Lazy evaluation">lazy evaluation</a> is sometimes implemented for <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i> ∧ <i>Q</i></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i> ∨ <i>Q</i></span>, so these connectives are not commutative if some of expressions <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">P</span>, <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">Q</span> has <a href="/wiki/Side_effect_(computer_science)" title="Side effect (computer science)">side effects</a>. Also, a <a href="/wiki/Conditional_(programming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conditional (programming)">conditional</a>, which in some sense corresponds to the <a href="/wiki/Material_conditional" title="Material conditional">material conditional</a> connective, is essentially non-Boolean because for <code>if (P) then Q;</code> the consequent&#160;Q is not executed if the <a href="/wiki/Antecedent_(logic)" title="Antecedent (logic)">antecedent</a>&#160;P is false (although a compound as a whole is successful ≈ "true" in such case). This is closer to intuitionist and <a href="/wiki/Constructive_mathematics" class="mw-redirect" title="Constructive mathematics">constructivist</a> views on the material conditional, rather than to classical logic's ones.
</p>
