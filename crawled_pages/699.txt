<p>Since 1998, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> has <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved</a> government within the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">government</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> are responsible for <a href="/wiki/Reserved_and_excepted_matters" title="Reserved and excepted matters">reserved and excepted matters</a>. Reserved matters are a list of policy areas (such as <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil aviation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" class="mw-redirect" title="Units of measurement">units of measurement</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_genetics" title="Human genetics">human genetics</a>), which the Westminster Parliament may devolve to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> at some time in future. Excepted matters (such as <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>, taxation and elections) are never expected to be considered for devolution. On all other matters, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> together with the 90-member Northern Ireland Assembly may legislate and govern for Northern Ireland. Additionally, devolution in Northern Ireland is dependent upon participation by members of the Northern Ireland Executive in the <a href="/wiki/North/South_Ministerial_Council" title="North/South Ministerial Council">North/South Ministerial Council</a>, which co-ordinates areas of co-operation (such as agriculture, education and health) between Northern Ireland and the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StormontGeneral.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/StormontGeneral.jpg/220px-StormontGeneral.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/StormontGeneral.jpg/330px-StormontGeneral.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/StormontGeneral.jpg/440px-StormontGeneral.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:StormontGeneral.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Parliament_Buildings_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Parliament Buildings (Northern Ireland)">Parliament Buildings</a> at <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Buildings_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Parliament Buildings (Northern Ireland)">Stormont</a>, Belfast, seat of the assembly</div></div></div>
<p>Elections to the Northern Ireland Assembly are by <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a> with five representatives (Members of the Legislative Assembly, MLAs) elected from 18 <a href="/wiki/List_of_parliamentary_constituencies_in_Northern_Ireland" title="List of parliamentary constituencies in Northern Ireland">parliamentary constituencies</a>. Eighteen representatives to the lower house of the British parliament (Members of Parliament, MPs) are elected from the same constituencies using the <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post_voting" title="First-past-the-post voting">first-past-the-post</a> system. However, not all of these take their seats. The seven <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> MPs refuse to take the required <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Allegiance_(United_Kingdom)" title="Oath of Allegiance (United Kingdom)">oath to serve</a> <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a>. In addition, the upper house of the UK's parliament, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, currently has some 25 appointed <a href="/wiki/List_of_Northern_Ireland_members_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="List of Northern Ireland members of the House of Lords">members from Northern Ireland</a>. Northern Ireland itself forms <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="Northern Ireland (European Parliament constituency)">a single constituency</a> for elections to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Office" title="Northern Ireland Office">Northern Ireland Office</a> represents the British government in Northern Ireland on reserved matters. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_Republic_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of the Republic of Ireland">Government of the Republic of Ireland</a> also has the right to "put forward views and proposals" on non-devolved matters in relation to Northern Ireland. The Northern Ireland Office is led by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Northern_Ireland" title="Secretary of State for Northern Ireland">Secretary of State for Northern Ireland</a>, who sits in the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cabinet of the United Kingdom">Cabinet of the United Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>Much of the population of Northern Ireland identifies with one of two different ideologies: <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionism</a> (which wants the region to remain part of the United Kingdom) and <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Irish nationalism</a> (which wants a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a>). Unionists are predominantly <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster Protestant">Ulster Protestant</a>, most of whom belong to the <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_in_Ireland" title="Presbyterian Church in Ireland">Presbyterian Church in Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a>. Irish nationalists are almost wholly <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>. There is also a tiny minority of <a href="/wiki/Ulster_nationalism" title="Ulster nationalism">Ulster nationalists</a> (those who want an independent Northern Irish state), whose religious convictions vary.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_representation">Political representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Northern Ireland currently has the following political representation:
</p>
<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> has 90 <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Legislative_Assembly" title="Member of the Legislative Assembly">Members of the Legislative Assembly</a> (MLAs) (currently 40 <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">unionists</a>, 40 <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">nationalists</a>, 10 others [constitutionally neutral]), which had its powers restored on 8 May 2007.</li>
<li>18 seats in the United Kingdom <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> (currently 11 unionist MPs and 7 nationalist MPs)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>there are several unionists but no nationalists on the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council of the United Kingdom</a> (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Northern_Ireland_members_of_the_Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Northern Ireland members of the Privy Council">List of Northern Ireland members of the Privy Council</a>).</li>
<li>three seats in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> (currently two unionist, one <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">republican</a>)</li>
<li>at local level there are 11 <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Local government in Northern Ireland">district councils</a></li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_systems">Electoral systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electoral systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In all elections in Northern Ireland the <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a> system of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> is used except for the House of Commons elections where a "first past the post" or <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">plurality voting system</a> is used.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_representation_in_the_Republic">Proposed representation in the Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proposed representation in the Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a>, currently the biggest of the nationalist parties in Northern Ireland, has campaigned for a broadening of the franchise of Northern Ireland voters to allow them to vote in elections to choose the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>. It has also demanded that all Northern Ireland <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">Members of the European Parliament</a> (MEPs) and MPs be allowed speaking rights in the lower house of the parliament of the Republic of Ireland, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a>. It was given to understand that the <a href="/wiki/Irish_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish government">Irish government</a> accepted this and had plans to introduce legislation in the autumn of 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a> (SDLP) backed the move. However, a spokesman for <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a> later rowed back, stating that it had never been intended that the MPs have a right to attend plenary sessions of the Dáil, but that they would be invited to participate in <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> committees dealing with Northern Ireland matters, and only if there was all-party agreement behind it. The unionist parties, along with <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Ireland)" title="Labour Party (Ireland)">Labour</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Democrats" title="Progressive Democrats">Progressive Democrats</a> have all declared their opposition to the move, as has much of the Irish media, with articles highly critical of the proposal published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Times" title="The Irish Times">The Irish Times</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_Independent_(Ireland)" title="Sunday Independent (Ireland)">Sunday Independent</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless on 22 November 2007, representatives from both Sinn Féin and the SDLP, (unionists declined the invitation) attended a meeting of the Oireachtas committee reviewing the workings of the Good Friday Agreement. The 18 Northern Ireland MPs can take part in this committee's debates (as well as other relevant committees by invitation), but will not have a right to vote or to move motions and amendments.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_parties">Political parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Northern_Ireland" title="List of political parties in Northern Ireland">List of political parties in Northern Ireland</a></div>
<p>Political parties in Northern Ireland can be divided into three distinct categories:
</p>
<ul><li>unionist parties, such as the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a> (DUP), the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster Unionist Party</a>, (UUP) and other smaller parties such as the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Unionist_Party" title="Progressive Unionist Party">Progressive Unionist Party</a> (PUP) and <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Unionist_Voice" title="Traditional Unionist Voice">Traditional Unionist Voice</a> (TUV)</li>
<li>nationalist parties like <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/SDLP" class="mw-redirect" title="SDLP">SDLP</a></li>
<li>cross-community parties such as the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Alliance Party of Northern Ireland">Alliance Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unionist_parties">Unionist parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unionist parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ulster Unionist Party was historically a cross-class <i>massenpartei</i> who ran the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_of_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive Committee of the Privy Council of Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland Government</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Dominant-party_system" title="Dominant-party system">dominant-party system</a> from its creation until 1972, although since the rise of the DUP in the 1970s, its support has been concentrated more in the middle class. Until 1972 the UUP's members of the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">British House of Commons</a> took the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> <a href="/wiki/Whip_(politics)" title="Whip (politics)">whip</a>, but currently sit as a party in their own right. The UUP's member of the European Parliament belongs to the <a href="/wiki/European_Conservatives_and_Reformists" title="European Conservatives and Reformists">European Conservatives and Reformists</a> Group.
</p><p>The DUP are a more complex mixture than the other major parties, combining support from rural <a href="/wiki/Evangelicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Evangelicals">evangelicals</a> and urban, secular, working class voters. The party is firmly to the right on issues such as abortion, capital punishment, <a href="/wiki/European_integration" title="European integration">European integration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equal_opportunities" class="mw-redirect" title="Equal opportunities">equal opportunities</a>, although it seems to be moderating its stance on <a href="/wiki/Gay_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Gay rights">gay rights</a> since the "<a href="/wiki/Save_Ulster_from_Sodomy" title="Save Ulster from Sodomy">Save Ulster from Sodomy</a>" campaign of the 1980s. Conversely, the DUP often support social programmes which benefit their working class or agricultural base, for example, <a href="/wiki/Free_public_transport" title="Free public transport">free public transport</a> for the elderly and <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_subsidies" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural subsidies">agricultural subsidies</a>. The DUP have grown in recent years as they were originally the only major party to oppose the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a>, although until September 2015 they were part of a government operating it.
</p><p>The smaller, left-leaning <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Unionist_Party" title="Progressive Unionist Party">Progressive Unionist Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Political_Research_Group" title="Ulster Political Research Group">Ulster Political Research Group</a> are linked with the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force" title="Ulster Volunteer Force">Ulster Volunteer Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Defence_Association" title="Ulster Defence Association">Ulster Defence Association</a> respectively.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nationalist_parties">Nationalist parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nationalist parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Similarly, on the nationalist side of the political spectrum, Sinn Féin has overtaken the traditionally dominant SDLP in recent elections. Sinn Féin is a left-wing <a href="/wiki/Irish_republican" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish republican">Irish republican</a> party, committed to espousing an <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">all-Ireland</a> republic. Traditionally the party of the urban Catholic working class and a number of republican rural areas, since the IRA ceasefires of the mid-1990s it has expanded its base considerably and has overtaken the long-dominant SDLP in terms of vote share.
</p><p>The SDLP are a nominally <a href="/wiki/Social_democratic" class="mw-redirect" title="Social democratic">social democratic</a> party and a full member of the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_European_Socialists" title="Party of European Socialists">Party of European Socialists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialist_International" title="Socialist International">Socialist International</a>. However, as the Northern Ireland party system is not based on socio-economic divisions, it attracts a wide spectrum of opinion and has a middle class support base. The SDLP support <a href="/wiki/Irish_reunification" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish reunification">Irish reunification</a>, but utterly reject the use of violence as a means to that end. The SDLP has lost considerable support in the past decade, with the retirement of key figures such as former leader <a href="/wiki/John_Hume" title="John Hume">John Hume</a> and deputy leader <a href="/wiki/Seamus_Mallon" title="Seamus Mallon">Seamus Mallon</a> and the IRA's cessation of violence. The party has members who wish to follow an agenda focusing primarily on "bread and butter issues" (taxation, employment, education, health, etc.) and those who wish to follow a more nationalist campaign to challenge Sinn Féin.
</p><p>Unlike in unionism, religion is—according to the study of Evans and Duffy—not a major factor in patterns of nationalist parties' supporters (although Sinn Féin supporters tend to be more secular). Age has a strong impact on party choice: the more radical Sinn Féin has more support among the young than the SDLP has. The most important factor is attachment to nationalist ideology: Sinn Féin has high levels of support among people strongly committed to nationalism<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cross-community_and_other_parties">Cross-community and other parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cross-community and other parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the cross-community parties, the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Alliance Party of Northern Ireland">Alliance Party of Northern Ireland</a> draws its support mainly from middle class professionals in the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>. It professes to be the only significant party which does not base its political stance around the constitutional question. The party has strong links with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> and is a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Liberal_Democrat_and_Reform_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="European Liberal Democrat and Reform Party">European Liberal Democrat and Reform Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_International" title="Liberal International">Liberal International</a>.
</p><p>Other parties who contest elections in Northern Ireland include the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Ireland" title="Workers&#39; Party of Ireland">Workers' Party</a> and the Northern Ireland branch of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Feminist" class="mw-redirect" title="Feminist">feminist</a> <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Women%27s_Coalition" title="Northern Ireland Women&#39;s Coalition">Northern Ireland Women's Coalition</a> briefly held seats in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, but is now defunct. <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Third_Way" title="Ulster Third Way">Ulster Third Way</a> was a small grouping advocating <a href="/wiki/Ulster_nationalism" title="Ulster nationalism">independence for Northern Ireland</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a>, the second-largest party in the Republic, opened a <a href="/wiki/Cumann" title="Cumann">cumann</a> (branch) in <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, and began recruiting at <a href="/wiki/Queens_University_Belfast" class="mw-redirect" title="Queens University Belfast">Queens University Belfast</a>. The leadership as of  2005<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> had decided not to take part in electoral politics in Northern Ireland, however in the latter part of 2007 the Taoiseach said his party was consulting its grassroots on the possibility of contesting elections in the North, and that in advance of this Fianna Fáil had registered as a political party in Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Some, within both Fianna Fáil and the SDLP (including former SDLP European elections candidate <a href="/wiki/Martin_Morgan" title="Martin Morgan">Martin Morgan</a>) have advocated an alliance, or even a merger, between both parties. However, many in both parties are hostile to the idea, with some in the SDLP pointing out the left-wing links between the party and the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Ireland)" title="Labour Party (Ireland)">Irish Labour Party</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_political_parties_in_Northern_Ireland">Future of political parties in Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Future of political parties in Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some commentators believe there are indications that the religious and ethnic basis of the party system may start to disintegrate. For example, in the 1998–2003 Assembly, there was a Catholic <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Legislative_Assembly" title="Member of the Legislative Assembly">Member of the Legislative Assembly</a> sitting for the <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster Unionist Party</a>. The SDLP have had a number of Protestant representatives in the past and a Protestant SDLP councillor defected to Sinn Féin in 2004. Up to now, these have been one-off events, which have occurred periodically throughout Northern Ireland's history without setting a trend—<i>cf</i> Sir <a href="/wiki/Denis_Henry" title="Denis Henry">Denis Henry</a> in the early part of the 20th century. In any event, social class is an important part of competition within the main ethnic political blocs,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and class-based party structures in other established democracies have weakened since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. Since the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">peace process</a>, the less overtly ethnic parties have declined, while the more radical Sinn Féin and DUP have prospered.
</p><p>There are indications that party stances on issues like <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a> are causing people who feel strongly about these issues to vote accordingly, rather than on the constitutional issue.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_demography">Political demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Political demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Demography_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Demography of Northern Ireland">Demography of Northern Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Religion in Northern Ireland">Religion in Northern Ireland</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png/450px-Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png" width="450" height="350" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png/675px-Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png/900px-Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png 2x" data-file-width="19059" data-file-height="14839" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Religion_or_religion_brought_up_in.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of religion or religion brought up in from the 2011 census in Northern Ireland. Stronger blue indicates a higher proportion of Catholics. Stronger red indicates a higher proportion of Protestants.</div></div></div>
<p>Once established under the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>, Northern Ireland, since it was an approximation of that area where those favouring remaining part of the UK were in the majority, was <a href="/wiki/Segregation_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Segregation in Northern Ireland">structured geographically</a> in a way which guaranteed a unionist majority in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a>.
</p><p>The proportion of people claiming to be Roman Catholic in the Northern Ireland Census has increased since the 1920s,<sup id="cite_ref-CAIN_Web_Service_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAIN_Web_Service-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> although the rate of this increase has slowed in recent years. In contrast, the proportion of people claiming to be Presbyterian and Church of Ireland in the census has decreased. A Catholic plurality over Protestants is predicted by the time of the 2021 census,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> with Catholics dominant to the west and south of Northern Ireland, while Protestants are expected to retain a majority primarily to the east and north. The anticipated Catholic plurality is based on the assumption that the current trends of demographic change will continue, but at a slower rate than previously. The last 20 years have seen a 10.5% reduction in the proportion of the population who state they are Protestant or brought up Protestant (from 58.5% to 48%), and a 3.5% increase in those of who state they are Catholic or brought up Catholic (from 41.5% to 45%).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between the 2001 and 2011 censuses there was an unprecedented wave of migration into Northern Ireland following <a href="/wiki/2004_enlargement_of_the_European_Union" title="2004 enlargement of the European Union">the accession of eight countries into the EU</a>. Since 2004, Northern Ireland has welcomed a disproportionate number of <a href="/wiki/Worker_Registration_Scheme#A8_Countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Worker Registration Scheme">A8</a> citizens (particularly Polish citizens) compared with the rest of the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Most of these new migrants from the A8 countries were Catholic. Of the entire Catholic population in the 2011 census, 3.1% were born in an A8 country.<sup id="cite_ref-DC2253NIa_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC2253NIa-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the 2011 census 1.24%<sup id="cite_ref-DC2253NIa_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC2253NIa-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> of the population of Northern Ireland were Catholics born in an A8 country. In comparison, between the 2001 and 2011 censuses the proportion of the total population claiming to be Catholic only increased by +0.50% (from 40.26%<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> to 40.76%<sup id="cite_ref-DC2253NIa_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DC2253NIa-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>). The period of this migration, from 2004 onwards, did not correspond with any rise in the share of the vote for nationalist political parties.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Views_on_the_Union">Views on the Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Views on the Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a 2014 Northern Ireland Life and Times Survey carried out by Queens University Belfast and Ulster University, 66% supported remaining part of the United Kingdom via devolved government or direct rule, with support for leaving the UK and forming a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a> at 17%. In terms of religion, 50% of Northern Ireland <a href="/wiki/Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholics">Catholics</a> supported remaining part of the United Kingdom via devolved government or direct rule, usually while also supporting nationalist political parties.<sup id="cite_ref-ark.ac.uk_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ark.ac.uk-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The proportion of Catholics supporting a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a> was 32%, according to the same poll. The proportion of Protestants in the study who wished to join the Republic was 4%, with 87% preferring to remain in the United Kingdom in some form.<sup id="cite_ref-ark.ac.uk_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ark.ac.uk-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> There are also considerable numbers of people who give ambiguous answers to questions about the future constitutional status of Northern Ireland. Some nationalists have historically sought a favourable arrangement for Ireland within the United Kingdom. Some Protestants (such as paramilitaries and their supporters) usually term themselves as <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">loyalists</a> as well as unionists. As a result, the term "loyalist" has become less popular among unionists in recent decades, especially with unionist politicians. A small minority of people from both religious backgrounds<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> advocate <a href="/wiki/Ulster_nationalism" title="Ulster nationalism">independence for Northern Ireland</a> (possibly accompanied by some form of realignment of the border with the Republic). Support for this, while fluctuating, is regarded as insignificant.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_identity">National identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: National identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png/450px-Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png" width="450" height="350" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png/675px-Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png/900px-Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png 2x" data-file-width="18996" data-file-height="14791" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_predominant_national_identity_in_the_2011_census_in_Northern_Ireland.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of predominant national identity in the 2011 census in Northern Ireland</div></div></div>
<p>In Northern Ireland, national identity is complex and diverse. Many in Northern Ireland have a <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British national identity</a> seeing the English, Scots and Welsh as fellow members of their common nation while seeing those from the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> as foreigners. Many others in Northern Ireland see those from the Republic of Ireland as being members of their common nation encompassing the island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> and see the English, Scots and Welsh as foreigners. Co-existing with this dichotomy is a Northern Irish identity which can be held alone or, as is also the case with Englishness, Scottishness and Welshness, alongside a British identity, or alongside an Irish identity. A small number of people see themselves as being both British and Irish.
</p><p>While there is a strong correlation in Northern Ireland between religious background and the perception of which geographical area forms the nation to which that person feels they belong, it is not a strict relationship and national identity is not simply distributed proportionally in accordance with the percentages of different religions in a particular area. For example, Catholics overall are almost five times more likely to view themselves as being British only, than Protestants are to view themselves as being Irish only. In the 2011 census there were four of the twenty-six districts in Northern Ireland, all on the eastern seaboard, where Catholics were more likely to view themselves as being British than Irish; Carrickfergus, Larne, North Down and Ards, whereas even in those districts where Protestants were most likely to view themselves as Irish, such as Derry, Fermanagh and Newry and Mourne, Protestants were still more than ten times more likely to view themselves as British than Irish.
</p><p>While in the 2011 census Protestants outnumbered Catholics in only half of the districts in Northern Ireland, those who considered themselves British outnumbered those who considered themselves Irish in twenty of the twenty-six districts. This is partly because Catholics were more likely to see themselves as British than Protestants were to see themselves as Irish, but is also partly because those of no religion were substantially more likely to see themselves as British as see themselves as Irish.

<p><b>National identity and constitutional preference</b>
</p><p>Like the relationship between religion and national identity the relationship between national identity and constitutional preference, whether Northern Ireland should stay part of the United Kingdom or become part of a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a> state, presents a strong correlation, but not an absolute one. In 2016 an Ipsos Mori poll asked "If there was a referendum on the border would you:" and answers for people giving different national identities were as follows,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>

<p>A 1997 publication by Democratic Dialogue financed by the Central Community Relations Unit of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Office" title="Northern Ireland Office">Northern Ireland Office</a> stated that "It is clear that many in Northern Ireland are willing to tolerate the Other's cultural identity only within the confines of their own core ideology... most nationalists have extreme difficulty in accepting unionists' Britishness or, even if they do, the idea that unionists do not constitute an Irish ethnic minority which can ultimately be accommodated within the Irish nation...." Discussion may be hindered by the lack of definitions which command cross-community support. For example, with regard to "Irishness", the 1997 publication stated that "Irishness is a highly contested identity, subject to fundamentally different nationalist and unionist perceptions which profoundly affect notions of allegiance and group membership.".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Four polls taken between 1989 and 1994 revealed that when asked to state their national identity, over 79% of Northern Ireland Protestants replied "British" or "Ulster" with 3% or less replying "Irish", while over 60% of Northern Irish Catholics replied "Irish" with 13% or less replying "British" or "Ulster".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many people in Northern Ireland consider themselves both British and Irish, or hold some other combination of identities, as can be seen in the annual results of the Northern Ireland Life and Times Survey. In 1999, for example, the survey found that 91% of Roman Catholics and 48% of Protestants thought of themselves as strongly or weakly Irish.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, 55% of people who declared themselves to be neither Protestant nor Catholic (and this would have included people of Protestant or Roman Catholic backgrounds as well as people of other faiths, none and immigrants) thought of themselves as strongly or weakly Irish.
