<p><b>Ask.com</b> (originally known as <b>Ask Jeeves</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Question_answering" title="Question answering">question answering</a>-focused <a href="/wiki/E-business" class="mw-redirect" title="E-business">e-business</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">web search engine</a> founded in 1996 by <a href="/wiki/Garrett_Gruener" title="Garrett Gruener">Garrett Gruener</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Warthen" title="David Warthen">David Warthen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berkeley,_California" title="Berkeley, California">Berkeley</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.
</p><p>The original software was implemented by <a href="/wiki/Gary_Chevsky" title="Gary Chevsky">Gary Chevsky</a> from his own design. Warthen, Chevsky, Justin Grant, and others built the early AskJeeves.com website around that core engine. In late 2010, facing insurmountable competition from more popular search engines, the company outsourced its web search technology and returned to its roots as a <a href="/wiki/Question_and_answer_site" class="mw-redirect" title="Question and answer site">question and answer site</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Leeds" class="mw-redirect" title="Douglas Leeds">Douglas Leeds</a> was elevated from president to CEO in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-IAC_Management_Bios_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAC_Management_Bios-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ask.com has been criticized for its <a href="/wiki/Browser_toolbar" title="Browser toolbar">browser toolbar</a>, which has been accused of behaving like <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a> due to its <a href="/wiki/Potentially_unwanted_program" title="Potentially unwanted program">bundling with other software</a> and the difficulty of its uninstallation.
</p><p>Three venture capital firms, <a href="/wiki/Highland_Capital_Partners" title="Highland Capital Partners">Highland Capital Partners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Institutional_Venture_Partners" title="Institutional Venture Partners">Institutional Venture Partners</a>, and The RODA Group were early investors.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Ask.com is currently owned by <a href="/wiki/IAC_(company)" title="IAC (company)">InterActiveCorp</a> (IAC) under the <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> symbol <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nasdaq.com/symbol/iac">IAC</a>.
</p><p>Ask.com's corporate headquarters are located at <a href="/wiki/555_City_Center" title="555 City Center">555 City Center</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_City_Center" title="Oakland City Center">Oakland City Center</a> development in downtown <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland, California</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ask.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:129px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jeeves-in-2009.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cd/Jeeves-in-2009.jpg" width="127" height="269" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="127" data-file-height="269" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jeeves-in-2009.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jeeves appears when users go to uk.ask.com, As of  April&#160;2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ask.com&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div><p>Ask.com was originally known as Ask Jeeves, "Jeeves" being the name of a "gentleman's personal gentleman", or <a href="/wiki/Valet" title="Valet">valet</a>, fetching answers to any question asked. The character was based on <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Wooster" title="Bertie Wooster">Bertie Wooster</a>'s valet <a href="/wiki/Jeeves" title="Jeeves">Jeeves</a>, in the fictional works of <a href="/wiki/P._G._Wodehouse" title="P. G. Wodehouse">P.&#160;G. Wodehouse</a>.
</p><p>The original idea behind Ask Jeeves was to allow users to get answers to questions posed in everyday, <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a>, as well as by traditional keyword searching. The current Ask.com still supports this, with support for math, dictionary, and conversion questions.
</p><p>In 2005 the company announced plans to phase out Jeeves and on February 27, 2006, the character disappeared from Ask.com. He was stated to be "going into retirement."  However, the UK/Ireland edition of the website prominently brought the character back in 2009.
</p><p>IAC owns a variety of sites including country-specific sites for UK, Germany, Italy, Japan, the Netherlands, and Spain along with Ask Kids, <a href="/wiki/Teoma" title="Teoma">Teoma</a> (now ExpertRank<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>) and <a href="/wiki/IAC_(company)#Properties" title="IAC (company)">several others</a>. On June 5, 2007, Ask.com relaunched with a 3D look.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 16, 2006, Ask implemented a "Binoculars Site Preview" into its search results. On search results pages, the "Binoculars" let searchers have a sneak peek of the page they could visit with a mouse-over activating a pop-up screenshot.
</p><p>
In December 2007, Ask released the AskEraser feature,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> allowing users to <a href="/wiki/Opt-out" title="Opt-out">opt-out</a> from tracking of <a href="/wiki/Web_search_query" title="Web search query">search queries</a> and <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP</a> and <a href="/wiki/HTTP_cookie" title="HTTP cookie">cookie</a> values. They also vowed to erase this data after 18 months if the AskEraser option is not set. <a href="/wiki/HTTP_cookies" class="mw-redirect" title="HTTP cookies">HTTP cookies</a> must be enabled for AskEraser to function.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ask_with_wikipedia.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c6/Ask_with_wikipedia.png/220px-Ask_with_wikipedia.png" width="220" height="349" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c6/Ask_with_wikipedia.png 1.5x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="397" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ask_with_wikipedia.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An Ask.com search of Wikipedia.</div></div></div><p> On July 4, 2008, <a href="/wiki/IAC_(company)" title="IAC (company)">InterActiveCorp</a> announced the acquisition of Lexico Publishing Group, which owns <a href="/wiki/Dictionary.com" title="Dictionary.com">Dictionary.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thesaurus.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Thesaurus.com">Thesaurus.com</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Reference.com" title="Reference.com">Reference.com</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 26, 2010, Ask.com released a closed-beta Q&amp;A service. The service was released to the public on July 29, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Ask.com launched its mobile Q&amp;A app for the iPhone in late 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ask.com now reaches 100 million global users per month<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> through its website with more than 2 million downloads of its flagship mobile app.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The company has also released additional apps spun out of its Q&amp;A experience, including Ask Around<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> in 2011 and PollRoll<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> in 2012.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Search_engine_shut-down">Search engine shut-down</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ask.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Search engine shut-down">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Ask.com abandoned the search industry, with the loss of 130 search engineering jobs, because it could not compete against more popular search engines such as <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>. Earlier in the year, Ask had launched a Q&amp;A community for generating answers from real people as opposed to search algorithms then combined this with its question-and–answer repository, utilizing its extensive history of archived query data to search sites that provide answers to questions people have.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> To avoid a situation in which no answers were available from its own resources, the company outsourced to an unnamed third-party search provider the comprehensive web search matches that it had gathered itself.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ask_Sponsored_Listings">Ask Sponsored Listings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ask.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ask Sponsored Listings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Formerly the direct sales engine for Ask.com, Ask Sponsored Listings is no longer available, having merged with Sendori, an operating business of IAC, in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_details">Corporate details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ask.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Corporate details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Askcomheadquarters.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Askcomheadquarters.jpg/220px-Askcomheadquarters.jpg" width="220" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Askcomheadquarters.jpg/330px-Askcomheadquarters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Askcomheadquarters.jpg/440px-Askcomheadquarters.jpg 2x" data-file-width="764" data-file-height="732" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Askcomheadquarters.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ask.com headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>, California</div></div></div><p>Ask Jeeves, Inc. stock traded on the NASDAQ stock exchange from July 1999 to July 2005, under the ticker symbol ASKJ. In July 2005, the ASKJ ticker was retired upon the acquisition by IAC, valuing  at US$1.85&#160;billion.
</p><p>In 2012 Ask.com made two acquisitions as part of a larger strategy to offer more content on the Ask.com website. On July 2, 2012, Ask.com purchased content discovery start-up<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> nRelate, for an undisclosed amount. That was followed by the company's acquisition of expert advice and information site About.com, which closed in September 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 14, 2014, Ask.com acquired popular social networking website <a href="/wiki/ASKfm" class="mw-redirect" title="ASKfm">ASKfm</a>, where users can ask other users questions, with the option of anonymity.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> As of August 14, 2014, Ask.fm had 180 million monthly unique users in more than 150 countries around the world,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> with its largest user base in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Available on the web and as a mobile app, ASKfm generates an estimated 20,000 questions per minute with approximately 45 percent of its mobile <a href="/wiki/Monthly_active_users" title="Monthly active users">monthly active users</a> logging in daily.<sup id="cite_ref-Techcrunch_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Techcrunch-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> To date, the mobile app has been downloaded more than 40 million times.<sup id="cite_ref-Techcrunch_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Techcrunch-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toolbar_criticism">Toolbar criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ask.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Toolbar criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ask <a href="/wiki/Browser_toolbar" title="Browser toolbar">browser toolbar</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Browser_extension" title="Browser extension">extension</a> that can appear as an extra bar added to the browser's window and/or menu. In early versions, it was often unintentionally installed during the installation of partner software, including Oracle <a href="/wiki/Java_(software_platform)" title="Java (software platform)">Java</a>, i.e., taking advantage of a user's lack of technical experience.<sup id="cite_ref-LookInto_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LookInto-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> As an operating business of IAC, Ask Partner Network had also historically entered into partnerships with some software security vendors,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> whereby they distributed the toolbar alongside their software.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Installer packages for partner companies had an option (opt-out) to install the Ask toolbar and/or change the user's default browser home page to Ask.com.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ask.com and its parent company IAC have therefore been criticized for promoting a toolbar that behaves like <a href="/wiki/Malware" title="Malware">malware</a>—that it was surreptitiously bundled with legitimate program installations, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Oracle_(software)" class="mw-redirect" title="Oracle (software)">Oracle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Java_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Java (language)">Java</a>, that it could not be easily removed from common browsers once installed, that consumers installed the software unwittingly, that the toolbar redefined the user's home page to Ask.com, and that Ask.com presented biased search results.<sup id="cite_ref-LookInto_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LookInto-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> As early toolbar versions could not be easily removed using built-in uninstall features, it was considered a "potentially unwanted program".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RemoveAsk_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RemoveAsk-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A further criticism was a ten-minute delay that was built into the installation, between updating Java and appearance of the Ask toolbar.<sup id="cite_ref-OracleCrapware_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OracleCrapware-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The company defended these early business decisions,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2016)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> pointing out that instructions to remove the toolbar could be found at the Ask.com Help Center.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of June 2015, Ask.com no longer bundles with Oracle's Java (which now features a <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> toolbar).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> As of June 2015, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> does not consider the toolbar that is being provided by Ask.com to be unwanted software, but they state that older versions of the toolbar pose "a high threat to your PC," and they provide tools for detecting and removing them.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_and_promotion">Marketing and promotion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ask.com&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marketing and promotion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Apostolos_Gerasoulis" title="Apostolos Gerasoulis">Apostolos Gerasoulis</a>, the co-creator of Ask's Teoma algorithmic search technology, starred in four television advertisements in 2007, extolling the virtues of Ask.com's usefulness for information relevance.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> A Jeeves balloon appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Macy%27s_Thanksgiving_Day_Parade" title="Macy&#39;s Thanksgiving Day Parade">Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade</a> through 2000-2004.
</p><p>After a hiatus from mass consumer marketing, Ask returned to TV advertising in the fall of 2011 after refocusing its site on questions and answers.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Instead of national advertising, Ask focused on local markets with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Basic_creative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Basic creative (page does not exist)">basic creative</a>. In the summer of 2012, Ask launched a national cinema campaign,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> along with other out-of-home tactics in certain markets such as New York and <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of a Seattle-based local market effort, Ask.com launched its
“You Asked We Answered”<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> campaign in 2012, in which the company “answered” residents' top complaints about living in their city, including easing morning commutes and stadium traffic, as well as keeping the local Parks and Rec department wading pools open.
</p><p>On January 14, 2009, Ask.com became the official sponsor of 2000 <a href="/wiki/NASCAR" title="NASCAR">NASCAR</a> <a href="/wiki/Sprint_Cup_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Sprint Cup Series">Sprint Cup Series</a> Champion <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Labonte" title="Bobby Labonte">Bobby Labonte</a>'s No.&#160;96 <a href="/wiki/Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford">Ford</a>. Ask would become the official search engine of NASCAR.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Ask.com was the primary sponsor for the No.&#160;96 for 18 of the first 21 races and had rights to increase this to a total of 29 races that season.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The Ask.com car debuted in the 2009 Bud Shootout where it failed to finish the race, but subsequently returned strongly, placing as high as 5th in March 1, 2009 <a href="/wiki/Shelby_427" class="mw-redirect" title="Shelby 427">Shelby 427</a> race at <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Motor_Speedway" title="Las Vegas Motor Speedway">Las Vegas Motor Speedway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Ask.com's foray into NASCAR represented the first instance of its venture into what it calls <a href="/wiki/Vertical_search" title="Vertical search">"Super Verticals"</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
