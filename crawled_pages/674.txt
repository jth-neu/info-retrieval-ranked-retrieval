<p><i><b>The BMJ</b></i> is a weekly <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer-reviewed</a> <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Healthcare journal">medical journal</a>. It is one of the world's oldest general medical journals. Originally called the <i><b>British Medical Journal</b></i>, the title was officially shortened to <i>BMJ</i> in 1988, and then changed to <i>The BMJ</i> in 2014. The journal is published by the global knowledge provider BMJ, a <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">wholly owned subsidiary</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Medical_Association" title="British Medical Association">British Medical Association</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Editor-in-chief" title="Editor-in-chief">editor in chief</a> of <i>The BMJ</i> is <a href="/wiki/Fiona_Godlee" title="Fiona Godlee">Fiona Godlee</a>, who was appointed in February 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The journal began publishing on 3 October 1840 as the <i>Provincial Medical and Surgical Journal</i> and quickly attracted the attention of physicians around the world through its publication of high-impact original research articles and unique case reports.<sup id="cite_ref-MoM_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <i>BMJ'</i>s first editors were <a href="/wiki/P._Hennis_Green" class="mw-redirect" title="P. Hennis Green">P. Hennis Green</a>, lecturer on the diseases of children at the <a href="/wiki/Hunterian_School_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunterian School of Medicine">Hunterian School of Medicine</a>, who also was its founder and Robert Streeten of <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a>, a member of the PMSA council.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:208px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bmjfirstcovershop.GIF" class="image"><img alt="Image of the cover of 1st issue of the Provincial Medical and Surgical Journal" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d1/Bmjfirstcovershop.GIF" width="206" height="278" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="206" data-file-height="278" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bmjfirstcovershop.GIF" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cover of the 1st issue of the <i>Provincial Medical and Surgical Journal</i></div></div></div>
<p>The first issue of the <i>British Medical Journal</i> was 16 pages long and contained three simple woodcut illustrations. The longest items were the editors' introductory editorial and a report of the Provincial Medical and Surgical Association's Eastern Branch. Other pages included a condensed version of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Warburton" title="Henry Warburton">Henry Warburton</a>'s medical reform bill, book reviews, clinical papers, and case notes. There were &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> columns of advertisements. Inclusive of stamp duty it cost 7d, a price which remained until 1844. In their main article, Green and Streeten noted that they had "received as many advertisements (in proportion to the quantity of letter press) for our first number, as the most popular Medical Journal, (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">The Lancet</a></i>) after seventeen years of existence."<sup id="cite_ref-MoM_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In their introductory editorial and later statements, Green and Streeten defined "the main objects of promotion of which the <i>Provincial Medical and Surgical Journal</i> is established". Summarised, there were two clear main objectives: the advancement of the profession, especially in the provinces and the dissemination of medical knowledge. Green and Streeten also expressed interest in promoting public well-being as well as maintaining 'medical practitioners, as a class in that rank of society which, by their intellectual acquirements, by their general moral character, and by the importance of the duties entrusted to them, they are justly entitled to hold'.<sup id="cite_ref-MoM_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The BMJ</i> published the first centrally randomised controlled trial.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The journal also carried the seminal papers on the causal effects of smoking on health<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and lung cancer and other causes of death in relation to smoking.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For a long time, the journal's sole competitor was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">The Lancet</a></i>, also based in the UK, but with increasing globalisation, <i>The BMJ</i> has faced tough competition from other medical journals, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_England_Journal_of_Medicine" title="The New England Journal of Medicine">The New England Journal of Medicine</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Medical_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Journal of the American Medical Association">Journal of the American Medical Association</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Journal_content">Journal content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Journal content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The BMJ</i> is an advocate of <a href="/wiki/Evidence-based_medicine" title="Evidence-based medicine">evidence-based medicine</a>. It publishes research as well as clinical reviews, recent medical advances, editorial perspectives, among others.
</p><p>A special "Christmas Edition" is published annually on the Friday before Christmas. This edition is known for research articles which apply a serious academic approach to investigating less serious medical questions.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tis_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tis-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The results are often humorous and widely reported by the mainstream media.<sup id="cite_ref-Tis_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tis-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The BMJ</i> has an <a href="/wiki/Open_peer_review" title="Open peer review">open peer review</a> system, wherein authors are told who reviewed their manuscript. About half the original articles are rejected after review in-house.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Manuscripts chosen for peer review are first reviewed by external experts, who comment on the importance and suitability for publication, before the final decision on a manuscript is made by the editorial ("hanging") committee. The acceptance rate is less than 7% for original research articles.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing_and_citations">Indexing and citations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Indexing and citations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The BMJ</i> is included in the major indexes <a href="/wiki/PubMed" title="PubMed">PubMed</a>, <a href="/wiki/MEDLINE" title="MEDLINE">MEDLINE</a>, <a href="/wiki/EBSCO_Industries" title="EBSCO Industries">EBSCO</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Science_Citation_Index" title="Science Citation Index">Science Citation Index</a>. The journal has long criticised the misuse of the impact factor to award grants and recruit researchers by academic institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid9056804_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9056804-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The five journals that as of  2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> have cited <i>The BMJ</i> most often are (in order of descending citation frequency) <i>The BMJ</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cochrane_Collaboration" class="mw-redirect" title="Cochrane Collaboration">Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">The Lancet</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/BMC_journals" class="mw-redirect" title="BMC journals">BMC Public Health</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/BMC_Health_Services_Research" title="BMC Health Services Research">BMC Health Services Research</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-WoS_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WoS-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the five journals that have been cited most frequently by articles published in <i>The BMJ</i> are <i>The BMJ</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lancet" title="The Lancet">The Lancet</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_England_Journal_of_Medicine" title="The New England Journal of Medicine">The New England Journal of Medicine</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_the_American_Medical_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Journal of the American Medical Association">Journal of the American Medical Association</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Cochrane_Collaboration" class="mw-redirect" title="Cochrane Collaboration">Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-WoS_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WoS-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Website_and_access_policies">Website and access policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Website and access policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The BMJ</i> went fully online in 1995 and has archived all its issues on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">web</a>. In addition to the print content, supporting material for original research articles, additional news stories, and electronic letters to the editors are its principal attractions. <i>The BMJ</i> website has the policy of publishing most e-letters to the journal, called Rapid Responses,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and is shaped like a fully moderated Internet forum. As of  January&#160;2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> there had been 88 500 rapid responses posted on the BMJ website.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ_RR_Jan2013_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ_RR_Jan2013-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Comments are screened for libellous and obscene content, however potential contributors are warned that once published, they will not have the right to remove or edit their response.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ_RR_Jan2013_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ_RR_Jan2013-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1999, all content of <i>The BMJ</i> was freely available online; however, in 2006 this changed to a subscription model. Original research articles continue to be available freely, but from January 2006, all other 'added value' contents, including clinical reviews and editorials, require a subscription. <i>The BMJ</i> allows complete free access for visitors from economically disadvantaged countries as part of the <a href="/wiki/HINARI" title="HINARI">HINARI</a> initiative.
</p><p>On 14 October 2008, <i>The BMJ</i> announced it would become an <a href="/wiki/Open_access_(publishing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access (publishing)">open access</a> journal. This only refers to their research articles. To view other articles, a subscription is required.<sup id="cite_ref-Suber-OA_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suber-OA-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Editions">Editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_BMJ&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The BMJ</i> is principally an online journal, and it is only the website which carries the full text content of every article. However, a number of print editions are produced, targeting different groups of readers with selections of content, some of it abridged, and different advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The print editions are:
</p>
<ul><li>General Practice (weekly) for <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">general practitioners</a></li>
<li>Clinical Research (weekly) for hospital doctors</li>
<li>Academic (monthly) for institutions, researchers and medical academics</li></ul>
<p>In addition,  <i>The BMJ</i> also publishes a number of overseas/ foreign language editions: Argentinian (in Spanish), Greek, Romanian, Chinese, and Middle Eastern (in English). There is also <i><a href="/wiki/Student_BMJ" title="Student BMJ">Student BMJ</a></i>, an online resource for medical students and junior doctors which publishes an annual print edition each September.
</p>
