<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Pax Britannica</i></h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><i><b>Pax Britannica</b></i> (Latin for "British Peace", modelled after <i><a href="/wiki/Pax_Romana" title="Pax Romana">Pax Romana</a></i>) was the <a href="/wiki/International_relations_of_the_Great_Powers_(1814%E2%80%931919)" title="International relations of the Great Powers (1814–1919)">period of relative peace between the Great Powers</a> during which the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> became the global <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemonic</a> power and adopted the role of a global police force.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1815 and 1914, a period referred to as Britain's "imperial century",<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> around 10,000,000 square miles (26,000,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of territory and roughly 400 million people were added to the British Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Victory over <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleonic France">Napoleonic France</a> left the British without any serious international rival, other than perhaps <a href="/wiki/The_Great_Game" title="The Great Game">Russia in central Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-#refOHBEv3&#124;Porter,_p._401_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-#refOHBEv3|Porter,_p._401-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> When Russia tried expanding its influence in the Balkans, the British and French defeated them in the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a> (1854–1856), thereby protecting the feeble <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>.
</p><p>Britain's <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> controlled most of the key maritime trade routes and enjoyed <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy#1815–1914" title="Royal Navy">unchallenged sea power</a>. Alongside the formal control exerted over its own colonies, Britain's dominant position in world trade meant that it effectively controlled access to many regions, such as <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a>. British merchants, shippers and bankers had such an overwhelming advantage over everyone else that in addition to its colonies it had an  "<a href="/wiki/Informal_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Informal Empire">informal empire</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pax_Britannica&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_foreign_relations_of_the_United_Kingdom#1814–1914" title="History of the foreign relations of the United Kingdom">History of the foreign relations of the United Kingdom §&#160;1814–1914</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_relations_of_the_Great_Powers_(1814%E2%80%931919)" title="International relations of the Great Powers (1814–1919)">International relations of the Great Powers (1814–1919)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_British_diplomatic_history#1815–1860" title="Timeline of British diplomatic history">Timeline of British diplomatic history §&#160;1815–1860</a></div>
<p>After losing the American colonies in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, Britain turned towards Asia, the Pacific and later Africa with subsequent exploration leading to the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Second_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Second British Empire">Second British Empire</a> (1783–1815). The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> began in Great Britain in the late 1700s and new ideas emerged about free markets, such as Adam Smith's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wealth_of_Nations" title="The Wealth of Nations">The Wealth of Nations</a></i> (1776). Free trade became a central principle that Britain practiced by the 1840s. It played a key role in Britain's <a href="/wiki/European_Miracle" class="mw-redirect" title="European Miracle">economic growth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_financial_system#Emergence_of_financial_globalization:_1870–1914" title="Global financial system">financial dominance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-refDarwin2012_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refDarwin2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the end of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> in 1815 until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in 1914, the United Kingdom played the role of global <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemon</a> (most powerful actor).  Imposition of a "British Peace" on key maritime trade routes began in 1815 with the annexation of <a href="/wiki/British_Ceylon#Kandyan_Wars" title="British Ceylon">British Ceylon</a> (now Sri Lanka).<sup id="cite_ref-refCrawfurd1830_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refCrawfurd1830-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/British_Residency_of_the_Persian_Gulf" class="mw-redirect" title="British Residency of the Persian Gulf">British Residency of the Persian Gulf</a>, local Arab rulers agreed to a number of treaties that formalised Britain’s protection of the region. Britain imposed an anti-piracy treaty, known as the <a href="/wiki/General_Maritime_Treaty_of_1820" title="General Maritime Treaty of 1820">General Maritime Treaty of 1820</a>, on all Arab rulers in the region. By signing the Perpetual Maritime Truce of 1853, Arab rulers gave up their right to wage war at sea in return for British protection against external threats.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The global superiority of <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British military</a> and commerce was aided by a divided and relatively weak continental Europe, and the presence of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> on all of the world's oceans and seas. Even outside its formal empire, Britain controlled trade with many countries such as China, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Siam</a>, and Argentina. Following the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> the British Empire's economic strength continued to develop through naval dominance<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and diplomatic efforts to maintain a <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_(international_relations)" title="Balance of power (international relations)">balance of power</a> in continental Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-refThackeray2002_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refThackeray2002-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In this era, the Royal Navy provided services around the world that benefited other nations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Piracy#History" title="Piracy">suppression of piracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blockade_of_Africa" title="Blockade of Africa">blocking the slave trade</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Slave_Trade_Act_1807" title="Slave Trade Act 1807">Slave Trade Act 1807</a> had banned the trade across the British Empire, after which the Royal Navy established the <a href="/wiki/West_Africa_Squadron" title="West Africa Squadron">West Africa Squadron</a> and the government negotiated <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_slavery_timeline#1800–1849" class="mw-redirect" title="Abolition of slavery timeline">international treaties</a> under which they could enforce the ban.<sup id="cite_ref-refFalola2007_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refFalola2007-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Sea power, however, did not project on land. Land wars fought between the major powers include the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Italian_War_of_Independence" title="Second Italian War of Independence">Franco-Austrian War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Prussian_War" title="Austro-Prussian War">Austro-Prussian War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>, as well as numerous conflicts between lesser powers. The Royal Navy prosecuted the <a href="/wiki/First_Opium_War" title="First Opium War">First Opium War</a> (1839–1842) and <a href="/wiki/Second_Opium_War" title="Second Opium War">Second Opium War</a> (1856–1860) against <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Imperial China</a>. The Royal Navy was superior to any other two navies in the world, combined. Between 1815 and the passage of the German naval laws of 1890 and 1898, only France was a potential naval threat.
</p><p>Britain <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_the_United_Kingdom#19th_century" title="Economic history of the United Kingdom">traded goods and capital</a> extensively with countries around the world, adopting a free trade policy after 1840. The growth of British imperial strength was further underpinned by the <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamship</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Telegraphy" title="Telegraphy">telegraph</a>, new technologies invented in the second half of the 19th century, allowing it to control and defend the empire. By 1902, the British Empire was linked together by a network of telegraph cables, the so-called <a href="/wiki/All_Red_Line" title="All Red Line">All Red Line</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Pax Britannica</i> was weakened by the breakdown of the continental order which had been established by the Congress of Vienna.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Relations between the Great Powers of Europe were strained to breaking point by issues such as the decline of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_War" title="Crimean War">Crimean War</a>, and later the emergence of new nation states in the form of Italy and Germany after the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>. Both of these wars involved Europe's largest states and armies. The industrialisation of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Empire of Japan</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> contributed to the relative decline of British industrial supremacy in the late 19th century.
</p>
