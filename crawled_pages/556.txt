Source Department for Business, Energy &amp; Industrial Strategy, DUKES 2016 Chapter 6: Renewable sources of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-UK_renewable_electricity_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UK_renewable_electricity-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Data for 2016, 2017 provisonal. <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div><p><b>Renewable energy in the United Kingdom</b> can be divided into the generation of renewable electricity, the generation of renewable heat and renewable energy use in the transport sector. 
</p><p>From the mid-1990s <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> began to contribute to the electricity generated in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, adding to a small <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> generating capacity. The total of all renewable electricity sources provided for 14.9% of the electricity generated in the United Kingdom in 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-DUKES_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DUKES-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> reaching 53.7 TWh of electricity generated. In the second quarter of 2015, renewable electricity generation exceeded 25% and coal generation for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian25_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian25-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> As of 2nd quarter 2017, renewables generated 29.8% of the UK's electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Renewable energy contributions to meeting the UK's 15% target reduction in total energy consumption by 2020, in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Renewables_Directive" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewables Directive">2009 EU Renewable Directive</a>, totalled 5.2% in 2013 as measured in accordance with the methodology set out in the Directive.<sup id="cite_ref-DUKES_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DUKES-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By 2016 provisional calculations show that the figure had risen again to 8.3 per cent of energy consumption (all sources) coming from renewable sources in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Interest in renewable energy in the UK has increased in recent years due to new UK and EU targets for reductions in carbon emissions and the promotion of renewable electricity power generation through commercial incentives such as the <a href="/wiki/Renewables_Obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewables Obligation">Renewable Obligation Certificate scheme (ROCs)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariffs_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Feed-in tariffs in the United Kingdom">Feed in tariffs (FITs)</a> and the promotion of renewable heat through the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Heat_Incentive" title="Renewable Heat Incentive">Renewable Heat Incentive</a>. Historically hydroelectric schemes were the largest producers of renewable electricity in the UK, but these have now been surpassed by wind power schemes, for which the UK has large potential resources.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Renewable heat energy, in the form of biofuels, dates back to 415,000 <a href="/wiki/Before_Present" title="Before Present">BP</a> in the UK. Uranium series dating and <a href="/wiki/Thermoluminescence_dating" title="Thermoluminescence dating">thermoluminescence dating</a> give evidence to the use of wood fires at the site of Beeches Pit, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Waterwheel technology was imported to the country by the Romans, with sites in Ikenham and Willowford in England being from the 2nd century AD.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At the time of the compilation of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> (1086), there were 5,624 watermills in <a href="/wiki/Norman_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman England">England</a> alone, only 2% of which have not been located by modern archaeological surveys.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Later research estimates a less conservative number of 6,082, and it has been pointed out that this should be considered a minimum as the northern reaches of England were never properly recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1300, this number had risen to between 10,000 and 15,000.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Windmills first appeared in Europe during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. The earliest certain reference to a windmill in Europe (assumed to have been of the vertical type) dates from 1185, in the former village of Weedley in Yorkshire which was located at the southern tip of the <a href="/wiki/The_Wolds" title="The Wolds">Wold</a> overlooking the <a href="/wiki/Humber_Estuary" class="mw-redirect" title="Humber Estuary">Humber Estuary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The first electricity-generating wind turbine was a battery charging machine installed in July 1887 by Scottish academic <a href="/wiki/James_Blyth_(engineer)" title="James Blyth (engineer)">James Blyth</a> to light his holiday home in <a href="/wiki/Marykirk" title="Marykirk">Marykirk</a>, Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1878 the world's first hydroelectric power scheme was developed at <a href="/wiki/Cragside" title="Cragside">Cragside</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> by <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">William George Armstrong</a>. It was used to power a single <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">Arc lamp</a> in his art gallery.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, almost all electricity generation thereafter was based on burning coal. In 1964 coal accounted for 88% of electricity generation, and oil was 11%.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The remainder was mostly supplied by hydroelectric power, which continued to grow its share of electricity generation as coal struggled to meet demand. The world's third <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a> power station, the <a href="/wiki/Cruachan_Power_Station" title="Cruachan Power Station">Cruachan Dam</a> in  <a href="/wiki/Argyll_and_Bute" title="Argyll and Bute">Argyll and Bute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, became fully operational in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The Central Electricity Generating Board attempted to experiment with wind energy on the Llŷn Peninsula in Wales during the 1950s, but this was shelved after local opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Renewable energy experienced a turning point in the 1970s with the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>, <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1972)" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1972)">miners' strike (1972)</a>, growing environmentalism and wind energy development in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> exerting pressure on the government. In 1974, the Central Policy Review Staff  made the recommendation that ‘the first stage of a full technical and economic appraisal of harnessing wave power for electricity generation should be put in hand at once.’ Wave power was seen to be the future of the nation's energy policy, and solar, wind, and tidal schemes were dismissed as 'impractical'. Nevertheless, an alternative energy research centre was opened in Harwell, although it was criticised for favouring nuclear power. By 1978, four wave energy generator prototypes had been designed which were later deemed too expensive. The Wave Energy Programme closed in the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During this period, there was a large increase in installations of <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">solar thermal collectors</a> to provide hot water. In 1986, Southampton began pumping heat from the geothermal borehole through a district heating network. Over the years, several combined heat and power (CHP) engines and backup boilers for heating have been added, along with absorption chillers and backupvapour compression machines for cooling.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1987 a 3.7MW demonstration wind turbine on <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> began supplying electricity to homes, the largest in Britain at the time. Privatisation of the energy sector in 1989 caused direct governmental research funding to cease. Two years later the UK's first onshore windfarm was opened in <a href="/wiki/Delabole_wind_farm" title="Delabole wind farm">Delabole, Cornwall</a>. The farm consists of 10 turbines and produces enough energy for 2,700 homes. This was followed by the UK's first offshore windfarm in North Hoyle, Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The share of renewables in the country's electricity generation has risen from below 2% in 1990 to 14.9% in 2013, helped by subsidy and falling costs. Introduced on 1 April 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Renewables_Obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewables Obligation">Renewables Obligation</a> requires all electricity suppliers who supply electricity to end consumers to supply a set portion of their electricity from eligible renewables sources; a proportion that will increase each year until 2015 from a 3% requirement in 2002-2003, via 10.4% in 2010-2012 up to 15.4% by 2015-2016. The UK Government announced in the 2006 Energy Review an additional target of 20% by 2020-21. For each eligible megawatt hour of renewable energy generated, a tradable certificate called a <a href="/wiki/Renewables_obligation_certificate" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewables obligation certificate">Renewables obligation certificate</a> (ROC) is issued by <a href="/wiki/OFGEM" class="mw-redirect" title="OFGEM">OFGEM</a>.
</p><p>In 2007, the United Kingdom Government agreed to an overall European Union target of generating 20% of the European Union's energy supply from renewable sources by 2020. Each European Union member state was given its own allocated target; for the United Kingdom it is 15%. This was formalised in January 2009 with the passage of the EU <a href="/wiki/Renewables_Directive" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewables Directive">Renewables Directive</a>. As renewable heat and fuel production in the United Kingdom are at extremely low bases, <a href="/wiki/RenewableUK" title="RenewableUK">RenewableUK</a> estimates that this will require 35–40% of the United Kingdom's electricity to be generated from renewable sources by that date,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> to be met largely by 33–35 GW of installed wind capacity. The 2008 Climate Change Act consists of a commitment to reducing net Greenhouse Gas Emissions by 80% by 2050 (on 1990 levels) and an intermediate target reduction of 26% by 2020.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_deal" class="mw-redirect" title="Green deal">Green Deal</a> is UK government policy, launched by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Energy_and_Climate_Change" title="Department of Energy and Climate Change">Department of Energy and Climate Change</a> on 1 October 2012. It permits loans for energy saving measures for properties in Great Britain to enable consumers to benefit from energy efficient improvements to their home.
</p><p>In June 2017 renewables plus nuclear generated more UK power than gas and coal together for the first time.  Britain has the fourth greenest power generation in Europe and the seventh worldwide.  In 2017 new offshore wind power became cheaper than new nuclear power for the first time.  The UK is still heavily dependent on gas and vulnerable to fluctuations in world gas prices.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc-20171228_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-20171228-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strike_prices">Strike prices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Strike prices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "strike price" forms the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Contract_for_Difference" class="mw-redirect" title="Contract for Difference">Contract for Difference</a> between the 'generator and the Low Carbon Contracts Company (LCCC), a government-owned company'<sup id="cite_ref-strikeprice2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strikeprice2-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and guarantees the price per MWh paid to the electricity producer. It is not the same as the <a href="/wiki/Levelized_cost_of_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Levelized cost of electricity">Levelized cost of electricity</a> (LCOE) which is a first order estimate of the average cost the producer must receive to break-even.
</p><p>Low-carbon generation sources have agreed "strike prices" in the range £50-£79.23/MWh for photovoltaic, £80/MWh for energy from waste, £79.23-£82.5/MWh for onshore wind, and £114.39-£119.89/MWh for offshore wind and conversion technologies (all expressed in 2012 prices).<sup id="cite_ref-actualstrikeprices_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-actualstrikeprices-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-actualstrikeprices2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-actualstrikeprices2-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> These prices are indexed to inflation.<sup id="cite_ref-strikepriceindexing_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strikepriceindexing-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With new interconnectors, specifically the ongoing construction of the <a href="/wiki/NSN_Link" class="mw-redirect" title="NSN Link">NSN Link</a> is expected to finish in 2020 after which the UK will get 1.4 GW of access to less expensive sources in the south Norway bidding area (NO2) of <a href="/wiki/Nord_Pool_Spot" class="mw-redirect" title="Nord Pool Spot">Nord Pool Spot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Viking_Link" title="Viking Link">Viking Link</a> is expected to start operations in 2022,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> after which the UK will get another 1.4 GW of access to the less expensive west Denmark bidding area (DK1) of Nord Pool Spot.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind">Wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Wind power in the United Kingdom">Wind power in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Wind power delivers a growing fraction of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Energy in the United Kingdom">energy in the United Kingdom</a> and at the beginning of January 2015, <b>wind power in the United Kingdom</b> consisted of 6,546 wind turbines with a total installed capacity of just under 12 <a href="/wiki/Gigawatts" class="mw-redirect" title="Gigawatts">gigawatts</a>: 7,950 <a href="/wiki/Megawatts" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatts">megawatts</a> of onshore capacity and 4,049 megawatts of offshore capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The United Kingdom is ranked as the world's sixth largest producer of wind power, having overtaken France and Italy in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Polling of public opinion consistently shows strong support for wind power in the UK, with nearly three quarters of the population agreeing with its use, even for people living near onshore wind turbines. Wind power is expected to continue growing in the UK for the foreseeable future, <a href="/wiki/RenewableUK" title="RenewableUK">RenewableUK</a> estimates that more than 2&#160;GW of capacity will be deployed per year for the next five years.<sup id="cite_ref-ruk20100923_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ruk20100923-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Within the UK, wind power is the second largest source of renewable energy after <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DUKES_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DUKES-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
In 2016 <a href="/wiki/Dong_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Dong Energy">Dong Energy</a> is the UK’s largest windfarm operator with stakes in planned or existing projects able to produce 5 GW wind energy. Dong Energy's chief executive has confirmed plans to sell company’s oil and gas division.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>2010 saw the completion of some significant projects in the UK wind industry with the <a href="/wiki/Gunfleet_Sands_Offshore_Wind_Farm" title="Gunfleet Sands Offshore Wind Farm">Gunfleet Sands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robin_Rigg_Wind_Farm" title="Robin Rigg Wind Farm">Robin Rigg</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Thanet_Offshore_Wind_Project" class="mw-redirect" title="Thanet Offshore Wind Project">Thanet</a><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> offshore wind farms coming on stream. Over 1.1&#160;GW of new wind power capacity was brought online during 2010, a 3% increase on 2009. There was a 38% drop in onshore installations to 503&#160;MW compared with 815&#160;MW in 2009 but there was a 230% increase in offshore installations with 653&#160;MW installed (compared with 285&#160;MW in 2009).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_power">Ocean power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ocean power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marine_energy" title="Marine energy">Marine energy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg/220px-Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg/330px-Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg/440px-Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wave_energy_power_generator_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1419261.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Islay_LIMPET" title="Islay LIMPET">Islay limpet</a> wave power device</div></div></div>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">island</a> location of the UK, the country has great potential for generating electricity from <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_power" title="Tidal power">tidal power</a>.
</p><p>To date, wave and tidal power have received very little money for development and consequently have not yet been exploited on a significant commercial basis due to doubts over their economic viability in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The European Marine Energy Centre in Orkney operates a grid connected wave power scheme at Billia Croo outside Stromness and a grid connected tidal test side  in a narrow channel between the Westray Firth and Stronsay Firth.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Funding for the UK's first <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">wave farm</a> was announced by then <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Executive">Scottish Executive</a> in February 2007. It will be the world's largest, with a capacity of 3 MW generated by four <a href="/wiki/Pelamis_wave_energy_converter" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelamis wave energy converter">Pelamis</a> machines and a cost of over 4 million <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pounds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In the south of Scotland,investigations have taken place into a <a href="/wiki/Tidal_Power" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal Power">Tidal Power</a> scheme involving the construction of a Solway Barage, possibly located south of <a href="/wiki/Annan,_Dumfries_and_Galloway" title="Annan, Dumfries and Galloway">Annan</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">wave farm</a> project to harness <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a>, using the PB150 <a href="/wiki/PowerBuoy" title="PowerBuoy">PowerBuoy</a> has been completed by <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Power_Technologies" title="Ocean Power Technologies">Ocean Power Technologies</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> and is under development off Cornwall at <a href="/wiki/Wave_Hub" title="Wave Hub">Wave Hub</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biofuels">Biofuels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biofuels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biofuel_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Biofuel in the United Kingdom">Biofuel in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sewage-to-energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Sewage-to-energy">Gas from sewage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a> (<a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>) has already been exploited in some areas. In 2004 it provided 129.3 GW·h (up 690% from 1990 levels), and was the UK's leading renewable energy source, representing 39.4% of all renewable energy produced (including hydro).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The UK has committed to a target of 10.3% of renewable energy in transport to comply with the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bioenergywiki.net/Renewable_Energy_Directive">Renewable Energy Directive</a> of the European Union but has <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bioenergywiki.net/Policy_implementation_in_the_United_Kingdom">not yet implemented legislation</a> to meet this target.
</p><p>Other <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a> can provide a close-to-carbon-neutral energy source, if locally grown. In <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, the production of biofuels for export has in some cases resulted in significant ecological damage, including the clearing of <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a>. In 2004 biofuels provided 105.9 GW·h, 38% of it <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>. This represented an increase of 500% from 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar">Solar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Solar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BedZED_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/BedZED_2007.jpg/220px-BedZED_2007.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/BedZED_2007.jpg/330px-BedZED_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/BedZED_2007.jpg/440px-BedZED_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BedZED_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Solar panels on the <a href="/wiki/BedZED" title="BedZED">BedZED</a> development in the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Sutton" title="London Borough of Sutton">London Borough of Sutton</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Solar power in the United Kingdom">Solar power in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>At the end of 2011, there were 230,000 solar power projects in the United Kingdom,<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> with a total installed generating capacity of 750 <a href="/wiki/Megawatts" class="mw-redirect" title="Megawatts">megawatts</a> (MW).<sup id="cite_ref-epia2012_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-epia2012-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> By February 2012 the installed capacity had reached 1,000 MW.<sup id="cite_ref-Jonathan_Gifford_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jonathan_Gifford-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">Solar power</a> use has increased very rapidly in recent years, albeit from a small base, as a result of reductions in the cost of <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> (PV) panels, and the introduction of a <a href="/wiki/Feed-in_tariff" title="Feed-in tariff">Feed-in tariff</a> (FIT) subsidy in April 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, the government said that 4 million homes across the UK will be powered by the sun within eight years,<sup id="cite_ref-Fiona_Harvey_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fiona_Harvey-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> representing 22,000 MW of installed solar power capacity by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-uk12_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uk12-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydroelectric">Hydroelectric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hydroelectric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Hydroelectricity in the United Kingdom">Hydroelectricity in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg/220px-Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg/330px-Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg/440px-Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1004" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dinorwig_Power_Station.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Dinorwig_Power_Station" title="Dinorwig Power Station">Dinorwig Power Station</a> lower reservoir, a 1,800 MW pumped-storage hydroelectric scheme, in north Wales, and the largest hydroelectric power station in the UK</div></div></div>
<p>As of 2012, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric power">hydroelectric power</a> stations in the United Kingdom accounted for 1.67 GW of installed electrical generating capacity, being 1.9% of the UK's total generating capacity and 14% of UK's <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> generating capacity. Annual electricity production from such schemes is approximately 5,700 GWh, being about 1.5% of the UK's total electricity production.<sup id="cite_ref-DECC_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DECC-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">pumped-storage</a> power stations in the UK. These power stations are net consumers of electrical energy however they contribute to balancing the grid, which can facilitate renewable generation elsewhere, for example by 'soaking up' surplus renewable output at off-peak times and release the energy when it is required.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal_power">Geothermal power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geothermal power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Geothermal power in the United Kingdom">Geothermal power in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Investigations into the exploitation of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Geothermal power in the United Kingdom">Geothermal power in the United Kingdom</a>, prompted by the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a>, were abandoned as fuel prices fell. Only one scheme is operational, in <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>. In 2004 it was announced that a further scheme would be built to heat the UK's first geothermal energy <a href="/wiki/Model_village" title="Model village">model village</a> near <a href="/wiki/Eastgate,_County_Durham" title="Eastgate, County Durham">Eastgate</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microgeneration">Microgeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Microgeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Microgeneration" title="Microgeneration">Microgeneration</a> technologies are seen as having considerable potential by the Government. However, the microgeneration strategy launched in March 2006<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> was seen as a disappointment by many commentators.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Microgeneration involves the local production of electricity by homes and businesses from low-energy sources including small scale <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbines" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind turbines">wind turbines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_electricity" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar electricity">solar electricity</a> installations. The <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_and_Sustainable_Energy_Act_2006" title="Climate Change and Sustainable Energy Act 2006">Climate Change and Sustainable Energy Act 2006</a><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> is expected to boost the number of microgeneration installations,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> however, funding for grants under the <a href="/wiki/Low_Carbon_Building_Programme" title="Low Carbon Building Programme">Low Carbon Building Programme</a> is proving insufficient to meet demand<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> with funds for March 2007 being spent in 75 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
FiTs (Feed-in-Tariffs, aka "Clean Energy Cashback") were introduced from 1 April 2010 to support microgeneration of electricity. The <a href="/wiki/Renewable_Heat_Incentive" title="Renewable Heat Incentive">Renewable Heat Incentive</a> was introduced from 28 November 2011 to support microgeneration of heat from <a href="/wiki/Ground_source_heat_pump" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground source heat pump">ground source heat pumps</a>, solar thermal panels and biomass boilers, but only for non-domestic dwellings.  The Government has plans to extend the RHI to domestic dwellings in 2013.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (March 2018)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The delay of introducing the RHI for renewable heat long after the subsidy for renewable electricity was introduced has dealt a savage blow to the renewable heat industries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_energy_systems">Community energy systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Community energy systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_community_energy_system" title="Sustainable community energy system">Sustainable community energy systems</a>, pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Woking" title="Woking">Woking</a> Borough Council, provide an integrated approach to using cogeneration, renewables and other technologies to provide sustainable energy supplies to an urban community. It is expected that the same approach will be developed in other towns and cities, including <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_Community_Energy_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Highlands and Islands Community Energy Company">Highlands and Islands Community Energy Company</a> based in <a href="/wiki/Inverness" title="Inverness">Inverness</a> are active in developing community-owned and led initiatives in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An energy positive house was built in Wales for £125,000 in July 2015. It is expected to generate £175 in electricity export for each £100 spent on electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
