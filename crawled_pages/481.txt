<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> possesses, or has possessed, a variety of <a href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" class="mw-redirect" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_weapon" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological weapon">biological</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapon" title="Chemical weapon">chemical weapons</a>. The United Kingdom is one of the five official nuclear weapon states under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Non-Proliferation_of_Nuclear_Weapons" title="Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons">Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons</a> and has an independent nuclear <a href="/wiki/Deterrence_theory" title="Deterrence theory">deterrent</a>. The UK has been estimated to have a stockpile of 120 active nuclear warheads and 215 nuclear warheads in total.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It had renounced the use of chemical and biological weapons in 1956 and subsequently destroyed its general stocks.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_weapons">Biological weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biological weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_biological_warfare" title="History of biological warfare">History of biological warfare</a></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, British scientists studied the use of biological weapons, including a test using <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a> on the Scottish island of <a href="/wiki/Gruinard" class="mw-redirect" title="Gruinard">Gruinard</a> which left it contaminated and fenced off for nearly fifty years, until an intensive four-year programme to eradicate the spores was completed in 1990. They also manufactured five million linseed-oil <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> cakes with a hole bored into them for addition of anthrax spores between 1942 and mid-1943. These were to be dropped on Germany using specially designed containers each holding 400 cakes, in a project known as <a href="/wiki/Operation_Vegetarian" title="Operation Vegetarian">Operation Vegetarian</a>. It was intended that the disease would destroy the German beef and dairy herds and possibly spread to the human population. Preparations were not complete until early 1944. Operation Vegetarian was only to be used in the event of a German anthrax attack on the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Offensive weapons development continued after the war into the 1950s with tests of <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">plague</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tularemia" title="Tularemia">tularemia</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Eastern_equine_encephalitis_virus" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern equine encephalitis virus">equine encephalomyelitis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vaccinia" title="Vaccinia">vaccinia</a> <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a> (the latter as a relatively safe simulant for <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>).
</p><p>In particular five sets of trials took place at sea using aerosol clouds and animals.
</p>
<ul><li>Operation Harness off <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a> in 1948–1949.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Operation_Cauldron" title="Operation Cauldron">Operation Cauldron</a> off <a href="/wiki/Stornoway,_Outer_Hebrides" class="mw-redirect" title="Stornoway, Outer Hebrides">Stornoway</a> in 1952. The <a href="/wiki/Commercial_trawler" class="mw-redirect" title="Commercial trawler">trawler</a> <i>Carella</i> unknowingly sailed through a cloud of pneumonic plague bacilli (<i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_pestis" title="Yersinia pestis">yersinia pestis</a></i>) during this trial. It was kept under covert observation until the incubation period had elapsed but none of the crew fell ill.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Operation Hesperus off Stornoway in 1953.</li>
<li>Operation Ozone off <a href="/wiki/Nassau,_Bahamas" title="Nassau, Bahamas">Nassau</a> in 1954.</li>
<li>Operation Negation off Nassau in 1954-5.</li></ul>
<p>The programme was cancelled in 1956 when the British government renounced the use of biological and chemical weapons. In 1974, <a href="/wiki/Biological_Weapons_Act_1974" title="Biological Weapons Act 1974">biological weapons were banned</a>, and the United Kingdom  ratified the <a href="/wiki/Biological_and_Toxin_Weapons_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention">Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention</a> in March 1975.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_weapons">Chemical weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapons_and_the_United_Kingdom" title="Chemical weapons and the United Kingdom">Chemical weapons and the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>The UK was a signatory of the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_(1899_and_1907)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hague Conventions (1899 and 1907)">Hague Conventions (1899 and 1907)</a> which outlawed the use of poison gas in warfare. However, during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, in retaliation to the use of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> by Germany against British troops from April 1915 onwards, British forces deployed chlorine themselves for the first time during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Loos" title="Battle of Loos">Battle of Loos</a> on 25 September 1915. By the end of the war, poison gas use had become widespread on both sides and by 1918 a quarter of artillery shells were filled with gas and Britain had produced around 25,400 tons of toxic chemicals.
</p><p>Britain used a range of poison gases, originally <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Phosgene" title="Phosgene">phosgene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diphosgene" title="Diphosgene">diphosgene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mustard_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Mustard gas">mustard gas</a>. They also used relatively small amounts of the irritant gases <a href="/wiki/Chloromethyl_chloroformate" title="Chloromethyl chloroformate">chloromethyl chloroformate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloropicrin" title="Chloropicrin">chloropicrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromacetone" class="mw-redirect" title="Bromacetone">bromacetone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethyl_iodoacetate" title="Ethyl iodoacetate">ethyl iodoacetate</a>. Gases were frequently mixed, for example <i>white star</i> was the name given to a mixture of equal volumes of chlorine and phosgene, the chlorine helping to spread the denser but more toxic phosgene. Despite the technical developments, chemical weapons suffered from diminishing effectiveness as the war progressed because of the protective equipment and training which the use engendered on both sides. See <a href="/wiki/Use_of_poison_gas_in_World_War_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Use of poison gas in World War I">Use of poison gas in World War I</a>.
</p><p>After the war, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> dropped mustard gas on <a href="/wiki/Bolshevik" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> troops in 1919, and <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, secretary of state for war and air, suggested that the RAF use it in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> in 1920 during a major revolt there. Historians are divided as to whether or not gas was in fact used.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The UK ratified the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Protocol" title="Geneva Protocol">Geneva Protocol</a> on 9 April 1930. The UK signed the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Weapons_Convention" title="Chemical Weapons Convention">Chemical Weapons Convention</a> on 13 January 1993 and ratified it on 13 May 1996.
</p><p>Despite the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Protocol" title="Geneva Protocol">Geneva Protocol</a>, the UK carried out extensive testing of chemical weapons from the early 1930s onwards. In the <a href="/wiki/Rawalpindi_experiments" title="Rawalpindi experiments">Rawalpindi experiments</a>, hundreds of <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">Indian soldiers</a> were exposed to <a href="/wiki/Mustard_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Mustard gas">mustard gas</a> in an attempt to determine the appropriate dosage to use on battlefields. Many of the subjects suffered severe burns from their exposure to the gas.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many ex-<a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">servicemen</a> have complained about suffering long term illnesses after taking part in tests on nerve agents. It was alleged that before volunteering they were not provided with adequate information about the <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experiments</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risk</a>, in breach of the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_Code" title="Nuremberg Code">Nuremberg Code</a> of 1947. Alleged abuses at <a href="/wiki/Porton_Down" title="Porton Down">Porton Down</a> became the subject of a lengthy police investigation called <a href="/wiki/Operation_Antler_(Porton_Down_investigation)" title="Operation Antler (Porton Down investigation)">Operation Antler</a>, which covered the use of volunteers in testing a variety of chemical weapons and countermeasures from 1939 until 1989. An inquest was opened on 5 May 2004 into the death on 6 May 1953 of a serviceman, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Maddison" title="Ronald Maddison">Ronald Maddison</a>, during an experiment using <a href="/wiki/Sarin" title="Sarin">sarin</a>. His death had earlier been found by a private <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">MoD</a> inquest to have been as a result of "misadventure" but this was quashed by the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> in 2002. The 2004 hearing closed on 15 November, after a jury found that the cause of Maddison's death was "application of a nerve agent in a non-therapeutic experiment".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_weapons">Nuclear weapons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Kingdom_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nuclear weapons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_and_the_United_Kingdom" title="Nuclear weapons and the United Kingdom">Nuclear weapons and the United Kingdom</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trident_II_missile_image.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Trident_II_missile_image.jpg/170px-Trident_II_missile_image.jpg" width="170" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Trident_II_missile_image.jpg/255px-Trident_II_missile_image.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Trident_II_missile_image.jpg/340px-Trident_II_missile_image.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2486" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trident_II_missile_image.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Trident_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident missile">Trident missile</a> launched from a submerged <a href="/wiki/Ballistic_missile_submarine" title="Ballistic missile submarine">ballistic missile submarine</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>British nuclear weapons are designed and developed by the UK's <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Weapons_Establishment" title="Atomic Weapons Establishment">Atomic Weapons Establishment</a>. The United Kingdom has four <a href="/wiki/Vanguard_class_submarine" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanguard class submarine"><i>Vanguard</i> class</a> submarines armed with nuclear-tipped <a href="/wiki/Trident_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident missile">Trident missiles</a>. The principle of operation is based on maintaining deterrent effect by always having at least one submarine at sea, and was designed during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> period. One submarine is normally undergoing maintenance and the remaining two are in port or on training exercises.
</p><p>Each submarine carries up to sixteen <a href="/wiki/Trident_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident II">Trident II</a> D-5 missiles, which can each carry up to twelve warheads, for a maximum of 192 warheads per vessel. However, the British government announced in 1998 that each submarine would carry only 48 warheads (halving the limit specified by the previous government), which is an average of three per missile. However one or two missiles per submarine are probably armed with fewer warheads for "sub-strategic" use causing others to be armed with more.
</p><p>The British-designed warheads are thought to be selectable between 0.3 <a href="/wiki/Kilotons" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilotons">kilotons</a>, 5–10 kt and 100 kt; the yields obtained using either the unboosted primary, the boosted primary, or the entire "physics package". The United Kingdom has purchased the rights to 58 missiles under the <a href="/wiki/Polaris_Sales_Agreement" title="Polaris Sales Agreement">Polaris Sales Agreement</a> (modified for Trident) from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s "pool". These missiles are fitted with United Kingdom–built warheads and are exchanged when requiring maintenance. Under the agreement the United States was given certain assurances by the UK regarding the use of the missiles, however the United States does not have any veto on the use of British nuclear weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Some non-nuclear components for the British nuclear warhead are procured from the U.S. for reasons of cost effectiveness.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The United Kingdom is one of the five "Nuclear Weapons States" (NWS) under the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Non-Proliferation_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty">Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty</a>, which the UK ratified in 1968.
</p><p>The UK permits the U.S. to deploy nuclear weapons from its territory, the first having arrived in 1954.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> During the 1980s nuclear armed USAF <a href="/wiki/Ground_Launched_Cruise_Missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground Launched Cruise Missile">Ground Launched Cruise Missiles</a> were deployed at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Greenham_Common" title="RAF Greenham Common">RAF Greenham Common</a> and <a href="/wiki/RAF_Molesworth" title="RAF Molesworth">RAF Molesworth</a>. As of 2005 it is believed that about 110 tactical <a href="/wiki/B61_nuclear_bomb" title="B61 nuclear bomb">B61 nuclear bombs</a> are stored at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Lakenheath" title="RAF Lakenheath">RAF Lakenheath</a> for deployment by <a href="/wiki/USAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAF">USAF</a> <a href="/wiki/F-15E" class="mw-redirect" title="F-15E">F-15E</a> aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-nrdc_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nrdc-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2007, the UK Parliament voted to <a href="/wiki/British_replacement_of_the_Trident_system" class="mw-redirect" title="British replacement of the Trident system">renew the country's Trident nuclear submarine system</a> at a cost of £20bn.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In July 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> claimed that the decision had already been made to replace and upgrade Britain's nuclear warhead stockpile at a cost of £3bn, extending the life of the warheads until 2055.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
