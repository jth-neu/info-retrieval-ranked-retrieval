<p><i><b>Scientific American</b></i> (informally abbreviated <i><b>SciAm</b></i> or sometimes <i><b>SA</b></i>) is an American <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">popular science</a> magazine. Many famous scientists, including <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, have contributed articles to it. It is the oldest continuously published monthly magazine in the United States (though it only became monthly in 1921).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_American&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Scientific American</i> was founded by inventor and publisher <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Porter_(inventor)" title="Rufus Porter (inventor)">Rufus M. Porter</a> in 1845<sup id="cite_ref-scientificamerican1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> as a four-page weekly newspaper. Throughout its early years, much emphasis was placed on reports of what was going on at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Patent_and_Trademark_Office" title="United States Patent and Trademark Office">U.S. Patent Office</a>. It also reported on a broad range of inventions including <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_motion" title="Perpetual motion">perpetual motion</a> machines, an 1860 device for buoying vessels by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Universal_joint" title="Universal joint">universal joint</a> which now can be found in nearly every <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobile</a> manufactured. Current issues include a "this date in history" section, featuring excerpts from articles originally published 50, 100, and 150 years earlier. Topics include humorous incidents, wrong-headed theories, and noteworthy advances in the history of science and technology.
</p><p>Porter sold the publication to <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Ely_Beach" title="Alfred Ely Beach">Alfred Ely Beach</a> and <a href="/wiki/Orson_Desaix_Munn" title="Orson Desaix Munn">Orson Desaix Munn</a> a mere ten months after founding it. Until 1948, it remained owned by Munn &amp; Company.<sup id="cite_ref-scientificamerican1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scientificamerican1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Under Munn's grandson, Orson Desaix Munn III, it had evolved into something of a "workbench" publication, similar to the twentieth-century incarnation of <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_Science_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Popular Science (magazine)">Popular Science</a></i>.
</p><p>In the years after World War II, the magazine fell into decline. In 1948, three partners who were planning on starting a new popular science magazine, to be called <i>The Sciences</i>, purchased the assets of the old <i>Scientific American</i> instead and put its name on the designs they had created for their new magazine. Thus the partners—publisher <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Piel" title="Gerard Piel">Gerard Piel</a>, editor <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Flanagan" title="Dennis Flanagan">Dennis Flanagan</a>, and general manager Donald H. Miller, Jr.—essentially created a new magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Miller retired in 1979, Flanagan and Piel in 1984, when Gerard Piel's son Jonathan became president and editor; circulation had grown fifteen-fold since 1948. In 1986, it was sold to the <a href="/wiki/Georg_von_Holtzbrinck_Publishing_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Georg von Holtzbrinck Publishing Group">Holtzbrinck</a> group of Germany, which has owned it since.
</p><p>In the fall of 2008, <i>Scientific American</i> was put under the control of <a href="/wiki/Nature_Publishing_Group" title="Nature Publishing Group">Nature Publishing Group</a>, a division of Holtzbrinck.<sup id="cite_ref-FOLIO_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOLIO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Donald Miller died in December 1998,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Gerard Piel in September 2004 and Dennis Flanagan in January 2005. <a href="/wiki/Mariette_DiChristina" title="Mariette DiChristina">Mariette DiChristina</a> is the current editor-in-chief, after <a href="/wiki/John_Rennie_(editor)" title="John Rennie (editor)">John Rennie</a> stepped down in June 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-FOLIO_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOLIO-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_editions">International editions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_American&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International editions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small><i>(August 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><i>Scientific American</i> published its first foreign edition in 1890, the Spanish-language <i>La America Cientifica</i>. Publication was suspended in 1905, and another 63 years would pass before another foreign-language edition appeared: In 1968, an Italian edition, <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Scienze" title="Le Scienze">Le Scienze</a></i>, was launched, and a Japanese edition, <i>Nikkei Science</i> (<a href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%97%A5%E7%B5%8C%E3%82%B5%E3%82%A4%E3%82%A8%E3%83%B3%E3%82%B9" class="extiw" title="ja:日経サイエンス">日経サイエンス</a>), followed three years later. A new Spanish edition, <i>Investigación y Ciencia</i> was launched in Spain in 1976, followed by a French edition, <i><a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pour_la_Science" class="extiw" title="fr:Pour la Science">Pour la Science</a></i>, in France in 1977, and a German edition, <i><a href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spektrum_der_Wissenschaft" class="extiw" title="de:Spektrum der Wissenschaft">Spektrum der Wissenschaft</a></i>, in Germany in 1978. A Russian edition <i>V Mire Nauki</i> was launched in the Soviet Union in 1983, and continues in the present-day Russian <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">Federation</a>. <i>Kexue</i> (科学, "Science" in Chinese), a simplified Chinese edition launched in 1979, was the first Western magazine published in the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>. Founded in <a href="/wiki/Chongqing" title="Chongqing">Chongqing</a>, the simplified Chinese magazine was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> in 2001. Later in 2005, a newer edition, <i>Global Science</i> (环球科学), was published instead of <i>Kexue</i>, which shut down due to financial problems. A traditional Chinese edition, known as <a href="https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E7%A7%91%E5%AD%A6%E7%BE%8E%E5%9B%BD%E4%BA%BA" class="extiw" title="zh:科学美国人">科學人</a> ("Scientist" in Chinese), was introduced to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> in 2002. The Hungarian edition <i>Tudomány</i> existed between 1984 and 1992. In 1986, an Arabic edition, <i>Oloom magazine</i> (<a href="https://ar.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D8%A7%D9%84%D8%B9%D9%84%D9%88%D9%85_(%D9%85%D8%AC%D9%84%D8%A9)" class="extiw" title="ar:العلوم (مجلة)">مجلة العلوم</a>), was published. In 2002, a Portuguese edition was launched in Brazil.
</p><p>Today, <i>Scientific American</i> publishes 18 foreign-language editions around the globe: Arabic, Brazilian Portuguese, <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Simplified Chinese">Simplified Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional Chinese">Traditional Chinese</a>, Czech, Dutch, French, German, Greek, Hebrew, Italian, Japanese, Korean, Lithuanian (discontinued after 15 issues), Polish, Romanian, Russian, and Spanish.
</p><p>From 1902 to 1911, <i>Scientific American</i> supervised the publication of the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_Americana" title="Encyclopedia Americana">Encyclopedia Americana</a></i>, which during some of that period was known as <i>The Americana</i>.
</p>

<p>It originally styled itself "The Advocate of Industry and Enterprise" and "Journal of Mechanical and other Improvements". On the front page of the first issue was the engraving of "Improved Rail-Road Cars". The masthead had a commentary as follows:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><i>Scientific American</i> published every Thursday morning at No. 11 Spruce Street, New York, No. 16 State Street, Boston, and No. 2l Arcade Philadelphia, (The principal office being in New York) by Rufus Porter. Each number will be furnished with from two to five original Engravings, many of them elegant, and illustrative of New Inventions, Scientific Principles, and Curious Works; and will contain, in high addition to the most interesting news of passing events, general notices of progress of Mechanical and other Scientific Improvements; American and Foreign. Improvements and Inventions; Catalogues of American Patents; Scientific Essays, illustrative of the principles of the sciences of Mechanics, Chemistry, and Architecture: useful information and instruction in various Arts and Trades; Curious Philosophical Experiments; Miscellaneous Intelligence, Music and Poetry. This paper is especially entitled to the patronage of Mechanics and Manufactures, being the only paper in America, devoted to the interest of those classes; but is particularly useful to farmers, as it will not only appraise them of improvements in agriculture implements, But instruct them in various mechanical trades, and guard them against impositions. As a family newspaper, it will convey more useful intelligence to children and young people, than five times its cost in school instruction. Another important argument in favor of this paper, is that it will be worth two dollars at the end of the year when the volume is complete, (Old volumes of the <i>New York Mechanic</i>, being now worth double the original cost, in cash.) Terms: The <i>Scientific American</i> will be furnished to subscribers at $2.00 per annum, - one dollar in advance, and the balance in six months. Five copies will be sent to one address six months for four dollars in advance. Any person procuring two or more subscribers, will be entitled to a commission of 25 cents each.
</p></blockquote>
<p>The commentary under the illustration gives the flavor of its style at the time:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>There is perhaps no mechanical subject, in which improvement has advanced so rapidly, within the last ten years, as that of railroad passenger cars. Let any person contrast the awkward and uncouth cars of '35 with the superbly splendid long cars now running on several of the eastern roads, and he will find it difficult to convey to a third party, a correct idea of the vast extent of improvement. Some of the most elegant cars of this class, and which are of a capacity to accommodate from sixty to eighty passengers, and run with a steadiness hardly equaled by a steamboat in still water, are manufactured by Davenport &amp; Bridges, at their establishment in Cambridgeport, Mass. The manufacturers have recently introduced a variety of excellent improvements in the construction of trucks, springs, and connections, which are calculated to avoid atmospheric resistance, secure safety and convenience, and contribute ease and comfort to passengers, while flying at the rate of 30 or 40 miles per hour.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Also in the first issue is commentary on Signor Muzio Muzzi's proposed device for aerial navigation.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_American_50_award">Scientific American 50 award</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_American&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scientific American 50 award">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>Scientific American 50</b> award was started in 2002 to recognize contributions to science and technology during the magazine's previous year. The magazine's 50 awards cover many categories including agriculture, communications, defence, environment, and medical diagnostics. The complete list of each year's winners appear in the December issue of the magazine, as well as on the magazine's web site.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_and_political_debate">Scientific and political debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_American&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Scientific and political debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1950, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">Atomic Energy Commission</a> ordered <i>Scientific American</i> to cease publication of an issue containing an article by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Bethe" title="Hans Bethe">Hans Bethe</a> that appeared to reveal classified information about the thermonuclear <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrogen bomb">hydrogen bomb</a>. Subsequent review of the material determined that the AEC had overreacted. The incident was important for the "new" <i>Scientific American'</i>s history, as the AEC's decision to burn 3000 copies of an early press-run of the magazine containing the offending material appeared to be "<a href="/wiki/Book_burning" title="Book burning">book burning</a> in a free society" when publisher Gerard Piel leaked the incident to the press.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In its January 2002 issue, <i>Scientific American</i> published a series of criticisms of the <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg" title="Bjørn Lomborg">Bjørn Lomborg</a> book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Skeptical_Environmentalist" title="The Skeptical Environmentalist">The Skeptical Environmentalist</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Cato_Institute" title="Cato Institute">Cato Institute</a> fellow <a href="/wiki/Patrick_J._Michaels" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrick J. Michaels">Patrick J. Michaels</a> said the attacks came because the book "threatens billions of taxpayer dollars that go into the global change kitty every year."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Journalist <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Bailey" title="Ronald Bailey">Ronald Bailey</a> called the criticism "disturbing" and "dishonest", writing, "The subhead of the review section, 'Science defends itself against <i>The Skeptical Environmentalist</i>,' gives the show away: Religious and political views need to defend themselves against criticism, but science is supposed to be a process for determining the facts."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The May 2007 issue featured a column by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Shermer" title="Michael Shermer">Michael Shermer</a> calling for a United States pullout from the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In response, <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> online columnist <a href="/wiki/James_Taranto" title="James Taranto">James Taranto</a> jokingly called <i>Scientific American</i> "a liberal political magazine".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The publisher was criticized in 2009 when it notified collegiate libraries that yearly subscription prices for the magazine would increase by nearly 500% for print and 50% for online access to $1500 yearly.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An editorial in the September 2016 issue of Scientific American attacked U.S. presidential candidate Donald Trump for alleged "anti-science" attitudes and rhetoric. This marked the first time that the publication forayed into commenting on U.S. presidential politics.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_American&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Danielle_N._Lee" title="Danielle N. Lee">Danielle N. Lee</a>, a female scientist who blogged at <i>Scientific American</i>, was called a "whore" in an email by an editor at the science website <i>Biology Online</i> after refusing to write professional content without compensation. When Lee, outraged about the email, wrote a rebuttal on her <i>Scientific American</i> blog, the editor-in-chief of <i>Scientific American</i>, <a href="/wiki/Mariette_DiChristina" title="Mariette DiChristina">Mariette DiChristina</a>, removed the post, sparking an outrage by supporters of Lee. While DiChristina cited legal reasons for removing the blog, others criticized her for censoring Lee.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The editor at Biology Online was fired after the incident.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The controversy widened in the ensuing days. The magazine's blog editor, Bora Zivkovic, was the subject of allegations of sexual harassment by another blogger, Monica Byrne.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-creep_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creep-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Although the alleged incident had occurred about a year earlier, editor Mariette DiChristina informed readers that the incident had been investigated and resolved to Ms. Byrne's satisfaction.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> However, the incident involving Dr. Lee had prompted Ms. Byrne to reveal the identity of Zivkovic, following the latter's support of Dr. Lee. Zivkovic responded on Twitter and his own blog, admitting the incident with Ms. Byrne had taken place.<sup id="cite_ref-Zivkovic_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zivkovic-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> His blog post apologized to Ms. Byrne, and referred to the incident as "singular", stating that his behavior was not "engaged in before or since."<sup id="cite_ref-Zivkovic_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zivkovic-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the allegations, Zivkovic resigned from the board of <a href="/wiki/Science_Online" title="Science Online">Science Online</a>, the popular science blogging conference that he helped establish.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Following Zivkovic's admission, several prominent female bloggers, including other bloggers for the magazine, wrote their own accounts that contradicted Zivkovic's assertions, alleging additional incidents of sexual harassment.<sup id="cite_ref-creep_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creep-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> A day after these new revelations, Zivkovic resigned his position at <i>Scientific American</i>, according to a press release from the magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
