<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Chairman</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Chairman of the Board" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Board_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Chairman of the Board (disambiguation)">Chairman of the Board (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ThompsonWatergate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/ThompsonWatergate.jpg/220px-ThompsonWatergate.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/ThompsonWatergate.jpg/330px-ThompsonWatergate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/ThompsonWatergate.jpg/440px-ThompsonWatergate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="606" data-file-height="403" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ThompsonWatergate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of a chairman in action – <a href="/wiki/Sam_Ervin" title="Sam Ervin">Sam Ervin</a> (right), chairing the Senate <a href="/wiki/Watergate" class="mw-redirect" title="Watergate">Watergate</a> hearings, 1973</div></div></div>	
<p>The <b>chairman</b> (also <b>chair</b>) is the highest officer of an organized group such as a <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Committee" title="Committee">committee</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_assembly" title="Deliberative assembly">deliberative assembly</a>. The person holding the office is typically elected or appointed by the members of the group, and the chairman presides over <a href="/wiki/Meeting" title="Meeting">meetings</a> of the assembled group and conducts its business in an orderly fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>When the group is not in session, the officer's duties often include acting as its head, its representative to the outside world and its spokesperson. In some organizations, this position is also called <i><a href="/wiki/President_(corporate_title)" title="President (corporate title)">president</a></i> (or other title),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in others, where a board appoints a president (or other title), the two different terms are used for distinctly different positions.
</p>

<p>Other terms sometimes used for the office and its holder include <i>chair</i>, <i>chairperson</i>, <i>chairwoman</i>, <i>presiding officer</i>, <i>president</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Moderator_(town_official)" title="Moderator (town official)">moderator</a></i>, <i>facilitator</i>, and <i>convenor</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dictionary2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The chairman of a parliamentary chamber is often called the <i><a href="/wiki/Speaker_(politics)" title="Speaker (politics)">speaker</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
The term <i>chair</i> is sometimes used in lieu of chairman, in response to criticisms that using chairman is <a href="/wiki/Sexist" class="mw-redirect" title="Sexist">sexist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dicrefman_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dicrefman-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zinsser_2007_81_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zinsser_2007_81-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is commonly used today, and has been used as a substitute for chairman since the middle of the 17th century, with its earliest citation in the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> dated 1658–1659, only four years after the first citation for chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></p><table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">

</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 1994 Canadian study found the <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Star</a></i> newspaper referring to most presiding men as "chairman", and to most presiding women as "chairperson" or as "chairwoman". The <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_Higher_Education" class="mw-redirect" title="Chronicle of Higher Education">Chronicle of Higher Education</a></i> uses "chairman" for men and "chairperson" for women. An analysis of the <a href="/wiki/British_National_Corpus" title="British National Corpus">British National Corpus</a> found chairman used 1,142 times, chairperson 130 times and chairwoman 68 times.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Parliamentarians" title="National Association of Parliamentarians">National Association of Parliamentarians</a> does not approve using "chairperson".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/United_Press_International" title="United Press International">United Press International</a> all use "chairwoman" or "chairman" when referring to women, and forbid use of "chair" or of "chairperson" except in direct quotations.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/World_Schools_Style_debating" class="mw-redirect" title="World Schools Style debating">World Schools Style debating</a>, male chairs are called "Mr. Chairman" and female chairs are called "Madame Chair".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/FranklinCovey" title="FranklinCovey">FranklinCovey</a> Style Guide for Business and Technical Communication, as well as the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a> style guide, advocate using "chair" or "chairperson", rather than "chairman".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Oxford Dictionary of American Usage and Style suggests that the gender-neutral forms are gaining ground. It advocates using "chair" to refer both to men and to women.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word <i>chair</i> can refer to the place from which the holder of the office presides, whether on a chair, at a lectern, or elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> During meetings, the person presiding is said to be "in the chair" and is also referred to as "the chair".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_procedure" title="Parliamentary procedure">Parliamentary procedure</a> requires that members address the "chair" as "Mr. (or Madam) Chairman (or Chair or Chairperson)" rather than using a name –  one of many customs intended to maintain the presiding officer's impartiality and to ensure an objective and impersonal approach.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, the presiding officer of the lower house of a legislative body, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a>, is frequently titled the <i>Speaker</i>, while the upper house, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a>, is commonly chaired by a <i>President</i>. In his 1992 <a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Union_address" class="mw-redirect" title="State of the Union address">State of the Union address</a>, then-U.S. President <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a> used "chairman" for men and "chair" for women.
</p><p>In the British <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> tradition, the Chairman was the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_ceremonies" title="Master of ceremonies">master of ceremonies</a> who announced the performances and was responsible for controlling any rowdy elements in the audience.  The role was popularised on British TV in the 1960s and 1970s by <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Sachs" title="Leonard Sachs">Leonard Sachs</a>, the Chairman on the variety show <i><a href="/wiki/The_Good_Old_Days_(UK_TV_series)" title="The Good Old Days (UK TV series)">The Good Old Days</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-baker_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-baker-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Chairman" as a quasi-title gained particular resonance when socialist states from 1917 onward shunned more traditional <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">leadership</a> labels and stressed the collective control of <i>soviets</i> (councils or committees) by beginning to refer to executive figureheads as "Chairman of the X Committee". <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a>, for example, officially functioned as the head of Soviet Russia not as tsar or as president but in roles such as "Chairman of the Council of People's Commissars of the Russian SFSR".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
Note in particular the popular standard method for referring to <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a>: "Chairman Mao" (officially: <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Chairman of the Communist Party of China">Chairman of the Communist Party of China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Central_Military_Commission" title="Chairman of the Central Military Commission">Chairman of the Central Military Commission</a>).
</p>
<h2><span id="Roles_.26_Responsibilities"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles_&amp;_Responsibilities">Roles &amp; Responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roles &amp; Responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Duties_at_meetings">Duties at meetings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Duties at meetings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg/220px-Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg/330px-Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg/440px-Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1202" data-file-height="909" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Christina_Magnuson_group_2016.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>As Chairman, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Christina,_Mrs._Magnuson" title="Princess Christina, Mrs. Magnuson">Princess Christina, Mrs. Magnuson</a> presides over the 2016 annual meeting of the <i>Friends of the <a href="/wiki/Confidencen" title="Confidencen">Ulriksdal Palace Theater</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p>In addition to the administrative or executive duties in organizations, the chairman has the duties of presiding over meetings.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Such duties at meetings include:
</p>
<ul><li>Calling the meeting to order</li>
<li>Determining if a <a href="/wiki/Quorum" title="Quorum">quorum</a> is present</li>
<li>Announcing the items on the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_business" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of business">order of business</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agenda_(meeting)" title="Agenda (meeting)">agenda</a> as they come up</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Recognition_(parliamentary_procedure)" title="Recognition (parliamentary procedure)">Recognition</a> of members to have the <a href="/wiki/Floor_(legislative)" title="Floor (legislative)">floor</a></li>
<li>Enforcing the rules of the group</li>
<li>Putting all questions (<a href="/wiki/Motion_(parliamentary_procedure)" title="Motion (parliamentary procedure)">motions</a>) to a vote</li>
<li>Adjourning the meeting</li></ul>
<p>While presiding, the chairman should remain impartial and not interrupt a speaker if the speaker has the floor and is following the rules of the group.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In committees or small boards, the chairman votes along with the other members. However, in assemblies or larger boards, the chairman should vote only when it can affect the result.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> At a meeting, the chairman only has one vote (i.e. the chairman cannot vote twice and cannot override the decision of the group unless the organization has specifically given the chairman such authority).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers_and_authority">Powers and authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Powers and authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The powers of the chairman vary widely across organizations. In some organizations the chairman has the authority to hire staff and make financial decisions, while in others the chairman only makes recommendations to a <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a>, and still others the chairman has no executive powers and is mainly a <a href="/wiki/Spokesperson" title="Spokesperson">spokesman</a> for the organization. The amount of power given to the chairman depends on the type of organization, its structure, and the rules it has created for itself.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disciplinary_procedures">Disciplinary procedures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disciplinary procedures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If the chairman exceeds the given authority, engages in misconduct, or fails to perform the duties, the chairman may face disciplinary procedures. Such procedures may include <a href="/wiki/Censure" title="Censure">censure</a>, suspension, or <a href="/wiki/Declare_the_chair_vacant" title="Declare the chair vacant">removal from office</a>. The rules of the particular organization would provide details on who can perform these disciplinary procedures and the extent that they can be done.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Usually, whoever appointed or elected the chairman has the power to discipline this officer.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_corporations">Public corporations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Public corporations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three common types of chairman in public corporations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chairman_and_CEO">Chairman and CEO<span id="Chairman_and_CEO"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chairman and CEO">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Chairman and CEO – The CEO may also hold the title of chairman, in which case the board frequently names an independent member of the board as a lead director.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Executive_chairman">Executive chairman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Executive chairman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Executive chairman – An office separate from that of CEO, where the titleholder wields influence over company operations, such as <a href="/wiki/Larry_Ellison" title="Larry Ellison">Larry Ellison</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Flint" title="Douglas Flint">Douglas Flint</a> of <a href="/wiki/HSBC" title="HSBC">HSBC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Case" title="Steve Case">Steve Case</a> of the former <a href="/wiki/AOL_Time_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="AOL Time Warner">AOL Time Warner</a>. In particular, the group chairmanship of HSBC is considered the top position of that institution, outranking the chief executive, and is responsible for leading the board and representing the company in meetings with government figures. Prior to the creation of the group management board in 2006, HSBC's chairman essentially held the duties of a chief executive at an equivalent institution, while HSBC's chief executive served as the deputy. After the 2006 reorganization, the management cadre ran the business, while the chairman oversaw the controls of the business through compliance and audit and the direction of the business.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-executive_chairman">Non-executive chairman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Non-executive chairman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Non-executive chairman – also a separate post from the CEO, unlike an executive chairman, a non-executive chairman does not interfere in day-to-day company matters. Across the world, many companies have separated the roles of chairman and CEO, often resulting in a non-executive chairman, saying that this move improves corporate governance.</li></ul>
<p>The non-executive chairman's duties are typically limited to matters directly related to the board, such as:<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Chairing the meetings of the board.</li>
<li>Organizing and coordinating the board's activities, such as by setting its annual agenda.</li>
<li>Reviewing and evaluating the performance of the CEO and the other board members.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_2">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many U.S. companies have an executive chairman, and this method of organization is sometimes called the American model. Having a non-executive chair is common in the United Kingdom and Canada, and is sometimes called the British model. Expert opinion is rather evenly divided over which is the preferable model overall.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Companies with both an executive chairman and a CEO include <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/HSBC" title="HSBC">HSBC</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hewlett-Packard" title="Hewlett-Packard">HP</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vice_chairman_and_deputy_chairman">Vice chairman and deputy chairman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chairman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Vice chairman and deputy chairman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i>vice-chairman</i> or <i>deputy chairman</i>, subordinate to the chairman, is sometimes chosen to assist the chairman<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and to serve as chairman in the absence of the chairman, or when a motion involving the chairman is being discussed.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In the absence of the chairman and vice chairman, groups sometimes elect a <i>chairman <a href="/wiki/Pro_tempore" title="Pro tempore">pro tempore</a></i> to fill the role for a single meeting.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In some organizations that have both titles, deputy chairman ranks higher than vice chairman, as there are often multiple vice chairs but only a single deputy chair.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> This type of deputy chairman title on its own usually has only an advisory role and not an operational one (such as <a href="/wiki/Ted_Turner" title="Ted Turner">Ted Turner</a> at Time Warner).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An unrelated definition of vice chair and deputy chair describes an executive who is higher ranking or has more seniority than an <a href="/wiki/Executive_Vice_President" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive Vice President">executive vice president</a> (EVP). Sometimes, EVPs report to a vice chair, who in turn reports directly to the <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> (CEO) (so vice chairs in effect constitute an additional layer of management), while other vice chairs have more responsibilities but are otherwise on an equal tier with EVPs. Executives with the title vice chair and deputy chair are usually <i>not</i> members of the board of directors. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Canada" title="Royal Bank of Canada">Royal Bank of Canada</a> previously used "deputy chair" (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Anthony_S._Fell" title="Anthony S. Fell">Anthony S. Fell</a>, Deputy Chairman of RBC, who was also Chairman and CEO of RBC Dominion Securities) and "vice chair" (i.e. Peter Currie, Vice Chairman and Chief Financial Officer) in their inner management circle until 2004.
</p>
