<p>The <b>Firth of Clyde</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inlet" title="Inlet">inlet</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> off the southwest coast of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, named for the <a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">River Clyde</a> which empties into it. It encloses the largest and deepest coastal waters in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, sheltered from the Atlantic Ocean by the <a href="/wiki/Kintyre" title="Kintyre">Kintyre</a> peninsula which encloses the outer <a href="/wiki/Firth" title="Firth">firth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Argyll" title="Argyll">Argyll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ayrshire" title="Ayrshire">Ayrshire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kilbrannan_Sound" title="Kilbrannan Sound">Kilbrannan Sound</a> is a large arm of the Firth of Clyde, separating the Kintyre Peninsula from the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Isle of Arran</a>. Within the Firth of Clyde is another major island – the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Bute" title="Isle of Bute">Isle of Bute</a>.  Given its strategic location, at the entrance to the middle/upper Clyde, Bute played a vitally important military (naval) role during World War II.
</p><p>The Firth's climate enjoys the benefit of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> from America.
</p><p>At its entrance the firth is some 26 miles (42&#160;km) wide. Its upper reaches include an area where it is joined by <a href="/wiki/Loch_Long" title="Loch Long">Loch Long</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gare_Loch" title="Gare Loch">Gare Loch</a>. This includes the large anchorage off <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Tail_of_the_Bank" title="Tail of the Bank">Tail of the Bank</a> in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Sandbar" class="mw-redirect" title="Sandbar">sandbar</a> which separates the firth from the <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> of the River Clyde. The Clyde is still almost 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) wide at the sandbar, and its upper <a href="/wiki/Tidal_limit" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal limit">tidal limit</a> is at the tidal <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weir</a> <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Green#Tidal_Weir" title="Glasgow Green">adjacent to Glasgow Green</a>.
</p><p>The cultural and geographical distinction between the firth and the River Clyde is vague, and people will sometimes refer to <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton" title="Dumbarton">Dumbarton</a> as being on the <i>Firth of Clyde</i>, while the population of <a href="/wiki/Port_Glasgow" title="Port Glasgow">Port Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a> frequently refer to the firth to their north as "the river". In <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> the landward end is called <i>Linne Chluaidh</i> (<small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA/Scottish Gaelic">[ʎiɲəˈxlˠ̪uəj]</a></span>) (meaning the same as the English), while the area around the south of Arran, Kintyre and Ayrshire/<a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a> is <i>An Linne Ghlas</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA/Scottish Gaelic">[ə ʎiɲə ɣlˠ̪as̪]</a></span>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firth_of_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Firth encompasses many <a href="/wiki/Islands_of_the_Clyde" title="Islands of the Clyde">islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peninsulas" class="mw-redirect" title="Peninsulas">peninsulas</a> and has twelve ferry routes connecting them to the mainland and each other. Sometimes called the <b>Clyde Waters</b>, this water body is customarily considered an element of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The ferry services are run by <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_MacBrayne" title="Caledonian MacBrayne">Caledonian MacBrayne</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Western_Ferries" title="Western Ferries">Western Ferries</a> and many of the routes are lifeline services for communities living in remote areas.  A great number of <a href="/wiki/Loch" title="Loch">sea lochs</a> adjoin the Firth, the largest being <a href="/wiki/Loch_Fyne" title="Loch Fyne">Loch Fyne</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Holy_Loch_06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Holy_Loch_06.jpg/220px-Holy_Loch_06.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Holy_Loch_06.jpg/330px-Holy_Loch_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Holy_Loch_06.jpg/440px-Holy_Loch_06.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2197" data-file-height="1437" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Holy_Loch_06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Loch" title="Holy Loch">Holy Loch</a> seen across the upper Firth of Clyde from <a href="/wiki/Gourock" title="Gourock">Gourock</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Hunter%27s_Quay" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter&#39;s Quay">Hunter's Quay</a> to the left and <a href="/wiki/Strone" title="Strone">Strone</a> to the right, with the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_MacBrayne" title="Caledonian MacBrayne">Caledonian MacBrayne</a> ferry <a href="/wiki/MV_Saturn" title="MV Saturn">MV&#160;<i>Saturn</i></a> arriving from <a href="/wiki/Dunoon" title="Dunoon">Dunoon</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cowal_Peninsula">Cowal Peninsula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firth_of_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cowal Peninsula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cowal" title="Cowal">Cowal</a> peninsula extends into the Firth of Clyde and forms the main upper firth west shoreline.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The main town on the Cowal peninsula is <a href="/wiki/Dunoon" title="Dunoon">Dunoon</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ferries">Ferries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firth_of_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ferries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>These routes across the Firth of Clyde save time, rather than traveling "round by road", via <a href="/wiki/Loch_Eck" title="Loch Eck">Loch Eck</a> side (<a href="/wiki/A815_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A815 road">A815 road</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Rest_and_be_thankful" class="mw-redirect" title="Rest and be thankful">Rest and be thankful</a> (<a href="/wiki/A83_road" title="A83 road">A83 road</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Loch_Lomond" title="Loch Lomond">Loch Lomond</a> side (<a href="/wiki/A82_road" title="A82 road">A82 road</a>).
</p><p>The service between Dunoon and <a href="/wiki/Gourock" title="Gourock">Gourock</a> in <a href="/wiki/Inverclyde" title="Inverclyde">Inverclyde</a>, operated by the Caledonian MacBrayne subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Argyll_Ferries" title="Argyll Ferries">Argyll Ferries</a> is a foot passenger only service.  This service connects directly with the <a href="/wiki/Abellio_ScotRail" title="Abellio ScotRail">Abellio ScotRail</a> service to <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Western Ferries operate the service between <a href="/wiki/Hunters_Quay" title="Hunters Quay">Hunters Quay</a> and <a href="/wiki/McInroy%27s_Point" class="mw-redirect" title="McInroy&#39;s Point">McInroy's Point</a> near Gourock and carry all types of vehicular transport and foot passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firth_of_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg/220px-Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg/330px-Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg/440px-Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1934" data-file-height="1451" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Newark_Castle_and_Clyde.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Shipbuilders" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferguson Shipbuilders">Ferguson Shipbuilders</a>, the last shipyard on the Lower <a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">Clyde</a>, close by <a href="/wiki/Newark_Castle,_Port_Glasgow" title="Newark Castle, Port Glasgow">Newark Castle, Port Glasgow</a>, at the upper end of the firth</div></div></div>
<p>The Clyde formed an important sea route from the earliest times, and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Largs" title="Battle of Largs">Battle of Largs</a> in 1263 marked the turning point for the end of <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> ambitions in Britain. Progressively from the 16th century onwards the Clyde became the conduit for commerce and industry, including herring, timber, wine, sugar, tobacco, textiles, iron and steel, coal, oil, chemicals, distilling and brewing, ships, locomotives, vehicles and other manufactured products. During World War II <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and the Clyde became Britain's main entry point for Allied merchant shipping, military personnel and equipment, and for the assembly, despatch and control of ocean convoys. The Clyde formed the largest base of naval ships. Among many wartime innovations to support air, maritime and territorial combat the world's first deep water test of a submarine oil pipeline was conducted in 1942 on a pipeline laid across the Firth of Clyde in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Pluto" title="Operation Pluto">Operation Pluto</a>.
</p><p>In the middle of the 19th century the sport of yachting became popular on the Clyde.  The area became famous worldwide for its significant contribution to yachting and yachtbuilding with notable designers including: <a href="/wiki/William_Fife" title="William Fife">William Fife</a> III; <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Mylne" title="Alfred Mylne">Alfred Mylne</a>; G L Watson; David Boyd. It was also the location of many famous yacht yards. Clyde-built wooden yachts are still known for quality and style today.
</p><p>With the advent of <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> in <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> times, the area became popular with Glaswegians and residents of neighbouring towns and counties who travelled "doon the watter" on <a href="/wiki/Clyde_steamer" title="Clyde steamer">Clyde steamers</a> to holiday in the picturesque seaside towns and villages that line the firth, with the more wealthy building substantial holiday homes along its coasts. Many towns such as <a href="/wiki/Gourock" title="Gourock">Gourock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Largs" title="Largs">Largs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayr" title="Ayr">Ayr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunoon" title="Dunoon">Dunoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rothesay,_Argyll_and_Bute" class="mw-redirect" title="Rothesay, Argyll and Bute">Rothesay</a> flourished during this period and became fully fledged resorts with well-appointed hotels and attractions. Golf courses increased in large number, including major championship courses. The steam-powered <a href="/wiki/PS_Waverley" title="PS Waverley">PS&#160;<i>Waverley</i></a> still makes cruising trips to these coastal towns, in addition to regular services. Tourism, sport and recreation, and heritage history attract visitors from across the world. The Firth is ringed by many castles and buildings of historical importance which are open to the public, including <a href="/wiki/Inveraray_Castle" title="Inveraray Castle">Inveraray Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brodick_Castle" title="Brodick Castle">Brodick Castle</a>, the opulent <a href="/wiki/Mount_Stuart_House" title="Mount Stuart House">Mount Stuart House</a> on the Isle of Bute, and <a href="/wiki/Culzean_Castle" title="Culzean Castle">Culzean Castle</a>, which is the most visited attraction owned by <a href="/wiki/National_Trust_for_Scotland" title="National Trust for Scotland">National Trust for Scotland</a>. Ocean liners are frequent callers at Greenock, and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow International Airport">Glasgow International Airport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Prestwick_Airport" title="Glasgow Prestwick Airport">Glasgow Prestwick Airport</a> are nearby. Rail services to and from the coast, including links to <a href="/wiki/Oban" title="Oban">Oban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fort_William,_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort William, Scotland">Fort William</a>, are frequent, with city terminals in Glasgow and Edinburgh. Daily ferry services connect with Belfast.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg/220px-Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg/330px-Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg/440px-Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Upper_firth_of_clyde.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The inner firth seen from the <a href="/wiki/Cowal" title="Cowal">Cowal</a> peninsula near <a href="/wiki/Dunoon" title="Dunoon">Dunoon</a>, looking north east to the coast of <a href="/wiki/Inverclyde" title="Inverclyde">Inverclyde</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cloch" title="Cloch">Cloch</a> point south of <a href="/wiki/Gourock" title="Gourock">Gourock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>, and beyond that the <a href="/wiki/Tail_of_the_Bank" title="Tail of the Bank">Tail of the Bank</a>. <a href="/wiki/PS_Waverley" title="PS Waverley">PS&#160;<i>Waverley</i></a> can be seen cruising south "doon the watter"</div></div></div>
<p>The "lower Clyde" shipyards of <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Port_Glasgow" title="Port Glasgow">Port Glasgow</a>, most notably <a href="/wiki/Scott_Lithgow" title="Scott Lithgow">Scott Lithgow</a>, played an important role in shipbuilding, with the <a href="/wiki/PS_Comet" title="PS Comet">PS&#160;<i>Comet</i></a> being the first successful <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamboat</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and a large proportion of the world's shipping being built there until well into the 20th century. In more recent times the natural environment of the firth has been compromised in places by a succession of industrial and military developments along the shoreline, including <a href="/wiki/Hunterston_B_nuclear_power_station" title="Hunterston B nuclear power station">Hunterston</a> and Hunterston deepwater terminal, while at the same time shipbuilding has declined. In the upper Clyde two major shipyards continue at Glasgow Govan and Scotstoun, run by BAE, whose major client is the Royal Navy. Today only one lower Clyde shipyard exists, <a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Shipbuilders" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferguson Shipbuilders">Ferguson Shipbuilders</a>, next to  <a href="/wiki/Newark_Castle,_Port_Glasgow" title="Newark Castle, Port Glasgow">Newark Castle, Port Glasgow</a>, The <span id="Garvel_Drydock"></span>Garvel <a href="/wiki/Dry_dock" title="Dry dock">dry dock</a> in Greenock continues in operation for ship repair, and the large Inchgreen dry dock in Greenock is in occasional use. The sites of former shipyards are being regenerated by housing, leisure facilities and commercial premises.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_wildlife">Marine wildlife</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firth_of_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marine wildlife">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Common_seal" class="mw-redirect" title="Common seal">Common</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grey_seal" title="Grey seal">grey seals</a> abound in the firth. <a href="/wiki/Harbour_porpoise" title="Harbour porpoise">Harbour porpoises</a> are also common and while <a href="/wiki/Dolphin" title="Dolphin">dolphins</a> are much less so, they have been spotted in the upper reaches of the firth in the summer of 2005. <a href="/wiki/Whales" class="mw-redirect" title="Whales">Whales</a> do not favour the Clyde and although there have been instances of larger whales beaching themselves or becoming stranded in the upper firth, only smaller <a href="/wiki/Pilot_whale" title="Pilot whale">pilot</a> or <a href="/wiki/Minke_whale" title="Minke whale">minke whales</a> seem to visit with any kind of regularity.
</p><p>In 2005 the firth was listed as having the 2nd highest incidence of <a href="/wiki/Basking_shark" title="Basking shark">basking shark</a> sightings in Scotland (after <a href="/wiki/The_Minch" title="The Minch">The Minch</a>).  In particular these huge sharks seem to favour the warm, shallow waters surrounding <a href="/wiki/Pladda" title="Pladda">Pladda</a>. Although at one time heavily fished from the Firth's many fishing towns, the only catches remaining in Clyde waters today of commercial interest are <a href="/wiki/Prawn" title="Prawn">prawns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Homarus_gammarus" title="Homarus gammarus">lobster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herring" title="Herring">herring</a>. Mussels and crayfish remain important.
</p><p>There is a <a href="/wiki/University_Marine_Biological_Station_Millport" title="University Marine Biological Station Millport">Marine Biological Station</a> run by the Universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">London</a> on <a href="/wiki/Great_Cumbrae" title="Great Cumbrae">Great Cumbrae</a>.
</p><p>In September 2008, Scotland's first No Take Zone (NTZ) was introduced in Lamlash Bay, Isle of Arran. This was instigated by a community effort driven by the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Arran_Seabed_Trust" title="Community of Arran Seabed Trust">Community of Arran Seabed Trust (C.O.A.S.T)</a>.  The NTZ was specifically introduced to protect delicate communities such as <a href="/wiki/Maerl" title="Maerl">maerl</a>. Maerl is a very slow-growing (1&#160;mm per year) coral-like calcareous red algae and is an important Scottish species. Maerl beds are reservoirs of biodiversity, important both as nursery grounds for young scallops and young fish. Studies show that organic waste from fish farms significantly reduces live maerl and that scallop dredging has profound and long lasting impacts. Scallop dredging on a maerl bed has been found to kill over 70% of live maerl, with no discernible recovery over the following four years. Recovery of maerl beds would be expected to require many years without disturbance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shipping_in_the_Firth">Shipping in the Firth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firth_of_Clyde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Shipping in the Firth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clyde_shipping.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Clyde_shipping.jpg/220px-Clyde_shipping.jpg" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Clyde_shipping.jpg/330px-Clyde_shipping.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Clyde_shipping.jpg/440px-Clyde_shipping.jpg 2x" data-file-width="935" data-file-height="798" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clyde_shipping.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>At <a href="/wiki/Gourock" title="Gourock">Gourock</a> a ship heads for the <a href="/wiki/Portainer" class="mw-redirect" title="Portainer">container cranes</a> at <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>, passing the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_MacBrayne" title="Caledonian MacBrayne">Caledonian MacBrayne</a> <a href="/wiki/Dunoon" title="Dunoon">Dunoon</a> ferry. Across the firth <a href="/wiki/MV_Kenilworth" title="MV Kenilworth">MV&#160;<i>Kenilworth</i></a> leaves <a href="/wiki/Kilcreggan" title="Kilcreggan">Kilcreggan</a> for <a href="/wiki/Helensburgh" title="Helensburgh">Helensburgh</a>.</div></div></div><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg/220px-Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg/330px-Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg/440px-Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2046" data-file-height="1407" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Golden_Princess_esplanade.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Container cranes at Greenock's Ocean Terminal, with the berth occupied by the cruise liner <i><a href="/wiki/Golden_Princess" title="Golden Princess">Golden Princess</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Firth of Clyde like the <a href="/wiki/River_Clyde" title="River Clyde">River Clyde</a> has historically been an important centre of <a href="/wiki/Shipbuilding" title="Shipbuilding">shipbuilding</a> and shipping.  In addition to the shipbuilding and engineering centres up river of <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Govan" title="Govan">Govan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clydebank" title="Clydebank">Clydebank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton" title="Dumbarton">Dumbarton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renfrew" title="Renfrew">Renfrew</a> the lower river developed major yards at <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_Glasgow" title="Port Glasgow">Port Glasgow</a> and smaller ones at <a href="/wiki/Irvine,_North_Ayrshire" title="Irvine, North Ayrshire">Irvine</a>, Ardrossan, <a href="/wiki/Troon" title="Troon">Troon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Campbeltown" title="Campbeltown">Campbeltown</a> and  boatyards including <a href="/wiki/Hunters_Quay" title="Hunters Quay">Hunters Quay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_Bannatyne" title="Port Bannatyne">Port Bannatyne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fairlie,_North_Ayrshire" title="Fairlie, North Ayrshire">Fairlie</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Shipbuilders" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferguson Shipbuilders">Ferguson Shipbuilders</a> yard, adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Newark_Castle,_Port_Glasgow" title="Newark Castle, Port Glasgow">Newark Castle, Port Glasgow</a>, remains. Greenock is the site of one of the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Dry_dock" title="Dry dock">dry dock</a> and ship-repair facilities at Inchgreen.  The dry dock there is 305&#160;m long and 44&#160;m wide and is operated by Northwestern Shiprepairers Limited using the name Scott Lithgow, although unrelated to the famous Port Glasgow <a href="/wiki/Scott_Lithgow" title="Scott Lithgow">Scott Lithgow</a> shipbuilding company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Firth of Clyde has one of the deepest sea entrance channels in northern Europe, which can accommodate the largest <a href="/wiki/Capesize" title="Capesize">Capesize</a> vessels afloat, and as such the Clyde is one of the UK's leading ports, handling some 7.5 million tonnes of cargo each year. <a href="/wiki/Hunterston_Terminal" title="Hunterston Terminal">Hunterston Terminal</a> was constructed to bring in bulk ore, but now mainly deals with coal imports.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">Supertankers</a> up to 324,000 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">tonnes</a> travel up the firth to deliver crude oil to <a href="/wiki/Finnart_Oil_Terminal" title="Finnart Oil Terminal">Finnart Oil Terminal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Loch_Long" title="Loch Long">Loch Long</a>, which is connected by pipeline to the <a href="/wiki/Grangemouth_Refinery" title="Grangemouth Refinery">Grangemouth Refinery</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Forth" title="Firth of Forth">Firth of Forth</a>. A second pipeline brings back refined oil products for export in smaller oil tankers, mainly to <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-www.helensburgh-heritage.co.uk_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.helensburgh-heritage.co.uk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Port_of_Greenock" class="mw-redirect" title="Port of Greenock">Greenock's Ocean Terminal</a> facility handles cargo from smaller container ships. More recently, regular <a href="/wiki/Cruise_ship" title="Cruise ship">cruise liner</a> traffic has built up, making the port very busy in season. Clydeport, North Ayrshire Council and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enterprise" title="Scottish Enterprise">Scottish Enterprise</a> propose a £200m international deep-water container terminal, also at <a href="/wiki/Hunterston" title="Hunterston">Hunterston</a>, which would effectively act as a worldwide gateway port, and possibly become the major container port for the northern half of Europe. Initial environmental and economic impact studies are currently being undertaken.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> has a significant presence on the Clyde, at <a href="/wiki/HMNB_Clyde" title="HMNB Clyde">HMNB Clyde</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Gare_Loch" title="Gare Loch">Gare Loch</a> and on Loch Long, connected to the nuclear stores in Coulport and Glen Douglas. The dockyard engineering and operations are managed by Babcock International, while one of the three main ports providing marine services support vessels is at <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>, previously operated directly by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Maritime_Auxiliary_Service" title="Royal Maritime Auxiliary Service">Royal Maritime Auxiliary Service</a>.
</p>
