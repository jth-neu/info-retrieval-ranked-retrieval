<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Conspiracy theory</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>

<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg/220px-Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg" width="220" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg/330px-Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg/440px-Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="726" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dollarnote_siegel_hq.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Providence" title="Eye of Providence">Eye of Providence</a>, or the all-seeing eye of God, seen here on the US $1 bill, has been taken by some to be evidence of a conspiracy involving the <a href="/wiki/Founding_Fathers_of_the_United_States" title="Founding Fathers of the United States">founders of the United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Illuminati" title="Illuminati">Illuminati</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>A <b>conspiracy theory</b> is an explanation of an event or situation that invokes a <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy" title="Conspiracy">conspiracy</a>—generally one involving an <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">illegal</a> or harmful act supposedly carried out by government or other powerful actors—without credible evidence. Conspiracy theories often produce hypotheses that contradict the prevailing understanding of history or simple facts. The term tends to be a <a href="/wiki/Derogatory" class="mw-redirect" title="Derogatory">derogatory</a> one.<sup id="cite_ref-Ayto2002p15_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayto2002p15-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the political scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Barkun" title="Michael Barkun">Michael Barkun</a>, conspiracy theories rely on the view that the universe is governed by design, and embody three principles: nothing happens by accident, nothing is as it seems, and everything is connected.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Another common feature is that conspiracy theories evolve to incorporate whatever evidence exists against them, so that they become, as Barkun writes, a closed system that is <a href="/wiki/Falsifiability" title="Falsifiability">unfalsifiable</a>, and therefore "a matter of faith rather than proof".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun20037_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun20037-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On a psychological level, studies show <a href="/wiki/Machiavellianism" title="Machiavellianism">Machiavellianism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">paranoia</a> are highly correlated with conspiratorial thinking.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_definition">Etymology and definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> defines <i>conspiracy theory</i> as "the theory that an event or phenomenon occurs as a result of a conspiracy between interested parties; <i>spec.</i> a belief that some covert but influential agency (typically political in motivation and oppressive in intent) is responsible for an unexplained event".  It cites a 1909 article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_American_Historical_Review" title="The American Historical Review">The American Historical Review</a></i> as the earliest usage example,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> although it also appears in journals as early as April 1870.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The word "conspiracy" derives from the Latin <i>con-</i> ("with, together") and <i>spirare</i> ("to breathe").
</p><p>According to John Ayto, the phrase was originally a neutral term, but since around the 1960s, has often been somewhat derogatory.<sup id="cite_ref-Ayto2002p15_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayto2002p15-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Lance deHaven-Smith has suggested that the term was deployed in the 1960s by the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> (CIA) to discredit <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_assassination_conspiracy_theories" title="John F. Kennedy assassination conspiracy theories">John F. Kennedy assassination conspiracy theories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Robert Blaskiewicz rejects such claims, asserting instead that the term has always been derogatory and pointing to examples demonstrating that this has been so since the nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-CSICOP_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSICOP-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brotherton2015_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brotherton2015-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A conspiracy theory is not simply a <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy" title="Conspiracy">conspiracy</a>. Barkun writes that conspiracies are "actual covert plots planned and/or carried out by two or more persons". A conspiracy theory, on the other hand, is "an intellectual construct", a "template imposed upon the world to give the appearance of order to events". Positing that "some small and hidden group" has manipulated events, a conspiracy theory can be local or international, focused on single events or covering multiple incidents and entire countries, regions and periods of history. Conspiracy theorists see themselves as having privileged access to special knowledge or a special mode of thought that separates them from the masses who believe the official account.<sup id="cite_ref-Barkun2016_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barkun2016-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_conspiracy_theories" title="List of conspiracy theories">List of conspiracy theories</a></div>
<p>A conspiracy theory may take any matter as its subject, but certain subjects attract greater interest than others. Favored subjects include famous deaths, government activities, new technologies, terrorism and questions of alien life.  Among the longest-standing and most widely recognized conspiracy theories are notions concerning the assassination of John F. Kennedy, the 1969 Apollo moon landings and the 9/11 terrorist attacks, as well as numerous theories pertaining to alleged plots for world domination by various groups both real and imaginary.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popularity">Popularity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Popularity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some scholars argue that conspiracy theories once limited to fringe audiences have become commonplace in <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>, contributing to conspiracism emerging as a <a href="/wiki/Cultural_phenomenon" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultural phenomenon">cultural phenomenon</a> in the United States of the late 20th and early 21st centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun200358_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun200358-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Camp_1997_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Camp_1997-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Goldberg_2001_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldberg_2001-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fenster_2008_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenster_2008-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> According to anthropologists Todd Sanders and Harry G. West, evidence suggests that a broad cross-section<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This term requires quantification. (October 2014)">quantify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of Americans today gives credence to at least some conspiracy theories.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Belief in conspiracy theories has therefore become a topic of interest for sociologists, psychologists and experts in <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>.
</p><p>Conspiracy theories are widely present on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">Web</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> videos, as well as on <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a>. Whether the Web has increased the prevalence of conspiracy theories or not is an open research question.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The presence and representation of conspiracy theories in <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">search engine</a> results has been monitored and studied, showing significant variation across different topics, and a general absence of reputable, high-quality links in the results.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_conspiracy_theory">Types of conspiracy theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types of conspiracy theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Walker.27s_five_kinds"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Walker's_five_kinds">Walker's five kinds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Walker&#039;s five kinds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jesse_Walker" title="Jesse Walker">Jesse Walker</a> (2013) has identified five kinds of conspiracy theories:
</p>
<ul><li>The "Enemy Outside" refers to theories based on figures alleged to be scheming against a community from without.</li>
<li>The "Enemy Within" finds the conspirators lurking inside the nation, indistinguishable from ordinary citizens.</li>
<li>The "Enemy Above" involves powerful people manipulating events for their own gain.</li>
<li>The "Enemy Below" features the lower classes working to overturn the social order.</li>
<li>The "Benevolent Conspiracies" are angelic forces that work behind the scenes to improve the world and help people.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span id="Barkun.27s_three_types"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Barkun's_three_types">Barkun's three types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Barkun&#039;s three types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Barkun has identified three classifications of conspiracy theory:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Event conspiracy theories</i>.  This refers to limited and well-defined events.  Examples may include such conspiracies theories as those concerning the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_assassination_conspiracies" class="mw-redirect" title="Kennedy assassination conspiracies">Kennedy assassination</a>, <a href="/wiki/9/11" class="mw-redirect" title="9/11">9/11</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Discredited_AIDS_origins_theories" class="mw-redirect" title="Discredited AIDS origins theories">spread of AIDS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun20036_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun20036-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Systemic conspiracy theories</i>.  The conspiracy is believed to have broad goals, usually conceived as securing control of a country, a region, or even the entire world. The goals are sweeping, whilst the conspiratorial machinery is generally simple: a single, evil organization implements a plan to infiltrate and subvert existing institutions. This is a common scenario in conspiracy theories that focus on the alleged machinations of <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Masonic_conspiracy_theory" title="Judeo-Masonic conspiracy theory">Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masonic_conspiracy_theories" title="Masonic conspiracy theories">Freemasons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Scare" title="Red Scare">Communism</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_conspiracy_theories" title="Vatican conspiracy theories">Catholic Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun20036_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun20036-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Superconspiracy theories</i>. For Barkun, such theories link multiple alleged conspiracies together hierarchically.  At the summit is a distant but all-powerful evil force.  His cited examples are the ideas of <a href="/wiki/David_Icke" title="David Icke">David Icke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Milton_William_Cooper" title="Milton William Cooper">Milton William Cooper</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun20036_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun20036-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rothbard:_shallow_vs._deep">Rothbard: shallow vs. deep</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rothbard: shallow vs. deep">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Murray_Rothbard" title="Murray Rothbard">Murray Rothbard</a> argues in favor of a model that contrasts "deep" conspiracy theories to "shallow" ones. According to Rothbard, a "shallow" theorist observes an event and asks <i><a href="/wiki/Cui_bono" title="Cui bono">Cui bono</a></i>? ("Who benefits?"), jumping to the conclusion that a posited beneficiary is responsible for covertly influencing events.  On the other hand, the "deep" conspiracy theorist begins with a hunch and then seeks out evidence. Rothbard describes this latter activity as a matter of confirming with certain facts one's initial paranoia.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_vs._conspiracy_theory">Evidence vs. conspiracy theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Evidence vs. conspiracy theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Theories involving multiple conspirators that are proven to be correct, such as the <a href="/wiki/Watergate" class="mw-redirect" title="Watergate">Watergate</a> scandal,<sup id="cite_ref-knight-2003_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-2003-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> are usually referred to as "investigative journalism" or "historical analysis" rather than conspiracy theory. By contrast, the term "Watergate conspiracy theory" is used to refer to a variety of hypotheses in which those convicted in the conspiracy were in fact the victims of a deeper conspiracy.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> contrasts conspiracy theory to <a href="/wiki/Institutional_analysis" title="Institutional analysis">institutional analysis</a> which focuses mostly on the public, long-term behavior of publicly known institutions, as recorded in, for example, scholarly documents or <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_media" title="Mainstream media">mainstream media</a> reports. Conspiracy theory conversely posits the existence of secretive coalitions of individuals and speculates on their alleged activities.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clare_Birchall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Clare Birchall (page does not exist)">Clare Birchall</a> at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King's College London</a> describes conspiracy theory as a "form of popular knowledge or interpretation".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The use of the word 'knowledge' here suggests ways in which conspiracy theory may be considered in relation to legitimate modes of knowing.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup> The relationship between legitimate and illegitimate knowledge, Birchall claims, is closer than common dismissals of conspiracy theory contend.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conspiracism_as_a_world_view"><span id="Conspiracism"></span> Conspiracism as a world view</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conspiracism as a world view">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Richard_Hofstadter" title="Richard Hofstadter">Richard Hofstadter</a> addressed the role of <a href="/wiki/Paranoia" title="Paranoia">paranoia</a> and conspiracism throughout <a href="/wiki/American_history" class="mw-redirect" title="American history">American history</a> in his 1964 essay "<a href="/wiki/The_Paranoid_Style_in_American_Politics" title="The Paranoid Style in American Politics">The Paranoid Style in American Politics</a>". <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Bailyn" title="Bernard Bailyn">Bernard Bailyn's</a> classic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ideological_Origins_of_the_American_Revolution" title="The Ideological Origins of the American Revolution">The Ideological Origins of the American Revolution</a></i> (1967) notes that a similar phenomenon could be found in America during the time preceding the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. Conspiracism labels people's attitudes as well as the type of conspiracy theories that are more global and historical in proportion.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "conspiracism" was further popularized by academic Frank P. Mintz in the 1980s. According to Mintz, conspiracism denotes "belief in the primacy of conspiracies in the unfolding of history":<sup id="cite_ref-Mintz1985_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mintz1985-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>Conspiracism serves the needs of diverse political and social groups in America and elsewhere. It identifies elites, blames them for economic and social catastrophes, and assumes that things will be better once popular action can remove them from positions of power. As such, conspiracy theories do not typify a particular epoch or ideology.<sup id="cite_ref-Mintz1985_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mintz1985-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>199</span></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Justin Fox of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine is of the opinion that Wall Street traders are among the most conspiracy-minded group of people, and ascribes this to the reality of some financial market conspiracies, and to the ability of conspiracy theories to provide necessary orientation in the market's day-to-day movements. He believes as well that most good investigative reporters are also conspiracy theorists.<sup id="cite_ref-business.time.com_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-business.time.com-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harry G. West and others have noted that while conspiracy theorists may often be dismissed as a fringe minority, certain evidence suggests that a wide range of the American population maintains a belief in conspiracy theories. West also compares those theories to <a href="/wiki/Hypernationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypernationalism">hypernationalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Religious_fundamentalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious fundamentalism">religious fundamentalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Specific events and trends within US history have been cited as causes of the popularity of conspiratorial thinking in the US.
</p><p>Theologian Robert Jewett and philosopher <a href="/wiki/John_Shelton_Lawrence" title="John Shelton Lawrence">John Shelton Lawrence</a> attribute the enduring popularity of conspiracy theories in the US to the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, <a href="/wiki/McCarthyism" title="McCarthyism">McCarthyism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Counterculture_of_the_1960s" title="Counterculture of the 1960s">counterculture</a> rejection of authority. They state that among both the left-wing and right-wing there remains a willingness to use real events, such as Soviet plots, inconsistencies in the <a href="/wiki/Warren_Commission" title="Warren Commission">Warren Report</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11</a> attacks, to support the existence of unverified ongoing large-scale conspiracies.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate scandal</a> has also been used to bestow legitimacy to other conspiracy theories, with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> himself commenting that it served as a "<a href="/wiki/Rorschach_test" title="Rorschach test">Rorschach ink blot</a>" which invited others to fill-in the underlying pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-knight-2003_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-2003-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historian Kathryn S Olmstead cites three reasons why Americans are prone to believing in government conspiracies theories: 
</p>
<ol><li>Genuine government overreach and secrecy during the Cold War, listing as examples Watergate, the <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_syphilis_experiment" title="Tuskegee syphilis experiment">Tuskegee syphilis experiment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_MKUltra" title="Project MKUltra">Project MKUltra</a>, and the CIA <a href="/wiki/Assassination_attempts_on_Fidel_Castro" title="Assassination attempts on Fidel Castro">collaborating with Mobsters</a> to attempt to assassinate Fidel Castro.</li>
<li>The precedent set by official government-sanctioned conspiracy theories for propaganda, such as claims of German infiltration of the US during World War II or the debunked claim that <a href="/wiki/Saddam_Hussein_and_al-Qaeda_link_allegations" title="Saddam Hussein and al-Qaeda link allegations">Saddam Hussein played a role in 9/11</a>.</li>
<li>The distrust fostered by the government's spying and harassment of dissenters, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sedition_Act_of_1918" title="Sedition Act of 1918">Sedition Act of 1918</a>, <a href="/wiki/COINTELPRO" title="COINTELPRO">COINTELPRO</a>, and as part of various <a href="/wiki/Red_Scare" title="Red Scare">Red Scares</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theories_in_the_Arab_world" title="Conspiracy theories in the Arab world">Conspiracy theories in the Arab world</a></div>
<p>Matthew Gray has noted that conspiracy theories are a prevalent feature of <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arab</a> culture and politics.<sup id="cite_ref-Gray_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gray-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Variants include conspiracies involving colonialism, <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">Zionism</a>, superpowers, oil, and the <a href="/wiki/War_on_terrorism" class="mw-redirect" title="War on terrorism">war on terrorism</a>, which may be referred to as a <a href="/wiki/War_against_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="War against Islam">War against Islam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gray_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gray-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> For example, <i> <a href="/wiki/The_Protocols_of_the_Elders_of_Zion" title="The Protocols of the Elders of Zion">The Protocols of the Elders of Zion</a></i>, an infamous <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a> document purporting to be a Jewish plan for world domination, is commonly read and promoted in the Muslim world.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Cohen" title="Roger Cohen">Roger Cohen</a> has suggested that the popularity of conspiracy theories in the Arab world is "the ultimate refuge of the powerless".<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  Al-Mumin Said has noted the danger of such theories, for they "keep us not only from the truth but also from confronting our faults and problems".<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_interpretations">Psychological interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Psychological interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The widespread belief in conspiracy theories has become a topic of interest for sociologists, psychologists, and experts in folklore since at least the 1960s, when <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_assassination_conspiracy_theories" title="John F. Kennedy assassination conspiracy theories">a number of conspiracy theories</a> arose regarding the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_assassination" class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy assassination">assassination</a> of U.S. President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sociologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Sociologist">Sociologist</a> Türkay Salim Nefes underlines the political nature of conspiracy theories. He suggests that one of the most important characteristics of these accounts is their attempt to unveil the "real but hidden" power relations in social groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Turkay_Nefes_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turkay_Nefes-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nefes_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nefes-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_attractions_of_conspiracy_theory">The attractions of conspiracy theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The attractions of conspiracy theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The political scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Barkun" title="Michael Barkun">Michael Barkun</a>, discussing the usage of "conspiracy theory" in contemporary American culture, holds that this term is used for a belief that explains an event as the result of a secret plot by exceptionally powerful and cunning conspirators to achieve a malevolent end.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun20033_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun20033-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_Internationalist_1_2004_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Internationalist_1_2004-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> According to Barkun, the appeal of conspiracism is threefold:
</p>
<ul><li>"First, conspiracy theories claim to explain what <a href="/wiki/Institutional_analysis" title="Institutional analysis">institutional analysis</a> cannot. They appear to make sense out of a world that is otherwise confusing.</li>
<li>Second, they do so in an appealingly simple way, by dividing the world sharply between <a href="/wiki/Manichaeism" title="Manichaeism">the forces of light, and the forces of darkness</a>. They trace all evil back to a single source, the conspirators and their agents.</li>
<li>Third, conspiracy theories are often presented as special, <a href="/wiki/Esotericism" class="mw-redirect" title="Esotericism">secret knowledge</a> unknown or unappreciated by others. For conspiracy theorists, the masses are a <a href="/wiki/Sheeple" title="Sheeple">brainwashed herd</a>, while the conspiracy theorists in the know can congratulate themselves on penetrating the plotters' deceptions."<sup id="cite_ref-New_Internationalist_1_2004_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Internationalist_1_2004-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>This third point is supported by research of Roland Imhoff, professor in <a href="/wiki/Social_Psychology" class="mw-redirect" title="Social Psychology">Social Psychology</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg_University_Mainz" title="Johannes Gutenberg University Mainz">Johannes Gutenberg University Mainz</a>. The research suggests that the smaller the minority believing in a specific theory, the more attractive it is to conspiracy theorists.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Humanistic_psychology" title="Humanistic psychology">Humanistic psychologists</a> argue that even if a posited cabal behind an alleged conspiracy is almost always perceived as hostile, there often remains an element of reassurance for theorists. This is because it is a consolation to imagine that difficulties in human affairs are created by humans, and remain within human control.  If a cabal can be implicated, there may be a hope of breaking its power or of joining it.  Belief in the power of a cabal is an implicit assertion of human dignity—an unconscious affirmation that man is responsible for his own destiny.<sup id="cite_ref-Baigent,_Leigh_&amp;_Lincoln_1987_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent,_Leigh_&amp;_Lincoln_1987-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>People formulate conspiracy theories to explain, for example, power relations in social groups and the perceived existence of evil forces.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-New_Internationalist_1_2004_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Internationalist_1_2004-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Turkay_Nefes_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turkay_Nefes-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nefes_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nefes-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  Proposed psychological origins of conspiracy theorising include projection; the personal need to explain "a significant event [with] a significant cause;" and the product of various kinds and stages of thought disorder, such as paranoid disposition, ranging in severity to diagnosable mental illnesses. Some people prefer socio-political explanations over the insecurity of encountering <a href="/wiki/Randomness" title="Randomness">random</a>, unpredictable, or otherwise inexplicable events.<sup id="cite_ref-business.time.com_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-business.time.com-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Goertzel_1994_733–744_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goertzel_1994_733–744-54">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Douglas_2008_210–222_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas_2008_210–222-55">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-harpers=1964_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harpers=1964-56">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hodapp_2008_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodapp_2008-57">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Berlet and Lyons, "Conspiracism is a particular narrative form of scapegoating that frames demonized enemies as part of a vast insidious plot against the common good, while it valorizes the scapegoater as a hero for sounding the alarm".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological_origins">Psychological origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Psychological origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some psychologists believe that a search for meaning is common in conspiracism. Once cognized, <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_bias" title="Confirmation bias">confirmation bias</a> and avoidance of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_dissonance" title="Cognitive dissonance">cognitive dissonance</a> may reinforce the belief. In a context where a conspiracy theory has become popular within a social group, <a href="/wiki/Communal_reinforcement" title="Communal reinforcement">communal reinforcement</a> may equally play a part. Research carried out at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kent" title="University of Kent">University of Kent</a> suggested people may be influenced by conspiracy theories without being aware that their attitudes have changed. After reading popular conspiracy theories about the <a href="/wiki/Death_of_Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Death of Diana, Princess of Wales">death of Princess Diana</a>, participants in the study correctly estimated how much their peers' attitudes had changed, but significantly underestimated how much their own attitudes had grown to favor conspiracy theories.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas_2008_210–222_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas_2008_210–222-55">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study published in 2012 also found that conspiracy theorists frequently believe in multiple conspiracies, even when one conspiracy contradicts the other.<sup id="cite_ref-Contradictory_CT_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Contradictory_CT-59">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> For example, the study found that people who believe <a href="/wiki/Osama_Bin_Laden" class="mw-redirect" title="Osama Bin Laden">Osama Bin Laden</a> was captured alive by Americans are also likely to believe that Bin Laden was actually killed prior to the 2011 raid on his home in Pakistan.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Projection">Projection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Projection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some historians have argued that there is an element of <a href="/wiki/Psychological_projection" title="Psychological projection">psychological projection</a> in conspiracism. This projection, according to the argument, is manifested in the form of attribution of undesirable characteristics of the self to the conspirators. Historian Richard Hofstadter stated that:
</p>
<blockquote><p>...&#160;it is hard to resist the conclusion that this enemy is on many counts the projection of the self; both the ideal and the unacceptable aspects of the self are attributed to him. The enemy may be the cosmopolitan intellectual, but the paranoid will outdo him in the apparatus of scholarship&#160;... the Ku Klux Klan imitated Catholicism to the point of donning priestly vestments, developing an elaborate ritual and an equally elaborate hierarchy. The <a href="/wiki/John_Birch_Society" title="John Birch Society">John Birch Society</a> emulates Communist cells and quasi-secret operation through "front" groups and preaches a ruthless prosecution of the ideological war along lines very similar to those it finds in the Communist enemy. Spokesmen of the various fundamentalist anti-Communist "crusades" openly express their admiration for the dedication and discipline the Communist cause calls forth.<sup id="cite_ref-harpers=1964_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harpers=1964-56">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Hofstadter also noted that "sexual freedom" is a vice frequently attributed to the conspiracist's target group, noting that "very often the fantasies of true believers reveal strong sadomasochistic outlets, vividly expressed, for example, in the delight of anti-Masons with the cruelty of Masonic punishments."<sup id="cite_ref-harpers=1964_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harpers=1964-56">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  A 2011 study found that highly <a href="/wiki/Machiavellian" class="mw-redirect" title="Machiavellian">Machiavellian</a> people are more likely to believe in conspiracy theories, since they themselves would be more willing to engage in a conspiracy when placed in the same situation as the alleged conspirators.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Epistemic_bias">Epistemic bias</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Epistemic bias">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to the British Psychological Society, it is possible that certain basic human <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemic biases</a> are projected onto the material under scrutiny. One study cited by the group found that humans apply a rule of thumb by which we expect a significant event to have a significant cause.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The study offered subjects four versions of events, in which a foreign president (a) was successfully assassinated, (b) was wounded but survived, (c) survived with wounds but died of a heart attack at a later date, and (d) was unharmed. Subjects were significantly more likely to suspect conspiracy in the case of the major events—in which the president died—than in the other cases, despite all other evidence available to them being equal. Connected with <a href="/wiki/Apophenia" title="Apophenia">apophenia</a>, the genetic tendency of human beings to find patterns in coincidence, this allows the discovery of conspiracy in any significant event.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_psychology">Clinical psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Clinical psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>For some individuals, an obsessive compulsion to believe, prove, or re-tell a conspiracy theory may indicate one or a combination of well-understood psychological conditions, and other hypothetical ones: paranoia, <a href="/wiki/Denial" title="Denial">denial</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mean_world_syndrome" title="Mean world syndrome">mean world syndrome</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociological_interpretations">Sociological interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sociological interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hitchens" title="Christopher Hitchens">Christopher Hitchens</a> described conspiracy theory as the "exhaust fumes of democracy":<sup id="cite_ref-Hodapp_2008_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodapp_2008-57">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> the unavoidable result of a large amount of information circulating among a large number of people.
</p><p>Conspiracy theories may be emotionally satisfying, by assigning blame to a group to which the theorist does not belong and so absolving the theorist of moral or political responsibility in society.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Cohen" title="Roger Cohen">Roger Cohen</a> writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> has said that, "captive minds; ... resort to conspiracy theory because it is the ultimate refuge of the powerless. If you cannot change your own life, it must be that some greater force controls the world."<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_45-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sociological historian Holger Herwig found in studying German explanations for the origins of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, "Those events that are most important are hardest to understand because they attract the greatest attention from myth makers and charlatans."<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_critical_theory">Influence of critical theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Influence of critical theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>French sociologist <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Latour" title="Bruno Latour">Bruno Latour</a> suggests that the widespread popularity of conspiracy theories in mass culture may be due, in part, to the pervasive presence of <a href="/wiki/Marxist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist">Marxist</a>-inspired <a href="/wiki/Critical_theory" title="Critical theory">critical theory</a> and similar ideas in academia since the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-critiquesteam_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-critiquesteam-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Latour notes that about 90% of contemporary social criticism in academia displays one of two approaches, which he terms "the <i>fact position</i> and the <i>fairy position</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-critiquesteam_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-critiquesteam-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap">:<span>237</span></sup> The fairy position is anti-fetishist, arguing that "objects of belief" (e.g., religion, arts) are merely concepts onto which power is projected; Latour contends that those who use this approach show biases towards confirming their own dogmatic suspicions as most "scientifically supported". While the complete facts of the situation and correct methodology are ostensibly important to them, Latour proposes that the scientific process is instead laid on as a patina to one's pet theories to lend a sort of reputation high ground. The "fact position" argues that individuals are dominated, often covertly and without their awareness, by external forces (e.g., economics, gender).<sup id="cite_ref-critiquesteam_65-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-critiquesteam-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Latour concludes that each of these two approaches in Academia has led to a polarized, inefficient atmosphere highlighted (in both approaches) by its causticness. "Do you see now why it feels so good to be a critical mind?" asks Latour: no matter which position you take, "You're always right!"<sup id="cite_ref-critiquesteam_65-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-critiquesteam-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Latour notes that such social criticism has been appropriated by those he describes as conspiracy theorists, including <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_denial" title="Climate change denial">climate change denialists</a> and the <a href="/wiki/9/11_Truth_movement" title="9/11 Truth movement">9/11 Truth movement</a>: "Maybe I am taking conspiracy theories too seriously, but I am worried to detect, in those mad mixtures of knee-jerk disbelief, punctilious demands for proofs, and free use of powerful explanation from the social neverland, many of the weapons of social critique."<sup id="cite_ref-critiquesteam_65-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-critiquesteam-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fusion_paranoia">Fusion paranoia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Fusion paranoia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Michael_Kelly_(editor)" title="Michael Kelly (editor)">Michael Kelly</a>, a <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i> journalist and critic of <a href="/wiki/Anti-war" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-war">anti-war</a> movements on both the left and right, coined the term "fusion paranoia" to refer to a political convergence of left-wing and right-wing activists around anti-war issues and <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a>, which he said were motivated by a shared belief in conspiracism or shared <a href="/wiki/Anti-statism" title="Anti-statism">anti-government</a> views.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Barkun has adopted this term to refer to how the synthesis of paranoid conspiracy theories, which were once limited to American fringe audiences, has given them mass appeal and enabled them to become commonplace in <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun2003230_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun2003230-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> thereby inaugurating an unrivaled period of people actively preparing for <a href="/wiki/Apocalypticism" title="Apocalypticism">apocalyptic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Millenarianism" title="Millenarianism">millenarian</a> scenarios in the United States of the late 20th and early 21st centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun2003207,_210,_211_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun2003207,_210,_211-68">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Barkun notes the occurrence of lone-wolf conflicts with law enforcement acting as proxy for threatening the established political powers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarkun2003193,_197_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarkun2003193,_197-69">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viability_of_conspiracies">Viability of conspiracies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Viability of conspiracies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The physicist <a href="/wiki/David_Robert_Grimes" title="David Robert Grimes">David Robert Grimes</a> published in the <a href="/wiki/PLOS_ONE" class="mw-redirect" title="PLOS ONE">PLOS ONE</a> journal an estimation of the time it would take for a conspiracy to be exposed, based on the number of people involved.<sup id="cite_ref-pbs_plos_one_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pbs_plos_one-70">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Grimes_PLOS_ONE_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Grimes_PLOS_ONE-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>  
His calculations used data from verified events such as <a href="/wiki/PRISM_(surveillance_program)" title="PRISM (surveillance program)">The National Security Agency (NSA) PRISM affair</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuskegee_Syphilis_Experiment" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuskegee Syphilis Experiment">Tuskegee Syphilis Experiment</a> and the <a href="/wiki/FBI_Laboratory#Controversy" title="FBI Laboratory">FBI forensic scandal</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories" title="Moon landing conspiracy theories">Moon landing hoax</a> would require the involvement of 411,000 people and would be exposed within 3.68 years;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Global_warming_conspiracy_theory" title="Global warming conspiracy theory">Climate-change fraud</a> would require 405,000 people and would be exposed within 3.70 years;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vaccine_controversies" title="Vaccine controversies">Vaccination conspiracy</a> would require a minimum of 22,000 people (without drug companies) and would be exposed within at least 3.15 years and at most 34.78 years depending on the number involved;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Big_Pharma_conspiracy_theory" title="Big Pharma conspiracy theory">Suppressed cancer cure conspiracy</a> would require 714,000 people and would be exposed within 3.17 years.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_use">Political use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conspiracy_theory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Political use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Open_Society_and_Its_Enemies" title="The Open Society and Its Enemies">The Open Society and Its Enemies</a></i>, the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Karl_Popper" title="Karl Popper">Karl Popper</a> used the term "conspiracy theory" to criticize the ideologies driving <a href="/wiki/Historicism" title="Historicism">historicism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Popper argued that <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarianism</a> was founded on "conspiracy theories" which drew on imaginary plots driven by paranoid scenarios predicated on tribalism, chauvinism, or racism.  Popper acknowledged that genuine conspiracies do exist,<sup id="cite_ref-dohloo_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dohloo-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> but noted how infrequently conspirators have been able to achieve their goal.<sup id="cite_ref-dohloo_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dohloo-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The historian <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Cumings" title="Bruce Cumings">Bruce Cumings</a> similarly rejects the notion that history is controlled by conspiracies, stating that where real conspiracies have appeared they have usually had little effect on history and have had unforeseen consequences for the conspirators.  Cumings concludes that history is instead "moved by the broad forces and large structures of human collectivities".<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2009 article, the legal scholars <a href="/wiki/Cass_Sunstein" title="Cass Sunstein">Cass Sunstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Vermeule" title="Adrian Vermeule">Adrian Vermeule</a> considered a number of possible government responses to conspiracy theories, including censorship and taxation, and concluding that the authorities ought to engage in counter-speech and dialogue, which they termed "cognitive infiltration".<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
