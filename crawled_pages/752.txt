<p><b>Postchristianity</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is the loss of the primacy of the Christian worldview in political affairs, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a> where <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> had previously flourished, in favor of alternative worldviews such as <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It includes personal <a href="/wiki/World_views" class="mw-redirect" title="World views">world views</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ideologies" class="mw-redirect" title="Ideologies">ideologies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Religious_movements" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious movements">religious movements</a> or <a href="/wiki/Societies" class="mw-redirect" title="Societies">societies</a> that are no longer rooted in the language and assumptions of Christianity, at least explicitly, although they had previously been in an environment of ubiquitous Christianity (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a>).
</p><p>Some scholars<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (September 2018)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> have disputed the global decline of Christianity, and instead hypothesized of an evolution of Christianity which allows it to not only survive, but actively expand its influence in contemporary societies.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_Christianity">Decline of Christianity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postchristianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Decline of Christianity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, the majority of Christians have lived in <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a>, <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">White</a> nations, often conceptualized as "<a href="/wiki/Role_of_the_Christian_Church_in_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Role of the Christian Church in civilization">European Christian" civilization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A postchristian world is one in which Christianity is no longer the dominant <a href="/wiki/Civil_religion" title="Civil religion">civil religion</a> but has gradually assumed <a href="/wiki/Values" class="mw-redirect" title="Values">values</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, and <a href="/wiki/World_view" title="World view">worldviews</a> that are not necessarily <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a> (and also may not necessarily reflect any world religion's standpoint or may represent a combination of either several religions or none). Post-Christian tends to refer to the loss of Christianity's <a href="/wiki/Monopoly" title="Monopoly">monopoly</a>, if not its followers, in historically Christian societies.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Postchristian societies can be found across the Global North - for example, though the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Eurobarometer" title="Eurobarometer">Eurobarometer</a> survey indicated that the majority of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europeans</a> hold some form of belief in a <a href="/wiki/Higher_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Higher power">higher power</a>, fewer point explicitly to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_God" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian God">Christian God</a>. 
</p><p>Despite the declining, Christianity remains the <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Europe" title="Religion in Europe">dominant religion in Europe</a>, according to a 2010 study by the <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a>, 76.2% of the <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Europe" title="Demographics of Europe">European</a> population identified themselves as <a href="/wiki/Christians" class="mw-redirect" title="Christians">Christians</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Christianity_in_Europe_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christianity_in_Europe-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and according to a 2012 <a href="/wiki/Eurobarometer" title="Eurobarometer">Eurobarometer</a> survey, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> was the largest religion in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (account 72% of EU population).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> According to a 2010 study by the <a href="/wiki/Pew_Research_Center" title="Pew Research Center">Pew Research Center</a>, about 70.6% of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> population identified themselves as <a href="/wiki/Christians" class="mw-redirect" title="Christians">Christians</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (90% in <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> and 77% in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his 1961 <i>The Death of God</i>, the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> theologian <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Vahanian" title="Gabriel Vahanian">Gabriel Vahanian</a> argued that modern secular culture in most of <a href="/wiki/Western_civilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Western civilization">Western civilization</a> had lost all sense of the <a href="/wiki/Sacred" title="Sacred">sacred</a>, lacked any <a href="/wiki/Sacrament" title="Sacrament">sacramental</a> meaning, and disdained any <a href="/wiki/Transcendence_(religion)" title="Transcendence (religion)">transcendental</a> purpose or sense of <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">providence</a>, bringing him to the conclusion that for the modern mind, "<a href="/wiki/God_is_dead" title="God is dead">God is dead</a>". <a href="/wiki/Thomas_J._J._Altizer" title="Thomas J. J. Altizer">Thomas J. J. Altizer</a> and William Hamilton of <a href="/wiki/Emory_University" title="Emory University">Emory University</a> drew upon a variety of sources, including the <a href="/wiki/Aphorism" title="Aphorism">aphorisms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dietrich_Bonhoeffer" title="Dietrich Bonhoeffer">Dietrich Bonhoeffer</a>'s <i>Letters and Papers from Prison</i>, and brought this line of thought to public attention in a short-lived intellectual movement of the mid-to-late-1960s among <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> theologians and ministerial students. Conservative reaction on the right and social-advocacy efforts on the left blunted its impact, however, and it was quickly overlooked in favor of more ethically-oriented movements such as the <a href="/wiki/Social_Gospel" title="Social Gospel">Social Gospel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminist_theology" title="Feminist theology">feminist theologies</a>, within <a href="/wiki/Mainline_Protestant" title="Mainline Protestant">mainline Protestantism</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_perspectives">Alternative perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postchristianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alternative perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other scholars have disputed the global decline of Christianity, and instead hypothesized of an evolution of Christianity which allows it to not only survive, but actively expand its influence in contemporary societies.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Philip_Jenkins" title="Philip Jenkins">Philip Jenkins</a> hypothesized a "Christian Revolution" in the Southern nations, such as Africa, Asia and Latin America, where instead of facing decline, Christianity is actively expanding. The relevance of Christian teachings in the global South will allow the Christian population in these areas to continually increase, and together with the shrinking of the Western Christian population, will form a "new Christendom" in which the majority of the world's Christian population can be found in the South.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_Taylor_(philosopher)" title="Charles Taylor (philosopher)">Charles Taylor</a>, meanwhile, disputes the "God is Dead" thesis by arguing that the practices and understandings of faith changed long before the late 20th century, along with secularism itself. In <i><a href="/wiki/A_Secular_Age" title="A Secular Age">A Secular Age</a></i> Taylor argues that being "free from Christendom" has allowed Christianity to endure and express itself in various ways, particularly in Western society; he notes that otherwise secular ideas were, and continue to be, formed in light of some manner of faith. He stresses that "loss of faith" reflects simplistic notions on the nature of <a href="/wiki/Secularization" title="Secularization">secularization</a>, namely the idea of "subtraction." Thus "post-Christian" is, after a fashion, a product of Christianity itself.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Postchristianity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some American Christians (primarily Protestants) also use this term in reference to the <a href="/wiki/Evangelism" title="Evangelism">evangelism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Unchurched" title="Unchurched">unchurched</a> individuals who may have grown up in a non-Christian culture where traditional <a href="/wiki/Biblical" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical">Biblical</a> references may be unfamiliar concepts. This perspective argues that, among previous generations in the United States, such concepts and other artifacts of <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_Christianity" title="Glossary of Christianity">Christianese</a> would have been common cultural knowledge and that it would not have been necessary to teach this language to adult <a href="/wiki/Religious_conversion" title="Religious conversion">converts</a> to Christianity. In this sense, post-Christian is not used pejoratively, but is intended to describe the special remediative care that would be needed to introduce <a href="/wiki/New_Christian" title="New Christian">new Christians</a> to the nuances of Christian life and practice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Some groups use the term "post-Christian" as a self-description. <a href="/wiki/Dana_McLean_Greeley" title="Dana McLean Greeley">Dana McLean Greeley</a>, the first president of the <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association</a>, described <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalism" title="Unitarian Universalism">Unitarian Universalism</a> as postchristian, insofar as Christians no longer considered it Christian, while persons of other religions would likely describe it as Christian, at least historically.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
