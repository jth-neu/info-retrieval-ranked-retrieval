<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Chris Ofili</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Christopher Ofili</b>, <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a></span> known as <b>Chris Ofili</b> (born 10 October 1968), is a <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_British" class="mw-redirect" title="Nigerian British">Nigerian British</a> <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a>-winning painter who is best known for his paintings incorporating elephant dung. He was one of the <a href="/wiki/Young_British_Artists" title="Young British Artists">Young British Artists</a>. Since 2005, Ofili has been living and working in <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, where he currently resides in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Spain" title="Port of Spain">Port of Spain</a>. He also lives and works in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Ofili&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ofili was born in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> to May and Michael Ofili. When he was eleven, his father left the family and moved back to <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Ofili was for some years educated at St. Pius X High School for Boys, and then at <a href="/wiki/Xaverian_College" title="Xaverian College">Xaverian College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Park,_Manchester" title="Victoria Park, Manchester">Victoria Park, Manchester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Ofili completed a foundation course in art at <a href="/wiki/Tameside_College" title="Tameside College">Tameside College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ashton-under-Lyne" title="Ashton-under-Lyne">Ashton-under-Lyne</a> in Greater Manchester<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and then studied in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, at the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_School_of_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelsea School of Art">Chelsea School of Art</a> from 1988 to 1991 and at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Art" title="Royal College of Art">Royal College of Art</a> from 1991 to 1993. In the fall of 1992, he got a one-year exchange scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Berlin_University_of_the_Arts" title="Berlin University of the Arts">Universität der Künste Berlin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ofili visited <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> for the first time in 2000, when he was invited by an international art trust to attend a painting workshop in Port of Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He permanently moved to Trinidad in 2005. He lives with his wife Roba, former singer with <a href="/wiki/Trip-hop" class="mw-redirect" title="Trip-hop">trip-hop</a> band <a href="/wiki/Attica_Blues_(band)" title="Attica Blues (band)">Attica Blues</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Attica_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Attica-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> whom he married in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He maintains a studio on Lady Chancellor Road, about ten minutes from downtown Port of Spain, in Trinidad.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Ofili&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ofili's early work was heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Michel_Basquiat" title="Jean-Michel Basquiat">Jean-Michel Basquiat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Baselitz" title="Georg Baselitz">Georg Baselitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philip_Guston" title="Philip Guston">Philip Guston</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Condo" title="George Condo">George Condo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Doig" title="Peter Doig">Peter Doig</a> was doing graduate work at the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_College_of_Arts" title="Chelsea College of Arts">Chelsea College of Arts</a> when Ofili was an undergraduate, and they soon became friends.<sup id="cite_ref-newyorker.com_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newyorker.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, art critic <a href="/wiki/Roberta_Smith" title="Roberta Smith">Roberta Smith</a> held that Ofili has much in common with painters like <a href="/wiki/Mickalene_Thomas" title="Mickalene Thomas">Mickalene Thomas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerry_James_Marshall" title="Kerry James Marshall">Kerry James Marshall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Colescott" title="Robert Colescott">Robert Colescott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Gallagher" title="Ellen Gallagher">Ellen Gallagher</a>, and with more distant precedents such as <a href="/wiki/Bob_Thompson_(painter)" title="Bob Thompson (painter)">Bob Thompson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beauford_Delaney" title="Beauford Delaney">Beauford Delaney</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Johnson_(artist)" title="William Johnson (artist)">William H. Johnson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ofili was established through exhibitions by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Saatchi" title="Charles Saatchi">Charles Saatchi</a> at his gallery in north London and the travelling exhibition <i><a href="/wiki/Sensation_exhibition" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensation exhibition">Sensation</a></i> (1997), becoming recognised as one of the few British artists of African / Caribbean descent to break through as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Young_British_Artists" title="Young British Artists">Young British Artists</a> group. Ofili has also had numerous solo shows since the early 1990s, including at the <a href="/wiki/Serpentine_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Serpentine Gallery">Serpentine Gallery</a>. In 1998, Ofili won the <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a>, and in 2003 he was selected to represent Britain at the <a href="/wiki/Venice_Biennale" title="Venice Biennale">Venice Biennale</a> of that year, where his work for the British Pavilion was done in collaboration with the architect <a href="/wiki/David_Adjaye" title="David Adjaye">David Adjaye</a>.
</p><p>In 1992 he won a scholarship that allowed him to travel to <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>. Ofili studied <a href="/wiki/Cave_painting" title="Cave painting">cave paintings</a> there, which had some effect on his style.
</p><p>Between 1995 and 2005, Ofili focused on a series of watercolors, each about 9½ by 6½ inches and produced in a single sitting.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> They predominantly feature heads of men and women, as well as some studies of flowers and birds.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Ofili's paintings also make reference to <a href="/wiki/Blaxploitation" title="Blaxploitation">blaxploitation</a> films and <a href="/wiki/Gangsta_rap" title="Gangsta rap">gangsta rap</a>, seeking to question racial and sexual stereotypes in a humorous way. In a series of faces that Ofili called <i>Harems</i>, each arrangement consists of one man with as many as four women on each side of him.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ofili's work is often built up in layers of paint, resin, glitter, dung (mainly elephant) and other materials to create a <a href="/wiki/Collage" title="Collage">collage</a>. Though Ofili's detractors often state that he "splatters"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> elephant <a href="/wiki/Feces" title="Feces">dung</a> on his pictures, this is inaccurate: he sometimes applies it directly to the canvas in the form of dried spherical lumps, and sometimes, in the same form, uses it as varnished foot-like supports on which the paintings stand.
</p><p>Ofili has been founder and prime mover behind the short-lived <i>Freeness Project</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This project involved the coming together of artists, producers and musicians of minority ethnic groups (Asian and African) in an attempt to expose the music that may be unheard in other spaces. Freeness allowed the creativity of unsigned contemporary British ethnic minority artists to be heard. The result of months of tours to 10 cities in the UK resulted in <i>Freeness Volume 1</i> - a compilation of works that were shown during the tour.
</p><p>After relocating to Trinidad in 2005, Ofili began a series of blue paintings inspired by the <i>Jab Jab</i> or "blue devils" who participate in the <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago_Carnival" title="Trinidad and Tobago Carnival">Trinidad and Tobago Carnival</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">Expressionist</a> group of German and Russian artists, <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Blaue_Reiter" title="Der Blaue Reiter">Der Blaue Reiter</a>.</i> These paintings often employed the use of a silver, acrylic background with layers of dark oil pigment on top.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Later iterations of these works were shown at Ofili's solo show <i>Chris Ofili: Day and Night</i> at <a href="/wiki/New_Museum" title="New Museum">The New Museum of New York</a> which were installed in a very dimly lit room, causing viewers to adjust their eyes to the darkness in order to see the paintings.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ofili was appointed <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">Commander of the Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE) in the <a href="/wiki/2017_New_Year_Honours" title="2017 New Year Honours">2017 New Year Honours</a> for services to art.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Ofili&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ofili's work was featured in a museum in the 1995 exhibition <i>Brilliant! New Art from London</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Walker_Art_Center" title="Walker Art Center">Walker Art Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Significant solo exhibitions include the <a href="/wiki/Arts_Club_of_Chicago" title="Arts Club of Chicago">Arts Club of Chicago</a> (2010), <a href="/wiki/Kestnergesellschaft" title="Kestnergesellschaft">Kestnergesellschaft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hanover" title="Hanover">Hanover</a> (2006), the <a href="/wiki/Studio_Museum_in_Harlem" title="Studio Museum in Harlem">Studio Museum in Harlem</a>, New York (2005), and Southampton City Art Gallery (1998). In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">Tate Britain</a> presented the most extensive exhibition of his work to date.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, <a href="/wiki/New_Museum" title="New Museum">The New Museum in New York</a> presented the first, major solo show of Ofili's work in the U.S. titled <i>Chris Ofili: Night and Day</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Ofili&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Holy_Virgin_Mary"><i>The Holy Virgin Mary</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Ofili&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Holy Virgin Mary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of his paintings, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Virgin_Mary" title="The Holy Virgin Mary">The Holy Virgin Mary</a></i>, a depiction of the <a href="/wiki/Mary,_the_mother_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary, the mother of Jesus">Virgin Mary</a>, was at issue in a lawsuit between the mayor of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Giuliani" title="Rudy Giuliani">Rudy Giuliani</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum_of_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="Brooklyn Museum of Art">Brooklyn Museum of Art</a> when it was exhibited there in 1999 as a part of the <a href="/wiki/Sensation_exhibition" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensation exhibition">"Sensation"</a> exhibit. The painting depicted a <a href="/wiki/Black_Madonna" title="Black Madonna">Black Madonna</a> surrounded by images from <a href="/wiki/Blaxploitation" title="Blaxploitation">blaxploitation</a> movies and close-ups of female genitalia cut from pornographic magazines, and elephant dung.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> These were formed into shapes reminiscent of the <a href="/wiki/Cherubim" class="mw-redirect" title="Cherubim">cherubim</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seraphim" class="mw-redirect" title="Seraphim">seraphim</a> commonly depicted in images of the <a href="/wiki/Immaculate_Conception" title="Immaculate Conception">Immaculate Conception</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption of Mary</a>. Following the scandal surrounding this painting, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Goldberg" title="Bernard Goldberg">Bernard Goldberg</a> ranked Ofili No. 86 in <i><a href="/wiki/100_People_Who_Are_Screwing_Up_America" title="100 People Who Are Screwing Up America">100 People Who Are Screwing Up America</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Red_Grooms" title="Red Grooms">Red Grooms</a> showed his support of the artist by purchasing one of Ofili's paintings in 1999, even after Giuliani famously exclaimed, "There’s nothing in the First Amendment that supports horrible and disgusting projects!”<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The painting was owned by <a href="/wiki/David_Walsh_(art_collector)" title="David Walsh (art collector)">David Walsh</a> and was on display at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Old_and_New_Art" title="Museum of Old and New Art">Museum of Old and New Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hobart" title="Hobart">Hobart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Steven A. Cohen then owned it for three years and donated the painting to the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Upper_Room_and_the_Tate_Gallery"><i>The Upper Room</i> and the Tate Gallery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Ofili&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Upper Room and the Tate Gallery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tate%27s_purchase_of_The_Upper_Room" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate&#39;s purchase of The Upper Room">Tate's purchase of The Upper Room</a></div>
<p><i>The Upper Room</i> is an installation of 13 paintings of <a href="/wiki/Rhesus_macaque" title="Rhesus macaque">rhesus macaque</a> monkeys by Ofili in a specially designed room. It was bought by the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a> in 2005 and caused controversy as Ofili was on the board of the Tate Trustees at the time of the purchase.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In 2006 the <a href="/wiki/Charity_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Charity Commission">Charity Commission</a> censured the Tate for this purchase.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_market">Art market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chris_Ofili&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Art market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His <i>Orgena</i>, a glittery portrait of a black woman created by the artist for his Turner Prize-winning exhibit at the Tate in 1998 was sold to an American collector for a record GBP 1.8 million, over its GBP 1 million high estimate, at <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s" title="Christie&#39;s">Christie's</a> London in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, art collector <a href="/wiki/David_Walsh_(art_collector)" title="David Walsh (art collector)">David Walsh</a> sold Ofili’s 8-foot-tall <i>The Holy Virgin Mary</i> for 2.9 million pounds at <a href="/wiki/Christie%E2%80%99s" class="mw-redirect" title="Christie’s">Christie’s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
