<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Aliweb</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>ALIWEB</b> (<i>A</i>rchie <i>L</i>ike <i>I</i>ndexing for the <i>WEB</i>) is considered the first Web <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">search engine</a>, as its predecessors were either built with different purposes (<a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Wanderer" title="World Wide Web Wanderer">the Wanderer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gopher_(protocol)" title="Gopher (protocol)">Gopher</a>) or were literally just indexers (<a href="/wiki/Archie_search_engine" title="Archie search engine">Archie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Veronica_(computer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Veronica (computer)">Veronica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jughead_(computer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jughead (computer)">Jughead</a>).
</p><p>First announced in November 1993<sup id="cite_ref-Announcement_html_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Announcement_html-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>   by developer <a href="/wiki/Martijn_Koster" title="Martijn Koster">Martijn Koster</a> while working at <a href="/wiki/Nexor" title="Nexor">Nexor</a>, and presented in May 1994<sup id="cite_ref-CERN_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CERN-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  at the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_World_Wide_Web#Web_organization" title="History of the World Wide Web">First International Conference</a> on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, ALIWEB preceded <a href="/wiki/WebCrawler" title="WebCrawler">WebCrawler</a> by several months.<sup id="cite_ref-Birthday_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birthday-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>ALIWEB allowed users to submit the locations of index files on their sites<sup id="cite_ref-Birthday_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birthday-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-History_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  which enabled the search engine to include webpages and add user-written page descriptions and keywords. This empowered webmasters to define the terms that would lead users to their pages, and also avoided setting bots (<i>e.g.</i> <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Wanderer" title="World Wide Web Wanderer">the Wanderer</a>, <a href="/wiki/JumpStation" title="JumpStation">JumpStation</a>) which used up bandwidth.  As relatively few people submitted their sites, ALIWEB was not very widely used.
</p><p>Martijn Koster, who was also instrumental in the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Robots_Exclusion_Standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Robots Exclusion Standard">Robots Exclusion Standard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Exclusion_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Exclusion-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Robots_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robots-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  detailed the background and objectives of ALIWEB with an overview of its functions and framework in the paper he presented at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CERN_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CERN-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Koster is not associated with a <a href="/wiki/Link_farm" title="Link farm">commercial</a> website which uses the aliweb name.<sup id="cite_ref-Koster_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koster-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
