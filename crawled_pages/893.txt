<p><b>Sir Joshua Reynolds</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Royal_Academician" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academician">RA</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Arts">FRSA</a></span> (16 July 1723 – 23 February 1792) was an English painter, specialising in portraits.<a href="/wiki/John_Russell_(art_critic)" title="John Russell (art critic)"> John Russell</a> said he was one of the major European painters of the 18th century. <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He promoted the <a href="/wiki/Grand_manner" title="Grand manner">"Grand Style"</a> in painting which depended on idealization of the imperfect. He was a founder and first president of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a>, and was <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighted</a> by <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III</a> in 1769.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_%282%29.jpg/220px-Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_%282%29.jpg" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_%282%29.jpg/330px-Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_%282%29.jpg/440px-Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4833" data-file-height="3986" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Self-portrait_c.1747-9_by_Joshua_Reynolds_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Joshua Reynolds, <i>Self-portrait</i>, aged about 24</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG/220px-PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG/330px-PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG/440px-PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PlymptonOldGrammarSchool_Devon.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Plympton_Grammar_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Plympton Grammar School">Old Grammar School, Plympton</a>, founded 1658, built 1664, attended by Joshua Reynolds whose father was headmaster</div></div></div>
<p>Reynolds was born in <a href="/wiki/Plympton" title="Plympton">Plympton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, on 16 July 1723<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the third son of the Rev. Samuel Reynolds, master of the <a href="/wiki/Plympton_Grammar_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Plympton Grammar School">Plympton Free Grammar School</a> in the town. His father had been a fellow of <a href="/wiki/Balliol_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Balliol College">Balliol College</a>, Oxford, but did not send any of his sons to the university.<sup id="cite_ref-penny1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
One of his sisters was <a href="/wiki/Mary_Palmer" title="Mary Palmer">Mary Palmer</a> (1716–1794), seven years his senior, author of <i>Devonshire Dialogue</i>, whose fondness for drawing is said to have had much influence on him when a boy. In 1740 she provided £60, half of the premium paid to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hudson_(painter)" title="Thomas Hudson (painter)">Thomas Hudson</a> the portrait-painter, for Joshua's pupilage, and nine years later advanced money for his expenses in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His other siblings included <a href="/wiki/Frances_Reynolds" title="Frances Reynolds">Frances Reynolds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Johnson_(pamphleteer)" title="Elizabeth Johnson (pamphleteer)">Elizabeth Johnson</a>.
</p><p>As a boy, he came under the influence of <a href="/wiki/Zachariah_Mudge_(clergyman)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zachariah Mudge (clergyman)">Zachariah Mudge</a>, whose Platonistic philosophy stayed with him all his life. Reynolds made extracts in his <a href="/wiki/Commonplace_book" title="Commonplace book">commonplace book</a> from <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">Plutarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">Seneca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Antonius" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcus Antonius">Marcus Antonius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">Alexander Pope</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Addison" title="Joseph Addison">Joseph Addison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Steele" title="Richard Steele">Richard Steele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aphra_Behn" title="Aphra Behn">Aphra Behn</a>, and passages on art theory by <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alphonse_Du_Fresnoy" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Alphonse Du Fresnoy">Charles Alphonse Du Fresnoy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_F%C3%A9libien" title="André Félibien">André Félibien</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The work that came to have the most influential impact on Reynolds was <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Richardson" title="Jonathan Richardson">Jonathan Richardson</a>'s <i>An Essay on the Theory of Painting</i> (1715). Reynolds' annotated copy was lost for nearly two hundred years until it appeared in a Cambridge bookshop, inscribed with the signature ‘J. Reynolds Pictor’, and is now in the collection of the Royal Academy of Arts, London.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg/150px-Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg" width="150" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg/225px-Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg/300px-Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1387" data-file-height="2250" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Joshua_Reynolds_by_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Self-portrait, aged 17, entitled, <i>Uffizi Self-portrait</i></div></div></div>
<p>Having shown an early interest in art, Reynolds was apprenticed in 1740 to the fashionable London portrait painter <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hudson_(painter)" title="Thomas Hudson (painter)">Thomas Hudson</a>, who had been born in Devon.<sup id="cite_ref-penny1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Hudson had a collection of Old Master drawings, including some by <a href="/wiki/Guercino" title="Guercino">Guercino</a>, of which  Reynolds made copies.<sup id="cite_ref-penny1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Although apprenticed to Hudson for four years, Reynolds only remained with him until summer 1743.<sup id="cite_ref-penny1_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Having left Hudson, Reynolds worked for some time as a portrait-painter in Plymouth Dock (now <a href="/wiki/Devonport,_Devon" class="mw-redirect" title="Devonport, Devon">Devonport</a>). He returned to London before the end of 1744, but following his father's death in late 1745 he shared a house in Plymouth Dock with his sisters.<sup id="cite_ref-penny1_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1749, Reynolds met Commodore <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Keppel,_1st_Viscount_Keppel" title="Augustus Keppel, 1st Viscount Keppel">Augustus Keppel</a>, who invited him to join <i><a href="/wiki/HMS_Centurion_(1732)" title="HMS Centurion (1732)">HMS Centurion</a></i>, of which he had command, on a voyage to the Mediterranean. While with the ship he visited Lisbon, Cadiz, Algiers, and Minorca. From Minorca he travelled to Livorno in Italy, and then to Rome,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> where he spent two years,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> studying the Old Masters and acquiring a taste for the "Grand Style".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Lord Edgcumbe, who had known Reynolds as a boy and introduced him to Keppel, suggested he should study with <a href="/wiki/Pompeo_Batoni" title="Pompeo Batoni">Pompeo Batoni</a>, the leading painter in Rome, but Reynolds replied that he had nothing to learn from him.<sup id="cite_ref-penny1_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While in Rome he suffered a severe cold, which left him partially deaf, and, as a result, he began to carry a small <a href="/wiki/Ear_trumpet" title="Ear trumpet">ear trumpet</a> with which he is often pictured.
</p><p>Reynolds travelled homeward overland via Florence, Bologna, Venice,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He was accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Filippo_Liberati_Marchi" title="Giuseppe Filippo Liberati Marchi">Giuseppe Marchi</a>, then aged about 17.<sup id="cite_ref-marchi_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchi-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Apart from a brief interlude in 1770, Marchi remained in Reynolds' employment as a studio assistant for the rest of  the artist's career.<sup id="cite_ref-marchi_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marchi-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Following his arrival in England in October 1752, Reynolds spent three months in Devon,<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> before establishing himself in London, where he remained for the rest of his life. He took rooms in <a href="/wiki/St_Martin%27s_Lane" title="St Martin&#39;s Lane">St Martin's Lane</a>, before moving to Great Newport Street, his sister Frances acted as his housekeeper.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He achieved success rapidly, and was extremely prolific.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Lord Edgecumbe recommended the Duke of Devonshire and Duke of Grafton to sit for him, and other peers followed, including the Duke of Cumberland, third son of George II,<sup id="cite_ref-penny22_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny22-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> in whose portrait, according to <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Penny" title="Nicholas Penny">Nicholas Penny</a> "bulk is brilliantly converted into power".<sup id="cite_ref-penny22_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny22-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1760 Reynolds moved into a large house, with space to show his works and accommodate his assistants, on the west side of <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Square" title="Leicester Square">Leicester Fields</a> (now Leicester Square).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg/170px-Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg" width="170" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg/255px-Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7f/Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg 2x" data-file-width="313" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Joshua_Reynolds_Self_Portrait.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Self-portrait</i></div></div></div>
<p>Alongside ambitious full-length portraits, Reynolds painted large numbers of smaller works. In the late 1750s, at the height of the social season, he received five or six sitters a day, each for an hour.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> By 1761 Reynolds could command a fee of 80 <a href="/wiki/Guinea_(coin)" title="Guinea (coin)">guineas</a> for a full-length portrait; in 1764 he was paid 100 guineas for a portrait of <a href="/wiki/John_Fane,_9th_Earl_of_Westmorland" title="John Fane, 9th Earl of Westmorland">Lord Burghersh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The clothing of Reynolds' sitters was usually painted either by one of his pupils,<sup id="cite_ref-northcote_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northcote-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> his studio assistant Giuseppe Marchi,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> or the specialist drapery painter <a href="/wiki/Peter_Toms_(painter)" title="Peter Toms (painter)">Peter Toms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-northcote_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northcote-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> James Northcote, his pupil, wrote of this arrangement that "the imitation of particular stuffs is not the work of genius, but is to be acquired easily by practice, and this was what his pupils could do by care and time more than he himself chose to bestow; but his own slight and masterly work was still the best."<sup id="cite_ref-northcote_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northcote-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Lay figures were used to model the clothes.<sup id="cite_ref-penny22_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penny22-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reynolds often adapted the poses of his subjects from the works of earlier artists, a practice mocked by <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Hone_the_Elder" title="Nathaniel Hone the Elder">Nathaniel Hone</a> in a painting called <i>The Conjuror</i> submitted to the Royal Academy exhibition of 1775, and now in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Ireland" title="National Gallery of Ireland">National Gallery of Ireland</a>. It shows a figure representing, though not resembling, Reynolds, seated in front of a cascade of prints from which Reynolds had borrowed with varying degrees of subtlety.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although not principally known for his landscapes, Reynolds did paint in this genre. He had an excellent vantage from his house, <a href="/wiki/Wick_House,_Richmond_Hill" title="Wick House, Richmond Hill">Wick House</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Hill,_London" title="Richmond Hill, London">Richmond Hill</a>, and painted the view in about 1780.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton,_2005,_24..jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton%2C_2005%2C_24..jpg/220px-.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton%2C_2005%2C_24..jpg" width="220" height="252" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton%2C_2005%2C_24..jpg/330px-.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton%2C_2005%2C_24..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton%2C_2005%2C_24..jpg/440px-.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton%2C_2005%2C_24..jpg 2x" data-file-width="642" data-file-height="735" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:.Fig_1_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds_The_Age_of_Innocence._Painted_circa_1788._Frame_contemporary_with_picture._From_Houghton,_2005,_24..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Innocence_(painting)" title="The Age of Innocence (painting)">The Age of Innocence</a></i> <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1788</span>, Reynolds emphasized the natural grace of children in his paintings</div></div></div>
<p>Reynolds also was recognized for his portraits of children. He emphasized the innocence and natural grace of children when depicting them. His 1788 portrait, <a href="/wiki/The_Age_of_Innocence_(1788_painting)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Age of Innocence (1788 painting)"><i>Age of Innocence</i></a>, is his best known character study of a child. The subject of the painting is not known, although conjecture includes Theophila Gwatkin, his great niece, and Lady Anne Spencer, the youngest daughter of the fourth Duke of Marlborough.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Club">The Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Reynolds worked long hours in his studio, rarely taking a holiday. He was gregarious and keenly intellectual, with many friends from London's intelligentsia, numbered amongst whom were <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Johnson" title="Samuel Johnson">Dr Samuel Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Goldsmith" title="Oliver Goldsmith">Oliver Goldsmith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Baretti" class="mw-redirect" title="Giuseppe Baretti">Giuseppe Baretti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Thrale" title="Henry Thrale">Henry Thrale</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Garrick" title="David Garrick">David Garrick</a>, and artist <a href="/wiki/Angelica_Kauffman" title="Angelica Kauffman">Angelica Kauffman</a>. Johnson said in 1778: "Reynolds is too much under [Charles James] Fox and Burke at present. He is under the <i>Fox star</i> and the <i>Irish constellation</i> [meaning Burke]. He is always under some planet".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of his popularity as a portrait painter, Reynolds enjoyed constant interaction with the wealthy and famous men and women of the day, and it was he who brought together the figures of <a href="/wiki/The_Club_(dining_club)" title="The Club (dining club)">"The" Club</a>. It was founded in 1764 and met in a suite of rooms on the first floor of the Turks Head at 9 Gerrard Street, now marked by a plaque. Original members included Burke, <a href="/wiki/Bennet_Langton" title="Bennet Langton">Bennet Langton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topham_Beauclerk" title="Topham Beauclerk">Topham Beauclerk</a>, Goldsmith, Anthony Chamier, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hawkins_(literary_editor)" title="Thomas Hawkins (literary editor)">Thomas Hawkins</a>, and Nugent, to be joined by Garrick, <a href="/wiki/James_Boswell" title="James Boswell">Boswell</a>, and Sheridan. In ten years the membership had risen to 35. The Club met every Monday evening for supper and conversation and continued into the early hours of Tuesday morning. In later years, it met fortnightly during Parliamentary sessions. When in 1783 the landlord of the Turks Head died and the property was sold, The Club moved to Sackville Street.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Academy">Royal Academy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Royal Academy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Loton_Park_c1870.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Loton_Park_c1870.jpg/220px-Loton_Park_c1870.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Loton_Park_c1870.jpg/330px-Loton_Park_c1870.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Loton_Park_c1870.jpg/440px-Loton_Park_c1870.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3041" data-file-height="2433" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Loton_Park_c1870.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The hall at <a href="/wiki/Loton_Park" title="Loton Park">Loton Park</a>, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1870</span>. Showing, in situ, on the far wall Reynolds' <i>Frances Anne Crewe (miss Greville), as St. <a href="/wiki/Genevieve" title="Genevieve">Genevieve</a></i>, <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1773</span></div></div></div>
<p>Reynolds  was one of the earliest members of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Royal Society of Arts">Royal Society of Arts</a>, helped found the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Artists_of_Great_Britain" title="Society of Artists of Great Britain">Society of Artists of Great Britain</a>, and in 1768 became the first president of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a>, a position he was to hold until his death. In 1769, he was <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighted</a> by <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">George III</a>, only the second artist to be so honored.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  His <i>Discourses</i>, a series of lectures delivered at the Academy  between 1769 and 1790, are remembered for their sensitivity and perception. In one lecture he expressed the opinion that "invention, strictly speaking, is little more than a new combination of those images which have been previously gathered and deposited in the memory." William Jackson in his contemporary essays said of Reynolds ' there is much ingenuity and originality in all his academic discourses, replete with classical knowledge of his art, acute remarks on the works of others,  and general taste and discernment'.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reynolds and the Royal Academy received a mixed reception. Critics included <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a> who published the vitriolic <i>Annotations to Sir Joshua Reynolds' Discourses</i> in 1808. <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">J. M. W. Turner</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Northcote_(painter)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Northcote (painter)">James Northcote</a> were fervent acolytes: Turner requested he be laid to rest at Reynolds' side, and Northcote, who spent four years as Reynolds' pupil, wrote to his family "I know him thoroughly, and all his faults, I am sure, and yet almost worship him."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_Keppel"><i>Lord Keppel</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lord Keppel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Augustus_Keppel,_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Augustus_Keppel%2C_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg/220px-Augustus_Keppel%2C_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Augustus_Keppel%2C_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg/330px-Augustus_Keppel%2C_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Augustus_Keppel%2C_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg/440px-Augustus_Keppel%2C_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="2999" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Augustus_Keppel,_Viscount_Keppel_by_Sir_Joshua_Reynolds.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Augustus_Keppel" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus Keppel">Lord Keppel</a></i> (1779)</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ushant_(1778)" title="Battle of Ushant (1778)">Battle of Ushant</a> against the French in 1778, <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Keppel,_1st_Viscount_Keppel" title="Augustus Keppel, 1st Viscount Keppel">Lord Keppel</a> commanded the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">Channel</a> Fleet and the outcome resulted in no clear winner; Keppel ordered the attack be renewed and was obeyed except by Sir <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Palliser" title="Hugh Palliser">Hugh Palliser</a>, who commanded the rear, and the French escaped bombardment. A dispute between Keppel and Palliser arose and Palliser brought charges of misconduct and neglect of duty against Keppel and the Admiralty decided to court-martial him. On 11 February 1779 Keppel was acquitted of all charges and became a national hero. One of Keppel's lawyers commissioned Sir <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Dance-Holland" title="Nathaniel Dance-Holland">Nathaniel Dance-Holland</a> to paint a portrait of Keppel but Keppel redirected it to Reynolds. Reynolds alluded to Keppel's trial in the painting by painting his hand on his sword, reflecting the presiding officer's words at the court-martial: "In delivering to you your sword, I am to congratulate you on its being restored to you with so much honour".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_Painter_in_Ordinary_to_the_King">Principal Painter in Ordinary to the King</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Principal Painter in Ordinary to the King">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 10 August 1784 <a href="/wiki/Allan_Ramsay_(artist)" title="Allan Ramsay (artist)">Allan Ramsay</a> died and the office of <a href="/wiki/Principal_Painter_in_Ordinary" title="Principal Painter in Ordinary">Principal Painter in Ordinary</a> to the king became vacant. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gainsborough" title="Thomas Gainsborough">Gainsborough</a> felt that he had a good chance of securing it, but Reynolds felt he deserved it and threatened to resign the presidency of the Royal Academy if he did not receive it. Reynolds noted in his pocket book: "Sept. 1, 2½, to attend at the Lord Chancellor's Office to be sworn in painter to the King".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It did not make Reynolds happy, however, as he wrote to Boswell: "If I had known what a shabby miserable place it is, I would not have asked for it; besides as things have turned out I think a certain person is not worth speaking to, nor speaking of", presumably meaning the king.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre,_p._427_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre,_p._427-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Reynolds wrote to <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Shipley" title="Jonathan Shipley">Jonathan Shipley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_St_Asaph" title="Bishop of St Asaph">Bishop of St Asaph</a>, a few weeks later: "Your Lordship congratulation on my succeeding Mr. Ramsay I take very kindly, but it is a most miserable office, it is reduced from two hundred to thirty-eight pounds per annum, the Kings Rat catcher I believe is a better place, and I am to be paid only a fourth part of what I have from other people, so that the Portraits of their Majesties are not likely to be better done now, than they used to be, I should be ruined if I was to paint them myself".<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre,_p._427_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre,_p._427-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lord_Heathfield"><i>Lord Heathfield</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lord Heathfield">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Augustus_Eliott,_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/George_Augustus_Eliott%2C_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg/220px-George_Augustus_Eliott%2C_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg" width="220" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/George_Augustus_Eliott%2C_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg/330px-George_Augustus_Eliott%2C_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/George_Augustus_Eliott%2C_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg/440px-George_Augustus_Eliott%2C_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3360" data-file-height="4226" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:George_Augustus_Eliott,_1st_Baron_Heathfield_-_by_Joshua_Reynolds_-_Project_Gutenberg_eText_19009.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/George_Augustus_Eliott,_1st_Baron_Heathfield" title="George Augustus Eliott, 1st Baron Heathfield">Lord Heathfield</a></i> (1787)</div></div></div>
<p>In 1787 Reynolds painted the portrait of <a href="/wiki/George_Augustus_Eliott,_1st_Baron_Heathfield" title="George Augustus Eliott, 1st Baron Heathfield">Lord Heathfield</a>, who became a national hero for the successful defence of <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Siege_of_Gibraltar" title="Great Siege of Gibraltar">Great Siege</a> from 1779 to 1783 against the combined forces of France and Spain. Heathfield is depicted against a background of clouds and cannon smoke, wearing the uniform of the 15th Light Dragoons and clasping the key of the Rock, its chain wrapped twice around his right hand.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Constable" title="John Constable">John Constable</a> said in the 1830s that it was "almost a history of the defence of Gibraltar".<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Desmond Shawe-Taylor has claimed that the portrait may have a religious meaning, Heathfield holding the key similar to St. Peter (Jesus' "rock") possessing the keys to Heaven, Heathfield "the rock upon which Britannia builds her military interests".<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
In 1789 Reynolds lost the sight of his left eye, which forced him into retirement. In 1791 <a href="/wiki/James_Boswell" title="James Boswell">James Boswell</a> dedicated his <i><a href="/wiki/Life_of_Samuel_Johnson" title="Life of Samuel Johnson">Life of Samuel Johnson</a></i> to Reynolds. Reynolds  agreed with Burke's <i><a href="/wiki/Reflections_on_the_Revolution_in_France" title="Reflections on the Revolution in France">Reflections on the Revolution in France</a></i> and, writing in early 1791, expressed his belief that the <i>ancien régime</i> of France had fallen due to spending too much time tending, as he puts it; </p><blockquote><p>to the splendor of the foliage, to the neglect of the stirring the earth about the roots. They cultivated only those arts which could add splendor to the nation, to the neglect of those which supported it – They neglected Trade &amp; substantial Manufacture...but does it follow that a total revolution is necessary that because we have given ourselves up too much to the ornaments of life, we will now have none at all.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p> When attending a dinner at Holland House, Fox's niece Caroline was sat next to Reynolds and "burst out into glorification of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">Revolution</a> &#8211; and was grievously chilled and checked by her neighbour's cautious and unsympathetic tone".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 4 June 1791 at a dinner at the Freemasons' Tavern to mark the king's birthday, Reynolds drank to the toasts "GOD <i>save the</i> KING!" and "May our glorious Constitution under which the arts flourish, be immortal!", in what was reported by the <i>Public Advertiser</i> as "a fervour truly patriotick". Reynolds "filled the chair with a most convivial glee".<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre,_p._523_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre,_p._523-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> He returned to town from Burke's house in <a href="/wiki/Beaconsfield" title="Beaconsfield">Beaconsfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Malone" title="Edmond Malone">Edmond Malone</a> wrote that "we left his carriage at the Inn at Hayes, and walked five miles on the road, in a warm day, without his complaining of any fatigue".<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre,_p._523_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre,_p._523-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
Later that month Reynolds suffered from a swelling over his left eye and had to be purged by a surgeon. In October he was too ill to take the president's chair and in November Fanny Burney recorded that </p><blockquote><p>"I had long languished to see that kindly zealous friend, but his ill health had intimidated me from making the attempt": "He had a bandage over one eye, and the other shaded with a green half-bonnet. He seemed serious even to sadness, though extremely kind. 'I am very glad,' he said, in a meek voice and dejected accent, 'to see you again, and I wish I could see you better! but I have only one eye now, and hardly that.' I was really quite touched".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p> On 5 November Reynolds, fearing he might not have an opportunity to write a will, wrote a memorandum intended to be his last will and testament, with <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Malone" title="Edmond Malone">Edmond Malone</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Metcalfe" title="Philip Metcalfe">Philip Metcalfe</a> named as executors. On 10 November Reynolds wrote to Benjamin West to resign the presidency, but the General Assembly agreed he should be re-elected, with Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Chambers_(architect)" title="William Chambers (architect)">William Chambers</a> and West to deputise for him.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre,_pp._524–525_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre,_pp._524–525-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Doctors <a href="/wiki/Richard_Warren" title="Richard Warren">Richard Warren</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Baker,_1st_Baronet" class="mw-redirect" title="George Baker, 1st Baronet">George Baker</a> believed Reynolds' illness to be psychological and they bled his neck "with a view of drawing the humour from his eyes" but the effect, in the view of his niece, was that it seemed "as if the 'principle of life' were gone" from Reynolds. On New Year's Day 1792 Reynolds became "seized with sickness" and from that point could not keep down food.<sup id="cite_ref-McIntyre,_pp._524–525_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McIntyre,_pp._524–525-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
Reynolds died on 23 February 1792 at his house in <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Fields" class="mw-redirect" title="Leicester Fields">Leicester Fields</a> in London between eight and nine in the evening.
</p><p>Burke was present on the night Reynolds died, and was moved within hours to write a eulogy of Reynolds starting with the following sentiments: "Sir Joshua Reynolds was on very many accounts one of the most memorable men of his Time. He was the first Englishman who added the praise of the elegant Arts to the other Glories of his Country. In Taste, in grace, in facility, in happy invention, and in the richness and Harmony of colouring, he was equal to the great masters of the renowned Ages." Burke's tribute was well received and one journalist called it "the eulogium of <a href="/wiki/Apelles" title="Apelles">Apelles</a> pronounced by <a href="/wiki/Pericles" title="Pericles">Pericles</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reynolds was buried at <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_Cathedral" title="St Paul&#39;s Cathedral">St Paul's Cathedral</a>. In 1903, <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Joshua_Reynolds" title="Statue of Joshua Reynolds">a statue</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Drury" title="Alfred Drury">Alfred Drury</a>, was erected in his honour in Annenberg Courtyard of <a href="/wiki/Burlington_House" title="Burlington House">Burlington House</a>, home of the Royal Academy.<sup id="cite_ref-LR_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LR-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Around the statue are fountains and lights, installed in 2000, arranged in the pattern of a star chart at midnight on the night of Reynolds' birth. The planets are marked by granite discs, and the Moon by a water recess. <sup id="cite_ref-AU_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AU-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_characteristics">Personal characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joshua_Reynolds&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Dated 1811</div></div></div>
<p>In appearance Reynolds was not striking. Slight, he was about 5'6" with dark brown curls, a florid complexion and features that <a href="/wiki/James_Boswell" title="James Boswell">James Boswell</a> thought were "rather too largely and strongly limned." He had a broad face and a cleft chin, and the bridge of his nose was slightly dented; his skin was scarred by smallpox and his upper lip disfigured as a result of falling from a horse as a young man. <a href="/wiki/Edmond_Malone" title="Edmond Malone">Edmond Malone</a> asserted that "his appearance at first sight impressed the spectator with the idea of a well-born and well-bred English gentleman."
</p><p>In his mature years he suffered from deafness, as recorded by <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Burney" class="mw-redirect" title="Fanny Burney">Fanny Burney</a>, although this did not impede his lively social life (he used an ear trumpet).<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Renowned for his placidity, Reynolds often claimed that he "hated nobody". Never quite losing his Devonshire accent, he was not only an amiable and original conversationalist, but a friendly and generous host, so that <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Burney" class="mw-redirect" title="Fanny Burney">Fanny Burney</a> recorded in her diary that he had <i>"a suavity of disposition that set everybody at their ease in his society"</i>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Makepeace_Thackeray" title="William Makepeace Thackeray">William Makepeace Thackeray</a> believed "of all the polite men of that age, Joshua Reynolds was the finest gentleman." Dr. Johnson commented on the "inoffensiveness" of his nature; <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a> noted his "strong turn for humor". Thomas Bernard, who later became <a href="/wiki/List_of_Anglican_Bishops_of_Killaloe" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Anglican Bishops of Killaloe">Bishop of Killaloe</a>, wrote in his closing verses on Reynolds stating:
</p>
<blockquote><div class="poem">
<p>Thou say'st not only skill is gained<br />
But genius too may be attained<br />
By studious imitation;<br />
Thy temper mild, thy genius fine<br />
I'll copy till I make them mine<br />
By constant application.
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>Some, such as <a href="/wiki/Hester_Lynch_Piozzi" class="mw-redirect" title="Hester Lynch Piozzi">Hester Lynch Piozzi</a>, construed Reynolds' equable calm as cool and unfeeling.
</p><p>It is to this lukewarm temperament that <a href="/wiki/Frederick_W._Hilles" title="Frederick W. Hilles">Frederick W. Hilles</a>, Bodman Professor of English Literature at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a> attributes Reynolds' never having married. In the editorial notes of his compendium <i>Portraits by Sir Joshua Reynolds</i>, Hilles theorizes that <i>"as a corollary one might say that he</i> [Reynolds] <i>was somewhat lacking in a capacity for love"</i>, and cites Boswell's notary papers: <i>"He said the reason he would never marry was that every woman whom he liked had grown indifferent to him, and he had been <u>glad</u> he did not marry her."</i> Reynolds' own sister, Frances, who lived with him as housekeeper, took her own negative opinion further still, thinking him "a gloomy tyrant". The presence of family compensated Reynolds for the absence of a wife; he wrote on one occasion to his friend <a href="/wiki/Bennet_Langton" title="Bennet Langton">Bennet Langton</a>, that both his sister and niece were away from home <i>"so that I am quite a bachelor"</i>. 
</p><p>Biographer <a href="/wiki/Ian_McIntyre" title="Ian McIntyre">Ian McIntyre</a> discusses the possibility of Reynolds having enjoyed sexual rendezvous with certain clients, such as Nelly O'Brien (or "My Lady O'Brien", as he playfully dubbed her) and <a href="/wiki/Kitty_Fisher" title="Kitty Fisher">Kitty Fisher</a>, who visited his house for more sittings than were strictly necessary. <a href="/wiki/Dan_Cruickshank" title="Dan Cruickshank">Dan Cruickshank</a> in his book <i>London's Sinful Secret</i> summarized Reynolds as having visited and re-visited various reputed red light districts in London after his return from Italy as a possible contributor to his medical condition and appearance due to commonly contracted disease in those areas of London.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
