<p><b>Heathrow Terminal 5</b>  is an <a href="/wiki/Airport_terminal" title="Airport terminal">airport terminal</a> at <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">Heathrow Airport</a>, the main airport serving <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. Opened in 2008, the main building in the complex is the largest free-standing structure in the United Kingdom. Terminal 5 is currently used exclusively as one of the three global hubs of <a href="/wiki/International_Airlines_Group" title="International Airlines Group">International Airlines Group</a>, served by <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a>, with the others being <a href="/wiki/London_Gatwick" class="mw-redirect" title="London Gatwick">London Gatwick</a> South and <a href="/wiki/Madrid_Barajas_Terminal_4" class="mw-redirect" title="Madrid Barajas Terminal 4">Madrid Barajas Terminal 4</a>. Prior to 2012, the terminal was used solely by British Airways.
</p><p>The terminal was designed to handle 35 million passengers a year. In 2015, Terminal 5 handled 33.1&#160;million passengers on 215,716 flights; 44.6% of the airport's passengers on 46.6% of its flights with an average of 153 passengers per flight. It was the busiest terminal at the airport, measured both by passenger numbers and flight movements.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The building's leading architects were from the <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Stirk_Harbour_%2B_Partners" title="Rogers Stirk Harbour + Partners">Richard Rogers Partnership</a> and production design was completed by aviation architects <a href="/wiki/Pascall%2BWatson" title="Pascall+Watson">Pascall+Watson</a>. The engineers for the structure were <a href="/wiki/Arup_Group_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Arup Group Limited">Arup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mott_MacDonald" title="Mott MacDonald">Mott MacDonald</a>. The building cost £4&#160;billion and took almost 20 years from conception to completion, including the longest <a href="/wiki/Public_inquiry" title="Public inquiry">public inquiry</a> in British history.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plans">Plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The possibility of a fifth terminal at Heathrow emerged as early as 1982, when there was debate over whether the expansion of <a href="/wiki/London_Stansted_Airport" title="London Stansted Airport">Stansted</a> or the expansion of Heathrow (advocated by BA) was the way forward for the UK aviation industry.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Planning studies for the terminal commenced in February 1988 and Richard Rogers was selected to design the terminal in 1989. Rogers compared his design to the <a href="/wiki/Centre_Pompidou" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre Pompidou">Centre Pompidou</a>, an earlier project that has similar flexibility in the use of its space.<sup id="cite_ref-rogers_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogers-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>BAA formally announced its proposal for construction of T5 in May 1992, submitting a formal planning application on 17 February 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Public_inquiry" title="Public inquiry">public inquiry</a> into the proposals began on 16 May 1995 and lasted nearly four years, finally ending on 17 March 1999 after sitting for 525 days. The inquiry, based at the Renaissance Hotel Heathrow, was the longest planning inquiry ever held in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Finally, more than eight years after the initial planning application, then-transport minister <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Byers" title="Stephen Byers">Stephen Byers</a> announced on 20 November 2001 the British government's decision to grant planning permission for the building of a fifth passenger terminal at Heathrow.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ZEMA0003.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/ZEMA0003.jpg/220px-ZEMA0003.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/ZEMA0003.jpg/330px-ZEMA0003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/ZEMA0003.jpg/440px-ZEMA0003.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ZEMA0003.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Terminal 5 under construction in July 2006. The frontal car park building can be seen to the left, air side is to the right</div></div></div>
<p>Construction, which was undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Laing_O%27Rourke" title="Laing O&#39;Rourke">Laing O'Rourke</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> began in September 2002, with earthworks for the construction of the buildings' foundation. A preparatory <a href="/wiki/Archaeological_dig" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeological dig">archaeological dig</a> at the site found more than 80,000 artefacts.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In November of the following year, work started on the steel superstructure of the main terminal building. By January 2005, the nine tunnels needed to provide road and rail access, and to provide drainage, were completed. In March that year, the sixth and final section of the main terminal roof was lifted into position, and in December the building was made weatherproof. The roof could not have been lifted with conventional cranes because it would have penetrated vertically into the airport's <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> field. Therefore, the roof was assembled on the ground using smaller cranes, then lifted into place by eight custom-built towers, each fitted with two hydraulic jacks to pull the roof up. At peak there were around 8,000 people working on the construction site, whilst over the life of the project over 60,000 people were involved in the construction.<sup id="cite_ref-t5tl_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t5tl-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fvt5_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fvt5-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Over 15,000 volunteers were recruited for a total of 68 trials lasting from September 2007 until March 2008 to test the operational readiness of Terminal 5 prior to its opening.<sup id="cite_ref-t5tl_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t5tl-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening">Opening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Opening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> officially opened Terminal 5 in a ceremony on 14 March 2008. Used exclusively by <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> (and now IAG (<a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a>)), the terminal opened for passenger use on 27 March 2008, with flight BA26 from Hong Kong its first arrival at 04:50 GMT. The first passenger to enter Terminal 5 was Paul Walker, a UK ex-pat from Kenya who entered through security at 04:30 on 27 March 2008 and was presented with a boarding pass by the British Airways CEO <a href="/wiki/Willie_Walsh_(businessman)" title="Willie Walsh (businessman)">Willie Walsh</a> for the first departing flight, BA302 to Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-t5tl_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-t5tl-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbcop_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbcop-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the day of opening, it quickly became apparent that the new terminal was not operating as planned, forcing British Airways to cancel 34 flights and suspend baggage <a href="/wiki/Airport_Check-in" class="mw-redirect" title="Airport Check-in">check-in</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Over the following 10 days some 42,000 bags failed to travel with their owners,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and over 500 flights were cancelled. British Airways was not able to operate its full schedule from Terminal 5 until 8 April 2008 and had to postpone the transfer of its long-haul flights from Terminal 4 to Terminal 5.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The difficulties were later blamed on a number of problems with the terminal's IT systems, coupled with car parking.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Four months after opening, British Airways launched an advertising campaign to assure the public that things were working normally.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Site">Site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Terminal 5 building is on a 260-hectare (640-acre) site on the western side of the airport, between the western ends of the northern and southern runways. The site was previously occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Perry_Oaks_sewage_works" class="mw-redirect" title="Perry Oaks sewage works">Perry Oaks sewage works</a> and an area of <a href="/wiki/Smallholding" title="Smallholding">smallholdings</a> called Burrows Hill Close Estate, and is east of the <a href="/wiki/M25_motorway" title="M25 motorway">M25 motorway</a>; see also <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_(hamlet)#20th_century" title="Heathrow (hamlet)">Heathrow (hamlet)</a>. Two artificial watercourses, the <a href="/wiki/Longford_River" title="Longford River">Longford River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Northumberland%27s_River" title="Duke of Northumberland&#39;s River">Duke of Northumberland's River</a>, originally ran through the middle of the site.<sup id="cite_ref-fvt5_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fvt5-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Most of the terminal is in the ecclesiastical parish of Harmondsworth. The southern section, including the train station, is in the ecclesiastical parish of <a href="/wiki/Stanwell" title="Stanwell">Stanwell</a>. The whole area is in the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Hillingdon" title="London Borough of Hillingdon">London Borough of Hillingdon</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Twin_Rivers_Diversion_Scheme">Twin Rivers Diversion Scheme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Twin Rivers Diversion Scheme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most time-critical civil engineering sub-projects of the Terminal's construction programme was the diversion of the Longford River and Duke of Northumberland's River around the western perimeter of the airport. This was a complex scheme, which involved not only the re-routing of the two rivers but also the realignment of the A3044 dual carriageway and Western Perimeter Road. The challenge was complicated by strict time constraints and the proximity of the works to local residents. Further restrictions to site activities resulted from the overhead flight path from both runways. 95% of the diverted rivers were placed in two open man-made channels 6 kilometres (3.7&#160;mi) in length, compared with only 50% of the original rivers which were conveyed beneath the runways in culverts. The Twin Rivers Diversion scheme achieved a Civil Engineering Environmental Quality (<a href="/wiki/CEEQUAL" title="CEEQUAL">CEEQUAL</a>) Award for its ability to maintain high environmental standards and quality during design and construction of the project.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It involved much moving and planting of river wildlife and plants.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings">Buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg/220px-Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg" width="220" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg/330px-Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg/440px-Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathrow_Terminal_5_from_the_air.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The main building from the air</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg/220px-Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg/330px-Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg/440px-Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathrow_Terminal_5_-_Transport_links.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Check-in Area in the main building</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg/220px-Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg/330px-Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg/440px-Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="797" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heathrow_Terminal_5C_Iwelumo-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite Terminal 5C</div></div></div>
<p>The Terminal 5 complex includes several buildings, a new control tower and a railway station. The main terminal building is referred to as Terminal 5A. There are two satellite buildings, Terminal 5B and Terminal 5C. At the time of opening, Terminal 5A and Terminal 5B were completed, whilst Terminal 5C, which was built by <a href="/wiki/Carillion" title="Carillion">Carillion</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> was under construction and scheduled to open later in May 2010; however it only opened in June 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-heathrow-london.co.uk_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heathrow-london.co.uk-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The whole complex is fronted by a separate building containing road transport facilities. Within the complex there are more than 100 shops and restaurants.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminal_5">Terminal 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Terminal 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main terminal building is 396 metres (1,299&#160;ft) long, 176 metres (577&#160;ft) wide and 40 metres (130&#160;ft) tall.<sup id="cite_ref-aoftw_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aoftw-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It is the largest building in the Terminal 5 complex and is the largest free-standing building in the United Kingdom<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="&#39;largest free-standing building in the United Kingdom&#39; needs source, date (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Its four stories are covered by a single-span undulating steel frame roof, with glass façades angled at 6.5 degrees to the vertical. The area covered by the roof is the size of five football pitches, and each section weighs 2,200 tonnes.
</p><p>T5A contains a <a href="/wiki/Check-in" title="Check-in">check-in</a> hall, a departure lounge with retail stores and other passenger services, and a baggage reclaim hall. T5A contains the bulk of the terminal's <a href="/wiki/Baggage_handling_system" title="Baggage handling system">baggage handling system</a>. This baggage handling system is the largest in the world with 5 miles (8.0&#160;km) of high-speed track and 11 miles (18&#160;km) of regular conveyor belts. It is designed to handle 4,000 bags per hour, and also has an "early bag store" which can temporarily store up to 4,000 bags.
</p><p>Departing passengers enter the departures level on the third floor by lift or escalator from the interchange plaza. Upon entering the departures concourse, passengers see views across Heathrow and the surrounding area, and are in a space that is unobstructed to the rising roof above. After check-in and security screening, the airside departure lounge also provides views across the airport, its runways and beyond.
</p><p>British Airways maintains offices overlooking the main Terminal 5 passenger handling areas, designed so that staff can have, in the words of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aviation_Transport_Weekly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aviation Transport Weekly (page does not exist)">Aviation Transport Weekly</a></i>, a "visual connection to customers". When Terminal 5 opened on 27 March 2008, British Airways staff, including crew check-in staff, relocated from the <a href="/wiki/Compass_Centre" title="Compass Centre">Compass Centre</a> to Terminal 5.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> London based interior designer Katharine Pooley was commissioned to design the interior of the Windsor Suite which receives and hosts VIPs.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_terminal_buildings">Satellite terminal buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Satellite terminal buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Terminal 5B was the first satellite building to be built. Terminal 5C is the second satellite building, opening unofficially on 20 May 2011, with official opening on 1 June 2011, in conjunction with the relaunch of British Airways service to San Diego. Terminal 5B measures 442 metres (1,450&#160;ft) long by 52 metres (171&#160;ft) wide and 19.5 metres (64&#160;ft) high, and contains 37 <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">lifts</a> and 29 <a href="/wiki/Escalator" title="Escalator">escalators</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fvt5_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fvt5-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is also the potential for an additional satellite building, T5D, to be located to the east of T5C, as displayed in Heathrow's Capital Investment Plan for 2009.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Terminal_5_people_mover" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Terminal 5 people mover">An underground automated people mover</a> (APM), known as the <i>Transit</i>, connects passengers between Terminal 5A, Terminal 5B and Terminal 5C.
</p><p>In a level below the APM are pedestrian walkways, which were opened for passenger use in 2015.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frontal_building">Frontal building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Frontal building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike most airport terminals, the main terminal building does not have direct road access. Instead it is fronted by a 6-level frontal building, which contains a bus station and taxi rank (at ground level), a 3,800 space short stay <a href="/wiki/Multi-storey_car_park" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-storey car park">multi-storey car park</a> (levels 1 to 4) and a drop off zone (level 5). A walkway at level 1 of the frontal building provides under-cover access to the <a href="/wiki/Sofitel" title="Sofitel">Sofitel</a> Heathrow Airport T5 Hotel, whilst a section of level 2 is used for the link to the long term business car park (see below).<sup id="cite_ref-fvt5_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fvt5-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The frontal building is connected to the main terminal by covered walkways at ground level (the arrivals level of the main terminal building) and skybridges at level 5 (departures level). The combination of the two buildings with the linking walkways creates a series of open courtyards. Whilst one of these courtyards is occupied by the access structures for the railway station below, others contain a dancing fountain and a grove of 40 <a href="/wiki/Platanus_x_hispanica" class="mw-redirect" title="Platanus x hispanica">London Plane trees</a>, and are accessible to passengers and other terminal visitors.<sup id="cite_ref-fvt5_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fvt5-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_control_tower">New control tower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: New control tower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the time of its design Terminal 5's proposed height was so tall that it would have blocked runway views from Heathrow Airport's then control tower. Therefore, before construction began on the terminal building, a new taller air traffic <a href="/wiki/Control_tower" class="mw-redirect" title="Control tower">control tower</a> was constructed. Costing £50&#160;million it was assembled off-site before being manoeuvred into position within the central terminal area near to <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_3" title="Heathrow Terminal 3">Heathrow Terminal 3</a>, during 2004. This new control tower weighs nearly 1000 tons and is 87 metres (285&#160;ft) in height, making it one of the tallest in Europe and twice the height of London's <a href="/wiki/Nelson%27s_Column" title="Nelson&#39;s Column">Nelson's Column</a>. It became operational in April 2007.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Airlines">Airlines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Airlines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> use Terminal 5 to operate the majority of their flights serving Heathrow. On 30 March 2014, the flight to Tel Aviv moved to this terminal from Terminal 1. On 30 June 2015 when Terminal 1 closed, flights to Amman- Queen Alia, Baku, Beirut, Cairo, and Hannover moved to this terminal from the former Terminal 1. Some destinations are served from <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Terminal_3" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Terminal 3">Terminal 3</a> due to capacity restrictions at Terminal 5. <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a> to Palermo formerly operated from Terminal 5, but in March 2017, it transferred its flights to Terminal 3. Since March 2012, <a href="/wiki/International_Airlines_Group" title="International Airlines Group">International Airlines Group</a> subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Iberia_(airline)" title="Iberia (airline)">Iberia</a> has operated from Terminal 5 as well.<sup id="cite_ref-iberia_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iberia-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Terminal 5 also serves UK and Irish flights like <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_2" title="Heathrow Terminal 2">Heathrow Terminal 2</a>, unlike <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_3" title="Heathrow Terminal 3">Heathrow Terminal 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_4" title="Heathrow Terminal 4">Heathrow Terminal 4</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_transport">Ground transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ground transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The transport network around the airport has been extended to cope with the increase in passenger numbers. This has involved widening of the <a href="/wiki/M25_motorway" title="M25 motorway">M25 motorway</a> and the construction of new branches of both the <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Express" title="Heathrow Express">Heathrow Express</a> and the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> <a href="/wiki/Piccadilly_line" title="Piccadilly line">Piccadilly line</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railway_links">Railway links</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Railway links">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Terminal 5 is served by <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Terminal_5_station" title="Heathrow Terminal 5 station">Heathrow Terminal 5 station</a>, which is located beneath the main terminal building, and serves both the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Express" title="Heathrow Express">Heathrow Express</a> rail connections to the terminal. The railway station also has two additional, currently unused, platforms for use by a possible westward railway connection.
</p><p>Heathrow Express provides an express service to <a href="/wiki/London_Paddington_station" title="London Paddington station">Paddington station</a> in central London, stopping only at <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Central_railway_station" title="Heathrow Central railway station">Heathrow Central station</a>. Trains run every 15 minutes and the journey time to Paddington is 21 minutes. Premium fares are charged for the service to Paddington. However no fares are charged for the journey to Heathrow Central, which provides access to Terminals 1 to 3, to the <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport_Central_bus_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Heathrow Airport Central bus station">Heathrow Airport Central bus station</a>, and to the <a href="/wiki/TfL_Rail" title="TfL Rail">TfL Rail</a> semi-fast rail service to Paddington. Terminal 4 can be reached by changing trains to the Heathrow Express Shuttle service at Heathrow Central, which is also fare-free for this journey.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Piccadilly_line" title="Piccadilly line">Piccadilly line</a> of the London Underground provides a slower, but considerably cheaper, stopping service to central London, with a journey time of between 45 minutes and one hour depending on exact destination. Trains run every 10 minutes, and provide service to many stations en route to and within central London, with interchange available to the rest of the London Underground network. The Piccadilly line is integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Transport_for_London" title="Transport for London">Transport for London</a> fare system.
</p><p>Terminal 5 is also served by <a href="/wiki/RailAir" title="RailAir">RailAir</a> express buses, which link the terminal's bus station (see below) with <a href="/wiki/Reading_railway_station" title="Reading railway station">Reading station</a>, for rail services to the west, and <a href="/wiki/Woking_railway_station" title="Woking railway station">Woking station</a>, for rail services to the south.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_links">Road links</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Road links">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A dedicated <a href="/wiki/Spur_route" title="Spur route">motorway spur</a> has been built from the <a href="/wiki/M25_motorway" title="M25 motorway">M25</a> between junctions 14 and 15 to the terminal. The spur also connects to the airport's perimeter road, and provides direct connections to the frontal building at ground level (for bus station and taxi rank), level 4 (for car parking) and level 5 (for departure set down). The car parking on levels 1 to 3 is accessed from a series of spiral ramps that descend from level 4 to ground level.
</p><p>Besides the short-stay car parking in front of the main terminal, Terminal 5 is also served by separate business and long-term car parks. These are accessed from the airport's perimeter road and are some distance from the terminal. The long-term car park is linked to the terminal by bus, and the business car park is served by an elevated personal rapid transit system (see below).
</p><p>The terminal is also connected to Terminals 1, 2 and 3 by the <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airside_Road_Tunnel" title="Heathrow Airside Road Tunnel">Heathrow Airside Road Tunnel</a>, although, as its name suggests, this is not available for public traffic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus_links">Bus links</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Bus links">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bus and coach station in the frontal building is served by a number of bus and coach services, including long-distance <a href="/wiki/National_Express_Coaches" title="National Express Coaches">National Express</a> coach service, "The Airline" service running from Oxford, RailAir buses, local public bus services, shuttle buses to airport hotels, long term car parking and car hire lots, and staff shuttle buses.
</p><p>Because Terminal 5 is close to the boundary of <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>, some local buses are part of the <a href="/wiki/London_Buses" title="London Buses">London Buses</a> network, whilst others are run by operators from outside the London Buses area.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_rapid_transit_system">Personal rapid transit system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Personal rapid transit system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Terminal_5_PRT" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Terminal 5 PRT">London Heathrow Terminal 5 PRT</a></div>
<p>A 2.4-mile (3.9&#160;km)-long elevated and at grade <a href="/wiki/Personal_rapid_transit" title="Personal rapid transit">personal rapid transit</a> system links the business car park nearby and the main Terminal 5A building. The system opened to public usage in May 2011, although its usage was initially described as passenger trials, with full service commencing in September of the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> This system has been designed by <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> based Advanced Transport Systems to their <a href="/wiki/ULTra" class="mw-redirect" title="ULTra">ULTra</a> design, and the intention is that it will eventually transport passengers around the perimeter fence to Terminals 2 and 3.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Automated_people_mover">Automated people mover</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heathrow_Terminal_5&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Automated people mover">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An underground <a href="/wiki/Automated_people_mover" class="mw-redirect" title="Automated people mover">automated people mover</a> (APM) system, to the <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Innovia_APM_200" title="Bombardier Innovia APM 200">Bombardier Innovia APM 200</a> design,<sup id="cite_ref-bombardier_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bombardier-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> is used to transport passengers between the 5A, 5B and 5C buildings. Known as the <i>Transit Train</i>, the APM system is located <a href="/wiki/Airside_(airport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Airside (airport)">air side</a><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and is thus only available to passengers and other authorised personnel.
</p><p>The system can accommodate up to 6,000 passengers per hour<sup id="cite_ref-bombardier_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bombardier-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and the trains run at 50 kilometres per hour (31&#160;mph) with a journey time of 45 seconds.
</p><p>Passengers descend to the station via a pair of escalators "thought to be the largest in Europe in an open environment".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The escalators are also the longest in the United Kingdom, longer than those at <a href="/wiki/Angel_tube_station" title="Angel tube station">Angel Underground station</a> on the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>, which had held the title since 1992.
</p>
