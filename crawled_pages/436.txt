<p>In <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, a town traditionally was a settlement which had a charter to hold a market or fair and therefore became a "market town".  In <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, the equivalent is known as a <a href="/wiki/Burgh" title="Burgh">burgh</a> (pronounced <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ˈbʌɾə]</a></span>).  There are two types of burgh: <a href="/wiki/Royal_burgh" title="Royal burgh">royal burghs</a> and burghs of barony.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> allows <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parishes</a> in England and Wales to resolve themselves to be <a href="/wiki/Town_council" title="Town council">Town Councils</a>, under section (245 subsection 6), which also gives the chairman of such parishes the title 'town mayor'.  Many former <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban districts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal boroughs</a> have such a status, along with other settlements with no prior town status.
</p><p>In more modern times it is often considered that a town becomes a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> (or a <a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">village</a> becomes a town) as soon as it reaches a certain <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>, although this is an informal definition and no particular numbers are agreed upon.
</p><p>The cultural importance placed on charters remains, and it is not an unusual event for towns across the UK to celebrate their charter in an annual <b>Charter Day</b> (normally a <a href="/wiki/Fair" title="Fair">fair</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mediaeval" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediaeval">mediaeval</a> <a href="/wiki/Market_(place)" class="mw-redirect" title="Market (place)">market</a>).
</p>
