<p><b>Netscape</b> is a brand name associated with the development of the <a href="/wiki/Netscape_(web_browser)" title="Netscape (web browser)">Netscape web browser</a>. It is now owned by <a href="/wiki/Oath,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Oath, Inc.">Oath, Inc.</a>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Verizon_Communications" title="Verizon Communications">Verizon</a>. 
</p><p>The brand belonged to the <b>Netscape Communications Corporation</b> (formerly <b>Mosaic Communications Corporation</b>), an independent American computer services company, whose headquarters were in <a href="/wiki/Mountain_View,_California" title="Mountain View, California">Mountain View, California</a>, and later <a href="/wiki/Dulles,_Virginia" title="Dulles, Virginia">Dulles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The browser was once dominant but lost to <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> and other competitors after the so-called <a href="/wiki/First_browser_war" class="mw-redirect" title="First browser war">first browser war</a>, its market share falling from more than 90 percent in the mid-1990s<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> to less than 1 percent in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Netscape is credited with creating the <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> programming language, the most widely used language for client-side scripting of web pages. The company is also known for developing <a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">SSL</a> which was used for securing online communications before its successor <a href="/wiki/Transport_Layer_Security" title="Transport Layer Security">TLS</a> took over.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Netscape <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stock</a> traded from 1995 until 1999 when the company was acquired by <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a> in a pooling-of-interests transaction ultimately worth US $10 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-AOL1999_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOL1999-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Morn1999_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morn1999-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Shortly before its acquisition by AOL, Netscape released the source code for its browser and created the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Organization">Mozilla Organization</a> to coordinate future development of its product.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  The Mozilla Organization rewrote the entire browser's <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Gecko_rendering_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Gecko rendering engine">Gecko rendering engine</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> all future Netscape releases were based on this rewritten code. The Gecko engine would later be used to power the Mozilla Foundation's <a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Firefox</a> browser. 
</p><p>Under AOL, Netscape's browser development continued until December 2007 when AOL announced that the company would stop supporting the Netscape browser as of early 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-BrowserEndFeb_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrowserEndFeb-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BrowserEndMar_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrowserEndMar-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> As of 2011, AOL has continued to use the Netscape brand to market a discount <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service provider</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>AOL renamed the Netscape Communications Corporation to <b>New Aurora Corporation</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and transferred the Netscape brand to themselves. AOL sold the former Netscape company now known as New Aurora Corporation to <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, who in turn sold the former Netscape company again to <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>; the Netscape brand remained with AOL.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The former Netscape company is currently a non-operating subsidiary of Facebook, still known as New Aurora Corporation.
</p><p>Netscape Communications is now part of America Online (AOL). AOL initially envisioned the Netscape Web site as a Web portal, providing a source of revenue through advertising and e-commerce. After the antitrust ruling found that Microsoft had held and abused monopolistic power, Microsoft settled with AOL for $750 million dollars. As part of the settlement, AOL gained the rights to use and distribute Internet Explorer. Although Time Warner formally disbanded the company in 2003, the latest version of the Netscape browser may still be downloaded from Netscape's Web site.
</p><p>Entrepreneur Jason Calcanis leveraged the Netscape brand to create "Propeller," a social bookmarking and news site similar to Digg.com.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_Netscape_Communications_Corporation">History of the Netscape Communications Corporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the Netscape Communications Corporation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="toccolours" style="float: right; margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 2em; font-size: 85%; background:#c6dbf7; color:black; width:25em; max-width: 100%;" cellspacing="5">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="text-align: left;">Netscape Communications wants you to forget all the highway metaphors you've ever heard about the Internet. Instead, think about an encyclopedia—one with unlimited, graphically rich pages, connections to E-mail and files, and access to Internet newsgroups and online shopping.
</td></tr>

<tr>
<td style="text-align: left;">—<i>Netscape Navigator</i>, <a href="/wiki/Macworld" title="Macworld">Macworld</a> (May 1995)<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Netscape was the first company to attempt to capitalize on the nascent <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LATimes2007_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LATimes2007-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fortune_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It was founded under the name <b>Mosaic Communications Corporation</b> on April 4, 1994, the brainchild of <a href="/wiki/James_H._Clark" title="James H. Clark">Jim Clark</a> who had recruited <a href="/wiki/Marc_Andreessen" title="Marc Andreessen">Marc Andreessen</a> as co-founder and <a href="/wiki/Kleiner_Perkins_Caufield_%26_Byers" class="mw-redirect" title="Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers">Kleiner Perkins Caufield &amp; Byers</a> as investors. The first meeting between Clark and Andreessen was never truly about a software or service like Netscape, but more about a product that was similar to Nintendo.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Clark recruited other early team members from <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Graphics" title="Silicon Graphics">SGI</a> and <a href="/wiki/NCSA_Mosaic" class="mw-redirect" title="NCSA Mosaic">NCSA Mosaic</a>. <a href="/wiki/James_L._Barksdale" title="James L. Barksdale">Jim Barksdale</a> came on board as CEO in January 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Jim Clark and Marc Andreessen originally created a 20-page concept pitch for an online gaming network to <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Nintendo_64" title="Nintendo 64">Nintendo 64</a> console, but a deal was never reached. Marc Andreessen explains, "If they had shipped a year earlier, we probably would have done that instead of Netscape."<sup id="cite_ref-SFGate.com_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate.com-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SFGate.com_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate.com-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company's first product was the web browser, called <i>Mosaic Netscape 0.9,</i> released on October 13, 1994. Within four months of its release, it had already taken three-quarters of the browser market.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It became the main browser for Internet users in such a short time due to its superiority over other competition, like Mosaic.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This browser was subsequently renamed <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Navigator" title="Netscape Navigator">Netscape Navigator</a>, and the company took the "Netscape" name (coined by employee Greg Sands,<sup id="cite_ref-Fortune_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fortune-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> although it was also a trademark of <a href="/wiki/Cisco_Systems" title="Cisco Systems">Cisco Systems</a><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>) on November 14, 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> to avoid trademark ownership problems with <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Supercomputing_Applications" title="National Center for Supercomputing Applications">NCSA</a>, where the initial Netscape employees had previously created the <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(web_browser)" title="Mosaic (web browser)">NCSA Mosaic</a> web browser. The Mosaic Netscape web browser did not use any NCSA Mosaic code.<sup id="cite_ref-Netscape_Time_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Netscape_Time-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The internal <a href="/wiki/Codename" class="mw-redirect" title="Codename">codename</a> for the company's browser was <i>Mozilla</i>, which stood for "Mosaic killer", as the company's goal was to displace NCSA Mosaic as the world's number one <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> A cartoon <a href="/wiki/Godzilla" title="Godzilla">Godzilla</a>-like lizard mascot was drawn by artist-employee Dave Titus,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> which went well with the theme of crushing the competition. The Mozilla mascot featured prominently on Netscape's website in the company's early years. However, the need to project a more "professional" image (especially towards corporate clients) led to this being removed.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mozilla_boxing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/49/Mozilla_boxing.jpg/175px-Mozilla_boxing.jpg" width="175" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/49/Mozilla_boxing.jpg/263px-Mozilla_boxing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/49/Mozilla_boxing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="292" data-file-height="340" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mozilla_boxing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The original green and purple <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_(mascot)" title="Mozilla (mascot)">Mozilla mascot</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Godzilla" title="Godzilla">Godzilla</a>-like lizard which represented the company's goal of producing the browser that would be the "Mosaic killer"</div></div></div>
<p>On August 9, 1995, Netscape made an extremely successful <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">IPO</a>. The stock was set to be offered at US$14 per share, but a last-minute decision doubled the initial offering to US$28 per share. The stock's value soared to US$75 during the first day of trading, nearly a record for first-day gain. The stock closed at US$58.25, which gave Netscape a market value of US$2.9 billion. While it was unusual for a company to go public prior to becoming profitable, Netscape's revenues had, in fact, doubled every quarter in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The success of this IPO subsequently inspired the use of the term "Netscape moment" to describe a high-visibility IPO that signals the dawn of a new industry.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> During this period, Netscape also pursued a publicity strategy (crafted by <a href="/wiki/Rosanne_Siino" title="Rosanne Siino">Rosanne Siino</a>, then head of public relations) packaging Andreessen as the company's "rock star."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The events of this period ultimately landed Andreessen, barefoot, on the cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Netscape advertised that "the web is for everyone" and stated one of its goals was to "level the playing field" among operating systems by providing a consistent web browsing experience across them. The Netscape web browser interface was identical on any computer. Netscape later experimented with prototypes of a web-based system which would enable users to access and edit their files anywhere across a network, no matter what computer or operating system they happened to be using. This did not escape the attention of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>, which viewed the <a href="/wiki/Commoditization" title="Commoditization">commoditization</a> of operating systems as a direct threat to its bottom line, i.e. a move from Windows to another operating system would yield a similar browsing experience thus reducing barriers to change. It is alleged that several Microsoft executives visited the Netscape campus in June 1995 to propose dividing the market (an allegation denied by Microsoft and, if true, would have breached antitrust laws), which would have allowed Microsoft to produce web browser software for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a> while leaving all other operating systems to Netscape.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Netscape refused the proposition.
</p><p>Microsoft released version 1.0 of <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> as a part of the <a href="/wiki/Windows_95" title="Windows 95">Windows 95</a> <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Plus!" title="Microsoft Plus!">Plus Pack</a> add-on. According to former <a href="/wiki/Spyglass,_Inc." title="Spyglass, Inc.">Spyglass</a> developer <a href="/wiki/Eric_Sink" title="Eric Sink">Eric Sink</a>, Internet Explorer was based not on NCSA Mosaic as commonly believed, but on a version of Mosaic developed at Spyglass<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> (which itself was based upon NCSA Mosaic). Microsoft quickly released several successive versions of Internet Explorer, bundling them with Windows, never charging for them, financing their development and marketing with revenues from other areas of the company. This period of time became known as the <a href="/wiki/Browser_wars" title="Browser wars">browser wars</a>, in which Netscape Communicator and Internet Explorer added many new features and went through many version numbers (not always in a logical fashion) in attempts to outdo each other. But Internet Explorer had the upper hand, as the amount of manpower and capital dedicated to it eventually surpassed the resources available in Netscape's entire business. By version 3.0, IE was roughly a feature-for-feature equivalent of Netscape Communicator, and by version 4.0, it was generally considered to be more stable on Windows than on the Macintosh platform. Microsoft also targeted other Netscape products with free workalikes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Information_Server" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Information Server">Internet Information Server</a> (IIS), a web server which was bundled with Windows NT.
</p><p>Netscape could not compete with this strategy. In fact, it didn't attempt to. Netscape Navigator was not gratis to the general public until January 1998,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> while Internet Explorer and IIS have always been gratis or came bundled with an operating system and/or other applications. Meanwhile, Netscape faced increasing criticism for the bugs in its products; critics claimed that the company suffered from "<a href="/wiki/Creeping_featurism" class="mw-redirect" title="Creeping featurism">featuritis</a>" – putting a higher priority on adding new features than on making them work properly. This was particularly true with Netscape Navigator 2, which was only on the market for five months in early 1996 before being replaced by Netscape Navigator 3. The tide of public opinion, having once lauded Netscape as the <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a> to Microsoft's <a href="/wiki/Goliath" title="Goliath">Goliath</a>, steadily turned negative, especially when Netscape experienced its first bad quarter at the end of 1997 and underwent a large round of layoffs in January 1998. Later, former Netscape executives <a href="/wiki/Mike_Homer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mike Homer">Mike Homer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Currie_(businessman)" title="Peter Currie (businessman)">Peter Currie</a> described the period as "hectic and crazy" and that the company was undone by factors both internal and external.<sup id="cite_ref-twsOctE23_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twsOctE23-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_sourcing">Open sourcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Open sourcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>January 1998 was also the month that Netscape started the <a href="/wiki/Open_source" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source">open source</a> <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> project. Netscape publicly released the <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> of Netscape Communicator 4.0 in the hopes that it would become a popular open source project. It placed this code under the <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Public_License" title="Netscape Public License">Netscape Public License</a>, which was similar to the <a href="/wiki/GNU_General_Public_License" title="GNU General Public License">GNU General Public License</a> but allowed Netscape to continue to publish proprietary work containing the publicly released code. However, after having released the Communicator 4.0 code this way, Netscape proceeded to work on Communicator 4.5 which was focused on improving email and enterprise functionality. It eventually became clear that the Communicator 4.0 browser was too difficult to develop, and open source development was halted on this codebase. Instead, the open source development shifted to a next-generation browser built from scratch. Using the newly built <a href="/wiki/Gecko_(layout_engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gecko (layout engine)">Gecko</a> <a href="/wiki/Browser_engine" title="Browser engine">layout engine</a>, this browser had a much more modular architecture than Communicator 4.0 and was, therefore, easier to develop with a large number of programmers. It also included an XML user interface language named <a href="/wiki/XUL" title="XUL">XUL</a> that allowed single development of a user interface that ran on Windows, Macintosh, and Unix.  The slogan for this open sourcing effort, "Free The Lizard", carried comedic sexual overtones.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> filed an <a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Microsoft" class="mw-redirect" title="United States v. Microsoft">antitrust case</a> against Microsoft in May 1998. Netscape was not a plaintiff in the case, though its executives were subpoenaed and it contributed much material to the case, including the entire contents of the 'Bad Attitude' internal discussion forum.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In October 1998, Netscape acquired web directory site <a href="/wiki/DMOZ" title="DMOZ">NewHoo</a> for the sum of US$1 million, renamed it the Open Directory Project, and released its database under an <a href="/wiki/Open_content" title="Open content">open content</a> license.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_by_America_Online">Acquisition by America Online</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acquisition by America Online">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(October 2008)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>On November 24, 1998, <a href="/wiki/America_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="America Online">America Online</a> (AOL) announced it would acquire Netscape Communications in a tax-free stock-swap valued at US$4.2 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> During this time, Andreessen's view of Netscape changed; to him, it was no longer just a browser, intranet, extranet, or a software company, but rather an amalgamation of products and services.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> By the time the deal closed on March 17, 1999, it was valued at US$10 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-AOL1999_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOL1999-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Morn1999_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morn1999-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This merger was ridiculed by many who believed that the two corporate cultures could not possibly mesh; one of its most prominent critics was longtime Netscape developer <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Zawinski" title="Jamie Zawinski">Jamie Zawinski</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The acquisition was seen as a way for AOL to gain a bargaining chip against Microsoft, to let it become less dependent on the Internet Explorer web browser. Others believed that AOL was interested in Netcenter, or Netscape's web properties, which drew some of the highest traffic worldwide. Eventually, Netscape's server products and its Professional Services group became part of <a href="/wiki/IPlanet" title="IPlanet">iPlanet</a>, a joint marketing and development alliance between AOL and Sun Microsystems. On November 14, 2000, AOL released Netscape 6, based on the Mozilla 0.6 source code. (Version 5 was skipped.) Unfortunately, Mozilla 0.6 was far from being stable yet, and so the effect of Netscape 6 was to further drive people away from the Netscape brand. It was not until August 2001 that Netscape 6.1 appeared, based on Mozilla 0.9.2 which was significantly more robust. A year later came Netscape 7.0, based on the Mozilla 1.0 core.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disbanding">Disbanding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disbanding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the acquisition of Netscape by AOL, joint development and marketing of Netscape software products would occur through the Sun-Netscape Alliance. The software, in the newly branded iPlanet, included "messaging and calendar, collaboration, web, application, directory, and certificate servers", as well as "production-ready applications for e-commerce, including commerce exchange, procurement, selling, and billing."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In March 2002, when the alliance was ended, "iPlanet became a division of Sun... Sun retained the intellectual property rights for all products and the engineering"<sup id="cite_ref-SunNetscape_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SunNetscape-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 15, 2003, <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner" class="mw-redirect" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a> (formerly AOL Time Warner) disbanded Netscape. Most of the programmers were laid-off, and the Netscape logo was removed from the building. However, the Netscape 7.2 web browser (developed in-house rather than with Netscape staff, with some work outsourced to <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun's</a> <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> development center<sup id="cite_ref-Beijing_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beijing-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>) was released by <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a> on August 18, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 12, 2004, the popular developer website Netscape DevEdge was shut down by AOL. DevEdge was an important resource for Internet-related technologies, maintaining definitive documentation on the Netscape browser, documentation on associated technologies like HTML and JavaScript, and popular articles written by industry and technology leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Danny_Goodman" title="Danny Goodman">Danny Goodman</a>. Some content from DevEdge has been republished at the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> website.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Sun_acquisition_by_Oracle" title="Sun acquisition by Oracle">Sun acquisition by Oracle</a> in January 2010, Oracle continued to sell iPlanet branded applications, which originated from Netscape.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Applications include Oracle iPlanet Web Server and Oracle iPlanet Web Proxy Server.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_release_of_the_browser">Final release of the browser</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Final release of the browser">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:130px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Netscape-logo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/Netscape-logo.png/128px-Netscape-logo.png" width="128" height="36" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/Netscape-logo.png/192px-Netscape-logo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/Netscape-logo.png/256px-Netscape-logo.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="140" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Netscape-logo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Netscape logo 2005–2007, still used in some portals</div></div></div>
<p>The Netscape brand name continued to be used extensively. The company once again had its own programming staff devoted to the development and support for the series of <a href="/wiki/Netscape_(web_browser)" title="Netscape (web browser)">web browsers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, Netscape also maintained the <a href="/wiki/Propeller_web_portal" class="mw-redirect" title="Propeller web portal">Propeller web portal</a>, which was a popular social-news site, similar to <a href="/wiki/Digg" title="Digg">Digg</a>, which was given a new look in June 2006. AOL marketed a <a href="#Netscape_Internet_Service">discount ISP service</a> under the Netscape brand name.
</p><p>A new version of the Netscape browser, <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Navigator_9" title="Netscape Navigator 9">Netscape Navigator 9</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Firefox</a> 2, was released in October 2007. It featured a green and grey interface. In November 2007, IE had 77.4% of the browser market, Firefox 16.0%, and Netscape 0.6%, according to Net Applications, an Internet metrics firm.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>  On December 28, 2007, AOL announced that on February 1, 2008 it would drop support for the Netscape web browser and would no longer develop new releases.<sup id="cite_ref-BrowserEndFeb_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrowserEndFeb-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The date was later extended to March 1<sup id="cite_ref-BrowserEndMar_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrowserEndMar-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> to allow a major security update and to add a tool to assist users in migrating to other browsers. These additional features were included in the final version of <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Navigator_9" title="Netscape Navigator 9">Netscape Navigator 9</a> (version 9.0.0.6), released on February 20, 2008.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classic_releases">Classic releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Classic releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Netscape_Navigator_.28versions_0.9.E2.80.934.08.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Navigator_(versions_0.9–4.08)">Netscape Navigator (versions 0.9–4.08)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Netscape Navigator (versions 0.9–4.08)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Navigator" title="Netscape Navigator">Netscape Navigator</a></div>
<p>Netscape Navigator was Netscape's web browser from versions 1.0–4.8. The first <a href="/wiki/Beta_version" class="mw-redirect" title="Beta version">beta versions</a> were released in 1994 and were called Mosaic and later Mosaic Netscape. Then, a legal challenge from the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Supercomputing_Applications" title="National Center for Supercomputing Applications">National Center for Supercomputing Applications</a> (makers of <a href="/wiki/NCSA_Mosaic" class="mw-redirect" title="NCSA Mosaic">NCSA Mosaic</a>), which many of Netscape's founders used to develop, led to the name Netscape Navigator. The company's name also changed from Mosaic Communications Corporation to Netscape Communications Corporation.
</p><p>The browser was easily the most advanced available<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and so was an instant success, becoming a market leader while still in beta.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Netscape's feature-count and market share continued to grow rapidly after version 1.0 was released. Version 2.0 added a full email reader called Netscape Mail, thus transforming Netscape from a single-purpose web browser to an <a href="/wiki/Internet_suite" title="Internet suite">Internet suite</a>. The main distinguishing feature of the email client was its ability to display <a href="/wiki/HTML_email" title="HTML email">HTML email</a>. During this period, the entire suite was called Netscape Navigator.
</p><p>Version 3.0 of Netscape (the first beta was codenamed "Atlas") was the first to face any serious competition in the form of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer" title="Internet Explorer">Internet Explorer</a> 3.0.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> But Netscape easily remained the number one browser for the time being.
</p><p>Netscape also released a Gold version of Navigator 3.0 that incorporated <a href="/wiki/WYSIWYG" title="WYSIWYG">WYSIWYG</a> editing with <a href="/wiki/Drag_and_drop" title="Drag and drop">drag and drop</a> between web editor and email components.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Netscape_Communicator_.28versions_4.0.E2.80.934.8.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Communicator_(versions_4.0–4.8)">Netscape Communicator (versions 4.0–4.8)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Netscape Communicator (versions 4.0–4.8)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:OS2_Netscape_Communicator_4.61.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/OS2_Netscape_Communicator_4.61.png/220px-OS2_Netscape_Communicator_4.61.png" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2d/OS2_Netscape_Communicator_4.61.png/330px-OS2_Netscape_Communicator_4.61.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2d/OS2_Netscape_Communicator_4.61.png 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="263" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:OS2_Netscape_Communicator_4.61.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Netscape Communicator 4.61 for OS/2 Warp</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Communicator" title="Netscape Communicator">Netscape Communicator</a></div>
<p>Netscape 4 addressed the problem of Netscape Navigator being used as both the name of the suite and the browser contained within it by renaming the suite to Netscape Communicator. After five preview releases in 1996–1997, Netscape released the final version of Netscape Communicator in June 1997. This version, more or less based on Netscape Navigator 3 Code, updated and added new features. The new suite was successful, despite increasing competition from Internet Explorer (IE) 4.0 and problems with the outdated browser core. IE was slow and unstable on the Mac platform until version 4.5.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Despite this, Apple entered into an agreement with Microsoft to make IE the default browser on new Mac OS installations, a further blow to Netscape's prestige.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Communicator suite was made up of Netscape Navigator, <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Mail_%26_Newsgroups" title="Netscape Mail &amp; Newsgroups">Netscape Mail &amp; Newsgroups</a>, Netscape Address Book and <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Composer" title="Netscape Composer">Netscape Composer</a> (an HTML editor).
</p><p>In January 1998, <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Communications_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Netscape Communications Corporation">Netscape Communications Corporation</a> announced that all future versions of its software would be available free of charge and developed by an <a href="/wiki/Open_source" class="mw-redirect" title="Open source">open source</a> community, Mozilla. <a href="/wiki/Netscape_5" class="mw-redirect" title="Netscape 5">Netscape Communicator 5.0</a> was announced (codenamed "Gromit"). However, its release was greatly delayed, and meanwhile, there were newer versions of Internet Explorer, starting with <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer_4" title="Internet Explorer 4">version 4</a>. These had more features than the old Netscape version, including better support of <a href="/wiki/HTML" title="HTML">HTML</a> 4, <a href="/wiki/Cascading_Style_Sheets" title="Cascading Style Sheets">CSS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Document_Object_Model" title="Document Object Model">DOM</a>, and <a href="/wiki/ECMAScript" title="ECMAScript">ECMAScript</a>; eventually, the more advanced <a href="/wiki/Internet_Explorer_5.0" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Explorer 5.0">Internet Explorer 5.0</a> became the market leader.
</p><p>In October 1998, Netscape Communicator 4.5 was released. It featured various functionality improvements, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Mail_%26_Newsgroups" title="Netscape Mail &amp; Newsgroups">Mail and Newsgroups</a> component, but did not update the browser core, whose functionality was essentially identical to that of version 4.08. One month later, <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Communications_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Netscape Communications Corporation">Netscape Communications Corporation</a> was bought by <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a>. In November, work on Netscape 5.0 was canceled in favor of developing a <a href="/wiki/Rewrite_(programming)" title="Rewrite (programming)">completely new program from scratch</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mozilla-based_releases">Mozilla-based releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mozilla-based releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Netscape_6_.28versions_6.0.E2.80.936.2.3.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_6_(versions_6.0–6.2.3)">Netscape 6 (versions 6.0–6.2.3)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Netscape 6 (versions 6.0–6.2.3)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Netscape_6" title="Netscape 6">Netscape 6</a></div>
<p>In 1998, an informal group called the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Organization">Mozilla Organization</a> was formed and largely funded by Netscape (the vast majority of programmers working on the code were paid by Netscape) to coordinate the development of Netscape 5 (codenamed "Gromit"), which would be based on the Communicator source code. However, the aging Communicator code proved difficult to work with and the decision was taken to scrap Netscape 5 and re-write the source code. The re-written source code was in the form of the <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Application_Suite" title="Mozilla Application Suite">Mozilla</a> web browser, on which, with a few additions, Netscape 6 was based.
</p><p>This decision meant that Netscape's next major version was severely delayed. In the meantime, Netscape was taken over by AOL who, acting under pressure from the <a href="/wiki/Web_Standards_Project" title="Web Standards Project">Web Standards Project</a>, forced its new division to release Netscape 6.0 in 2000. The suite again consisted of Netscape Navigator and the other Communicator components, with the addition of a built-in <a href="/wiki/AOL_Instant_Messenger" class="mw-redirect" title="AOL Instant Messenger">AOL Instant Messenger</a> client, Netscape Instant Messenger. However, it was clear that Netscape 6 was not yet ready for release and it flopped badly. It was based on Mozilla 0.6, which was not ready to be used by the general public yet due to many serious bugs that would cause it to crash often or render web pages slowly. Later versions of Netscape 6 were much-improved<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (especially 6.2.x was regarded as a good release)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2013)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, but the browser still struggled to make an impact on a disappointed community.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Netscape_7_.28versions_7.0.E2.80.937.2.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_7_(versions_7.0–7.2)">Netscape 7 (versions 7.0–7.2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Netscape 7 (versions 7.0–7.2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Netscape_7" title="Netscape 7">Netscape 7</a></div>
<p>Netscape 7.0 (based on Mozilla 1.0.1) was released in August 2002 as a direct continuation of Netscape 6 with very similar components. It picked up a few users, but was still very much a minority browser. It did, however, come with the popular <a href="/wiki/AOL_Radio" title="AOL Radio">Radio@Netscape</a> Internet radio client. AOL had decided to deactivate Mozilla's popup-blocker functionality in Netscape 7.0, which created an outrage in the community. AOL reversed the decision and allowed Netscape to reinstate the popup-blocker for Netscape 7.01. Netscape also introduced a new AOL-free-version (without the usual AOL add-ons) of the browser suite. Netscape 7.1 (codenamed "Buffy" and based on Mozilla 1.4) was released in June 2003.
</p><p>In 2003, AOL closed down its Netscape division and laid-off or reassigned all of Netscape's employees. Mozilla.org continued, however, as the independent <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Foundation" title="Mozilla Foundation">Mozilla Foundation</a>, taking on many of Netscape's ex-employees. AOL continued to develop Netscape in-house (with help from <a href="/wiki/Sun_Microsystems" title="Sun Microsystems">Sun's</a> <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> development center<sup id="cite_ref-Beijing_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beijing-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>), but, due to there being no staff committed to it, improvements were minimal. One year later, in August 2004, the last version based on Mozilla was released: Netscape 7.2, based on Mozilla 1.7.2.
</p><p>After an official poll posted on Netscape's community support board in late 2006, speculation arose of the Netscape 7 series of suites being fully supported and updated by Netscape's in-house development team.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> This was not to be.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mozilla_Firefox-based_releases">Mozilla Firefox-based releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Mozilla Firefox-based releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Netscape_Browser_.28version_8.0.E2.80.938.1.3.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Browser_(version_8.0–8.1.3)">Netscape Browser (version 8.0–8.1.3)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Netscape Browser (version 8.0–8.1.3)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Between 2005 and 2007, Netscape's releases became known as <i>Netscape Browser</i>. AOL chose to base Netscape Browser on the relatively successful <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a>, a re-written version of Mozilla produced by the Mozilla Foundation. This release is not a full Internet suite as before, but is solely a web browser. Other controversial decisions include the browser's being made only for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> and its featuring both the <a href="/wiki/Gecko_(layout_engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gecko (layout engine)">Gecko</a> <a href="/wiki/Web_browser_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Web browser engine">rendering engine</a> of previous releases and the <a href="/wiki/Trident_(layout_engine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trident (layout engine)">Trident</a> engine used in Internet Explorer. AOL's acquisition of Netscape Communications in November 1998<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> made it less of a surprise when the company laid off the Netscape team and outsourced development to <a href="/wiki/Mercurial_Communications" title="Mercurial Communications">Mercurial Communications</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2010)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Netscape Browser 8.1.3 was released on April 2, 2007, and included general bug fixes identified in versions 8.0–8.1.2<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Netscape_Navigator_.28version_9.0.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Navigator_(version_9.0)">Netscape Navigator (version 9.0)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Netscape Navigator (version 9.0)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Netscape9.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Netscape9.png/220px-Netscape9.png" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f5/Netscape9.png/330px-Netscape9.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/f/f5/Netscape9.png 2x" data-file-width="362" data-file-height="274" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Netscape9.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Netscape Navigator 9.0</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Netscape_Navigator_9" title="Netscape Navigator 9">Netscape Navigator 9</a>'s features were said to include <a href="/wiki/Newsfeed" class="mw-redirect" title="Newsfeed">newsfeed</a> support and become more integrated with the Propeller Internet portal,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> alongside more enhanced methods of discussion, submission and voting on web pages.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> It also sees the browser return to multi-platform support across <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Windows</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Like Netscape version 8.x, the new release was based upon the popular <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a> (version 2.0), and supposedly had full support of all Firefox <a href="/wiki/Add-on_(Mozilla)" title="Add-on (Mozilla)">add-ons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plugins" class="mw-redirect" title="Plugins">plugins</a>, some of which Netscape was already providing.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Also for the first time since 2004, the browser was produced in-house with its own programming staff.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> A beta of the program was first released on June 5, 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The final version was released on October 15, 2007.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_development_and_support">End of development and support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: End of development and support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>AOL officially announced<sup id="cite_ref-BrowserEndFeb_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrowserEndFeb-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BrowserEndMar_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BrowserEndMar-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> that support for Netscape Navigator would end on March 1, 2008, and recommended that its users download either the <a href="/wiki/Flock_browser" class="mw-redirect" title="Flock browser">Flock</a> or Firefox browsers, both of which were based on the same technology.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The decision met mixed reactions from communities, with many arguing that the termination of product support is significantly belated. Internet security site <i>Security Watch</i> stated that a trend of infrequent security updates for AOL's Netscape caused the browser to become a "security liability", specifically the 2005–2007 versions, <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Browser" title="Netscape Browser">Netscape Browser 8</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Asa Dotzler, one of <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Firefox">Firefox's</a> original bug testers, greeted the news with "good riddance" in his blog post, but praised the various members of the Netscape team over the years for enabling the creation of Mozilla in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Others protested and petitioned AOL to continue providing vital security fixes to unknowing or loyal users of its software, as well as protection of a well-known brand.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mozilla_Thunderbird-based_releases">Mozilla Thunderbird-based releases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mozilla Thunderbird-based releases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Messenger_9">Netscape Messenger 9</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Netscape Messenger 9">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Messenger_9" title="Netscape Messenger 9">Netscape Messenger 9</a></div>
<p>On June 11, 2007, Netscape announced Netscape Mercury, a standalone email and news client that was to accompany Navigator 9. Mercury was based on <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Thunderbird" title="Mozilla Thunderbird">Mozilla Thunderbird</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The product was later renamed Netscape Messenger 9, and an alpha version was released. In December 2007, AOL announced it was canceling Netscape's development of Messenger 9 as well as Navigator 9.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_Netscape_products">Later Netscape products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Later Netscape products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Netscape's later products included:
</p>
<ul><li>Netscape Personal Edition (the browser along with <a href="/wiki/Point-to-Point_Protocol" title="Point-to-Point Protocol">PPP</a> software and an account creation wizard to sign up with an ISP)</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propeller">Propeller</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Propeller">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Propeller.com" title="Propeller.com">Propeller.com</a></div>
<p>Between June 2006 and September 2007, AOL operated Netscape's website as social news website similar to <a href="/wiki/Digg" title="Digg">Digg</a>. The format did not do well as traffic dropped 55.1 percent between November 2006 and August 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-searchenginewatch.com_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-searchenginewatch.com-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> In September 2007, AOL reverted Netscape's website to a traditional news portal, and rebranded the social news portal as "Propeller", moving the site to the domain "propeller.com." AOL shut down the Propeller website on October 1, 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-searchenginewatch.com_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-searchenginewatch.com-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Search">Netscape Search</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Netscape Search">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Netscape operated a search engine, Netscape Search, which now redirects to <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL Search</a> (which itself now merely serves <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>) search results).<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Another version of Netscape Search was incorporated into Propeller.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sites">Other sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Other sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Netscape also operates a number of country-specific Netscape portals, including <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.netscape.ca">Netscape Canada</a> among others. The portal of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070926212815/http://www.netscape.de/">Netscape Germany</a> was shut down in June 2008.
</p><p>The Netscape Blog was written by Netscape employees discussing the latest on Netscape products and services. Netscape NewsQuake (formerly <i>Netscape Reports</i>) is Netscape's news and opinion blog, including video clips and discussions. As of January 2012, no new posts have been made on either of these blogs since August 2008.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_technologies">Netscape technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Netscape technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Netscape created the <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> web page scripting language. It also pioneered the development of <a href="/wiki/Push_technology" title="Push technology">push technology</a>, which effectively allowed websites to send regular updates of information (weather, stock updates, package tracking, etc.) directly to a user's desktop (aka "webtop"); Netscape's implementation of this was named Netcaster.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Unfortunately, businesses quickly recognized the use of push technology to deliver ads to users that annoyed them, so Netcaster was short-lived.
</p><p>Netscape was notable for its <a href="/wiki/Cross-platform" title="Cross-platform">cross-platform</a> efforts. Its client software continued to be made available for Windows (<a href="/wiki/Windows_3.1x" title="Windows 3.1x">3.1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windows_95" title="Windows 95">95</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windows_98" title="Windows 98">98</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT" title="Windows NT">NT</a>), Macintosh, Linux, <a href="/wiki/OS/2" title="OS/2">OS/2</a>, <a href="/wiki/BeOS" title="BeOS">BeOS</a>, and many versions of Unix including <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">DEC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solaris_(operating_system)" title="Solaris (operating system)">Sun Solaris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berkeley_Software_Design" title="Berkeley Software Design">BSDI</a>, <a href="/wiki/IRIX" title="IRIX">IRIX</a>, <a href="/wiki/IBM_AIX" title="IBM AIX">IBM AIX</a>, and <a href="/wiki/HP-UX" title="HP-UX">HP-UX</a>. Its server software generally was only available for Unix and Windows NT, though some of its servers were made available on Linux, and a version of Netscape FastTrack Server was made available for Windows 95/98. Today, most of Netscape's server offerings live on as the <a href="/wiki/Sun_Java_System" title="Sun Java System">Sun Java System</a>, formerly under the <a href="/wiki/Sun_ONE" title="Sun ONE">Sun ONE</a> branding. Although Netscape Browser 8 was Windows only, multi-platform support exists in the <a href="/wiki/Netscape_Navigator_9" title="Netscape Navigator 9">Netscape Navigator 9</a> series of browsers.<sup id="cite_ref-wired_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wired-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Internet_Service">Netscape Internet Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Netscape Internet Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Netscape ISP is a 56 kbit/s dial-up service offered at US$9.95 per month<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> (US$6.95 with 12-month commitment). The company serves web pages in a compressed format to increase effective speeds up to 1300 kbit/s (average 500 kbit/s). The <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service provider</a> is run by <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a> under the Netscape brand. The low-cost ISP was officially launched on January 8, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Its main competitor is <a href="/wiki/NetZero" title="NetZero">NetZero</a>. Netscape ISP's advertising is generally aimed at a younger demographic, e.g., college students, and people just out of school, as an affordable way to gain access to the Internet.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape.com">Netscape.com</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Netscape.com">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Netscape always drove lots of traffic from various links included in the browser menus to its web properties. Some say it was very late to leverage this traffic<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> for what would become the start of the major online portal wars.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> When it did, Netcenter, the new name for its site entered the race with Yahoo!, <a href="/wiki/Infoseek" title="Infoseek">Infoseek</a>, and MSN, which Google would only join years later.
</p><p>The original Netscape.com was discontinued in June 2006, replaced by the site that would eventually become <a href="/wiki/Propeller.com" title="Propeller.com">Propeller.com</a>. Two continuations of the original Netscape.com portal are available; Compuserve.com, the Web site of <a href="/wiki/Compuserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Compuserve">Compuserve</a>, and ISP.Netscape.com, the website for Netscape's dial-up discount ISP service, continue to use the Netscape.com layout as it was before June 2006. Of the two, only the latter explicitly uses the Netscape branding.
</p><p>Netscape.com is currently an AOL Netscape-branded mirror duplicate of the AOL.com portal with the <a href="/wiki/URL" title="URL">URL</a>, replacing the former social news website in September 2007. The social news site moved to the Propeller.com domain, where it stayed until ending operations in October 2010. It features facilities such as news, sports, horoscopes, dating, movies, music and more. The change has come to much criticism amongst many site users, because the site has effectively become an AOL clone, and simply re-directs to regional AOL portals in some areas across the globe. Netscape's exclusive features, such as the Netscape Blog, Netscape NewsQuake, Netscape Navigator, My Netscape and Netscape Community pages, are less accessible from the AOL Netscape designed portal and in some countries not accessible at all without providing a full URL or completing an <a href="/wiki/Internet_search" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet search">Internet search</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The new AOL Netscape site was originally previewed in August 2007 before moving the existing site in September 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Netscape.co.uk now redirects to AOL Search, with no Netscape branding at all.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DMOZ">DMOZ</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: DMOZ">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/DMOZ" title="DMOZ">DMOZ</a></div>
<p>DMOZ (from <b>d</b>irectory.<b>moz</b>illa.org, its original <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a>, also known as the <b>Open Directory Project</b> or <b>ODP</b>), was a multilingual <a href="/wiki/Open_content" title="Open content">open content</a> <a href="/wiki/Web_directory" title="Web directory">directory</a> of <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> links owned<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> by Netscape that was constructed and maintained by a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_community" title="Virtual community">community</a> of volunteer editors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netscape_Forum_Center">Netscape Forum Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Netscape&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Netscape Forum Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Netscape also has a wide variety of community-based <a href="/wiki/Internet_forum" title="Internet forum">forums</a> within Netscape Forum Center, including its <a href="/wiki/Netscape_(web_browser)" title="Netscape (web browser)">browser</a>'s community support board. To post on the forums, users must possess an <a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">AOL Screenname</a> account in which to sign in, referred to within the site as the <i>Netscape Network</i>. The same service is also available through Compuserve Forum Center.
</p>
