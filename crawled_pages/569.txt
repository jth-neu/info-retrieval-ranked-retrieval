<p>In the United Kingdom an <b>Act of Parliament</b> is <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a> passed by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1998-c-42_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1998-c-42-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-parliament.uk_new-laws_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parliament.uk_new-laws-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As a result of the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> and the assertion of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">parliamentary sovereignty</a>, any such Act is in theory supreme law that cannot be overturned by any body other than Parliament, although it has been recognised though the United Kingdom's membership of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> that Acts or parts of Acts which conflict with <a href="/wiki/EU_law" class="mw-redirect" title="EU law">EU law</a> can be disapplied.
</p><p>An Act of Parliament can be enforced in all four of the <a href="/wiki/Constituent_countries_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Constituent countries of the United Kingdom">UK constituent countries</a> (<a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>); however as a result of <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution</a> the majority of Acts that are now passed by Parliament apply either to England and Wales only, or England only; whilst generally Acts only relating to <a href="/wiki/Reserved_and_excepted_matters" title="Reserved and excepted matters">constitutional and reserved matters</a> now apply to the whole of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>A draft piece of legislation is called a <a href="/wiki/Bill_(law)" title="Bill (law)">Bill</a>; when this is passed by Parliament and given <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a>, it becomes an Act and part of <a href="/wiki/Statute_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Statute law">statute law</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_legislation">Classification of legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification of legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:263px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parliamentary_archives.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Parliamentary_archives.jpg/261px-Parliamentary_archives.jpg" width="261" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Parliamentary_archives.jpg/392px-Parliamentary_archives.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Parliamentary_archives.jpg/522px-Parliamentary_archives.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1707" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parliamentary_archives.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scrolls containing Acts of Parliament in the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Archives" title="Parliamentary Archives">Parliamentary Archives</a> at Victoria Tower, <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_(HM_Government).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_%28HM_Government%29.svg/150px-Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_%28HM_Government%29.svg.png" width="150" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_%28HM_Government%29.svg/225px-Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_%28HM_Government%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_%28HM_Government%29.svg/300px-Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_%28HM_Government%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1530" data-file-height="1256" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom_(HM_Government).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/HM_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="HM Government">HM Government</a> version of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Arms of the United Kingdom">Royal Arms of the United Kingdom</a> have appeared on all published copies of Acts passed by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> since 1953.</div></div></div>

<p>Acts of Parliament are classified as either "Public General Acts" or "Local and Personal Acts" (also known as "Private Acts").  Bills are also classified as "public", "private", or "hybrid".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_General_Acts">Public General Acts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Public General Acts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Public General Acts form the largest category of legislation, in principle affecting the public general law applying to everyone across the entire United Kingdom (or at least to one or more of its constituent countries of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-rogers2006_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogers2006-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Most Public General Acts proceed through Parliament as a public bill; occasionally, however, a bill is treated as <a href="#Hybrid_bills">hybrid</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Local_and_Personal_Acts_.28Private_Acts.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_and_Personal_Acts_(Private_Acts)">Local and Personal Acts (Private Acts)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Local and Personal Acts (Private Acts)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Local_and_personal_Acts_of_Parliament_(United_Kingdom)" title="Local and personal Acts of Parliament (United Kingdom)">Local and personal Acts of Parliament (United Kingdom)</a></div>
<p>Private Acts are either local or personal in their effect, applying to a specifically named locality or legal person in a manner different from all others. Private bills are "usually promoted by organisations, like local authorities or private companies, to give themselves powers beyond, or in conflict with, the general law. Private bills only change the law as it applies to specific individuals or organisations, rather than the general public. Groups or individuals potentially affected by these changes can petition Parliament against the proposed bill and present their objections to committees of MPs and Lords."<sup id="cite_ref-canterbury_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canterbury-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They include acts to confer powers on certain local authorities, a recent example being the Canterbury City Council Bill, which makes provisions relating to street trading and consumer protection in the city.<sup id="cite_ref-canterbury_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-canterbury-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Private bills can also affect certain companies: the Northern Bank Bill allowed the statutory right of Northern Bank to issue bank notes to be transferred to <a href="/wiki/Danske_Bank" title="Danske Bank">Danske Bank</a> which had acquired it.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Other private bills may affect particular companies established by Act of Parliament such as <a href="/wiki/Trustee_Savings_Bank" title="Trustee Savings Bank">TSB Bank</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Transas (page does not exist)">Transas</a>.
</p><p>Personal Acts are a sub-category of private Acts, which confer specific rights or duties on a named individual or individuals, for example allowing two persons to marry even though they are within a "prohibited degree of consanguinity or affinity" such as stepfather and stepdaughter.
</p><p>Private bills, common in the 19th century, are now rare, as new planning legislation introduced in the 1960s removed the need for many of them;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> only a few, if any, are passed each year.
</p><p>Parliamentary authorities maintain a list of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://services.parliament.uk/bills/private.html">all private bills before parliament</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_bills">Hybrid bills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hybrid bills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hybrid bills combine elements of both public and private bill. While they propose to make changes to the general law, they also contain provisions applying to specific individuals or bodies. Recent examples are the Crossrail Bill, a hybrid bill to build <a href="/wiki/Crossrail" title="Crossrail">a railway across London from west to east</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and the 1976 Aircraft and Shipbuilding Industries Bill, which was a particularly controversial bill that was ruled to be a hybrid bill, forcing the government to withdraw some of its provisions to allow its passage as a public bill. Once passed, hybrid bills are printed as part of the Public General Acts.
</p><p>Parliamentary authorities maintain a list of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.parliament.uk/business/bills-and-legislation/current-bills/previous-bills/hybrid-bills/">all hybrid bills before parliament</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_types_of_classification">Other types of classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other types of classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Private_members.27_bills"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_members'_bills">Private members' bills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Private members&#039; bills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Private_Members%27_Bills_in_the_Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Private Members&#39; Bills in the Parliament of the United Kingdom">Private Members' Bills in the Parliament of the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>It is important not to confuse private bills with private members' bills, which are public bills intended to effect a general change in the law. The only difference from other public bills is that they are brought forward by a private member (a backbencher) rather than by the government.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp191_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp191-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Twenty private members' bills per session are allowed to be introduced, with the sponsoring private members selected by a ballot of the whole house, and additional bills may be introduced under the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Minute_Rule" title="Ten Minute Rule">Ten Minute Rule</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_bills">Financial bills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Financial bills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Financial bills raise revenue and authorise how money is spent. The best-known such bills are the (normally annual) Finance Bills introduced by the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Budget_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Budget of the United Kingdom">Budget</a>. This usually encompasses all the changes to be made to tax law for the year. Its formal description is "a Bill to grant certain duties, to alter other duties, and to amend the law relating to the National Debt and the Public Revenue, and to make further provision in connection with finance".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Consolidated Fund and Appropriation Bills authorise government spending.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp193_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp193-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Housekeeping_bills">Housekeeping bills</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Housekeeping bills">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This type of bill is designed to keep the business of government and public affairs up to date. These bills may not be substantial or controversial in party political terms. Two sub-classes of the housekeeping bill are <i>consolidation bills</i>, which set out existing law in a clearer and more up-to-date form without changing its substance; and the <a href="/wiki/Tax_law_rewrite_project" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax law rewrite project">tax law rewrite bills</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">are there enough of these to constitute a class?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, which do the same for tax law.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp193_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp193-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Delegated_legislation">Delegated legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Delegated legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Delegated_legislation_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Delegated legislation in the United Kingdom">Delegated legislation in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>An Act of Parliament will often confer power on the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Council</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Crown" title="Minister of the Crown">Minister</a>, or another public body to create delegated legislation, usually by means of a <a href="/wiki/Statutory_Instrument_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutory Instrument (UK)">Statutory Instrument</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stages_of_a_bill">Stages of a bill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stages of a bill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Legislative_procedure_uk.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Legislative_procedure_uk.svg/300px-Legislative_procedure_uk.svg.png" width="300" height="364" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Legislative_procedure_uk.svg/450px-Legislative_procedure_uk.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Legislative_procedure_uk.svg/600px-Legislative_procedure_uk.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="1700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Legislative_procedure_uk.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A representation of the legislative procedure.</div></div></div>
<p>Bills may start their passage in either the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> or <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, although bills which are mainly or entirely financial will start in the Commons. Each bill passes through the following stages:
</p><p>Pre-legislative Scrutiny: Joint committee of both houses review the bill and vote on amendments that government can accept or reject. Reports are influential in later stages as rejected committee recommendations are revived to be voted on.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/File:Number-1_(dark_green).png" class="image"><img alt="Number-1 (dark green).png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Number-1_%28dark_green%29.png/35px-Number-1_%28dark_green%29.png" width="35" height="29" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Number-1_%28dark_green%29.png/53px-Number-1_%28dark_green%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Number-1_%28dark_green%29.png/70px-Number-1_%28dark_green%29.png 2x" data-file-width="89" data-file-height="75" /></a><a href="#First_reading">First Reading</a>: No vote occurs. Bill is presented, printed, and in private members' bills, a Second Reading date is set.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/File:Number-2_(dark_green).png" class="image"><img alt="Number-2 (dark green).png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Number-2_%28dark_green%29.png/35px-Number-2_%28dark_green%29.png" width="35" height="29" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Number-2_%28dark_green%29.png/53px-Number-2_%28dark_green%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Number-2_%28dark_green%29.png/70px-Number-2_%28dark_green%29.png 2x" data-file-width="89" data-file-height="75" /></a><a href="#Second_reading">Second Reading</a>: A debate on the general principles of the bill is followed by a vote.
</p><p><a href="#Committee_stage">Committee Stage</a>: A committee considers each clause of the bill, and may make amendments.
</p><p><a href="#Report_stage">Report Stage</a>: An opportunity to amend the bill. The House consider clauses to which amendments have been tabled.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/File:Number-3_(dark_green).png" class="image"><img alt="Number-3 (dark green).png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Number-3_%28dark_green%29.png/35px-Number-3_%28dark_green%29.png" width="35" height="29" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Number-3_%28dark_green%29.png/53px-Number-3_%28dark_green%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Number-3_%28dark_green%29.png/70px-Number-3_%28dark_green%29.png 2x" data-file-width="89" data-file-height="75" /></a><a href="#Third_reading">Third Reading</a>: A debate on final text as amended.
</p><p>Passage: The bill is then sent to the other House which may amend it.
</p><p><span style="padding-left:.2em;">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/File:NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg" class="image"><img alt="NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg/25px-NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg.png" width="25" height="25" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg/38px-NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg/50px-NYCS-bull-trans-1.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="75" data-file-height="75" /></a> First Reading: <i>Same procedures</i>
</p><p><span style="padding-left:.2em;">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/File:NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg" class="image"><img alt="NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg/25px-NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg.png" width="25" height="25" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg/38px-NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg/50px-NYCS-bull-trans-2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="75" data-file-height="75" /></a> Second Reading: <i>Same procedures</i>
</p><p>Committee Stage: <i>Same procedures</i>
</p><p>Report Stage: <i>Same procedures</i>
</p><p><span style="padding-left:.2em;">&#160;</span><a href="/wiki/File:NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg" class="image"><img alt="NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg/25px-NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg.png" width="25" height="25" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg/38px-NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg/50px-NYCS-bull-trans-3.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="75" data-file-height="75" /></a> Third Reading: Same procedures as in the Commons, but further amendments may also be made.
</p><p>Passage: The bill is then returned to the original House.
</p><p>Pre-legislative Scrutiny to consider all amendments.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> for the Bill is then requested; if this is granted, the Bill becomes an Act.
</p>
<h3><span id="Consultation.2C_drafting_and_pre-legislative_scrutiny"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Consultation,_drafting_and_pre-legislative_scrutiny">Consultation, drafting and pre-legislative scrutiny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Consultation, drafting and pre-legislative scrutiny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although not strictly part of the legislative process, a period of consultation will take place before a bill is drafted. Within government, the Treasury and other departments with an interest will be consulted along with the devolved administrations in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. Outside government, interested parties such as <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a>, industry bodies and pressure groups will be asked for their views on any proposals. The Cabinet Office Code of Practice specifies a minimum consultation period of twelve weeks. Consultation documents are widely circulated (see for example the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.homeoffice.gov.uk/documents/cons-extreme-porn-3008051/cons-extreme-pornography?view=Binary">consultation on extreme pornography</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since June 2017">permanent dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>'s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.scotland.gov.uk/Publications/2008/01/23111646/9">consultation on food policy</a>).
</p><p>The character of the consultation is shaped by the government's determination to press forward with a particular set of proposals. A government may publish a <a href="/wiki/Green_paper" title="Green paper">green paper</a> outlining various legislative options or a <a href="/wiki/White_paper" title="White paper">white paper</a>, which is a clear statement of intent.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp194_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp194-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It is increasingly common for a small number of Government bills to be published in draft before they are presented in Parliament.  These bills are then considered either by the relevant select committee of the House of Commons or by an ad hoc joint committee of both Houses.  This provides an opportunity for the committee to express a view on the bill and propose amendments before it is introduced. Draft bills allow more lengthy scrutiny of potential legislation and have been seen as a response to time pressures which may result in the use of <i>programme orders</i> to impose a strict timetable on the passage of bills and what is known as 'drafting on the hoof', where the government introduces amendments to its own bills. With increased time for scrutiny backed up with considered evidence, draft bills may present governments with difficulty in getting their way.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The sponsoring government department will then write to the relevant policy committee of the Cabinet. The proposals are only discussed at a meeting if disagreements arise. Even an uncontroversial proposal may face administrative hurdles. A potential change in the law may have to wait for a more extensive bill in that policy area to be brought forward before it is worthwhile devoting parliamentary time to it. The proposal will then be bundled together with more substantive measures in the same Bill. The Ministerial Committee on the Legislative Programme (LP), including the leaders and government chief whips in both houses, is responsible for the timetable of legislation. This committee decides which house a bill will start in, recommends to the Cabinet which proposals will be in the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen&#39;s Speech">Queen's Speech</a>, which will be published in draft and how much parliamentary time will be required.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp194_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp194-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following a process of consultation, the sponsoring department will send drafting instructions to parliamentary counsel, expert lawyers working for the government responsible for writing legislation. These instructions will describe what the bill should do but not the detail of how this is achieved. The Parliamentary counsel must draft the legislation clearly to minimise the possibility of legal challenge and to fit the bill in with existing UK, <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> and delegated legislation. A finished bill must be approved or scrutinised by the sponsoring department and minister, parliamentary counsel and LP.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp195_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp195-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The final stage is the submission of the bill to the authorities of the House in which it is to start its legislative journey. In the Commons, this is the Clerk of Legislation and the Public Bill Office in the Lords. They will check the following:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>That the bill complies with the rules of the house</li>
<li>That everything in the bill is covered by its 'long title' (text describing the purposes of the bill)</li>
<li>In the Commons, that every provision requiring expenditure or levying taxes is identified and printed in italics</li>
<li>Whether the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prerogative" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Prerogative">Royal Prerogative</a> is affected</li>
<li>Whether it conflicts with or duplicates any bill which has already been introduced</li></ul>
<p>After this process, the bill is then ready for introduction.
</p>

<p>A bill introduced in this way is known as a <i>presentation bill</i>. Bills can also be introduced in the Commons by being brought in from the Lords, being <i>brought in by resolution</i> (like the Finance Bill) or when an MP gets leave to bring in a <i>ten-minute rule bill</i>.
</p><p>A Government Bill can be introduced first into either House. Bills which begin in the Lords have '[Lords]' suffixed to its title when in the Lords and '[HL]' when in the Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp191_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp191-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Bills which deal primarily with taxation or public expenditure begin their passage in the Commons since the financial privileges of that House mean that it has primacy in these matters (see <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949" title="Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949">Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949</a>).  Conversely, bills relating to the judicial system, <a href="/wiki/Law_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Law Commission">Law Commission</a> bills and <a href="/wiki/Consolidation_bill" title="Consolidation bill">consolidation bills</a> begin their passage in the House of Lords which by convention has primacy in these matters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_reading">Second reading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Second reading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second reading, which in theory is supposed to occur two weekends after the first reading, a debate on the general principles of the bill is followed by a vote. This is the main opportunity to debate the principle of the bill rather than individual clauses. A division at this stage, therefore, represents a direct challenge to the principle of the bill. If the bill is read a second time, it proceeds to the committee stage.
</p><p>Normally, the Second Reading of a Government bill is approved. A defeat for a Government bill on this Reading usually signifies a major loss. The last time this happened was at the second reading of the Shops Bill for the government of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> in 1986. The bill, which liberalised controls on Sunday trading, was defeated in the Commons by 14 votes.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp210_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp210-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Second reading debates on government bills usually take a day, in practice about six hours. Smaller and less controversial bills will receive less time and wholly uncontroversial measures will receive a second reading 'on the nod' with no debate whatsoever.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp209_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp209-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The National Insurance Contributions bill appeared in the Order Papers as follows:
</p>

<p>As we can see from <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.theyworkforyou.com/debates/?id=2007-12-17b.637.1">video footage of the debate for this bill</a>, second readings of government bills take place on a motion moved (for Government bills) by a minister in the department responsible for the legislation "that the bill now be read a second time". The minister outlines the overall purpose of the Bill and highlights particular parts of the Bill they consider most important. The official Opposition spokesperson responds with his or her views on the Bill. The debate continues with other Opposition parties and backbench MPs giving their opinions on the principles of the Bill.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The minister will eventually bring the debate to a conclusion by saying, of the bill, "I commend it to the House". The Speaker will then <i>put the question</i> by saying, for example, "The Question is, that the Bill be now read a second time". The Speaker then invites supporters of the bill to say "aye" and then opponents say "no": "All members of that opinion say 'aye' [supporters say 'aye'], contrary 'no' [opponents say 'no']. In what is known as <i>collecting the voices</i> the Speaker makes a judgement as to the loudest cry. A clear majority, either way, will prompt the response "I think the Ayes/Noes have it" (this can be forced to a division by continued cries either way). If the result is at all in doubt a division will be called and the Speaker will say "Division. Clear the Lobby". This refers not to the division lobbies used for voting but the Members' Lobby beyond the chamber which is cleared by the <a href="/wiki/Doorkeeper_(Houses_of_Parliament)" title="Doorkeeper (Houses of Parliament)">doorkeepers</a>. At this point, <a href="/wiki/Division_bell" title="Division bell">division bells</a> will ring throughout the palace and also in nearby flats, pubs and restaurants whose owners pay to be connected to the system. This allows MPs who may not be in the debate to come and vote on the issue in question.
</p><p>After two minutes, the Speaker will <i>put the Question again</i> to assess whether there is still disagreement. He will then name the tellers, whose job it is to count the votes. These will usually be government and opposition whips. In the example we have looked at: "Tellers for the Ayes <a href="/wiki/David_Watts_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="David Watts (politician)">Mr. Dave Watts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_McCabe_(politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Steve McCabe (politician)">Mr. Steve McCabe</a>; tellers for the Noes <a href="/wiki/Nick_Hurd" title="Nick Hurd">Mr. Nick Hurd</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Baron_(politician)" title="John Baron (politician)">Mr. John Baron</a>". If no teller has come forward (or only one) the Speaker declares the result for the other side.
</p><p>One teller from each side goes to the end of each division lobby and they count MPs as they emerge. Whips are also at the other end of the lobby to try to ensure that their MPs vote with the party line. The names are taken by division clerks and are published in Hansard the next day (see <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://publications.parliament.uk/pa/cm200708/cmhansrd/cm071217/debtext/71217-0015.htm#07121722000544">example from Hansard</a> or with greater clarity <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.publicwhip.org.uk/division.php?date=2007-12-17&amp;number=33&amp;showall=yes#voters">from the Public Whip</a>). Eight minutes after first calling the division, the Speaker says "lock the doors"; the doorkeepers lock the doors leading into the lobbies and no more MPs can get in to vote. When every member has passed the division clerks and tellers, a <i>division slip</i> is produced by the Clerks at the Table and is given to one of the tellers on the winning side. The tellers then form up at the Table in front of the Mace facing the Speaker, and the teller with the slip reads the result to the House, for example: "The Ayes to the right, 291. The Noes to the left, 161". The Clerk then takes the slip to the Speaker, who repeats the result and adds "So the Ayes have it, the Ayes have it. Unlock". The doorkeepers then unlock the doors to the division lobbies.<sup id="cite_ref-rogers172_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogers172-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A division vote concerning a National Insurance Contributions Bill is illustrated from Hansard as follows:

<p>Divisions on second readings can be on a straight opposition or a vote on a 'reasoned amendment', detailing the reasons the bill's opponents do not want it read a second time, which may be selected by the Speaker. Bills defeated at a second reading cannot progress further or be reintroduced with exactly the same wording in the same session.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedural_orders_and_resolutions">Procedural orders and resolutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Procedural orders and resolutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the case of Government Bills, the House normally passes forthwith (i.e. without debate but almost always with a vote) a Programme Order in the form of a <i>programme motion</i>, setting out the timetable for the committee and remaining stages of the Bill. This takes place immediately after the second reading.<sup id="cite_ref-rogersp210_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rogersp210-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> For example:
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Committee_stage">Committee stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Committee stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This usually takes place in a standing committee in the Commons and on the Floor of the House in the Lords.  In the United Kingdom, the House of Commons utilises the following committees on bills:
</p>
<ul><li>Standing Committee: Despite the name, a standing committee is a committee specifically constituted for a certain bill. Its membership reflects the strengths of the parties in the House. Now known as a Public Bill Committee.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Special Standing Committee: The committee investigates the issues and principles of the bill before sending it to a regular Standing Committee.  This procedure has been used very rarely in recent years (the Adoption and Children Bill in 2001–2002 is the only recent example); the pre-<a href="/wiki/Legislative_scrutiny" title="Legislative scrutiny">legislative scrutiny</a> process (see above) is now preferred. Now also known as a Special Public Bill Committee.</li>
<li>Select Committee: A specialised committee that normally conducts oversight hearings for a certain Department considers the bill. This procedure has not been used in recent years, with the exception of the quinquennial <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed Forces Bill">Armed Forces Bill</a>, which is always referred to a select committee.</li>
<li>Committee of the Whole House: The whole house sits as a committee in the House of Commons to consider a bill.  Bills usually considered in this way are the principal parts of the annual <a href="/wiki/Finance_Bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Finance Bill">Finance Bill</a>, bills of first-class constitutional importance (for example, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_(Withdrawal)_Act_2018" title="European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018">European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018</a>) and bills that are so uncontroversial that the committee stage may be dispensed with quickly and easily on the floor of the House, without the need to nominate a committee (some Private Members' Bills are usually dealt with this way each year).  This is also the procedure used in the upper house.</li>
<li>Grand Committee (House of Lords): This is a recent new procedure used for some bills which is intended to speed up business.  Although it takes place in a separate room, it is technically still a committee of the whole House in that all members can attend and participate.  Procedure is the same as for a Committee in the main Chamber, but there are no votes.</li></ul>
<p>The committee considers each clause of the bill and may make amendments to it.  Significant amendments may be made at committee stage.  In some cases, whole groups of clauses are inserted or removed.  However, almost all the amendments which are agreed to in committee will have been tabled by the Government to correct deficiencies in the bill, to enact changes to policy made since the bill was introduced (or, in some cases, to import material which was not ready when the bill was presented), or to reflect concessions made as a result of earlier debate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Report_stage">Report stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Report stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The report state, known formally as "consideration", takes place on the Floor of the House, and is a further opportunity to amend the bill. Unlike committee stage, the House need not consider every clause of the bill, only those to which amendments have been tabled.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_reading">Third reading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Third reading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Third_reading" class="mw-redirect" title="Third reading">third reading</a>, a debate on the final text of the bill, as amended. In the Lords, further amendments may be made on third reading, in the Commons, it is usually a short debate followed by a single vote; amendments are not permitted.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Passage">Passage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Passage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bill is then sent to the other House (to the Lords, if it originated in the Commons; to the Commons if it is a Lords bill), which may amend it. The Commons may reject a bill from the Lords outright; the Lords may amend a bill from the Commons but, if they reject it, the Commons may force it through without the Lords' consent in the following Session of Parliament, as is detailed below.  Furthermore, the Lords can neither initiate nor amend money bills, bills dealing exclusively with public expenditure or the raising of revenue. If the other House amends the bill, the bill and amendments are sent back for a further stage.
</p><p>There is a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_convention_(political_custom)" title="Constitutional convention (political custom)">constitutional convention</a> that the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> should not spend more than 60 days over bills sent to them by the Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-historyandpolicy_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historyandpolicy-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>The Parliament Acts:</b> Under the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Acts_1911_and_1949" title="Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949">Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949</a>, which do not apply for bills seeking to extend Parliament's length to more than five years, if the Lords reject a bill originated in the House of Commons, then the Commons may pass that bill again in the next session. The bill is then submitted for <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> even though the Lords did not pass it. Also, if the Lords do not approve of a money bill within thirty days of passage in the Commons, the bill is submitted for Royal Assent nevertheless.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consideration_of_amendments">Consideration of amendments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Consideration of amendments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The House in which the bill originated considers the amendments made in the other House. It may agree to them, amend them, propose other amendments in lieu or reject them. A bill may pass backwards and forwards several times at this stage, as each House amends or rejects changes proposed by the other. If each House insists on disagreeing with the other, the bill is lost under the 'double insistence' rule, unless avoiding action is taken.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Devolution">Devolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Devolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Act_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Act of the Scottish Parliament">Act of the Scottish Parliament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Act_of_the_National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="Act of the National Assembly for Wales">Act of the National Assembly for Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Act_of_the_Northern_Ireland_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of the Northern Ireland Assembly">Act of the Northern Ireland Assembly</a></div>
<p>As a result of <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> are also able to create primary legislation for their respective devolved institutions. These devolved <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislatures</a> are able to create legislation regarding all but <a href="/wiki/Reserved_and_excepted_matters" title="Reserved and excepted matters">reserved and excepted matters</a>. However, Acts of the Parliament of the United Kingdom remain supreme and can overrule the devolved legislatures. By convention, the Parliament of the United Kingdom does not normally do this without a <a href="/wiki/Legislative_consent_motion" title="Legislative consent motion">legislative consent motion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1998-c-46_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1998-c-46-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2006-c-32_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2006-c-32-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-1998-c-47_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1998-c-47-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sovereignty">Sovereignty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Sovereignty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the UK there is still a presumption that <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">Parliament is sovereign</a>, however it is recognised that "Acts of Parliament are no longer sovereign but can be overruled if they are incompatible with <a href="/wiki/European_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="European Law">European Laws</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Within that constraint Acts of Parliament are generally without limit. European Law can overturn such Acts because the <a href="/wiki/European_Communities_Act_1972_(UK)" title="European Communities Act 1972 (UK)">European Communities Act 1972</a> agreed that UK law should not wish to conflict with European Law. However, if <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual_and_Temporal_and_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Lords Spiritual and Temporal and Commons">Parliament</a> decided to, it could withdraw from the European Union and withdraw from all European law. Parliament has also devolved significant powers to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>, it is free to overrule or even abolish those institutions, although this would be unlikely in practice.
</p><p>British law is also made through <a href="/wiki/Statutory_Instrument_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutory Instrument (UK)">Statutory Instruments</a> (SIs). These are laws which are made in the name of a government minister, exercising legislative powers delegated to him or her by Act of Parliament (known as the <i>parent Act</i> or <i>enabling Act</i>).  Some of these must be approved by Parliament before they can become law, others need only be laid before Parliament a certain number of days (usually 40) before coming into force.  They are used because they are much faster and simpler to implement than a full Act of Parliament, and are more easily amended to reflect changing circumstances. Provided they fall within the power delegated by the enabling Act, they have the same force as an Act of Parliament, and much of the UK's law is made in this way.  There are thousands of SIs each year, compared with around 50 Acts. Statutory Instruments are also used to bring Acts into force. Most Acts have sections that come into effect upon Royal Assent, or at a set date thereafter. However, other sections are brought into force using an SI which is titled [<i>Act Name</i>] (Commencement No. #) Order. Whilst most legislation has had a maximum of half a dozen Commencement Orders, this is not a strict limit as some Acts have had over 10.
</p><p>Nothing can bind <a href="/wiki/Lords_Spiritual_and_Temporal_and_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Lords Spiritual and Temporal and Commons">Parliament</a> as a successive parliament can repeal the legislation.
</p><p>International treaties are not effective in domestic UK law until enforced by an Act of Parliament (e.g. The European Communities Act, which brought the UK into the European Union, the Single European Act which allowed for the creation of the single European <a href="/wiki/Single_market" title="Single market">internal market</a> or the Outer Space Act which deals with international treaties on Space).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_records">Historical records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Historical records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the end of a medieval <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament of England</a> a collection of Acts of a public character was made in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Statute_Roll" title="Statute Roll">Statute Roll</a> and given the title of the King’s regnal year; each particular Act forming a section, or a chapter, of the complete Statute, so that, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Vagabonds_Act_1383" title="Vagabonds Act 1383">Vagabonds Act 1383</a> became VII Ric. II, c.5. Enrolment of Public Acts on manuscript <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchment</a> "<a href="/wiki/Parliament_Rolls" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliament Rolls">Parliament Rolls</a>" continued until 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-parliament.uk_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parliament.uk-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The longest Act of Parliament in the form of a scroll is an Act regarding taxation passed in 1821. It is nearly a quarter of a mile (348&#160;m) long, and used to take two men a whole day to rewind.<sup id="cite_ref-History_of_Parliamentary_Archives_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_Parliamentary_Archives-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Until 1850, a paper draft was brought into the House in which the Bill started; after the committee stage there the Bill was inscribed on a parchment roll and this parchment was then passed to the other House which could introduce amendments. The original Bill was never re-written and knives were used to scrape away the script from the top surface of the rolls before the new text was added. Since 1850 two copies of each Act were printed on <a href="/wiki/Vellum" title="Vellum">vellum</a>, one for preservation in the House of Lords and the other for transmission to the Public Record Office.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1483 annual volumes of Public Acts ("Statute Books") have been printed. In these volumes not only Public Acts but also some Private Acts and various "Local and Personal Acts declared Public" have been included.<sup id="cite_ref-parliament.uk_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parliament.uk-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All UK Acts of Parliament since 1497 are kept in the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Archives#The_House_of_Lords_Record_Office" title="Parliamentary Archives">House of Lords Record Office</a>, including the oldest Act: The "Taking of Apprentices for Worsteads in the County of Norfolk" Act 1497, a reference to the wool <a href="/wiki/Worsted" title="Worsted">worsted</a> manufacture at <a href="/wiki/Worstead" title="Worstead">Worstead</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Norfolk, England">Norfolk, England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History_of_Parliamentary_Archives_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_Parliamentary_Archives-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Acts passed before 1 January 1963 are cited by session and chapter. The session of parliament in which the Act was passed is referred to by the regnal year or years of the reigning Monarch and his name, which is usually abbreviated. So, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Treason_Act_1945" title="Treason Act 1945">Treason Act 1945</a> may be cited:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acts_in_force">Acts in force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Acts in force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The UK's <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Justice (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Justice</a> publishes most Acts of Parliament in an on-line <a href="/wiki/Statute_law_database" class="mw-redirect" title="Statute law database">statute law database</a>. It is the official revised edition of the primary <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The database shows Acts as amended by subsequent legislation and is the statute book of UK legislation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acts_of_constitutional_importance">Acts of constitutional importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Parliament_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Acts of constitutional importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Important acts in <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">UK constitutional</a> history include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a> – first law to limit the powers of the Monarch</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Treason_Act_1351" title="Treason Act 1351">Treason Act 1351</a> – codified the existing common law relating to Treason creating the first statutory act covering <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_Crown_Act_1533" class="mw-redirect" title="Succession to the Crown Act 1533">Succession to the Crown Act 1533</a> – altered the succession by declaring <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a>'s first daughter ineligible to the throne.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542</a> – united England and Wales</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_Crown_Act_1536" class="mw-redirect" title="Succession to the Crown Act 1536">Succession to the Crown Act 1536</a> – removed both of Henry VIII daughters from being eligible to the throne.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Ireland_Act_1542" title="Crown of Ireland Act 1542">Crown of Ireland Act 1542</a> – created the office of Monarch in Ireland</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_Crown_Act_1543" class="mw-redirect" title="Succession to the Crown Act 1543">Succession to the Crown Act 1543</a> – Reinstated both of Henry VIII's daughters in the line of succession behind his son establishing the principle of male primogeniture</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Petition_of_Right" title="Petition of Right">Petition of Right</a> Act 1628 sets out specific liberties which the Monarch was prohibited from infringing.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Habeas_Corpus_Act_1679" title="Habeas Corpus Act 1679">Habeas Corpus Act 1679</a> – forced courts to examine the lawfulness of a prisoner's detention</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689" title="Bill of Rights 1689">Bill of Rights 1689</a> – placed (or restated) limits on the monarch's power, formalising <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Act_of_Settlement_1701" title="Act of Settlement 1701">Act of Settlement 1701</a> – established the <a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_British_throne" title="Succession to the British throne">line of succession for the monarchy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1707">Act of Union 1707</a> – united the Kingdoms of England and Scotland into Great Britain</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1800">Act of Union 1800</a> – united the Kingdoms of Great Britain and Ireland into the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a> – with later <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act" title="Reform Act">Reform Acts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Representation_of_the_People_Act" title="Representation of the People Act">Representation of the People Acts</a>, extended the franchise and removed <a href="/wiki/Rotten_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotten borough">rotten boroughs</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act 1911</a> (amended 1949) – allowed the House of Commons to overrule the House of Lords after a delay</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Representation_of_the_People_Act_1918" title="Representation of the People Act 1918">Representation of the People Act 1918</a> (amended 1928) – extended the right to vote to women</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster 1931</a> – gave constitutional independence to the British dominions overseas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/His_Majesty%27s_Declaration_of_Abdication_Act_1936" title="His Majesty&#39;s Declaration of Abdication Act 1936">His Majesty's Declaration of Abdication Act 1936</a> – passed the abdication of <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Edward VIII">King Edward VIII</a> into law</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ireland_Act_1949" title="Ireland Act 1949">Ireland Act 1949</a> – formally recognised the Modern <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> by the United Kingdom and abolished the Monarchy in Ireland outside of Northern Ireland</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/European_Communities_Act_1972_(UK)" title="European Communities Act 1972 (UK)">European Communities Act 1972</a> – made the UK part of what is now the European Union providing for the application of European Law</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a> – enshrined the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> in domestic law</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Act_1999" title="House of Lords Act 1999">House of Lords Act 1999</a> - removed the automatic right of <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_peer" title="Hereditary peer">hereditary peers</a> to sit in the House of Lords</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Act_1998" title="Northern Ireland Act 1998">Northern Ireland Act 1998</a> – established a devolved <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> and passed the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> into law</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a> – established a devolved <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_1998" title="Government of Wales Act 1998">Government of Wales Act 1998</a> – created a devolved <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority_Act_1999" title="Greater London Authority Act 1999">Greater London Authority Act 1999</a> – established an elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a> and an assembly for Greater London</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a> - established the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> and reformed the office of Lord Chancellor</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> – conferred additional law-making powers to the National Assembly for Wales</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Act_2009" title="Northern Ireland Act 2009">Northern Ireland Act 2009</a> – devolved policing and justice to the Northern Ireland Assembly averting the assembly from being abolished</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_2012" title="Scotland Act 2012">Scotland Act 2012</a> – conferred additional powers to the Scottish Parliament</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Succession_to_the_Crown_Act_2013" title="Succession to the Crown Act 2013">Succession to the Crown Act 2013</a> – removed male primogeniture from eligibility to the throne</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wales_Act_2017" title="Wales Act 2017">Wales Act 2017</a> — changed Welsh devolution to the <a href="/wiki/Reserved_and_excepted_matters" title="Reserved and excepted matters">reserved powers model</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/European_Union_(Withdrawal)_Act_2018" title="European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018">European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018</a> — to withdraw the United Kingdom from the European Union and repeal the European Communities Act 1972.</li></ul>
