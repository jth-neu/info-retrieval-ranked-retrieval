<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Human Rights Act 1998</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>Human Rights Act 1998</b> (c42) is an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> which received <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">Royal Assent</a> on 9 November 1998, and mostly came into force on 2 October 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Its aim was to incorporate into UK law the rights contained in the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>. The Act makes a remedy for breach of a Convention right available in UK courts, without the need to go to the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECtHR) in <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>.
</p><p>In particular, the Act makes it unlawful for any public body to act in a way which is incompatible with the Convention, unless the wording of any other <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a> provides no other choice. It also requires the judiciary (including tribunals) to take account of any decisions, judgment or opinion of the European Court of Human Rights, and to interpret legislation, as far as possible, in a way which is compatible with Convention rights.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> However, if it is not possible to interpret an Act of Parliament so as to make it compatible with the Convention, the judges are not allowed to override it. All they can do is issue a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_incompatibility" title="Declaration of incompatibility">declaration of incompatibility</a>. This declaration does not affect the validity of the Act of Parliament: in that way, the Human Rights Act seeks to maintain the principle of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">Parliamentary sovereignty</a> (see: <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">Constitution of the United Kingdom</a>). However, judges may strike down <a href="/wiki/Secondary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary legislation">secondary legislation</a>. Under the Act, individuals retain the right to sue in the Strasbourg court.
</p><p>Former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> criticised the Act from 2007 and proposed to replace it with a "<a href="/wiki/Proposed_British_Bill_of_Rights" title="Proposed British Bill of Rights">British Bill of Rights</a>" during his <a href="/wiki/Second_Cameron_ministry" title="Second Cameron ministry">second ministry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(November 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The Convention was drafted by the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a> after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sir_David_Maxwell-Fyfe" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir David Maxwell-Fyfe">Sir David Maxwell-Fyfe</a> was the Chair of the Committee on Legal and Administrative Questions of the Council's Consultative Assembly from 1949 to 1952, and oversaw the drafting of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>. It was designed to incorporate a traditional <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> approach to securing "effective political democracy", from the strong traditions of freedom and liberty in the United Kingdom. As a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a>, the UK acceded to the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> in March 1951. However it was not until the 1960s that British citizens were able to bring claims in the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECtHR). During the 1980s, groups such as <a href="/wiki/Charter_88" title="Charter 88">Charter 88</a> (which invoked the 300th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> in 1688 and the <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689" title="Bill of Rights 1689">Bill of Rights 1689</a>) accused the executive of misusing its power and argued that a new British Bill of Rights was needed to secure human rights in the UK.
</p><p>In its manifesto for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997 general election</a>, the Labour party pledged to incorporate the European Convention into domestic law.  When the election resulted in a landslide Labour victory, the party, under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, fulfilled this pledge through Parliamentary passage of the Human Rights Act the following year.
</p><p>The 1997 white paper "Rights Brought Home"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> stated:
</p>
<blockquote><p>It takes on average five years to get an action into the European Court of Human Rights once all domestic remedies have been exhausted; and it costs an average of £30,000. Bringing these rights home will mean that the British people will be able to argue for their rights in the British courts – without this inordinate delay and cost.</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_of_the_Act">Structure of the Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure of the Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Human Rights Act places a duty on all courts and tribunals in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> to interpret legislation so far as possible in a way compatible with the rights laid down in the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> (<a href="/wiki/Section_3_of_the_Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Section 3 of the Human Rights Act 1998">section 3(1)</a>).  Where this is not possible, the court may issue a "declaration of incompatibility".  The declaration does not invalidate the legislation, but permits the amendment of the legislation by a special fast-track procedure under section 10 of the Act. As of August 2006, 20 declarations had been made, of which six were overturned on appeal.
</p><p>The Human Rights Act applies to all public bodies within the United Kingdom, including central government, local authorities, and bodies exercising public functions. However, it does not include Parliament when it is acting in its legislative capacities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Section_3">Section 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Section 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Section_3_of_the_Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Section 3 of the Human Rights Act 1998">Section 3 of the Human Rights Act 1998</a></div>
<p>Section 3 is a particularly wide provision that requires courts to interpret both <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Subordinate_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Subordinate legislation">subordinate legislation</a> so that their provisions are compatible with the articles of the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> which are also part of the Human Rights Act.<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman58_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman58-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  This interpretation goes far beyond normal <a href="/wiki/Statutory_interpretation" title="Statutory interpretation">statutory interpretation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman58_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman58-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and includes past and future legislation, therefore preventing the Human Rights Act from being <a href="/wiki/Implied_repeal" title="Implied repeal">impliedly repealed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Courts have applied this through three forms of interpretation:  "reading in", inserting words where there are none in a statute; "reading out", where words are omitted from a statute; and "reading down", where a particular meaning is chosen to be in compliance.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They do not interpret a statute so as to give it a meaning that would conflict with <a href="/wiki/Legislative_supremacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative supremacy">legislative intent</a>, and courts have been reluctant in particular to "read out" provisions for this reason. If it is not possible to so interpret, they may issue a declaration of incompatibility under section 4.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sections_4_and_10">Sections 4 and 10</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sections 4 and 10">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sections_4_and_10_of_the_Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Sections 4 and 10 of the Human Rights Act 1998">Sections 4 and 10 of the Human Rights Act 1998</a></div>
<p>Sections 4 and 10 allows courts to issue a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_incompatibility" title="Declaration of incompatibility">declaration of incompatibility</a> where it is impossible to use section 3 to interpret primary or subordinate legislation to be compatible with the articles of the European Convention on Human Rights, which are also part of the Human Rights Act.<sup id="cite_ref-section4_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-section4-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In these cases, interpretation to comply may conflict with <a href="/wiki/Legislative_supremacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative supremacy">legislative intent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  It is considered a measure of last resort. A range of <a href="/wiki/Superior_court" title="Superior court">superior courts</a> can issue a declaration of incompatibility.<sup id="cite_ref-section4_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-section4-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A declaration of incompatibility is not binding on the parties to the proceedings in which it is made,<sup id="cite_ref-section4_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-section4-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> nor can a declaration invalidate legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman6465_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman6465-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Section 4 therefore achieves its aim through political rather than legal means.
</p><p>Section 10 gives a government minister the power to make a "remedial order" in response to either
</p>
<ul><li>a declaration of incompatibility, from which there is no possibility of appeal,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> or</li>
<li>a ruling of the European Court of Human Rights</li></ul>
<p>A remedial order may "make such amendments to the legislation as [the Minister] considers necessary to remove the incompatibility".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Remedial orders do not require full legislative approval,<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman6465_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman6465-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> but must be approved by resolutions of each House of Parliament.  In especially urgent cases, Parliamentary approval may be retroactive.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Remedial orders may have retroactive effect, but no one may be guilty of a criminal offence solely as the result of the retroactive effect of a remedial order.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Section 10 has been used to make small adjustments to bring legislation into line with Convention rights although entirely new pieces of legislation are sometimes necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of December 2014, 29 declarations of incompatibility have been issued, of which<sup id="cite_ref-lse_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lse-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>8 have been struck down on appeal</li>
<li>1 is pending appeal, as of December 2014</li>
<li>16 have been remedied through the ordinary legislative process (including amendment or repeal of the offending legislation).</li>
<li>3 have been addressed through remedial orders</li>
<li>1 has not been remedied.</li></ul>
<p>The one case not to have been remedied, as of December 2014, is <i>Smith v. Scott</i>, concerning <a href="/wiki/Felony_disenfranchisement#United_Kingdom" title="Felony disenfranchisement">the right of serving prisoners to vote</a> in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-lse_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lse-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sections_6_to_9">Sections 6 to 9</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sections 6 to 9">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although the Act, by its own terms, applies only to <a href="/wiki/Public_bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Public bodies">public bodies</a>, it has had increasing influence on private law litigation between individual citizens leading some academics (source?) to state that it has <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_effect" title="Horizontal effect">horizontal effect</a> (as in disputes between citizens) as well as <a href="/wiki/Vertical_effect" title="Vertical effect">vertical effect</a> (as in disputes between the state and citizens). This is because section 6(1) of the Human Rights Act defines courts and tribunals as public bodies. That means their judgments must comply with human rights obligations of the state, whether a dispute is between the state and citizens, or between citizens, except in cases of declarations of incompatibility. Therefore, judges have a duty to act in compatibility with the Convention even when an action is a private one between two citizens.
</p><p>The way that public duty is exercised in private law was dealt with in a June 2016 decision <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bailii.org/cgi-bin/format.cgi?doc=/uk/cases/UKSC/2016/28.html">McDonald v McDonald &amp; Ors [2016] UKSC 28 (15 June 2016)</a> where the UK Supreme Court firstly considered the question <i>"... whether a court, when entertaining a claim for possession by a private sector owner against a residential occupier, should be required to consider the proportionality of evicting the occupier, in the light of section 6 of the Human Rights Act 1998 and article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights"</i>
</p><p>The Supreme Court decided (paragraph 46) that <i>"there are many cases where the court can be required to balance conflicting Convention rights of two parties, eg where a person is seeking to rely on her article 8 rights to restrain a newspaper from publishing an article which breaches her privacy, and where the newspaper relies on article 10. But such disputes arise not from contractual arrangements made between two private parties, but <b>tortious or quasi-tortious relationships</b>, where the legislature has expressly, impliedly or through inaction, left it to the courts to carry out the balancing exercise"</i>. 
</p><p>Therefore, in cases <i>"where the parties are in a <b>contractual relationship</b> in respect of which the legislature has prescribed how their respective Convention rights are to be respected"</i> then the Court decided, as set out in paragraph 59
<i>"In these circumstances, while we accept that the Strasbourg court jurisprudence relied on by the appellant does provide some support for the notion that article 8 was engaged when Judge Corrie was asked to make an order for possession against her, there is no support for the proposition that the judge could be required to consider the proportionality of the order which he would have made under the provisions of the 1980 and 1988 Acts. Accordingly, for the reasons set out in paras 40-46 above, we would dismiss this appeal on the first issue."</i>
</p><p>Paragraph 40 supposed that <i>"... it is not open to the tenant to contend that article 8 could justify a different order from that which is mandated by the <b>contractual relationship</b> between the parties, at least where, as here, there are legislative provisions which the democratically elected legislature has decided properly balance the competing interests of private sector landlords and residential tenants."</i>
</p><p>The duty of state judges to apply Convention rights to disputes between citizens is therefore about determining relationships between them, and applying domestic legislation accordingly. If the duty is carried out then it's likely there is <a href="/wiki/Article_6_of_the_European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="Article 6 of the European Convention on Human Rights">Article 6 compliance</a>.
</p><p>Section 7 limits a right to bring proceedings under section 6 only to victims (or potential victims) of the unlawful act of the public authority.
</p><p>Section 8 provides a right for a court to make any remedy they consider just and appropriate. A remedy under the Act is therefore not limited to a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_incompatibility" title="Declaration of incompatibility">Declaration of incompatibility</a> possibly taking into account the equitable maxim <a href="/wiki/Maxims_of_equity#Equity_delights_to_do_justice_and_not_by_halves" title="Maxims of equity">Equity delights to do justice and not by halves</a>.
</p><p>Section 9 provides a right to challenge the compliance of judicial acts made by the UK, but only by exercising a right of appeal as set out by the <a href="/wiki/Access_to_Justice_Act_1999" title="Access to Justice Act 1999">Access to Justice Act 1999</a> (although not precluding a right to judicial review). For example, whether a judicial act properly applies legislation, or not.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Sections">Other Sections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other Sections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Section 8 says that UK judges can grant any remedy that is considered just and appropriate.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rights_protected_under_the_Act">Rights protected under the Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rights protected under the Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many rights established under the Human Rights Act 1998 were already protected under UK law (such as freedom), but the purpose of the Act was largely to establish the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a> in British law.
</p><p>Section 21(5) of the Act completely abolished the <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Capital punishment in the United Kingdom">death penalty in the United Kingdom</a>, effective on royal assent. The death penalty had already been abolished for all civilian offences, including murder (<a href="/wiki/Murder_(Abolition_of_Death_Penalty)_Act_1965" title="Murder (Abolition of Death Penalty) Act 1965">Murder (Abolition of Death Penalty) Act 1965</a>) and treason (<a href="/wiki/Crime_and_Disorder_Act_1998" title="Crime and Disorder Act 1998">Crime and Disorder Act 1998</a>), but remained in force for certain military offences (although these provisions had not been used for several decades).
</p><p>This provision was not required by the European Convention (protocol 6 permits the death penalty in time of war; protocol 13, which prohibits the death penalty for all circumstances, did not then exist); rather, the government introduced it as a late amendment in response to parliamentary pressure.
</p><p>The Act provides that it is unlawful for a "public authority" to act in such a way as to contravene "Convention rights".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> For these purposes public authority includes any other person "whose functions are functions of a public nature."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It also explicitly includes the Courts.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Convention rights includes only those rights specified in section 1 of the Act (these are recited in full in Schedule 1).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In the interpretation of those rights the Act provides that the domestic Courts "may" take into account the jurisprudence of the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECtHR).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Section 7 enables any person with standing (as stipulated by Article 34 of the Convention) to raise an action against a public authority which has acted or proposes to act in such a Convention-contravening manner.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This is a more rigorous standard than is ordinarily applied to standing in English, although not Scottish, <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a>.
</p><p>If it is held that the public authority has violated the <a href="/wiki/Claimant" class="mw-redirect" title="Claimant">claimant</a>'s Convention rights, then the Court is empowered to "grant such relief or remedy, or make such order, within its powers as it considers just and appropriate."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This can include an award of <a href="/wiki/Damages" title="Damages">damages</a>, although the Act provides limitations on the Court's capacity to make such an award.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the Act also provides a defence for public authorities if their Convention violating act is in pursuance of a mandatory obligation imposed upon them by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Westminster</a> <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The Act envisages that this will ordinarily be a difficult standard to meet though since it requires the Courts to read such legislation (and for that matter <a href="/wiki/Subordinate_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Subordinate legislation">subordinate legislation</a>) "So far as it is possible to do so...in a way which is compatible with the Convention rights."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Where it is impossible to read primary legislation in a Convention compliant manner, the only sanction available to the Courts is to make a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Incompatibility" class="mw-redirect" title="Declaration of Incompatibility">Declaration of Incompatibility</a> in respect of it.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The power to do so is restricted to the higher Courts.<sup id="cite_ref-S4_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S4-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Such a Declaration has no direct impact upon the continuing force of the legislation but it is likely to produce public pressure upon the government to remove the incompatibility. It also strengthens the case of a claimant armed with such a decision from the domestic Courts in any subsequent appeal to ECtHR. In order to provide swift compliance with the Convention the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act</a> allows <a href="/wiki/Minister_(government)" title="Minister (government)">Ministers</a> to take remedial action to amend even offending primary <a href="/wiki/Legislation" title="Legislation">legislation</a> via <a href="/wiki/Subordinate_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Subordinate legislation">subordinate legislation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_human_rights_case_law">Notable human rights case law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable human rights case law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lee_Clegg" title="Lee Clegg">Lee Clegg</a>'s murder conviction gave rise to the first case invoking the Act, brought by  <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> in October 2000 which sought to overturn a libel ruling against the newspaper.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Campbell_v._MGN_Ltd." class="mw-redirect" title="Campbell v. MGN Ltd.">Campbell v. MGN Ltd.</a></i> [2002] EWCA Civ 1373, <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Campbell" title="Naomi Campbell">Naomi Campbell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sara_Cox" title="Sara Cox">Sara Cox</a> both sought to assert their <a href="/wiki/Right_to_privacy" title="Right to privacy">right to privacy</a> under the Act. Both cases were successful for the complainant (Campbell's on the second attempt; Cox's attempt was not judicially decided but an out of court settlement was reached before the issue could be tested in court) and an amendment to British law to incorporate a provision for privacy is expected to be introduced.</li>
<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Venables_and_Thompson_v._News_Group_Newspapers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Venables and Thompson v. News Group Newspapers (page does not exist)">Venables and Thompson v. News Group Newspapers</a></i> [2001] 1 All ER 908, the <a href="/wiki/James_Bulger_murder_case" class="mw-redirect" title="James Bulger murder case">James Bulger murder case</a> tested whether the Article 8 (privacy) rights of Venables and Thomson, the convicted murderers of Bulger, applied when four newspapers sought to publish their new identities and whereabouts, using their Article 10 rights of freedom of expression. The judge, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Butler-Sloss,_Baroness_Butler-Sloss" title="Elizabeth Butler-Sloss, Baroness Butler-Sloss">Dame Elizabeth Butler-Sloss</a>, granted permanent global injunctions ordering that the material not be published because of the disastrous consequences such disclosure might have for the former convicts, not least the possibility of physical harm or death (hence claims for Article 2 rights (right to life) were entertained, and sympathised with).</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/A_and_Others_v._Secretary_of_State_for_the_Home_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="A and Others v. Secretary of State for the Home Department">A and Others v. Secretary of State for the Home Department</a></i> [2004] <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20151007164149/http://www.parliament.the-stationery-office.co.uk/pa/ld200405/ldjudgmt/jd041216/a%26oth-1.htm">UKHL 56</a>, on 16 December 2004, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> held that Part 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-terrorism,_Crime_and_Security_Act_2001" title="Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001">Anti-terrorism, Crime and Security Act 2001</a>, under whose powers a number of non-UK nationals were detained in <a href="/wiki/Belmarsh_Prison" class="mw-redirect" title="Belmarsh Prison">Belmarsh Prison</a>, was incompatible with the Human Rights Act. This precipitated the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Prevention_of_Terrorism_Act_2005" title="Prevention of Terrorism Act 2005">Prevention of Terrorism Act 2005</a> to replace Part 4 of the 2001 Act.</li>
<li><i>R. v. Chauhan and Hollingsworth</i>: Amesh Chauhan and Dean Hollingsworth were photographed by a <a href="/wiki/Speed_camera" class="mw-redirect" title="Speed camera">speed camera</a> in 2000. As is standard practice for those caught in this way, they were sent a form by the police asking them to identify who was driving the vehicle at the time. They protested under the Human Rights Act, arguing that they could not be required to give evidence against themselves. An initial judgment, by Judge Peter Crawford at Birmingham Crown Court, ruled in their favour<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> but this was later reversed. The same issue came to light in Scotland with <i>Procurator Fiscal v Brown</i> [2000] UKPC D3,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> in which a woman, when apprehended on suspicion of theft of a bottle of gin, was drunk and was asked by police to identify who had been driving her car (which was nearby) at the time she arrived at the superstore.</li>
<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Price_v._Leeds_City_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Price v. Leeds City Council (page does not exist)">Price v. Leeds City Council</a></i> [2005]:<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> On 16 March 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a> upheld a High Court ruling that Leeds City Council could not infringe the right to a home of a <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> family, the Maloneys, by evicting them from public land. The court however referred the case to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> as this decision conflicted with a ruling from the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECtHR).</li>
<li>In March 2006,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the High Court in London ruled against a hospital's bid to turn off the ventilator that kept the child, known as Baby MB, alive. The 19-month-old baby has the genetic condition <a href="/wiki/Spinal_muscular_atrophy" title="Spinal muscular atrophy">spinal muscular atrophy</a>, which leads to almost total paralysis. The parents of the child fought for his right to life, despite claims from medics that the invasive ventilation would cause an 'intolerable life'.</li>
<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Connors_v._UK&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Connors v. UK (page does not exist)">Connors v. UK</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> a judgment given by ECtHR, declared that <a href="/wiki/Nomads" class="mw-redirect" title="Nomads">travellers</a> who had their licences to live on local authority-owned land suddenly revoked had been discriminated against, in comparison to the treatment of mobile-home owners who did not belong to the traveller population, and thus their Article 14 (protection from discrimination) and Article 8 (right to respect for the home) rights had been infringed. However, there has never been a case where the Act has been successfully invoked to allow travellers to remain on greenbelt land, and indeed the prospects of this ever happening seem highly unlikely after the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> decision in <i><a href="/wiki/Kay_v_Lambeth_LBC" title="Kay v Lambeth LBC">Kay v Lambeth LBC</a></i> which severely restricted the occasions on which Article 8 may be invoked to protect someone from eviction in the absence of some legal right over the land.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Afghan_hijackers_case_2006" class="mw-redirect" title="Afghan hijackers case 2006">Afghan hijackers case 2006</a></i>, in May 2006, a politically controversial decision regarding the treatment of nine Afghan men who hijacked a plane to flee from the <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a>, caused widespread condemnation by many <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_journalism" title="Tabloid journalism">tabloid</a> newspapers (most notably <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i>), the broadsheets and the leaders of both the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>. It was ruled by an <a href="/wiki/Immigration" title="Immigration">Immigration</a> <a href="/wiki/Tribunals_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Tribunals in the United Kingdom">Tribunal</a>, under the Human Rights Act, that the hijackers could remain in the United Kingdom; a subsequent court decision ruled that the government had abused its power in restricting the hijackers' right to work.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mosley_v_News_Group_Newspapers_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Mosley v News Group Newspapers Limited">Mosley v News Group Newspapers Limited</a></i> (2008), <a href="/wiki/Max_Mosley" title="Max Mosley">Max Mosley</a> challenged an invasion of his private life after the <i><a href="/wiki/News_of_the_World" title="News of the World">News of the World</a></i> exposed his involvement in a <a href="/wiki/BDSM" title="BDSM">sadomasochistic</a> sex act. The case resulted in Mr Mosley being awarded <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>60,000 in damages.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Excessive_rights">Excessive rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Excessive rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the campaign for the 2005 parliamentary elections the Conservatives under <a href="/wiki/Michael_Howard" title="Michael Howard">Michael Howard</a> declared their intention to "overhaul or scrap" the Human Rights Act:
</p>
<blockquote><p>
The time had come to liberate the nation from the avalanche of <a href="/wiki/Political_correctness" title="Political correctness">political correctness</a>, costly litigation, feeble justice, and culture of compensation running riot in Britain today and warning that the politically correct regime ushered in by Labour's enthusiastic adoption of human rights legislation has turned the age-old principle of fairness on its head.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (December 2016)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>The schoolboy <a href="/wiki/Arsonist" class="mw-redirect" title="Arsonist">arsonist</a> allowed back into the classroom because enforcing discipline apparently denied his right to education; the convicted <a href="/wiki/Rapist" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapist">rapist</a> given £4000 compensation because his second <a href="/wiki/Appeal" title="Appeal">appeal</a> was delayed; the burglar given taxpayers' money to sue the man whose house he broke into; <a href="/wiki/Irish_Travellers" title="Irish Travellers">travellers</a> who thumb their nose at the law allowed to stay on <a href="/wiki/Green_belt" title="Green belt">green belt</a> sites they have occupied in defiance of planning laws".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2016)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The schoolboy referred to was speculatively suing for compensation and was a university student at the time of the court case.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the claim was rejected.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Judicial_powers">Judicial powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Judicial powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Judicial_activism" title="Judicial activism">Judicial activism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Separation of powers in the United Kingdom">Separation of powers in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Constitutional critics since the Human Rights Bill was tabled at parliament claimed it would result in the unelected judiciary making substantive judgments about government policies and "mass-legislating" in their amendments to the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> resulting in a usurpation of Parliament's <a href="/wiki/Legislative_supremacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative supremacy">legislative supremacy</a> and an expansion of the UK courts' <a href="/wiki/Justiciability" title="Justiciability">justiciability</a>. A leading case of <i><a href="/wiki/R_(on_the_application_of_Daly)_v_Secretary_of_State_for_the_Home_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="R (on the application of Daly) v Secretary of State for the Home Department">R (on the application of Daly) v Secretary of State for the Home Department</a></i> highlights how the new proportionality test borrowed from ECtHR jurisprudence has allowed a greater scrutiny of the substantive merits of decisions of public bodies, meaning that actions against such bodies, Judicial Reviews, are more of an appeal than a traditional judicial review.<sup id="cite_ref-craig_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-craig-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They stress the overriding interpretative obligation of courts under section 3(1) of the Human Rights Act to read primary legislation as Convention-compliant, so far as is possible, is not dependent upon the presence of ambiguity in legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-craig_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-craig-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Section 3(1) could require the court to depart from the unambiguous meaning that legislation would otherwise bear subject to the constraint that this modified interpretation must be one "possible" interpretation of the legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Craig_(law_professor)" title="Paul Craig (law professor)">Paul Craig</a> argues that this results in the courts adopting linguistically strained interpretations instead of issuing <a href="/wiki/Declarations_of_incompatibility" class="mw-redirect" title="Declarations of incompatibility">declarations of incompatibility</a>.
</p><p>Rulings based on the Human Rights Act have been criticised by <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Hale,_Baroness_Hale_of_Richmond" title="Brenda Hale, Baroness Hale of Richmond">Lady Hale</a>. In 2011 she said that the Act was overused, and criticised the decision of European human rights judges to ensure all signatory states give some prisoners the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-ladyhale_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ladyhale-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalistic_freedom">Journalistic freedom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Journalistic freedom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dacre" title="Paul Dacre">Paul Dacre</a> (as editor of the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a>) criticised the Human Rights Act for allowing, in effect, a right to privacy at English law despite the fact that Parliament has not passed such legislation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He was referring to the <a href="/wiki/Indirect_horizontal_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Indirect horizontal effect">indirect horizontal effect</a> of the Human Rights Act on the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Breach_of_confidence" title="Breach of confidence">breach of confidence</a> which has moved <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a> closer towards a <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> right to privacy.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In response, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Baron_Falconer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Baron Falconer (page does not exist)">Baron Falconer</a> (as Lord Chancellor) stated that the Human Rights Act had been passed by Parliament, that people's private lives needed protection and that the judge in the case had interpreted relevant authorities correctly.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inadequacy">Inadequacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Inadequacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast, some have argued that the Human Rights Act does not give adequate protection to rights because of the ability for the government to derogate from Convention rights under article 15. Recent cases such as <i><a href="/wiki/R_(ProLife_Alliance)_v._BBC" class="mw-redirect" title="R (ProLife Alliance) v. BBC">R (ProLife Alliance) v. BBC</a></i> [2002] EWCA Civ 297 have been decided in reference to common law rights rather than statutory rights. Where there is no clear <a href="/wiki/Precedent" title="Precedent">precedent</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, judges remain accused of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_activism" title="Judicial activism">judicial activism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrorism-related_complaints">Terrorism-related complaints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Terrorism-related complaints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>More <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> politicians in the two largest parties, including some ministers, have criticised the Human Rights Act as to the willingness of the judiciary to make declarations on incompatibility against terrorism legislation. <a href="/wiki/John_Reid,_Baron_Reid_of_Cardowan" title="John Reid, Baron Reid of Cardowan">Baron Reid</a> argued that the Act was hampering the fight against global terrorism in regard to controversial <a href="/wiki/Control_order" title="Control order">control orders</a>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>
There is a very serious threat – and I am the first to admit that the means we have of fighting it are so inadequate that we are fighting with one arm tied behind our backs. So I hope when we bring forward proposals in the next few weeks that we will have a little less party politics and a little more support for national security.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planned_replacement">Planned replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_Rights_Act_1998&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Planned replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007, Howard's successor as <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(United_Kingdom)" title="Leader of the Opposition (United Kingdom)">Leader of the Opposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a>, vowed to repeal the Human Rights Act if he was elected, instead replacing it with a "Bill of Rights" for Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The human rights organisation <a href="/wiki/JUSTICE" title="JUSTICE">JUSTICE</a> released a discussion paper entitled <i>A Bill of Rights for Britain?</i>, examining the case for updating the Human Rights Act with an entrenched bill.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative%E2%80%93Liberal_Democrat_coalition_agreement" title="Conservative–Liberal Democrat coalition agreement">Conservative–Liberal Democrat coalition agreement</a> said that the Human Rights Act would be investigated.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, following controversial rulings from both the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="European Court of Human Rights">European Court of Human Rights</a> (ECtHR) and the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>, David Cameron suggested a "British Bill of Rights".<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The government commission set up to investigate the case for a Bill of Rights had a split of opinion.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Judge <a href="/wiki/Dean_Spielmann" title="Dean Spielmann">Dean Spielmann</a>, the President of ECtHR, warned in 2013 that the United Kingdom could not withdraw from the Convention on Human Rights without jeopardising its membership of the European Union.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> planned to repeal the Act and replace it with a "British Bill of Rights".<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015 election</a> win for the Conservative Party, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Gove" title="Michael Gove">Michael Gove</a>, the Secretary of State for Justice, was charged with implementing the reforms which were previously blocked by the Liberal Democrats in the coalition government.<sup id="cite_ref-gove_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gove-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The Conservative Party manifesto said that the new bill will "break the formal link between British Courts and the European Court of Human Rights".<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> As before 1998 claims relying on ECtHR jurisprudence which conflicted with the "British Bill of Rights" would have to go to a court in Strasbourg rather than being able to be heard in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Civil liberty advocates have expressed concern that the proposed changes would "erode the right to life, the right to privacy, the right to a fair trial, the right to protest and the right to freedom from torture and discrimination".<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the Conservatives' manifesto from the next general election in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017">2017</a> pledged to retain the Human Rights Act "while the process of <a href="/wiki/Brexit" title="Brexit">Brexit</a> is  underway".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
