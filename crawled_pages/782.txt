<p><b>Alan Alexander Milne</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 18 January 1882 – 31 January 1956) was an English author, best known for his books about the <a href="/wiki/Teddy_bear" title="Teddy bear">teddy bear</a> <a href="/wiki/Winnie-the-Pooh" title="Winnie-the-Pooh">Winnie-the-Pooh</a> and for various poems. Milne was a noted writer, primarily as a playwright, before the huge success of Pooh overshadowed all his previous work. Milne served in both World Wars, joining the British Army in World War I, and was a captain of the <a href="/wiki/Home_Guard_(United_Kingdom)" title="Home Guard (United Kingdom)">British Home Guard</a> in World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alan Alexander Milne was born in <a href="/wiki/Kilburn,_London" title="Kilburn, London">Kilburn, London</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> to parents John Vine Milne, who was born in Jamaica,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  and Sarah Marie Milne (née Heginbotham) and grew up at Henley House School, 6/7 Mortimer Road (now Crescent), Kilburn, a small <a href="/wiki/Independent_school_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent school (UK)">public school</a> run by his father.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBthwaite_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBthwaite-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> One of his teachers was <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a>, who taught there in 1889–90.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Milne attended <a href="/wiki/Westminster_School" title="Westminster School">Westminster School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a> <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> where he studied on a mathematics scholarship, graduating with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> in Mathematics in 1903.  He edited and wrote for <i><a href="/wiki/Granta" title="Granta">Granta</a></i>, a student magazine.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBthwaite_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBthwaite-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  He collaborated with his brother Kenneth and their articles appeared over the initials AKM. Milne's work came to the attention of the leading British humour magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i>, where Milne was to become a contributor and later an assistant editor. Milne played for the amateur English <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> team the <a href="/wiki/Allahakbarries" title="Allahakbarries">Allahakbarries</a> alongside authors <a href="/wiki/J._M._Barrie" title="J. M. Barrie">J. M. Barrie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Milne joined the British Army in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and served as an officer in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Warwickshire_Fusiliers" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Warwickshire Fusiliers">Royal Warwickshire Regiment</a> and later, after a debilitating illness, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Corps_of_Signals" title="Royal Corps of Signals">Royal Corps of Signals</a>. He was commissioned into the 4th Battalion, Royal Warwickshire Regiment on 1 February 1915 as a <a href="/wiki/Second_lieutenant" title="Second lieutenant">second lieutenant</a> (on probation).<sup id="cite_ref-LG_16_February_1915_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LG_16_February_1915-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> His commission was confirmed on 20 December 1915.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On 7 July 1916, he was injured while serving in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Somme" title="Battle of the Somme">Battle of the Somme</a> and invalided back to England. Having recuperated, he was recruited into Military Intelligence to write propaganda articles for <a href="/wiki/MI7" title="MI7">MI7 (b)</a> between 1916 and 1918. He was discharged on 14 February 1919,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and settled in Mallord Street, <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He relinquished his commission on 19 February 1920, retaining the rank of lieutenant.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the war, he wrote a denunciation of war titled <i>Peace with Honour</i> (1934), which he retracted somewhat with 1940's <i>War with Honour</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNBthwaite_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNBthwaite-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> During World War II, Milne was one of the most prominent critics of fellow English writer <a href="/wiki/P._G._Wodehouse" title="P. G. Wodehouse">P. G. Wodehouse</a>, who was captured at his country home in France by the <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> and imprisoned for a year. Wodehouse made radio broadcasts about his internment, which were broadcast from Berlin. Although the light-hearted broadcasts made fun of the Germans, Milne accused Wodehouse of committing an act of near treason by cooperating with his country's enemy. Wodehouse got some revenge on his former friend (e.g., in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mating_Season_(novel)" title="The Mating Season (novel)">The Mating Season</a></i>) by creating fatuous parodies of the Christopher Robin poems in some of his later stories, and claiming that Milne "was probably jealous of all other writers.... But I loved his stuff."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Milne married Dorothy "Daphne" de Sélincourt in 1913 and their son <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Robin_Milne" title="Christopher Robin Milne">Christopher Robin Milne</a> was born in 1920.  In 1925, Milne bought a country home, <a href="/wiki/Cotchford_Farm" title="Cotchford Farm">Cotchford Farm</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Hartfield" title="Hartfield">Hartfield</a>, East Sussex.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During World War II, Milne was Captain of the <a href="/wiki/British_Home_Guard" class="mw-redirect" title="British Home Guard">British Home Guard</a> in Hartfield &amp; Forest Row, insisting on being plain "Mr. Milne" to the members of his platoon. He retired to the farm after a stroke and brain surgery in 1952 left him an invalid, and by August 1953 "he seemed very old and disenchanted." <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Milne died in January 1956, aged 74.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_career">Literary career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1903_to_1925">1903 to 1925</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1903 to 1925">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_a_milne.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/A_a_milne.jpg/220px-A_a_milne.jpg" width="220" height="273" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/A_a_milne.jpg/330px-A_a_milne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/A_a_milne.jpg/440px-A_a_milne.jpg 2x" data-file-width="515" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_a_milne.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A. A. Milne in 1922</div></div></div>
<p>After graduating from Cambridge in 1903, A. A. Milne contributed humorous verse and whimsical essays to <i>Punch</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> joining the staff in 1906 and becoming an assistant editor.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During this period he published 18 plays and three novels, including the murder mystery <i><a href="/wiki/The_Red_House_Mystery" title="The Red House Mystery">The Red House Mystery</a></i> (1922).  His son was born in August 1920 and in 1924 Milne produced a collection of children's poems <i><a href="/wiki/When_We_Were_Very_Young" title="When We Were Very Young">When We Were Very Young</a></i>, which were illustrated by <i>Punch</i> staff cartoonist <a href="/wiki/E._H._Shepard" title="E. H. Shepard">E. H. Shepard</a>.  A collection of short stories for children <i><a href="/wiki/A_Gallery_of_Children" title="A Gallery of Children">A Gallery of Children</a></i>, and other stories that became part of the Winnie-the-Pooh books, were first published in 1925.
</p><p>Milne was an early screenwriter for the nascent British film industry, writing four stories filmed in 1920 for the company Minerva Films (founded in 1920 by the actor <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Howard_(actor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Leslie Howard (actor)">Leslie Howard</a> and his friend and story editor <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Brunel" title="Adrian Brunel">Adrian Brunel</a>).  These were <i>The Bump</i>, starring <a href="/wiki/C._Aubrey_Smith" title="C. Aubrey Smith">Aubrey Smith</a>; <i>Twice Two</i>; <i>Five Pound Reward</i>; and <i>Bookworms</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Some of these films survive in the archives of the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>. Milne had met Howard when the actor starred in Milne’s play <i>Mr Pim Passes By</i> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Looking back on this period (in 1926), Milne observed that when he told his agent that he was going to write a detective story, he was told that what the country wanted from a "<i>Punch</i> humorist" was a humorous story; when two years later he said he was writing nursery rhymes, his agent and publisher were convinced he should write another detective story; and after another two years, he was being told that writing a detective story would be in the worst of taste given the demand for children's books. He concluded that "the only excuse which I have yet discovered for writing anything is that I want to write it; and I should be as proud to be delivered of a Telephone Directory <i>con amore</i> as I should be ashamed to create a Blank Verse Tragedy at the bidding of others."<sup id="cite_ref-RedHouseMystery_Intro_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RedHouseMystery_Intro-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1926_to_1928">1926 to 1928</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1926 to 1928">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A._A._Milne_with_his_son_Christopher_Robin_Milne_and_Pooh_Bear_-_Howard_Coster_-_NPG_P715.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7c/A._A._Milne_with_his_son_Christopher_Robin_Milne_and_Pooh_Bear_-_Howard_Coster_-_NPG_P715.jpg/220px-A._A._Milne_with_his_son_Christopher_Robin_Milne_and_Pooh_Bear_-_Howard_Coster_-_NPG_P715.jpg" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/7c/A._A._Milne_with_his_son_Christopher_Robin_Milne_and_Pooh_Bear_-_Howard_Coster_-_NPG_P715.jpg/330px-A._A._Milne_with_his_son_Christopher_Robin_Milne_and_Pooh_Bear_-_Howard_Coster_-_NPG_P715.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/7/7c/A._A._Milne_with_his_son_Christopher_Robin_Milne_and_Pooh_Bear_-_Howard_Coster_-_NPG_P715.jpg 2x" data-file-width="341" data-file-height="292" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A._A._Milne_with_his_son_Christopher_Robin_Milne_and_Pooh_Bear_-_Howard_Coster_-_NPG_P715.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Milne with his son Christopher Robin and Pooh Bear, at Cotchford Farm, their home in Sussex. Photo by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Coster" title="Howard Coster">Howard Coster</a>, 1926.</div></div></div>
<p>Milne is most famous for his two <i>Pooh</i> books about a boy named <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Robin" title="Christopher Robin">Christopher Robin</a> after his son, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Robin_Milne" title="Christopher Robin Milne">Christopher Robin Milne</a>, and various characters inspired by his son's stuffed animals, most notably the bear named <a href="/wiki/Winnie-the-Pooh" title="Winnie-the-Pooh">Winnie-the-Pooh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Christopher Robin Milne's stuffed bear, originally named "Edward," <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> was renamed "Winnie" after a Canadian <a href="/wiki/American_Black_Bear" class="mw-redirect" title="American Black Bear">black bear</a> named <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_(bear)" title="Winnipeg (bear)">Winnie</a> (after <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>), which was used as a military mascot in World War I, and left to <a href="/wiki/London_Zoo" title="London Zoo">London Zoo</a> during the war. "The pooh" comes from a swan the young Milne named "Pooh." <a href="/wiki/E._H._Shepard" title="E. H. Shepard">E. H. Shepard</a> illustrated the original Pooh books, using his own son's teddy, Growler ("a magnificent bear"), as the model. The rest of Christopher Robin Milne's toys, <a href="/wiki/Piglet_(Winnie-the-Pooh)" title="Piglet (Winnie-the-Pooh)">Piglet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eeyore" title="Eeyore">Eeyore</a>, Kanga, Roo and <a href="/wiki/Tigger" title="Tigger">Tigger</a>, were incorporated into A. A. Milne's stories,<sup id="cite_ref-Express_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Express-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-birthday_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birthday-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and two more characters&#160;– Rabbit and Owl&#160;– were created by Milne's imagination. Christopher Robin Milne's own toys are now on display in New York where 750,000 people visit them every year.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg/200px-The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg/300px-The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg/400px-The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_original_Winnie_the_Pooh_toys.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The real stuffed toys owned by Christopher Robin Milne and featured in the <i>Winnie-the-Pooh</i> stories. They are on display in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library_Main_Branch" title="New York Public Library Main Branch">New York Public Library Main Branch</a> in New York. Missing is Roo, who was lost when Christopher Robin was very young.</div></div></div>
<p>The fictional <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Acre_Wood" title="Hundred Acre Wood">Hundred Acre Wood</a> of the Pooh stories derives from Five Hundred Acre Wood in <a href="/wiki/Ashdown_Forest" title="Ashdown Forest">Ashdown Forest</a> in East Sussex, South East England, where the Pooh stories were set. Milne lived on the northern edge of the forest at Cotchford Farm, <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=A._A._Milne&amp;params=51.09_N_0.107_E_"><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">51°05′24″N</span> <span class="longitude">0°06′25″E</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">51.090°N 0.107°E</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">51.090; 0.107</span></span></span></a></span>, and took his son walking there. E. H. Shepard drew on the landscapes of Ashdown Forest as inspiration for many of the illustrations he provided for the Pooh books. The adult Christopher Robin commented: "Pooh's Forest and Ashdown Forest are identical." <sup id="cite_ref-Express_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Express-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Popular tourist locations at Ashdown Forest include: <i>Galleon's Lap</i>, <i>The Enchanted Place</i>, the <i><a href="/wiki/Heffalump_trap" class="mw-redirect" title="Heffalump trap">Heffalump Trap</a></i> and <i>Lone Pine</i>, <i>Eeyore’s Sad and Gloomy Place</i>, and the wooden <i>Pooh Bridge</i> where Pooh and Piglet invented <a href="/wiki/Poohsticks" title="Poohsticks">Poohsticks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not yet known as Pooh, he made his first appearance in a poem, "Teddy Bear," published in <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i> magazine in February 1924 and republished in <i><a href="/wiki/When_We_Were_Very_Young" title="When We Were Very Young">When We Were Very Young</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Pooh first appeared in the <i><a href="/wiki/London_Evening_News" title="London Evening News">London Evening News</a></i> on Christmas Eve, 1925, in a story called "The Wrong Sort Of Bees." <sup id="cite_ref-birthday_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-birthday-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Winnie-the-Pooh_(book)" title="Winnie-the-Pooh (book)">Winnie-the-Pooh</a></i> was published in 1926, followed by <i><a href="/wiki/The_House_at_Pooh_Corner" title="The House at Pooh Corner">The House at Pooh Corner</a></i> in 1928. A second collection of nursery rhymes, <i><a href="/wiki/Now_We_Are_Six" title="Now We Are Six">Now We Are Six</a></i>, was published in 1927. All four books were illustrated by E. H. Shepard. Milne also published four plays in this period. He also "gallantly stepped forward" to contribute a quarter of the costs of dramatising P. G. Wodehouse's <i>A Damsel in Distress</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <i>The World of Pooh</i> won the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll_Shelf_Award" title="Lewis Carroll Shelf Award">Lewis Carroll Shelf Award</a> in 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1929_onwards">1929 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1929 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The success of his children's books was to become a source of considerable annoyance to Milne, whose self-avowed aim was to write whatever he pleased and who had, until then, found a ready audience for each change of direction: he had freed pre-war <i>Punch</i> from its ponderous facetiousness; he had made a considerable reputation as a playwright (like his idol <a href="/wiki/J._M._Barrie" title="J. M. Barrie">J. M. Barrie</a>) on both sides of the Atlantic; he had produced a witty piece of detective writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Red_House_Mystery" title="The Red House Mystery">The Red House Mystery</a></i> (although this was severely criticised by <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chandler" title="Raymond Chandler">Raymond Chandler</a> for the implausibility of its plot). But once Milne had, in his own words, "said goodbye to all that in 70,000 words" (the approximate length of his four principal children's books), he had no intention of producing any reworkings lacking in originality, given that one of the sources of inspiration, his son, was growing older.
</p><p>Another reason Milne stopped writing children's books, and especially about Winnie-the-Pooh, was that he felt "amazement and disgust" over the fame his son was exposed to, and said that "I feel that the legal Christopher Robin has already had more publicity than I want for him. I do not want CR Milne to ever wish that his name were Charles Robert."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his literary home, <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i>, where the <i>When We Were Very Young</i> verses had first appeared, Methuen continued to publish whatever Milne wrote, including the long poem "The Norman Church" and an assembly of articles entitled <i>Year In, Year Out</i> (which Milne likened to a benefit night for the author).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1930, Milne adapted <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Grahame" title="Kenneth Grahame">Kenneth Grahame</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wind_in_the_Willows" title="The Wind in the Willows">The Wind in the Willows</a></i> for the stage as <i><a href="/wiki/Toad_of_Toad_Hall" title="Toad of Toad Hall">Toad of Toad Hall</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The title was an implicit admission that such chapters as Chapter 7, "The Piper at the Gates of Dawn," could not survive translation to the theatre. A special introduction written by Milne is included in some editions of Grahame's novel.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Milne and his wife became estranged from their son, who came to resent what he saw as his father's exploitation of his childhood and came to hate the books that had thrust him into the public eye.<sup id="cite_ref-autobio2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autobio2-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Marrying his first cousin, Lesley de Sélincourt, distanced Christopher still further from his parents – Lesley's father and Christopher's mother hadn't spoken to each other for 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_commemoration">Legacy and commemoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy and commemoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatleft" style="width:28%;&#32;;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">"I suppose that every one of us hopes secretly for immortality; to leave, I mean, a name behind him which will live forever in this world, whatever he may be doing, himself, in the next."</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">—A. A. Milne.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>The rights to A. A. Milne's Pooh books were left to four beneficiaries: his family, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Literary_Fund" title="Royal Literary Fund">Royal Literary Fund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westminster_School" title="Westminster School">Westminster School</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Garrick_Club" title="Garrick Club">Garrick Club</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rights_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rights-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> After Milne's death in 1956, one week and six days after his 74th birthday, his widow sold her rights to the Pooh characters to <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Slesinger" title="Stephen Slesinger">Stephen Slesinger</a>, whose widow sold the rights after Slesinger's death to <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">the Walt Disney Company</a>, which has made many Pooh cartoon movies, a <a href="/wiki/Disney_Channel" title="Disney Channel">Disney Channel</a> television show, as well as Pooh-related merchandise. In 2001, the other beneficiaries sold their interest in the estate to the Disney Corporation for $350m. Previously Disney had been paying twice-yearly royalties to these beneficiaries. The estate of <a href="/wiki/E._H._Shepard" title="E. H. Shepard">E. H. Shepard</a> also received a sum in the deal. The UK copyright on the text of the original Winnie the Pooh books expires on 1 January 2027;<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  at the beginning of the year after the 70th anniversary of the author's death (PMA-70), and has already expired in those countries with a PMA-50 rule.  This applies to all of Milne's works except those first published posthumously.  The illustrations in the Pooh books will remain under copyright until the same amount of time has passed, after the illustrator's death; in the UK, this will be on 1 January 2047.  In the United States, copyright will not expire until 95 years after publication for each of Milne's books first published before 1978, but this includes the illustrations.
</p><p>In 2008, a collection of original illustrations featuring Winnie-the-Pooh and his animal friends sold for more than £1.2 million at auction in Sotheby's, London.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> <i>Forbes</i> magazine ranked Winnie the Pooh the most valuable fictional character in 2002; Winnie the Pooh merchandising products alone had annual sales of more than $5.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, Winnie the Pooh generated $6 billion, a figure surpassed by only <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse" title="Mickey Mouse">Mickey Mouse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_2006_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_2006-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg/230px-A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg" width="230" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg/345px-A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg/460px-A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A.A.Milne_and_E.H.Shephard_memorial_plaque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_58820.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A. A. Milne and E. H. Shepard memorial plaque at <a href="/wiki/Ashdown_Forest" title="Ashdown Forest">Ashdown Forest</a>, East Sussex, the setting for <i>Winnie the Pooh</i></div></div></div>
<p>A memorial plaque in <a href="/wiki/Ashdown_Forest" title="Ashdown Forest">Ashdown Forest</a>, unveiled by Christopher Robin in 1979, commemorates the work of A. A. Milne and Shepard in creating the world of Pooh.<sup id="cite_ref-Express_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Express-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Milne once wrote of Ashdown Forest: "In that enchanted place on the top of the forest a little boy and his bear will always be playing." <sup id="cite_ref-Express_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Express-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, <i>Winnie the Pooh</i> was listed at number 7 on the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s poll <a href="/wiki/The_Big_Read" title="The Big Read">The Big Read</a> which determined the UK's "best-loved novels" of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, Winnie the Pooh received a star on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">Hollywood Walk of Fame</a>, marking the 80th birthday of Milne's creation.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_2006_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_2006-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> That same year a UK poll saw Winnie the Pooh voted onto the list of icons of England.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Marking the 90th anniversary of Milne's creation of the character, and the 90th birthday of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>, in 2016 a new story sees Winnie the Pooh meet the Queen at <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a>. The illustrated and audio adventure is titled <i><a href="/wiki/Winnie-the-Pooh_Meets_the_Queen" title="Winnie-the-Pooh Meets the Queen">Winnie-the-Pooh Meets the Queen</a></i>, and has been narrated by actor <a href="/wiki/Jim_Broadbent" title="Jim Broadbent">Jim Broadbent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2016, a new character, a Penguin, was unveiled in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Best_Bear_in_All_the_World" class="mw-redirect" title="The Best Bear in All the World">The Best Bear in All the World</a></i>, which was inspired by a long-lost photograph of Milne and his son Christopher with a toy penguin.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several of Milne's children's poems were set to music by the composer <a href="/wiki/Harold_Fraser-Simson" title="Harold Fraser-Simson">Harold Fraser-Simson</a>. His poems have been parodied many times, including with the books <i>When We Were Rather Older</i> and <i>Now We Are Sixty</i>. The 1963 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_Breakfast_(film)" title="The King&#39;s Breakfast (film)">The King's Breakfast</a></i> was based on Milne's poem of the same name.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An exhibition entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Winnie-the-Pooh:_Exploring_a_Classic" title="Winnie-the-Pooh: Exploring a Classic">"Winnie-the-Pooh: Exploring a Classic"</a></i> appeared at the <i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">V &amp; A</a></i> from 9 December 2017 to 8 April 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archive">Archive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Archive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bulk of A. A. Milne's papers are housed at the <a href="/wiki/Harry_Ransom_Center" title="Harry Ransom Center">Harry Ransom Center</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a>. The collection, established at the center in 1964, consists of manuscript drafts and fragments for over 150 of Milne’s works, as well as correspondence, legal documents, genealogical records, and some personal effects.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The library division holds several books formerly belonging to Milne and his wife Dorothy.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The Harry Ransom Center also has small collections of correspondence from <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Robin_Milne" title="Christopher Robin Milne">Christopher Robin Milne</a> and Milne's frequent illustrator <a href="/wiki/E._H._Shepard" title="E. H. Shepard">Ernest Shepard</a>. 
</p><p>The original manuscripts for <i>Winnie the Pooh</i> and <i>The House at Pooh Corner</i> are archived separately at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College Library</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_views">Religious views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._A._Milne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religious views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Milne did not speak out much on the subject of religion, although he used religious terms to explain his decision, while remaining a pacifist, to join the <a href="/wiki/Home_Guard_(United_Kingdom)" title="Home Guard (United Kingdom)">British Home Guard</a>:  "In fighting Hitler," he wrote, "we are truly fighting the Devil, the Anti-Christ ... Hitler was a crusader against God."<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His best known comment on the subject was recalled on his death:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Old Testament is responsible for more atheism, agnosticism, disbelief—call it what you will—than any book ever written; it has emptied more churches than all the counter-attractions of cinema, motor bicycle and golf course.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>He wrote in the poem "Explained":
</p>
<div class="poem">
<p>Elizabeth Ann<br />
Said to her Nan:<br />
"Please will you tell me how God began?<br />
<i>Somebody</i> must have made Him. So<br />
Who could it be, 'cos I want to know?"<br />
<sup id="cite_ref-Milne_2009_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milne_2009-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</div>
<p>He also wrote in the poem "Vespers":
</p>
<div class="poem">
<p>"Oh! Thank you, God, for a lovely day.<br />
And what was the other I had to say?<br />
I said "Bless Daddy," so what can it be?<br />
Oh! Now I remember it. God bless Me."<br />
<sup id="cite_ref-Milne_2009_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Milne_2009-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</div>
