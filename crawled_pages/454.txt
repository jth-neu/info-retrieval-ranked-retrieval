<p>

<b>Socialism in the United Kingdom</b> is thought to stretch back to the 19th century from roots arising in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. Notions of socialism in Great Britain have taken many different forms from the <a href="/wiki/Utopian" class="mw-redirect" title="Utopian">utopian</a> <a href="/wiki/Philanthropist" class="mw-redirect" title="Philanthropist">philanthropism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Owen" title="Robert Owen">Robert Owen</a> through to the <a href="/wiki/Reformism" title="Reformism">reformist</a> electoral project enshrined in the birth of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Reformation</a> occurred later in Britain than in most of mainland Europe. As in the rest of Europe, various liberal thinkers such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_More" title="Thomas More">Thomas More</a> became prominent, but another important current was the emergence of the radical <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritans</a> who wanted to reform both religion and the nation. The Puritans were oppressed by both the monarchy and by the <a href="/wiki/Established_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Established church">established church</a>. Eventually these pressures exploded in the violent social revolution known as the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, which many <a href="/wiki/Marxist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist">Marxists</a> see as the world's first successful bourgeois revolution.
</p><p>After the war several proto-socialist groups emerged. The most important of these groups were the <a href="/wiki/Levellers" title="Levellers">Levellers</a>, who advocated <a href="/wiki/Electoral_reform" title="Electoral reform">electoral reform</a>, universal <a href="/wiki/Trial_by_jury" class="mw-redirect" title="Trial by jury">trial by jury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Progressive_taxation" class="mw-redirect" title="Progressive taxation">progressive taxation</a> and the abolition of the monarchy and <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a> and of censorship. This was strongly opposed by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>'s government, who also persecuted the moderate <a href="/wiki/Reformist" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformist">reformist</a> group the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Monarchy_Men" class="mw-redirect" title="Fifth Monarchy Men">Fifth Monarchy Men</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Radicalism_(historical)" title="Radicalism (historical)">radical</a> utopian group the <a href="/wiki/Diggers" title="Diggers">Diggers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_19th_century">The 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Industrial_Revolution_and_Robert_Owen">The Industrial Revolution and Robert Owen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Industrial Revolution and Robert Owen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, the transition from a farming economy to an industrial one, began in the UK over 30 years before the rest of the world.
Textile mills and coal mines sprang up across the whole country and peasants were taken from the fields to work down the mines, or into the "Dark, Satanic Mills", the chimneys of which blacked the sky over <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> and West <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>. Appalling conditions for workers, combined with support for the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> turned some intellectuals to socialism.
</p><p>The pioneering work of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Owen" title="Robert Owen">Robert Owen</a>, a Welsh radical, at <a href="/wiki/New_Lanark" title="New Lanark">New Lanark</a> in Scotland, is sometimes credited as being the birth of British Socialism. He stopped employing Children under the age of 10, and instead arranged for their education, and improved the working and living conditions of all his workers. He also lobbied <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a> over child labour, and helped to create the <a href="/wiki/Co-operative" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-operative">co-operative</a> movement, before attempting to create a utopian community at <a href="/wiki/New_Harmony,_Indiana" title="New Harmony, Indiana">New Harmony</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_Unions">Trade Unions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trade Unions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Trade_unions_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Trade unions in the United Kingdom">Trade unions in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>The trade union movement in Britain gradually developed from the Medieval <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guild</a> system. Unions were subject to often severe repression until 1824, but were already widespread in cities such as London. Workplace militancy had also manifested itself as <a href="/wiki/Luddism" class="mw-redirect" title="Luddism">Luddism</a> and had been prominent in struggles such as the <a href="/wiki/Radical_War" title="Radical War">Radical War</a> (or Scottish Insurrection) in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 1820, when 60,000 workers went on a <a href="/wiki/General_strike" title="General strike">general strike</a>, which was soon crushed.
</p><p>From 1830 on, attempts were made to set up national <a href="/wiki/General_union" title="General union">general unions</a>, most notably Robert Owen's <a href="/wiki/Grand_National_Consolidated_Trades_Union" title="Grand National Consolidated Trades Union">Grand National Consolidated Trades Union</a> in 1834, which attracted a range of socialists from <a href="/wiki/Owenism" title="Owenism">Owenites</a> to revolutionaries. It played a part in the protests after the <a href="/wiki/Tolpuddle_Martyrs" title="Tolpuddle Martyrs">Tolpuddle Martyrs</a>' case, but soon collapsed.
</p><p>Militants turned to <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">Chartism</a>, the aims of which were supported by most socialists, although none appear to have played leading roles.
</p><p>More permanent trade unions were established from the 1850s, better resourced but often less radical. The <a href="/wiki/London_Trades_Council" title="London Trades Council">London Trades Council</a> was founded in 1860, and the <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Outrages" title="Sheffield Outrages">Sheffield Outrages</a> spurred the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Trades_Union_Congress" title="Trades Union Congress">Trades Union Congress</a> in 1868. Union membership grew as unskilled and women workers were unionised, and socialists such as <a href="/wiki/Tom_Mann" title="Tom Mann">Tom Mann</a> played an increasingly prominent role.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_socialism">Christian socialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Christian socialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The rise of <a href="/wiki/Non-Conformist" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-Conformist">Non-Conformist</a> religions, in particular <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a>, played a large role in the development of trade unions and of British socialism. The influence of the radical chapels was strongly felt among some industrial workers, especially miners and those in the north of England and Wales.
</p><p>The first group calling itself <a href="/wiki/Christian_Socialists" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Socialists">Christian Socialists</a> formed in 1848 under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Denison_Maurice" title="Frederick Denison Maurice">Frederick Denison Maurice</a>. Its membership mainly consisted of Chartists (see below). The group became dormant after only six years, but there was a considerable revival of Christian socialism in the 1880s, and a number of groups sprang up. Ultimately, Christian socialists dominated the leadership of the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Labour_Party" title="Independent Labour Party">Independent Labour Party</a>, including <a href="/wiki/James_Keir_Hardie" class="mw-redirect" title="James Keir Hardie">James Keir Hardie</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Chartist_movement">The Chartist movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Chartist movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chartist_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartist movement">Chartist movement</a> of the 1830s and 1840s was the first mass revolutionary movement of the British working-class. Mass meetings and demonstrations involving millions of proletariat and petty-bourgeois were held throughout the country for years.
</p><p>The Chartists published several <a href="/wiki/Petition" title="Petition">petitions</a> to the British Parliament (ranging from 1,280,000 to 3,000,000 signatures), the most famous of which was called the <a href="/wiki/Chartism" title="Chartism">People's Charter</a> (hence their name) in 1842, which demanded:
</p>
<ol><li>Universal suffrage for men.</li>
<li>The secret ballot.</li>
<li>Removal of property qualifications for members of parliament.</li>
<li>Salaries for members of parliament.</li>
<li>Electoral districts representing equal numbers of people.</li>
<li>Annually elected parliaments.</li></ol>
<p>The government subsequently subjected the Chartists to brutal reprisals and arrested their leaders. The remaining party then split as a result of a divide in tactics: the <i>Moral Force Party</i> believed in bureaucratic reformism, while the <i>Physical Force Party</i> believed in workers' reformism (through strikes, etc.).
</p><p>The Chartist movement's reformist goals, although not immediately and directly attained, were gradually achieved. In the same year as the People's Charter was created, the <a href="/wiki/British_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="British Parliament">British Parliament</a> instead responded by passing the 1842 Mining Act. Carefully valving the steam of the working-class movement, Parliament reduced the working day to ten hours in 1847.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marx_and_early_Marxism">Marx and early Marxism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Marx and early Marxism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Karl Marx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Friedrich Engels</a> worked in England, and they influenced small émigré groups including the <a href="/wiki/Communist_League" title="Communist League">Communist League</a>. Engels' <i>Condition of the Working Class in England</i><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> became a popular expose of conditions for workers, but initially Marxism had little impact among Britain's working class.
</p><p>The first nominally Marxist organisation was the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Federation" title="Social Democratic Federation">Social Democratic Federation</a>, founded in 1882. Engels refused to support the organisation, although Marx's daughter <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Marx" title="Eleanor Marx">Eleanor</a> joined.
</p><p>The party soon split, with the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_League_(UK,_1885)" title="Socialist League (UK, 1885)">Socialist League</a> of <a href="/wiki/William_Morris" title="William Morris">William Morris</a> becoming divided between anarchists and Marxists such as Morris and Eleanor Marx. A much later split produced the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Socialist Party of Great Britain">Socialist Party of Great Britain</a>, Britain's oldest existing socialist party, and the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Labour_Party_(UK,_1903)" title="Socialist Labour Party (UK, 1903)">Socialist Labour Party</a>.
</p><p>Although Marxism had some impact in Britain, it was far less than in many other European countries, with philosophers such as <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Stuart_Mill" title="John Stuart Mill">John Stuart Mill</a> having much greater influence. Some non-Marxists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2013)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> theorise that this was because Britain was amongst the most democratic countries of Europe of the period, the ballot box provided an instrument for change, so a parliamentary, reformist socialism seemed a more promising route than elsewhere.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lib-Labs_and_the_ILP">Lib-Labs and the ILP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lib-Labs and the ILP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Reform_League" title="Reform League">Reform League</a>, which was founded in 1865 to press for universal male suffrage and vote by secret ballot, qualified its demand for suffrage with the phrase "registered and residential" shortly before the passing of the <a href="/wiki/1867_Reform_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="1867 Reform Act">1867 Reform Act</a>. This qualifier excluded a great number of British labourers, casual workers, and unemployed. The change in policy has been attributed to donations received by the League from <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> politicians in 1866 and 1867.<sup id="cite_ref-frfi_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frfi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> At the time, Marx wrote that he and Engels had been "betrayed [...] in the Reform League where, against our wishes, [Cremer and Oder] have made compromises with the bourgeoisie".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, a great deal of collaboration came to exist between the Liberal Party and the leaders of the labour movement, though Marx saw these as effective bribes by the bourgeoisie and the government.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1867_Reform_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="1867 Reform Act">1867 Reform Act</a> passed and enfranchised roughly three million people, around half of whom were working class. This was extended to five million by the <a href="/wiki/Representation_of_the_People_Act_1884" title="Representation of the People Act 1884">Representation of the People Act 1884</a>, which extended the householder's franchise.<sup id="cite_ref-frfi_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frfi-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> was worried about the prospect of a socialist party taking the bulk of the working-class vote, while their great rivals the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> initiated occasional intrigues to encourage socialist candidates to stand against the Liberals.
</p><p>In 1874, the Liberals agreed not to put candidates against <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burt" title="Thomas Burt">Thomas Burt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Macdonald_(Lib-Lab_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander Macdonald (Lib-Lab politician)">Alexander Macdonald</a>, two miners' leaders who were standing for <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>. Both were elected and became known as Liberal-Labour or <a href="/wiki/Lib-Lab" class="mw-redirect" title="Lib-Lab">Lib-Labs</a> for short. Other miner's leaders entered Parliament via the same route.
</p><p>In 1888, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cunninghame-Graham" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Cunninghame-Graham">Robert Cunninghame-Graham</a> the MP for <a href="/wiki/Lanarkshire_North-West" class="mw-redirect" title="Lanarkshire North-West">Lanarkshire North-West</a> since the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1886" title="United Kingdom general election, 1886">1886 general election</a> left the Liberal Party and formed his own, independent, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Labour_Party_(1888)" title="Scottish Labour Party (1888)">Scottish Labour Party</a>, becoming the first socialist MP in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1892" title="United Kingdom general election, 1892">1892 general election</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keir_Hardie" title="Keir Hardie">Keir Hardie</a>, another Liberal politician who had joined Cunninghame-Graham in the Scottish Labour Party, was elected as an Independent Labour MP, and this gave him the spur to found a UK-wide <a href="/wiki/Independent_Labour_Party" title="Independent Labour Party">Independent Labour Party</a> in 1893.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_20th_century">The 20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BritishLeft.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/BritishLeft.png/600px-BritishLeft.png" width="600" height="367" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/BritishLeft.png/900px-BritishLeft.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/31/BritishLeft.png 2x" data-file-width="1057" data-file-height="646" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BritishLeft.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Timeline of parties in the broad socialist movement</div></div></div>
<p>The early twentieth century saw a number of socialist groups and movements in Britain. As well as the Independent Labour Party and the Social Democratic Federation, there was a mass movement around <a href="/wiki/Robert_Blatchford" title="Robert Blatchford">Robert Blatchford</a>'s newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Clarion" title="The Clarion">The Clarion</a></i> from the 1890s to the 1930s; the more intellectual gradualist <a href="/wiki/Fabian_Society" title="Fabian Society">Fabian Society</a>; and more radical groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Labour_Party_(1903-1980)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Labour Party (1903-1980)">Socialist Labour Party</a>. However, the movement was increasingly dominated by the formation of the <a href="/wiki/British_Labour_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="British Labour Party">British Labour Party</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_birth_of_the_Labour_Party">The birth of the Labour Party</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The birth of the Labour Party">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_British_Labour_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the British Labour Party">History of the British Labour Party</a></div>
<p>In 1900, representatives of various trade unions and of the Independent Labour Party, Fabian Society and Social Democratic Federation agreed to form a Labour Party backed by the unions and with its own whips. The <a href="/wiki/British_Labour_Party#Labour_Representation_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="British Labour Party">Labour Representation Committee</a> was founded with Keir Hardie as its leader. At the 1900 general election, the LRC won only two seats, and the SDF disaffiliated, but more unions signed up.
</p><p>The LRC affiliated to the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_International" title="Socialist International">Socialist International</a> and in 1906 changed its name to <a href="/wiki/The_Labour_Party_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Labour Party (UK)">The Labour Party</a>. It formed <a href="/wiki/Gladstone-MacDonald_pact" class="mw-redirect" title="Gladstone-MacDonald pact">an electoral pact</a> with the Liberals, intending to cause maximum damage to the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Unionist</a> government at the forthcoming election. This was successful, and in the process, 29 Labour MPs were elected to the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Women.27s_suffrage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women's_suffrage">Women's suffrage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Women&#039;s suffrage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The campaign for women's suffrage in Britain began in the mid-nineteenth century, with many early campaigners including Eleanor Marx being socialists, but many established socialists, including Robert Blatchford and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Bax" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernest Bax">Ernest Bax</a> opposed or ignored the movement. By the early twentieth century, the campaign had become more militant, but some of its leaders were reluctant to involve working class women in it. <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Pankhurst" title="Sylvia Pankhurst">Sylvia Pankhurst</a> campaigned for enfranchisement among women in the East End of London and eventually built up the <a href="/wiki/Workers_Socialist_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Workers Socialist Federation">Workers Socialist Federation</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syndicalism_and_World_War_I">Syndicalism and World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Syndicalism and World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supporters of <a href="/wiki/Daniel_De_Leon" title="Daniel De Leon">Daniel De Leon</a> in the Social Democratic Federation chiefly in Scotland split to form the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Labour_Party_(1903-1980)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Labour Party (1903-1980)">Socialist Labour Party</a>. Their fellow <a href="/wiki/Impossibilism" title="Impossibilism">impossibilists</a> in London split from the SDF the following year to form the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Socialist Party of Great Britain">Socialist Party of Great Britain</a> (SPGB, still in existence). The remainder of the SDF attempted to form a broader Marxist party, the <a href="/wiki/British_Socialist_Party" title="British Socialist Party">British Socialist Party</a>. The SLP and BSP parties came to influence the <a href="/wiki/Shop_steward" title="Shop steward">shop steward</a> movement, which became particularly prominent in what became known as <a href="/wiki/Red_Clydeside" title="Red Clydeside">Red Clydeside</a>. Socialists such as <a href="/wiki/John_Maclean_(Scottish_socialist)" title="John Maclean (Scottish socialist)">John Maclean</a> led strikes and demonstrations for better working conditions and a forty-hour working week.
</p><p>This activity took place against the background of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. The Labour Party, like almost all the Socialist International, enthusiastically supported their country's leadership in the war, as did the leadership of the British Socialist Party. This split the BSP, and a new anti-war leadership emerging.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bolshevism_and_the_CPGB">Bolshevism and the CPGB</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Bolshevism and the CPGB">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The shop steward movement worried many right-wingers, who believed that socialists were fomenting a <a href="/wiki/Bolshevik" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolshevik">Bolshevik</a> revolution in Britain. A <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Communist Party of Great Britain">Communist Party of Great Britain</a> (CPGB) was founded, but it attracted only existing left-wing militants, with the British Socialist Party and Workers Socialist Federation joining many Socialist Labour Party activists in it.
</p><p>The CPGB soon became known for its loyalty to the line of the <a href="/wiki/Comintern" class="mw-redirect" title="Comintern">Comintern</a>, and proposed the motion to expel <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Leon Trotsky</a> from the international. Under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Harry_Pollitt" title="Harry Pollitt">Harry Pollitt</a>, it finally gained its first MP, and began to expel <a href="/wiki/Trotskyist" class="mw-redirect" title="Trotskyist">Trotskyists</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labour_and_the_General_Strike">Labour and the General Strike</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Labour and the General Strike">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Labour Party continued to grow as more unions affiliated and more Labour MPs were elected. In 1918, a new constitution was agreed, which laid out several aims of the party. These included <a href="/wiki/Clause_IV" title="Clause IV">Clause IV</a>, calling for "common ownership" of key industry. With their success at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1923" title="United Kingdom general election, 1923">1923 general election</a>, Labour were able to form their first <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority government</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Ramsay_MacDonald" title="Ramsay MacDonald">Ramsay MacDonald</a>. This government was undermined by the infamous <a href="/wiki/Zinoviev_Letter" class="mw-redirect" title="Zinoviev Letter">Zinoviev Letter</a>, which was used as evidence of Labour's links with the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. It was later revealed to be a forgery.
</p><p>In 1926, British miners went on strike over their appalling working conditions. The situation soon escalated into the <a href="/wiki/UK_General_Strike_1926" class="mw-redirect" title="UK General Strike 1926">General Strike</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Trade_Union_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade Union Congress">Trade Union Congress</a>, ostensibly worried about reports of starvation in the <a href="/wiki/Pit_village" title="Pit village">pit villages</a>, called the strike off. The miners tried to continue alone, but without TUC support had eventually to give in.
</p><p>Labour formed a minority government in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1929" title="United Kingdom general election, 1929">1929</a> again under MacDonald, but following the <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_1929" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Crash 1929">Stock Market Crash of 1929</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Great Depression in the United Kingdom">Great Depression</a> engulfed the country. The government split over its response to the crisis. MacDonald and a few supporters agreed to form a <a href="/wiki/UK_National_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK National Government">National Government</a> with the Liberals and the Conservatives. The majority of the Labour Party regarded this as a betrayal and expelled them, whereupon they founded <a href="/wiki/National_Labour_Organisation" title="National Labour Organisation">National Labour</a>.
</p><p>The Great Depression devastated the industrial areas of Northern England, Wales and Central Scotland, and the <a href="/wiki/Jarrow_March" title="Jarrow March">Jarrow March</a> of unemployed workers from the North East to London to demand jobs defined the period.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_socialism">Ethical socialism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ethical socialism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ethical_socialism" title="Ethical socialism">Ethical socialism</a> is a variant of <a href="/wiki/Liberal_socialism" title="Liberal socialism">liberal socialism</a> developed by British socialists.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ref52_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref52-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It became an important ideology within the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tansey_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tansey-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Ethical socialism was founded in the 1920s by <a href="/wiki/R._H._Tawney" title="R. H. Tawney">R. H. Tawney</a>, a British <a href="/wiki/Christian_socialism" title="Christian socialism">Christian socialist</a>, and its ideals were connected to Christian socialist, <a href="/wiki/Fabian_Society" title="Fabian Society">Fabian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guild_socialism" title="Guild socialism">guild socialist</a> ideals.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Ethical socialism has been publicly supported by British Prime Ministers <a href="/wiki/Ramsay_MacDonald" title="Ramsay MacDonald">Ramsay MacDonald</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tansey_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tansey-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mosley">Mosley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Mosley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oswald_Mosley" title="Oswald Mosley">Sir Oswald Mosley</a> had been a rising star in the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> but left over the government's policy of repression in Ireland and eventually joined Labour. Mosley rose just as quickly on the Labour benches and was a government minister charged with dealing with unemployment during the Great Depression. Mosley proposed the "Mosley Memorandum" which suggested the formation of Cabinet committees to deal with specific policy issues, rationalisation and mechanisation in industry to make it more competitive, and a programme of public works. Although the memorandum prefigured <a href="/wiki/Keynesian" class="mw-redirect" title="Keynesian">Keynesian</a> policies that would be accepted by later governments, it was too radical a set of proposals for 1930 and both the Labour government and the party rejected it. In response, Mosley left Labour in 1931 to found the <a href="/wiki/New_Party_(UK)" title="New Party (UK)">New Party</a>, taking four other Labour MPs with him. The New Party failed to win any seats in 1932 and Mosley subsequently came to support fascism, merging his party with several far-right groups to form the <a href="/wiki/British_Union_of_Fascists" title="British Union of Fascists">British Union of Fascists</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Spanish_Civil_War_and_World_War_II">The Spanish Civil War and World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The Spanish Civil War and World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG/220px-2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG" width="220" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG/330px-2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG/440px-2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2417" data-file-height="2280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2015-03-07_-_ThinkTank_-_Sasha_Taylor_-_11.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>WWII badge for the "Birmingham Socialist <a href="/wiki/Air_Raid_Precautions" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Raid Precautions">ARP</a> Canteen Fund"</div></div></div>
<p>The Independent Labour Party disaffiliated from the Labour Party in 1932, in protest at an erosion of their MPs' independence. For a time, they became a significant left-of-Labour force.
</p><p>In 1936, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> was viewed by many socialists as a contest against the rise of fascism which it was vital to win. Many CPGB and Independent Labour Party members went to fight for the Republic and with the Stalinist led International Brigades and the Workers' Party of Marxist Unification (<a href="/wiki/POUM" title="POUM">POUM</a>) <a href="/wiki/Anti-fascist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-fascist">anti-fascist</a> forces, including <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">George Orwell</a> who wrote about his experiences in <i><a href="/wiki/Homage_to_Catalonia" title="Homage to Catalonia">Homage to Catalonia</a></i>.
</p><p>The Labour Party leadership always supported British involvement in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and they joined a <a href="/wiki/National_Government_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Government (United Kingdom)">national government</a> with the Conservative Party and the Liberals, and agreed a non-contest pact in elections. The CPGB at first supported the war, but after <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a> signed a treaty with <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, opposed it. After the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">fascist</a> <a href="/wiki/Operation_Barbarossa" title="Operation Barbarossa">invasion of the Soviet Union</a>, they again supported the war, joined the non-contest pact, and did all in their power to prevent strikes. But strikes did occur, and they were supported by the anti-war Independent Labour Party and the newly formed Trotskyist <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Communist_Party_(1944-1949)" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolutionary Communist Party (1944-1949)">Revolutionary Communist Party</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1945_landslide_Labour_victory">The 1945 landslide Labour victory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: The 1945 landslide Labour victory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To widespread surprise, the Labour Party led by wartime Deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a> won a landslide victory over popular war leader <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1945" title="United Kingdom general election, 1945">1945 general election</a>, and implemented their <a href="/wiki/Social_democratic" class="mw-redirect" title="Social democratic">social democratic</a> programme. They established the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nationalised" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalised">nationalised</a> some industries (for example, coal mining), and created a <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>.
</p><p>The CPGB also grew on the back of Stalinist successes in Eastern Europe and China, and recorded their best-ever result, with two MPs elected (one in London and another in <a href="/wiki/Fife" title="Fife">Fife</a>). The Trotskyite Revolutionary Communist Party collapsed.
</p><p>Labour lost power in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1951" title="United Kingdom general election, 1951">1951</a> (despite polling 200,000 more votes than the Conservatives), and after Clement Attlee retired as party leader in 1955, he was succeeded by the figurehead of the "right-establishment" <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Gaitskell" title="Hugh Gaitskell">Hugh Gaitskell</a>, against <a href="/wiki/Aneurin_Bevan" title="Aneurin Bevan">Aneurin Bevan</a>.
</p><p>Although there were some disputes between the <a href="/wiki/Bevanites" class="mw-redirect" title="Bevanites">Bevanites</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gaitskellites" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaitskellites">Gaitskellites</a>, these disputes were more about personality than ideology, and the rift was healed when <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>, a Bevanite, was elected as Leader of the LabourParty after Gaitskell's sudden death.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1960s_and_1970s">The 1960s and 1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The 1960s and 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Nuclear_Disarmament" title="Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament">Campaign for Nuclear Disarmament</a> briefly gained leverage over Labour Party policy at the beginning of the decade, but soon went into a long eclipse. The <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, given lukewarm support by <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>, radicalised a new generation. Significant anti-war protests were organised. Trotskyist groups like the <a href="/wiki/International_Marxist_Group" title="International Marxist Group">International Marxist Group</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_Solidarity_Campaign" title="Vietnam Solidarity Campaign">Vietnam Solidarity Campaign</a> came to prominence, particularly due to high-profile members like the IMG's <a href="/wiki/Tariq_Ali" title="Tariq Ali">Tariq Ali</a>.
</p><p>After the Soviet Union's invasion of <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> in 1968, the CPGB became to divide between Stalinists and <a href="/wiki/Eurocommunist" class="mw-redirect" title="Eurocommunist">Eurocommunists</a>. The party suffered a series of splits. Various <a href="/wiki/Maoist" class="mw-redirect" title="Maoist">Maoist</a> inclined elements left, the most significant forming the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Britain_(Marxist-Leninist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Party of Britain (Marxist-Leninist)">Communist Party of Britain (Marxist-Leninist)</a>. Later in 1977, other traditionalist pro-Russian elements left to form the <a href="/wiki/New_Communist_Party_of_Britain" title="New Communist Party of Britain">New Communist Party</a>.
</p><p>In 1969, Wilson's Labour Government introduced <i><a href="/wiki/In_Place_of_Strife" title="In Place of Strife">In Place of Strife</a></i>, a white paper designed to circumvent strikes by imposing compulsory arbitration. Opposed by many trade unionists, including Home Secretary <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a>; it was soon withdrawn. Later legislation passed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a>'s Conservative government was successfully resisted as union militants, many close to the CPGB, led the successful <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1974)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1974)">1974 UK miners' strike</a>. More ominously for the left, the unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Grunwick_dispute" title="Grunwick dispute">Grunwick dispute</a>, and the 1978–79 <a href="/wiki/Winter_of_Discontent" title="Winter of Discontent">Winter of Discontent</a> were also blamed on them. The Labour leadership's failure to work with trade unions, or for their opponents an inability to keep them under control, resulted in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">election in 1979</a> of an economically liberal Conservative government, led by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, which finally ended the post-war political consensus.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1980s">The 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: The 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the 1979 Labour defeat, <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">Jim Callaghan</a> tried in vain to keep the left-wing of the party (in which <a href="/wiki/Tony_Benn" title="Tony Benn">Tony Benn</a> was prominent) and the right-wing (in which <a href="/wiki/Roy_Jenkins" title="Roy Jenkins">Roy Jenkins</a> was prominent) together. In 1980, the Labour Party conference was dominated by factional disputes and what Callaghan regarded as <a href="/wiki/Bennite" class="mw-redirect" title="Bennite">Bennite</a> motions. Callaghan resigned as party leader late that year and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Foot" title="Michael Foot">Michael Foot</a>, a left-winger who by then had distanced himself from Benn but failed to transmit this to the media or the voters. The following year, <a href="/wiki/Denis_Healey" title="Denis Healey">Denis Healey</a> only narrowly won the deputy leadership in a contest with Benn.
</p><p>In 1981, thirty MPs on the right-wing of the Labour Party defected to found the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Social Democratic Party</a>, which formed an <a href="/wiki/SDP-Liberal_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="SDP-Liberal Alliance">alliance</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> and opinion polls briefly saw the new alliance appear capable of winning a general election.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1983" title="United Kingdom general election, 1983">1983 general election</a>, Thatcher benefited from increased popularity, an after effect of the successful <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>, and a Labour manifesto which Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Kaufman" title="Gerald Kaufman">Gerald Kaufman</a> described as "the longest suicide note in history". Labour suffered their worst election defeat since 1918 with eight and a half million votes, over three million votes down on the previous general election. Many former Labour voters had voted for the <a href="/wiki/SDP-Liberal_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="SDP-Liberal Alliance">SDP-Liberal Alliance</a> instead. The Alliance came close to Labour in terms of votes, but had only a fraction of its seats due to the limitations of the <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post" class="mw-redirect" title="First-past-the-post">first-past-the-post</a> system.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the 1983 general election, <a href="/wiki/Neil_Kinnock" title="Neil Kinnock">Neil Kinnock</a>, long associated with the left-wing of the Labour Party, became the new leader. By that point in time, the Labour Party was factionalised between the right, including Healey and deputy leader <a href="/wiki/Roy_Hattersley" title="Roy Hattersley">Roy Hattersley</a>, a "<a href="/wiki/Soft_left" title="Soft left">soft left</a>" associated with the <a href="/wiki/Tribune_(magazine)" title="Tribune (magazine)">Tribune</a> group, and a "<a href="/wiki/Hard_left" title="Hard left">hard left</a>" associated with Benn and the new <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Campaign_Group" title="Socialist Campaign Group">Campaign Group</a>.
</p><p>The Trotskyist <a href="/wiki/Militant_tendency" class="mw-redirect" title="Militant tendency">Militant tendency</a>, using <a href="/wiki/Entryism" title="Entryism">entryist</a> tactics in the Labour Party, had gradually increased their profile. By 1982, they controlled <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_City_Council" title="Liverpool City Council">Liverpool City Council</a>, and had a presence in many Constituency Labour Parties. The Labour NEC began to expel Militant members, beginning with their newspaper's "editorial board", in effect their Central Committee. A revival in <a href="/wiki/Municipal_socialism" title="Municipal socialism">municipal socialism</a> seemed, for a time, a solution to Conservative hegemony for many on the left. The <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Ken_Livingstone" title="Ken Livingstone">Ken Livingstone</a>, gained the most attention, seeming genuinely innovative to its support base, but the GLC was abolished by the Conservatives in 1986.
</p><p>The defining event of the 1980s for British socialists was the <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1984-1985)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1984-1985)">1984–5 miners' strike</a>. Miners in the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Mineworkers_(Great_Britain)" title="National Union of Mineworkers (Great Britain)">National Union of Mineworkers</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Scargill" title="Arthur Scargill">Arthur Scargill</a>, struck against the closure of collieries. Despite support in the coalfields, including many miners' wives in <a href="/wiki/Women_Against_Pit_Closures" title="Women Against Pit Closures">Women Against Pit Closures</a>, the strike was eventually lost owing to a union split, amongst other reasons. The Conservatives had already begun to <a href="/wiki/Privatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Privatisation">privatise</a> other state industries. Labour lost the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1987" title="United Kingdom general election, 1987">1987 general election</a> by a wide margin, although it did manage to reduce the Conservative majority significantly.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socialism_and_nationalism">Socialism and nationalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Socialism and nationalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_nationalism" title="Welsh nationalism">Welsh nationalism</a> have been the concern of many socialists. Having been raised in the nineteenth century by Liberals also calling for <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Home Rule">Irish Home Rule</a>, Scottish Home Rule became the official policy of the ILP, and of the Labour Party until 1958. <a href="/wiki/John_Maclean_(Scottish_socialist)" title="John Maclean (Scottish socialist)">John Maclean</a> campaigned for a separate Communist Party in Scotland in the 1920s, and when the CPGB refused to support Scottish independence, he formed the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Workers_Republican_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Workers Republican Party">Scottish Workers Republican Party</a>. The poet <a href="/wiki/Hugh_MacDiarmid" title="Hugh MacDiarmid">Hugh MacDiarmid</a>, a Communist, was also an early member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Party_of_Scotland" title="National Party of Scotland">National Party of Scotland</a>. The CPGB eventually changed their position in the 1940s.
</p><p>The early nationalist parties had little connection with socialism, but by the 1980s they had become increasingly identified with the left, and in the 1990s <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> declared itself to be a socialist party.
</p><p>Following the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh Assembly">Welsh Assembly</a>, both the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> and Plaid have been challenged by socialists in recent years. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Socialist_Party" title="Scottish Socialist Party">Scottish Socialist Party</a>, who also support <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a> as an immediate goal, has had recent electoral success; it won six <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Member of the Scottish Parliament">MSPs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_general_election,_2003" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Parliament general election, 2003">Scottish Parliament general election, 2003</a>. <a href="/wiki/Forward_Wales" title="Forward Wales">Forward Wales</a>, with a less militant programme, aimed to replicate their success.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">Irish republicanism</a> came to be supported by socialists in Britain. Labour's election manifestos for 1983, 1987 and 1992 included a commitment to <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">Irish unification</a> by consent.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_1990s">The 1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: The 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989 in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and 1990 for the rest of the UK, the Conservatives introduced the deeply unpopular <a href="/wiki/Tax_per_head" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax per head">poll tax</a>. For the first time in the decade, socialists were able to organise effective opposition, culminating in the "<a href="/wiki/Poll_tax_riot" class="mw-redirect" title="Poll tax riot">Poll tax riot</a>" on 31 March 1990. Margaret Thatcher's own party compelled her to step down on 22 November that year, and she was replaced by <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a>, who abolished the charge in 1991.
</p><p>The CPGB dissolved itself in 1991, although their former newspaper, the <i><a href="/wiki/Morning_Star_(British_newspaper)" title="Morning Star (British newspaper)">Morning Star</a></i>, continues to be published and follows the programme of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Britain" title="Communist Party of Britain">Communist Party of Britain</a> which was founded in 1988 after an internal crisis in the CPGB led to a split. The Eurocommunists, who had controlled the party's magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Marxism_Today" title="Marxism Today">Marxism Today</a></i>, formed the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Left_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Left (United Kingdom)">Democratic Left</a>
</p><p>In the run-up to the <a href="/wiki/1992_UK_general_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1992 UK general election">1992 general election</a>, polling showed that there might be a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a>, but possibly a small Labour majority – the party's lead on the opinion polls had shrunk and some polls had even seen the Tories creep ahead in spite of the deepening recession. In the event, the Conservatives led by <a href="/wiki/John_Major" title="John Major">John Major</a>; won a fourth consecutive election with a majority of 21 seats. This has been attributed to both the Labour Party's premature triumphalism (in particular at the <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Rally" title="Sheffield Rally">Sheffield Rally</a>) and the Tories' "Tax Bombshell" advertising campaign, which highlighted the increased taxes that a Labour government would impose. This general election defeat was shortly followed by Kinnock's resignation after nearly a decade as leader. And, as had happened in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1959" title="United Kingdom general election, 1959">1959 general election defeat</a>, there was widespread public and media doubt as to whether a Labour government could be elected again – without an overhaul of the party's policies, it seemed too difficult to imagine the party being able to win a general election in any situation if it had been unable to do so in the event of a recession and rising unemployment.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the brief stewardship of <a href="/wiki/John_Smith_(Labour_Party_leader)" title="John Smith (Labour Party leader)">John Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> was elected leader following Smith's sudden death from a heart attack in May 1994. He immediately decided to amend Clause IV, dropping Labour's commitment to public ownership of key industries and utilities, along with other socialist policies.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many members of the party were unhappy with the proposed changes and several unions considered using their block vote to kill the motion, but in the end their leaderships backed down and settled for a new clause declaring the Labour Party a "democratic socialist party". However, Labour had been ascendant in the opinion polls since the <a href="/wiki/Black_Wednesday" title="Black Wednesday">Black Wednesday</a> economic fiasco a few months after the 1992 general election, and the increased lead of the polls under Blair's leadership remained strong in spite of the revolt, and the fact that the economy was growing again and unemployment was falling under Major's Conservative government. Labour's popularity was also helped by the fact that the Conservative government was now divided over Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several party members, such as <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Scargill" title="Arthur Scargill">Arthur Scargill</a>, regarded this as a betrayal of Labour's ideology and left the Labour Party. Scargill formed the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Socialist Labour Party (UK)">Socialist Labour Party</a> (SLP) which initially attracted some support, much of which transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Alliance_(England)" title="Socialist Alliance (England)">Socialist Alliance</a> on its formation, but the SA has since been wound up and the SLP has become marginalised.
</p><p>The Scottish Socialist Party have proven much more successful, while Ken Livingstone became the <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a>, standing against an official Labour Party candidate. Livingstone was re-admitted into the Labour Party in time for his re-election in 2004.
</p><p>Under Blair, Labour launched a PR campaign to rebrand as <i><a href="/wiki/New_Labour" title="New Labour">New Labour</a></i>. The party also introduced women-only shortlists in certain seats and central vetting of Parliamentary candidates to ensure that its candidates were seen as on-message. Labour won the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">1997 general election</a> with a landslide majority of 179 seats; their best result to date.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_21st_century">The 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: The 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_Left" title="British Left">British Left</a></div>
<p>The international <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalisation_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-globalisation movement">anti-globalisation movement</a>, while difficult to define, has become a focus for other socialists in the twenty-first century, and many see a reflection of it in the opposition of large sections of the population to the <a href="/wiki/2003_Iraq_War" class="mw-redirect" title="2003 Iraq War">2003 Iraq War</a>.
</p><p>Several minor socialist parties merged in 2003 to form the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_for_Green_Socialism" title="Alliance for Green Socialism">Alliance for Green Socialism</a> which is a socialist party that campaigns on a wide variety of policies including; economic, environmental and social.
</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/George_Galloway" title="George Galloway">George Galloway</a>'s expulsion from the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> in October 2003 following his controversial statements about the war in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> he became involved in <a href="/wiki/Respect_Party" title="Respect Party">Respect – The Unity Coalition</a> (later renamed the Respect Party) in an alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Workers_Party_(Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Workers Party (Britain)">Socialist Workers Party</a> and leading figures from the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_Association_of_Britain" title="Muslim Association of Britain">Muslim Association of Britain</a>. Galloway, who stood as a candidate for Respect, was elected as the Member of Parliament for <a href="/wiki/Bethnal_Green_and_Bow_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Bethnal Green and Bow (UK Parliament constituency)">Bethnal Green and Bow</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 general election</a>, where he defeated the sitting Labour MP; <a href="/wiki/Oona_King" title="Oona King">Oona King</a>. Galloway strongly opposed the 2003 invasion of Iraq while King strongly supported it.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The association with the Socialist Workers Party (SWP) ended in 2007. Galloway did not seek re-election for Bethnal Green and Bow at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a>, but stood at the <a href="/wiki/Poplar_and_Limehouse_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Poplar and Limehouse (UK Parliament constituency)">Poplar and Limehouse</a> constituency instead. However, he finished in third place, behind <a href="/wiki/Tim_Archer" title="Tim Archer">Tim Archer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Fitzpatrick_(politician)" title="Jim Fitzpatrick (politician)">Jim Fitzpatrick</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>. After a two-year absence from Parliament, Galloway returned to the House of Commons after winning the <a href="/wiki/Bradford_West_by-election,_2012" title="Bradford West by-election, 2012">Bradford West by-election, 2012</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Respect though has suffered from the resignation of leading members over the years,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and Galloway lost his seat to <a href="/wiki/Naz_Shah" title="Naz Shah">Naz Shah</a> of the Labour Party at the <a href="/wiki/2015_UK_general_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2015 UK general election">2015 general election</a>.
</p><p>In 2013, director <a href="/wiki/Ken_Loach" title="Ken Loach">Ken Loach</a> made an appeal seeking to create a United Left Party in light of the perceived successes seen by <a href="/wiki/Coalition_of_the_Radical_Left" class="mw-redirect" title="Coalition of the Radical Left">Syriza</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, the perceived failures of previous Left of Labour projects such as Socialist Alliance and the perceived failings of Respect. The <a href="/wiki/Left_Unity_(UK)" title="Left Unity (UK)">Left Unity</a> political party was founded in November 2013 as a result of the appeal.
</p>
<h3><span id="United_Kingdom_general_election.2C_2010"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010">United Kingdom general election, 2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: United Kingdom general election, 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">United Kingdom general election, 2010</a></div>
<p>The Labour Party was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a>, after talks of forming a coalition agreement with the Liberal Democrats failed. During their thirteen years in government, Labour made few changes to the trade union reforms passed by the previous Conservative governments, and the only nationalisation which took place during that time were of several leading banks facing collapse in the <a href="/wiki/Late_2000s_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="Late 2000s recession">recession of 2008 and 2009</a> under the premiership of <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a>. The Conservatives returned to power with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Coalition" title="Coalition">coalition</a> government following a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a>; the first in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_February_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, February 1974">36 years</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other socialists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2013)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> place their hopes in a trade union revival, perhaps around the "<a href="/wiki/Awkward_Squad_(trade_unions)" class="mw-redirect" title="Awkward Squad (trade unions)">Awkward Squad</a>" of the more left-wing trade union leaders, many of whom have joined the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Representation_Committee_(2004)" title="Labour Representation Committee (2004)">Labour Representation Committee</a>. Others have turned to more community-based politics. Yet others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2013)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> believe they can reclaim the Labour Party.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Trade_Unionist_and_Socialist_Coalition" title="Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition">Trade Unionist and Socialist Coalition</a> (TUSC) was formed in January 2010 to contest the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 general election</a>. Founding supporters include <a href="/wiki/Bob_Crow" title="Bob Crow">Bob Crow</a>, general secretary of the Rail, Maritime and Transport workers union (RMT), Brian Caton, general secretary of the POA and Chris Baugh, assistant general secretary of the PCS. RMT and <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(England_and_Wales)" title="Socialist Party (England and Wales)">Socialist Party</a> executive members, including Bob Crow, form the core of the steering committee. The coalition includes the Socialist Workers Party, which will also stand candidates under it's banner,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> RESPECT<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and other trade unionists and socialist groups. This followed the <a href="/wiki/No2EU" title="No2EU">No2EU</a> coalition which fought the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (United Kingdom)">European elections in 2009</a> gaining the official backing of the RMT. The RMT declined to officially back the new TUSC coalition, but granted their branches the right to stand and fund local candidates as part of the coalition.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Scottish_independence_referendum.2C_2014"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014">Scottish independence referendum, 2014</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Scottish independence referendum, 2014">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum, 2014</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Socialist_Party" title="Scottish Socialist Party">Scottish Socialist Party</a> (SSP) has been actively campaigning for Scottish independence since the announcement of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum, 2014</a>. Its co-convenor, <a href="/wiki/Colin_Fox_(politician)" title="Colin Fox (politician)">Colin Fox</a>, sits on the Advisory Board of the <a href="/wiki/Yes_Scotland" title="Yes Scotland">Yes Scotland</a> campaign organisation. The party's support for Scottish independence is rooted in a belief that "the tearing of the blue out of the Union Jack and the dismantling of the 300-year-old British state would [be] a traumatic psychological blow for the forces of capitalism and conservatism in Britain, Europe and the USA", and that it would be "almost as potent in its symbolism as the unravelling of the Soviet Union at the start of the 1990s". Representatives of the party have also claimed that while the break-up of the United Kingdom would not result in "instant socialism", it would cause "a decisive shift in the balance of ideological and class forces".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The campaign for independence has also enjoyed support from a minority of trade unionists. In 2013, a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Communication_Workers_Union_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Communication Workers Union (UK)">Communications Workers Union</a> covering Edinburgh, Lothians, Fife, Falkirk, and Stirling voted to back a motion describing independence as "the only way forward for workers in Scotland", and agreeing to "do all in our power to secure [a <i>Yes</i>] outcome".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Trades_Union_Congress" title="Scottish Trades Union Congress">Scottish Trades Union Congress</a> has refused to take a stance on the referendum, instead laying out "challenges for both sides of the debate", in particular calling on <a href="/wiki/Better_Together_(campaign)" title="Better Together (campaign)">Better Together</a> to "outline a practical vision of how social and economic justice can be achieved within the union".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Other left-wing sections refused to support a nationalist position, instead arguing either for working-class unity or a critical approach to both sides. Campaigns such as Socialism First and the Red Paper Collective were a challenge to the official campaigns on both sides arguing for "Class over Nation".
</p><p>Until 2006, the RMT was affiliated with the Scottish Socialist Party.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Labour Party campaigned in favour of a "No" vote through the referendum campaign, headed by former Labour <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a> <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Darling" title="Alistair Darling">Alistair Darling</a>, and through <a href="/wiki/United_with_Labour" title="United with Labour">United with Labour</a>, a campaign composed solely of Labour Party figures. However, some members of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Labour_Party" title="Scottish Labour Party">Scottish Labour</a> joined <a href="/wiki/Labour_for_Independence" title="Labour for Independence">Labour for Independence</a>, a pressure group of Labour members who back Scottish independence. The appeal of independence is attributed by the group's leader to a feeling of being "let down and betrayed by a party who no longer represent them or the people of Scotland".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="United_Kingdom_general_election.2C_2015"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015">United Kingdom general election, 2015</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: United Kingdom general election, 2015">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">United Kingdom general election, 2015</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Opinion_polling_for_the_next_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="Opinion polling for the next United Kingdom general election">Polls</a> had suggested a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a> at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015 general election</a>, which was the party's first general election with <a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a> at the helm. When the final results were counted however, Labour suffered a second consecutive defeat and the Conservatives led by <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a>; formed a majority government for the first time since <a href="/wiki/1992_UK_general_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1992 UK general election">1992</a>. The number of Labour MPs declined by 26 from 2010, after they lost 40 of the 41 seats they had in Scotland to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> and finished with 30.4% of the vote nationally and 232 seats in the House of Commons. Miliband resigned as party leader<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> following his party's defeat and subsequently triggered a <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)_leadership_election,_2015" title="Labour Party (UK) leadership election, 2015">Labour Party leadership election</a>. <a href="/wiki/Harriet_Harman" title="Harriet Harman">Harriet Harman</a> served as acting leader while the election was contested. The Labour Party are currently the official <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Loyal_Opposition_(United_Kingdom)" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Most Loyal Opposition (United Kingdom)">Opposition Party</a>.
</p><p>Miliband's election as Leader of the Labour Party on the back of trade union member votes had been seen by some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (July 2013)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as a return to the left following the New Labour years (1994-2010). Miliband was nicknamed "Red Ed" by some (predominantly right-wing) media.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> After assuming office as Leader of the Opposition, Miliband softened some of the more left-wing ideas he had adopted during the leadership election, but remained committed to causes such as a <a href="/wiki/Living_wage" title="Living wage">living wage</a> and the 50% tax rate on high earners. However, the Labour Party under Miliband focused on calls for "responsible capitalism" rather than socialism.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Labour's then-Shadow Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Ed_Balls" title="Ed Balls">Ed Balls</a> also committed to maintaining some spending cuts scheduled for 2015 and 2016 by the Conservative-led coalition,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and was accused of planning to cut the <a href="/wiki/State_pension" class="mw-redirect" title="State pension">state pension</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> These were unpopular ideas with traditional socialists.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Corbyn" title="Jeremy Corbyn">Jeremy Corbyn</a> became Leader of the Labour Party in <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)_leadership_election,_2015" title="Labour Party (UK) leadership election, 2015">September 2015</a>. Corbyn identifies as a democratic socialist.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In August 2015, prior to the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)_leadership_election,_2015" title="Labour Party (UK) leadership election, 2015">2015 leadership election</a>, the Labour Party reported 292,505 full members.<sup id="cite_ref-independent-20150910_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-20150910-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mirror-20150825_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mirror-20150825-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> As of  December 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the party had approximately 570,000 full members, making it the largest political party by membership in Western Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-huffpost-20170613_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffpost-20170613-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note--43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="United_Kingdom_general_election.2C_2017"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017">United Kingdom general election, 2017</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_socialist_movement_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: United Kingdom general election, 2017">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017">United Kingdom general election, 2017</a></div>
<p>On 18 April 2017, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a> announced she would seek an unexpected <a href="/wiki/Snap_election" title="Snap election">snap election</a> on Thursday 8 June 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Corbyn said he welcomed May's proposal and said his party would support the government's move in the parliamentary vote announced for 19 April.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The necessary <a href="/wiki/Supermajority" title="Supermajority">super-majority of two-thirds</a> was achieved when 522 of the <a href="/wiki/Number_of_Westminster_MPs" title="Number of Westminster MPs">650 members of parliament</a> voted in favour of an early election.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Some of the <a href="/wiki/Opinion_polling_for_the_United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="Opinion polling for the United Kingdom general election, 2017">opinion polls</a> had shown a 20-point Conservative lead over Labour before the election was called, but this lead had narrowed by the day of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017">2017 general election</a>; which resulted in a <a href="/wiki/Hung_parliament" title="Hung parliament">hung parliament</a>. Despite remaining in opposition for the third general election in a row, Labour won 40% of the popular vote, it's greatest share of the vote since <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">2001</a>. It was also the first time the Labour Party had made a net gain of seats since their <a href="/wiki/1997_UK_general_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1997 UK general election">1997 landslide victory</a>. Thirty new seats were gained to reach 262 total MPs, and, with a swing of 9.6%,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> achieved the biggest percentage-point increase in it's vote share at a single general election since <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1945" title="United Kingdom general election, 1945">1945</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-londoneconomic_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-londoneconomic-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Immediately following the election, party membership rose by 35,000.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In July 2017, opinion polling suggested Labour leads the Conservatives, 45% to 39%<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> while a <a href="/wiki/YouGov" title="YouGov">YouGov</a> poll gave Labour an 8-point lead over the Conservatives.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
