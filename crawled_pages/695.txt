<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Moody's Investors Service</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Moody's Investors Service</b>, often referred to as <b>Moody's</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Bond_credit_rating" title="Bond credit rating">bond credit rating</a> business of <a href="/wiki/Moody%27s_Corporation" title="Moody&#39;s Corporation">Moody's Corporation</a>, representing the company's traditional line of business and its historical name. Moody's Investors Service provides international financial research on <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> issued by commercial and government entities. Moody's, along with <a href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%27s" title="Standard &amp; Poor&#39;s">Standard &amp; Poor's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fitch_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Fitch Group">Fitch Group</a>, is considered one of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Three_(credit_rating_agencies)" title="Big Three (credit rating agencies)">Big Three credit rating agencies</a>. 
</p><p>The company ranks the <a href="/wiki/Credit_risk" title="Credit risk">creditworthiness</a> of borrowers using a standardized ratings scale which measures expected investor loss in the event of <a href="/wiki/Default_(finance)" title="Default (finance)">default</a>. Moody's Investors Service rates <a href="/wiki/Security_(finance)" title="Security (finance)">debt securities</a> in several <a href="/wiki/Bond_market" title="Bond market">bond market</a> segments. These include <a href="/wiki/Government_bond" title="Government bond">government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Municipal_bond" title="Municipal bond">municipal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_bond" title="Corporate bond">corporate bonds</a>; managed investments such as <a href="/wiki/Money_market_fund" title="Money market fund">money market funds</a> and fixed-income funds; financial institutions including banks and non-bank finance companies; and asset classes in <a href="/wiki/Structured_finance" title="Structured finance">structured finance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moody&#39;s_Market_Segments_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moody&#39;s_Market_Segments-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In Moody's Investors Service's ratings system, securities are assigned a rating from  Aaa to C, with Aaa being the highest quality and C the lowest quality.
</p><p>Moody's was founded by <a href="/wiki/John_Moody_(financial_analyst)" title="John Moody (financial analyst)">John Moody</a> in 1909 to produce manuals of statistics related to stocks and bonds and bond ratings. In 1975, the company was identified as a <a href="/wiki/Nationally_Recognized_Statistical_Rating_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organization">Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organization</a> (NRSRO) by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a>. Following several decades of ownership by <a href="/wiki/Dun_%26_Bradstreet" title="Dun &amp; Bradstreet">Dun &amp; Bradstreet</a>, Moody's Investors Service became a separate company in 2000. Moody's Corporation was established as a holding company.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_capital_markets">Role in capital markets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role in capital markets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Together, they are sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Big_Three_(credit_rating_agencies)" title="Big Three (credit rating agencies)">Big Three</a> credit rating agencies. While credit rating agencies are sometimes viewed as interchangeable, Moody's, S&amp;P and Fitch in fact rate bonds differently; for example, S&amp;P and <a href="/wiki/Fitch_Ratings" title="Fitch Ratings">Fitch Ratings</a> measure the probability that a security will default, while Moody's ratings seek to measure the expected losses in the event of a default.<sup id="cite_ref-Salmon_Aug_11_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salmon_Aug_11-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Although Fitch has a smaller percentage of the market, it is still much larger than other rating agencies. All three operate worldwide, maintaining offices on six continents, and rating tens of trillions of dollars in securities. However, only <a href="/wiki/Moody%27s_Corporation" title="Moody&#39;s Corporation">Moody's Corporation</a> is a free-standing company.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moody's Investors Service and its close competitors play a key role in global <a href="/wiki/Capital_market" title="Capital market">capital markets</a> as three supplementary credit analysis provider for banks and other financial institutions in assessing the credit risk of particular securities. This form of third party analysis is particularly useful for smaller and less sophisticated investors, as well as for all investors to use as an external comparison for their own judgments.<sup id="cite_ref-FSB_Oct_10_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FSB_Oct_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Credit rating agencies also play an important role in the laws and regulations of the United States and several other countries, such as those of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. In the United States their credit ratings are used in regulation by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" title="U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission">U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission</a> as <a href="/wiki/Nationally_Recognized_Statistical_Rating_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organization">Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organizations</a> (NRSROs) for a variety of regulatory purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Among the effects of regulatory use was to enable lower-rated companies to sell bond debt for the first time; their lower ratings merely distinguished them from higher-rated companies, rather than excluding them altogether, as had been the case.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, another aspect of mechanical use of ratings by regulatory agencies has been to reinforce "pro-cyclical" and "cliff effects" of downgrades. In October 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Stability_Board" title="Financial Stability Board">Financial Stability Board</a> (FSB) created a set of "principles to reduce reliance" on credit ranges agencies in the laws, regulations and market practices of <a href="/wiki/G-20_major_economies" class="mw-redirect" title="G-20 major economies">G-20 member countries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FSB_Oct_10_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FSB_Oct_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Since the early 1990s, the SEC has also used NRSRO ratings in measuring the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_paper" title="Commercial paper">commercial paper</a> held by <a href="/wiki/Money_market_fund" title="Money market fund">money market funds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The SEC has designated seven other firms as NRSROs,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> including, for example, <a href="/wiki/A._M._Best" title="A. M. Best">A. M. Best</a>, which focuses on obligations of <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance companies</a>. Companies with which Moody's competes in specific areas include investment research company <a href="/wiki/Morningstar,_Inc." title="Morningstar, Inc.">Morningstar, Inc.</a> and publishers of financial information for investors such as <a href="/wiki/Thomson_Reuters" title="Thomson Reuters">Thomson Reuters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bloomberg_L.P." title="Bloomberg L.P.">Bloomberg L.P.</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Especially since the early 2000s, Moody's frequently makes its analysts available to journalists, and issues regular public statements on credit conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Moody's, like S&amp;P, organizes public seminars to educate first-time securities issuers on the information it uses to analyze debt securities.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Moody.27s_credit_ratings"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Moody's_credit_ratings"><span id="credit_ratings"></span> Moody's credit ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Moody&#039;s credit ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Moody's, the purpose of its ratings is to "provide investors with a simple system of gradation by which future relative creditworthiness of securities may be gauged". To each of its ratings from Aa through Caa, Moody's appends numerical modifiers 1, 2 and 3; the lower the number, the higher-end the rating. Aaa, Ca and C are not modified this way. As Moody's explains, its ratings are "not to be construed as recommendations", nor are they intended to be a sole basis for investment decisions. In addition, its ratings don’t speak to market price, although market conditions may impact credit risk.<sup id="cite_ref-Moody&#39;s_Definitions_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moody&#39;s_Definitions-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IOSCO_03_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOSCO_03-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span id="History_of_Moody.27s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Moody's">History of Moody's</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History of Moody&#039;s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_and_early_history">Founding and early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Founding and early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Moody%27s_Corporation" title="Moody&#39;s Corporation">Moody's Corporation</a></div>
<p>Moody's traces its history back to two publishing companies established by <a href="/wiki/John_Moody_(financial_analyst)" title="John Moody (financial analyst)">John Moody</a>, the inventor of modern <a href="/wiki/Bond_credit_rating" title="Bond credit rating">bond credit ratings</a>. In 1900, Moody published his first market assessment, called <i>Moody's Manual of Industrial and Miscellaneous Securities</i>, and established John Moody &amp; Company.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The publication provided detailed statistics relating to stocks and bonds of financial institutions, government agencies, manufacturing, mining, utilities, and food companies. It experienced early success, selling out its first print run in its first two months. By 1903, <i><a href="/wiki/Moody%27s_Manual" title="Moody&#39;s Manual">Moody's Manual</a></i> was a nationally recognized publication.<sup id="cite_ref-Moody&#39;s_History_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moody&#39;s_History-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Moody was forced to sell his business, due to a shortage of capital, when the <a href="/wiki/Panic_of_1907" title="Panic of 1907">1907 financial crisis</a> fueled several changes in the markets.<sup id="cite_ref-Sylla_01_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sylla_01-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moody returned in 1909 with a new publication focused solely on railroad bonds, <i>Analysis of Railroad Investments</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Yasuyuki_Litan_06_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasuyuki_Litan_06-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and a new company, Moody's Analyses Publishing Company.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> While Moody acknowledged that the concept of bond ratings "was not entirely original" with him—he credited early bond rating efforts in Vienna and Berlin as inspiration—he was the first to publish them widely, in an accessible format.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sylla_01_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sylla_01-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Moody was also the first to charge subscription fees to investors.<sup id="cite_ref-Yasuyuki_Litan_06_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasuyuki_Litan_06-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  In 1913 he expanded the manual's focus to include industrial firms and utilities; the new <i>Moody's Manual</i> offered ratings of public securities, indicated by a letter-rating system borrowed from mercantile credit-reporting firms. The following year, Moody incorporated the company as Moody's Investors Service.<sup id="cite_ref-Moody&#39;s_History_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moody&#39;s_History-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Other rating companies followed over the next few years, including the antecedents of the "<a href="/wiki/Big_Three_(credit_rating_agencies)" title="Big Three (credit rating agencies)">Big Three</a>" credit rating agencies: Poor's in 1916, Standard Statistics Company in 1922,<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the Fitch Publishing Company in 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moody’s expanded its focus to include ratings for U.S. state and local government bonds in 1919<sup id="cite_ref-Sylla_01_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sylla_01-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and, by 1924, Moody's rated nearly the entire U.S. bond market.<sup id="cite_ref-Moody&#39;s_History_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moody&#39;s_History-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1930s_to_mid-century">1930s to mid-century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1930s to mid-century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Moody%27s_Corporation" title="Moody&#39;s Corporation">Moody's Corporation</a></div>
<p>The relationship between the U.S. bond market and rating agencies developed further in the 1930s. As the market grew beyond that of traditional investment banking institutions, new investors again called for increased transparency, leading to the passage of new, mandatory disclosure laws for issuers, and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Securities_and_Exchange_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Securities and Exchange Commission">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> (SEC).<sup id="cite_ref-Sylla_01_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sylla_01-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 1936 a new set of laws were introduced, prohibiting banks from investing in "speculative investment securities" ("junk bonds", in modern terminology) as determined by "recognized rating manuals". Banks were permitted only to hold "investment grade" bonds, following the judgment of Moody's, along with Standard, Poor's and Fitch. In the decades that followed, state insurance regulators approved similar requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1962, Moody's Investors Service was bought by <a href="/wiki/Dun_%26_Bradstreet" title="Dun &amp; Bradstreet">Dun &amp; Bradstreet</a>, a firm engaged in the related field of <a href="/wiki/Credit_bureau" title="Credit bureau">credit reporting</a>, although they continued to operate largely as independent companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Sylla_01_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sylla_01-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s_to_2000">1970s to 2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1970s to 2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the late 1960s and 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Commercial_paper" title="Commercial paper">commercial paper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deposit_account" title="Deposit account">bank deposits</a> began to be rated. As well, the major agencies began charging the issuers of bonds as well as investors — Moody's began doing this in 1970<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> — thanks in part to a growing <a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" class="mw-redirect" title="Free rider problem">free rider problem</a> related to the increasing availability of inexpensive <a href="/wiki/Photocopier" title="Photocopier">photocopy machines</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wash_Post_Jan_02_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wash_Post_Jan_02-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and the increased complexity of the financial markets.<sup id="cite_ref-Moody&#39;s_History_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moody&#39;s_History-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AEI_Jan_09_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AEI_Jan_09-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Rating agencies also grew in size as the number of issuers grew,<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke_09_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke_09-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> both in the United States and abroad, making the credit rating business significantly more profitable. In 2005 Moody's estimated that 90% of <a href="/wiki/Credit_rating_agency" title="Credit rating agency">credit rating agency</a> revenues came from issuer fees.<sup id="cite_ref-Moody&#39;s_10-K_05_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moody&#39;s_10-K_05-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The end of the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system" title="Bretton Woods system">Bretton Woods system</a> in 1971 led to the liberalization of financial regulations, and the global expansion of capital markets in the 1970s and 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1975, the SEC changed its minimum capital requirements for <a href="/wiki/Broker-dealer" title="Broker-dealer">broker-dealers</a>, using bond ratings as a measurement. Moody's and nine other agencies (later five, due to consolidation) were identified by the SEC as "nationally recognized statistical ratings organizations" (NRSROs) for broker-dealers to use in meeting these requirements.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SEC_NRSRO_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SEC_NRSRO-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1980s and beyond saw the global capital market expand; Moody's opened its first overseas offices in Japan in 1985, followed by offices in the United Kingdom in 1986, France in 1988, Germany in 1991, Hong Kong in 1994, India in 1998 and China in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The number of bonds rated by Moody's and the Big Three agencies grew substantially as well. As of 1997, Moody's was rating about $5 trillion in securities from 20,000 U.S. and 1,200 non-U.S. issuers.<sup id="cite_ref-Yasuyuki_Litan_06_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasuyuki_Litan_06-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The 1990s and 2000s were also a time of increased scrutiny, as Moody's was sued by unhappy issuers and investigation by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Tulsa_Mar_96_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tulsa_Mar_96-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> as well as criticism following the <a href="/wiki/Enron_scandal" title="Enron scandal">collapse of Enron</a>, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Subprime_mortgage_crisis" title="Subprime mortgage crisis">subprime mortgage crisis</a> and subsequent <a href="/wiki/Late-2000s_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-2000s financial crisis">late-2000s financial crisis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CGFS_May_08_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CGFS_May_08-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following several years of rumors and pressure from institutional shareholders,<sup id="cite_ref-Bond_Buyer_Dec_99_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bond_Buyer_Dec_99-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> in December 1999 Moody's parent Dun &amp; Bradstreet announced it would spin off Moody's Investors Service into a separate <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">publicly traded company</a>. Although Moody's had fewer than 1,500 employees in its division, it represented about 51% of Dun &amp; Bradstreet profits in the year before the announcement.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Dec_99_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Dec_99-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The spin-off was completed on September 30, 2000,<sup id="cite_ref-Airfinance_00_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Airfinance_00-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and, in the half decade that followed, the value of Moody's shares improved by more than 300%.<sup id="cite_ref-Yasuyuki_Litan_06_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasuyuki_Litan_06-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structured_finance_boom_and_after">Structured finance boom and after</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structured finance boom and after">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Structured finance went from 28% of Moody's revenue in 1998 to almost 50% in 2007, and "accounted for pretty much all of Moody's growth" during that time.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  According to the <a href="/wiki/Financial_Crisis_Inquiry_Commission#Report" title="Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission">Financial Crisis Inquiry Report</a>, during the years 2005, 2006, and 2007, rating of structured finance products such as <a href="/wiki/Mortgage-backed_security" title="Mortgage-backed security">mortgage-backed securities</a> made up close to half of Moody’s rating revenues. From 2000 to 2007, revenues from rating structured financial instruments increased more than fourfold.<sup id="cite_ref-FCIR-p118_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FCIR-p118-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> However, there was some question about the models Moody's used to give structured products high ratings. In June 2005, shortly before the subprime mortgage crisis, Moody’s updated its approach for estimating default correlation of non-prime/nontraditional mortgages involved in structured financial products like <a href="/wiki/Mortgage-backed_security" title="Mortgage-backed security">mortgage-backed securities</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collateralized_debt_obligation" title="Collateralized debt obligation">Collateralized debt obligations</a>. Its new model was based on trends from the previous 20 years, during which time housing prices had been rising, mortgage delinquencies very low, and nontraditional mortgage products a very small niche of the market.<sup id="cite_ref-FCIRupdate_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FCIRupdate-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 10, 2007, in "an unprecedented move", Moody's downgraded 399 subprime mortgage-backed securities that had been issued the year before. Three months later, it downgraded another 2506 <a href="/wiki/Tranche" title="Tranche">tranches</a>  ($33.4 billion).  By the end of the crisis,  Moody's downgraded 83% of all the 2006 Aaa mortgage backed security tranches and all of the Baa tranches.<sup id="cite_ref-FCIR-p221_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FCIR-p221-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AFGItimeline_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AFGItimeline-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2013, Moody's Investor Service has warned that <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>'s credit rating may be damaged due to an increasingly costly rice-pledging scheme which lost 200 billion baht ($6.5 billion) in 2011–2012.<sup id="cite_ref-investvine_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-investvine-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sovereign_downgrades">Sovereign downgrades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sovereign downgrades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Moody's, along with the other major credit rating agencies, is often the subject of criticism from countries whose public debt is downgraded, generally claiming increased cost of borrowing as a result of the downgrade.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Examples of sovereign debt downgrades that attracted significant media attention at the time include Australia in the 1980s, Canada and Japan in the 1990s, Thailand during the <a href="/wiki/1997_Asian_financial_crisis" title="1997 Asian financial crisis">1997 Asian financial crisis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and Portugal in 2011 following the <a href="/wiki/European_sovereign_debt_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="European sovereign debt crisis">European sovereign debt crisis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unsolicited_ratings">Unsolicited ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Unsolicited ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Moody's has occasionally faced litigation from entities whose bonds it has rated on an unsolicited basis, and investigations concerning such unsolicited ratings. In October 1995, the school district of <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Colorado" title="Jefferson County, Colorado">Jefferson County, Colorado</a> sued Moody's, claiming the unsolicited assignment of a "negative outlook" to a 1993 bond issue was based on Jefferson County having selected S&amp;P and Fitch to do its rating. Moody's rating raised the issuing cost to Jefferson County by $769,000.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Moody's argued that its assessment was "opinion" and therefore constitutionally protected; the court agreed, and the decision was upheld on appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-Yasuyuki_Litan_06_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasuyuki_Litan_06-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the mid-1990s, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Justice Department</a>'s antitrust division opened an investigation to determine whether unsolicited ratings amounted to an illegal exercise of <a href="/wiki/Market_power" title="Market power">market power</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Tulsa_Mar_96_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tulsa_Mar_96-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> however the investigation was closed with no antitrust charges filed. Moody's has pointed out that it has assigned unsolicited ratings since 1909, and that such ratings are the market's "best defense against rating shopping" by issuers. In November 1999, Moody's announced it would begin identifying which ratings were unsolicited as part of a general move toward greater transparency.<sup id="cite_ref-Sinclair_05_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sinclair_05-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The agency faced a similar complaint in the mid-2000s from <a href="/wiki/Hannover_Re" title="Hannover Re">Hannover Re</a>, a German insurer that lost $175 million in market value when its bonds were lowered to "junk" status.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wash_Post_Nov_04_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wash_Post_Nov_04-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, unsolicited ratings were at the center of a subpoena by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Attorney_General" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Attorney General">New York Attorney General</a>'s office under <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Spitzer" title="Eliot Spitzer">Eliot Spitzer</a>, but again no charges were filed.<sup id="cite_ref-Yasuyuki_Litan_06_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasuyuki_Litan_06-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the 2008 financial crisis, the SEC adopted new rules for the rating agency industry, including one to encourage unsolicited ratings. The intent of the rule is to counteract potential conflicts of interest in the issuer-pays model by ensuring a "broader range of views on the creditworthiness" of a security or instrument.<sup id="cite_ref-Federal_Register_Feb_09_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Federal_Register_Feb_09-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sidley_Austin_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sidley_Austin-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alleged_conflicts_of_interest">Alleged conflicts of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alleged conflicts of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "issuer pays" business model adopted in the 1970s by Moody's and other rating agencies has been criticized for creating a possible <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict of interest</a>, supposing that rating agencies may artificially boost the rating of a given security in order to please the issuer.<sup id="cite_ref-JEP_Spring_10_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JEP_Spring_10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The SEC recently acknowledged, however, in its September 30, 2011 summary report of its mandatory annual examination of NRSROs that the subscriber-pays model under which Moody’s operated prior to adopting the issuer pays model also "presents certain conflicts of interest inherent in the fact that subscribers, on whom the NRSRO relies, have an interest in ratings actions and could exert pressure on the NRSRO for certain outcomes".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Other alleged conflicts of interest, also the subject of a Department of Justice investigation the mid-1990s, raised the question of whether Moody's pressured issuers to use its consulting services upon threat of a lower bond rating.<sup id="cite_ref-Herald_June_96_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Herald_June_96-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Moody's has maintained that its reputation in the market is the balancing factor, and a 2003 study, covering 1997 to 2002, suggested that "reputation effects" outweighed conflicts of interest. Thomas McGuire, a former executive vice president, said in 1995 that: "[W]hat’s driving us is primarily the issue of preserving our track record. That’s our bread and butter".<sup id="cite_ref-JFE_2011_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFE_2011-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Financial_crisis_of_2007.E2.80.932008"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_crisis_of_2007–2008">Financial crisis of 2007–2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Financial crisis of 2007–2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Late-2000s_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Late-2000s financial crisis">global financial crisis</a> of the late 2000s brought increased scrutiny to credit rating agencies' assessments of complex <a href="/wiki/Structured_finance" title="Structured finance">structured finance</a> securities. Moody's and its close competitors were subject to criticism following large downgrade actions beginning in July 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-Financial_Crisis_Inquiry_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Financial_Crisis_Inquiry-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wash_Post_Aug_07_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wash_Post_Aug_07-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> According to the <i>Financial Crisis Inquiry Report,</i> 73% of the mortgage-backed securities Moody's had rated triple-A in 2006 were downgraded to junk by 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Financial_Crisis_Inquiry-p122_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Financial_Crisis_Inquiry-p122-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In its "Conclusions on Chapter 8", the Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission stated: "There was a clear failure of corporate governance at Moody’s, which did not ensure the quality of its ratings on tens of thousands of mortgage-backed securities and CDOs."<sup id="cite_ref-Financial_Crisis_Inquiry-p155_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Financial_Crisis_Inquiry-p155-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Faced with having to put more capital against lower rated securities, investors such as banks, pension funds and insurance companies sought to sell their <a href="/wiki/Residential_mortgage-backed_security" title="Residential mortgage-backed security">residential mortgage-backed securities</a> (RMBS) and <a href="/wiki/Collateralized_debt_obligation" title="Collateralized debt obligation">collateralized debt obligation</a> (CDO) holdings.<sup id="cite_ref-Anatomy_Apr_11-A_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anatomy_Apr_11-A-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The value of these securities held by financial firms declined, and the market for new subprime securitizations dried up.<sup id="cite_ref-Anatomy_Apr_11-B_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anatomy_Apr_11-B-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Some academics and industry observers have argued that the rating agencies' mass downgrades were part of the "<a href="/wiki/Perfect_storm" title="Perfect storm">perfect storm</a>" of events leading up to the financial crisis in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-Anatomy_Apr_11-A_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anatomy_Apr_11-A-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kenan_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kenan-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cato_Jan-Feb_10_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cato_Jan-Feb_10-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bloomberg_May_07_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloomberg_May_07-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, a study group established by the Committee on the Global Financial System (CGFS), a committee of the <a href="/wiki/Bank_for_International_Settlements" title="Bank for International Settlements">Bank for International Settlements</a>, found that rating agencies had underestimated the severity of the <a href="/wiki/Subprime_mortgage_crisis" title="Subprime mortgage crisis">subprime mortgage crisis</a>, as had "many market participants". According to the CGFS, significant contributing factors included "limited historical data" and an underestimation of "originator risk" factors. The CGFS also found that agency ratings should "support, not replace, investor due diligence" and that agencies should provide "better information on the key risk factors" of structured finance ratings. In October 2007, Moody's further refined its criteria for originators, "with loss expectations increasing significantly from the highest to the lowest tier". In May 2008, Moody's proposed adding "volatility scores and loss sensitivities" to its existing rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-CGFS_May_08_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CGFS_May_08-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Although the rating agencies were criticized for "technical failings and inadequate resources", the FSB stated that the agencies' "need to repair their reputation was seen as a powerful force" for change.<sup id="cite_ref-FSB_Oct_10_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FSB_Oct_10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Moody's has in fact lost market share in certain sectors due to its tightened rating standards on some asset-backed securities, for example the <a href="/wiki/Commercial_mortgage-backed_security" title="Commercial mortgage-backed security">commercial mortgage-backed securities</a> (CMBS) market in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2013, Moody's, along with <a href="/wiki/Standard_%26_Poor%27s" title="Standard &amp; Poor&#39;s">Standard &amp; Poor's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a>, settled fourteen lawsuits filed by groups including <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_Commercial_Bank" title="Abu Dhabi Commercial Bank">Abu Dhabi Commercial Bank</a> and <a href="/wiki/King_County,_Washington" title="King County, Washington">King County, Washington</a>. The lawsuits alleged that the agencies inflated their ratings on purchased structured investment vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-Neumann13_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neumann13-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2017, Moody's agreed to pay nearly $864 million to settle with the Department of Justice, other state authorities and Washington. A fine of $437.5 million would be paid to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">DOJ</a>, and the remaining $426.3 million penalty would be split among 21 states and the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">District of Columbia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_Credit_Research">Global Credit Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moody%27s_Investors_Service&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Global Credit Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2013 Moody's Investors Service published their report entitled <i>Cash Pile Grows 10% to $1.45 Trillion; Overseas Holdings Continue to Expand</i> in their Global Credit Research series, in which they examined companies they rate in the US non-financial corporate sector (NFCS). According to their report, by the end of 2012 the US NFCS held "$1.45 trillion in cash," 10% more than in 2011. At the end of 2011, US NFCS held $1.32 trillion in cash which was already a record level.<sup id="cite_ref-moodys18mar2013_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moodys18mar2013-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> "Of the $1.32 trillion for all the rated companies, Moody's estimates that $840 billion, or 58% of the total cash, is held overseas."<sup id="cite_ref-MoodysReportMar2013_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoodysReportMar2013-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
