<p><b>DuckDuckGo</b> (<b>DDG</b>) is an Internet <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">search engine</a> that emphasizes protecting searchers' <a href="/wiki/Internet_privacy" title="Internet privacy">privacy</a> and avoiding the <a href="/wiki/Filter_bubble" title="Filter bubble">filter bubble</a> of personalized search results.<sup id="cite_ref-Buys_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buys-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> DuckDuckGo distinguishes itself from other search engines by not profiling its users and by deliberately showing all users the same search results for a given search term,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and emphasizes returning the best results, rather than the most results, generating those results from over 400 individual sources, including <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourced</a> sites such as <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, and other search engines like <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_BOSS" title="Yahoo! Search BOSS">Yahoo!</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yandex_Search" title="Yandex Search">Yandex</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company is based in <a href="/wiki/Paoli,_Pennsylvania" title="Paoli, Pennsylvania">Paoli, Pennsylvania</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Philadelphia" class="mw-redirect" title="Greater Philadelphia">Greater Philadelphia</a>, and has 53 employees. The company name is a reference to the children's game <a href="/wiki/Duck,_duck,_goose" title="Duck, duck, goose">duck, duck, goose</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WashPostNov2012_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPostNov2012-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of DuckDuckGo's <a href="/wiki/Source_code" title="Source code">source code</a> is <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> hosted at <a href="/wiki/GitHub" title="GitHub">GitHub</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Apache_License" title="Apache License">Apache 2.0 License</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Githubduckduckgo_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Githubduckduckgo-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> but the core is <a href="/wiki/Proprietary_software" title="Proprietary software">proprietary</a>. The company registered the domain names <i>ddg<a href="/wiki/.gg" title=".gg">.gg</a></i> on February 22, 2011, and <i>ddg.co</i> on September 20, 2013, which are used as shortened URL aliases that redirect to <i>duckduckgo.com</i>.
</p><p>On September 18, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple</a> included DuckDuckGo in its <a href="/wiki/Safari_(web_browser)" title="Safari (web browser)">Safari</a> browser as an optional search engine.<sup id="cite_ref-Big_Win_For_DuckDuckGo_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Big_Win_For_DuckDuckGo-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Apple_-_OS_X_Yosemite_-_Apps_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Apple_-_OS_X_Yosemite_-_Apps-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> On November 10, 2014, <a href="/wiki/Mozilla" title="Mozilla">Mozilla</a> added DuckDuckGo as a search option to <a href="/wiki/Firefox_33" class="mw-redirect" title="Firefox 33">Firefox 33.1</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On May 30, 2016, <a href="/wiki/The_Tor_Project,_Inc" title="The Tor Project, Inc">The Tor Project, Inc</a> made DuckDuckGo the default search engine for <a href="/wiki/Tor_(anonymity_network)#Tor_Browser" title="Tor (anonymity network)">Tor Browser</a> 6.0.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DuckDuckGo was founded in 2008<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriel_Weinberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gabriel Weinberg (page does not exist)">Gabriel Weinberg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> an entrepreneur who previously launched <a href="/wiki/Names_Database" title="Names Database">Names Database</a>, a now-defunct social network. Initially self-funded by Weinberg, DuckDuckGo is now<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2017)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>-supported, but the user has the option to disable ads.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The search engine is written in <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a><sup id="cite_ref-Perl_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perl-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and runs on <a href="/wiki/Nginx" title="Nginx">nginx</a>, <a href="/wiki/FreeBSD" title="FreeBSD">FreeBSD</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Buys_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buys-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weinberg_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weinberg-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Architecture_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Architecture-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> DuckDuckGo is built primarily upon search <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">APIs</a> from various vendors. Because of this, <a href="/wiki/TechCrunch" title="TechCrunch">TechCrunch</a> characterized the service as a "hybrid" search engine.<sup id="cite_ref-TCFriday_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCFriday-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, it produces its own content pages, and thus is similar to <a href="/wiki/Mahalo.com" title="Mahalo.com">Mahalo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kosmix" class="mw-redirect" title="Kosmix">Kosmix</a>, and <a href="/wiki/SearchMe" title="SearchMe">SearchMe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Weinberg explained the beginnings of the name with respect to the children's game <a href="/wiki/Duck,_duck,_goose" title="Duck, duck, goose">duck, duck, goose</a>. He said of the origin of the name: "Really it just popped in my head one day and I just liked it. It is certainly influenced/derived from duck duck goose, but other than that there is no relation, e.g.,&#160;a metaphor."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> DuckDuckGo was featured on <a href="/wiki/TechCrunch" title="TechCrunch">TechCrunch</a>'s Elevator Pitch Friday in 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-TCFriday_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCFriday-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and it was a finalist in the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_BOSS" title="Yahoo! Search BOSS">BOSS</a> <a href="/wiki/Mashable" title="Mashable">Mashable</a> Challenge.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2010, Weinberg started a DuckDuckGo community website (duck.co) to allow the public to report problems, discuss means of spreading the use of the search engine, request features, and discuss <a href="/wiki/Open-sourcing" class="mw-redirect" title="Open-sourcing">open sourcing</a> the code.<sup id="cite_ref-Community_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Community-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The next month, <a href="/wiki/Union_Square_Ventures" title="Union Square Ventures">Union Square Ventures</a> invested in DuckDuckGo. Union Square partner Brad Burnham stated, "We invested in DuckDuckGo because we became convinced that it was not only possible to change the basis of competition in search, it was time to do it."<sup id="cite_ref-Burnham13Oct11_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burnham13Oct11-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Trisquel" title="Trisquel">Trisquel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linux_Mint" title="Linux Mint">Linux Mint</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Midori_(web_browser)" title="Midori (web browser)">Midori</a> web browser switched to use DuckDuckGo as their default search engine.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:30%;&#32;;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">We didn't invest in it because we thought it would beat Google. We invested in it because there is a need for a private search engine. We did it for the Internet anarchists, people that hang out on <a href="/wiki/Reddit" title="Reddit">Reddit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hacker_News" title="Hacker News">Hacker News</a>.</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style=""><a href="/wiki/Fred_Wilson_(financier)" title="Fred Wilson (financier)">Fred Wilson</a>, 2012 <a href="/wiki/TechCrunch_Disrupt" class="mw-redirect" title="TechCrunch Disrupt">TechCrunch Disrupt</a> Conference in New York<sup id="cite_ref-Ludwig1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ludwig1-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>By May 2012, the search engine was attracting 1.5&#160;million searches a day. Weinberg reported that it had earned <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$115,000</span> in revenue in 2011 and had three employees, plus a small number of contractors.<sup id="cite_ref-Farivar16May12_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farivar16May12-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Compete.com" title="Compete.com">Compete.com</a> estimated 277,512 monthly visitors to the site in August 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> On April 12, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Internet" title="Alexa Internet">Alexa</a> reported a 3-month growth rate of 51%.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> DuckDuckGo's own <a href="/wiki/Traffic_statistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Traffic statistics">traffic statistics</a> show that in August 2012 there were 1,393,644 visits per day, up from an average of 39,406 visits per day in April 2010 (the earliest data available).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> In a lengthy profile in November 2012, <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post"><i>The Washington Post</i></a> indicated that searches on DuckDuckGo numbered up to 45,000,000 per month in October 2012. The article concluded: "Weinberg's non-ambitious goals make him a particularly odd and dangerous competitor online. He can do almost everything that <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a> can't because it could damage their business models, and if users figure out that they like the DuckDuckGo way better, Weinberg could damage the big boys without even really trying. It's asymmetrical digital warfare, and his backers at Union Square Ventures say Google is vulnerable."<sup id="cite_ref-WashPostNov2012_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPostNov2012-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/GNOME" title="GNOME">GNOME</a> released <a href="/wiki/Web_(web_browser)" class="mw-redirect" title="Web (web browser)">Web (web browser)</a> 3.10 on September 26, 2013, and starting with this version, the default search engine is DuckDuckGo.<sup id="cite_ref-gnomepeopleblog_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gnomepeopleblog-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At its keynote speech at WWDC 2014, Apple announced that DuckDuckGo would be included as an option for search on both <a href="/wiki/IOS_8" title="IOS 8">iOS 8</a> and <a href="/wiki/OS_X_Yosemite" title="OS X Yosemite">OS X Yosemite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> On March 10, the <a href="/wiki/Pale_Moon_(web_browser)" title="Pale Moon (web browser)">Pale Moon</a> web browser, starting with version 24.4.0, included DuckDuckGo as its default search engine, as well as listed it on the browser's homepage.<sup id="cite_ref-palemoonrelease_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-palemoonrelease-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In May 2014, DuckDuckGo released a redesigned version to beta testers through DuckDuckHack.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> On 21 May 2014, DuckDuckGo officially released the redesigned version that focused on smarter answers and a more refined look. The new version added many new features such as images, local search, auto-suggest, weather, recipes, and more.<sup id="cite_ref-DuckDuckGo_Reimagined_&amp;_Redesigned_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuckDuckGo_Reimagined_&amp;_Redesigned-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>in July 2016, DuckDuckGo officially announced the extension of its partnership with <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search" title="Yahoo! Search">Yahoo!</a> that brought new features to all users of the search engine, including date filtering of results and additional site links. It also partners with Bing, Yandex, and Wikipedia to produce results or make use of features offered. The company also confirmed that it does not share user information with partner companies, as has always been its policy.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DuckDuckGo's results are a compilation of "over 400" sources,<sup id="cite_ref-Sources_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sources-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_BOSS" title="Yahoo! Search BOSS">Yahoo! Search BOSS</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_Alpha" title="Wolfram Alpha">Wolfram Alpha</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a>; its own <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">Web crawler</a> (the <i>DuckDuckBot</i>); and others.<sup id="cite_ref-Buys_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buys-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sources_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sources-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> It also uses data from <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourced</a> sites, including Wikipedia, to populate "Zero-click Info" boxes – grey boxes above the results that display topic summaries and related topics.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>DuckDuckGo positions itself as a search engine that puts privacy first and as such it does not store IP addresses, does not log user information, and uses cookies only when required. Weinberg states: "By default, DuckDuckGo does not collect or share personal information. That is our privacy policy in a nutshell." However, they do maintain logs of all search terms used.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Weinberg has refined the quality of his search engine results by deleting search results for companies he believes are <i><a href="/wiki/Content_farm" title="Content farm">content mills</a></i>, like <a href="/wiki/Demand_Media" class="mw-redirect" title="Demand Media">Demand Media</a>'s <a href="/wiki/EHow" title="EHow">eHow</a>, which publishes 4000 articles per day produced by paid freelance writers, which Weinberg says is, "...low-quality content designed specifically to rank highly in Google's search index." DuckDuckGo also filters pages with substantial advertising.<sup id="cite_ref-Mims26Jul10_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mims26Jul10-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Instant_Answers">Instant Answers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Instant Answers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the indexed search results, DuckDuckGo displays relevant results, called Instant Answers, on top of the search page. These Instant Answers are collected from either 3rd party APIs or static data sources like text files. The Instant Answers are called <i>zeroclickinfo</i> because the intention behind these is to provide what the user is searching for on the search result page itself so that the user does not have to click any results to find what they are looking for. As of August 9, 2018, there are 1231 Instant Answers active.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Instant Answers are open source.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> They are maintained on GitHub and anyone can build or work on them.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tor_hidden_service">Tor hidden service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tor hidden service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2010, DuckDuckGo introduced anonymous searching, including an exit enclave, for its search engine traffic using <a href="/wiki/Tor_(anonymity_network)" title="Tor (anonymity network)">Tor network</a> and enabling access through a <a href="/wiki/Tor_hidden_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Tor hidden service">Tor hidden service</a>. This allows anonymity by routing traffic through a series of encrypted relays. Weinberg stated: "I believe this fits right in line with our privacy policy. Using Tor and DDG, you can now be end to end anonymous with your searching. And if you use our encrypted homepage, you can be end to end encrypted as well."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voice_search">Voice search</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Voice search">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, DuckDuckGo introduced voice search for users of the <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a> <a href="/wiki/Voice_search" title="Voice search">voice search</a> extension.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangs">Bangs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bangs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DuckDuckGo includes "!Bang" keywords, which give users the ability to search on specific third-party websites – using the site's own search engine if applicable. As of 2017, approx. 10,000 "bangs" for a diverse range of Internet sites are available.<sup id="cite_ref-bang_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bang-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy_browser">Privacy browser</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Privacy browser">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DuckDuckGo has a mobile app available for <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> that forces websites to use <a href="/wiki/HTTPS" title="HTTPS">HTTPS</a>, blocks web trackers, and rates sites based on their privacy practices.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The service, released in January 2018, is also available as a browser extension for <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozilla_Firefox" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozilla Firefox">Mozilla Firefox</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Apple_Safari" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Safari">Apple Safari</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_model">Business model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Business model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DuckDuckGo earns revenue by serving ads from the Yahoo–Bing search alliance network, and through affiliate relationships with Amazon and eBay.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a June 2011 article, Harry McCracken of <a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)"><i>Time</i> magazine</a> commended DuckDuckGo, comparing it to his favorite hamburger restaurant, <a href="/wiki/In-N-Out_Burger" title="In-N-Out Burger">In-N-Out Burger</a>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It feels a lot like early Google, with a stripped-down home page. Just as In-N-Out doesn't have lattes or Asian salads or sundaes or scrambled eggs, DDG doesn't try to do news or blogs or books or images. There's no auto-completion or instant results. It just offers core Web search—mostly the "ten blue links" approach that's still really useful, no matter what its critics say ... As for the quality, I'm not saying that Weinberg has figured out a way to return more relevant results than Google's mighty search team. But DuckDuckGo ... is really good at bringing back useful sites. It all feels meaty and straightforward and filler-free ...<sup id="cite_ref-McCracken16Jun11_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCracken16Jun11-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote><p> The barebones approach cited in his quote have since changed; DuckDuckGo now has auto-completion and instant results for example. McCracken included the site in <i>Time'</i>s list of "50 Best Websites of 2011".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thom Holwerda, who reviewed the search engine for <i><a href="/wiki/OSNews" title="OSNews">OSNews</a></i>, praised its privacy features and shortcuts to site-specific searches as well as criticizing Google for "track[ing] pretty much everything you do", particularly because of the risk of such information being subject to a <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">U.S. government</a> subpoena.<sup id="cite_ref-Holwerda21Jun11_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holwerda21Jun11-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, in response to accusations that it was a monopoly, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> identified DuckDuckGo as a competitor. Weinberg was reportedly "pleased and entertained" by that acknowledgment.<sup id="cite_ref-WashPostNov2012_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WashPostNov2012-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic">Traffic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=DuckDuckGo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Traffic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:30%;&#32;;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303512"/>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">It took 1,445 days to get 1M searches,<br />483 days to get 2M searches,<br />and then just 8 days to pass 3M searches: <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="https://duckduckgo.com/traffic/">https://duckduckgo.com/traffic/</a></div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">DuckDuckGo <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account (@duckduckgo), 18 June 2013<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sel3m_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sel3m-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nextweb3m_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nextweb3m-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div><p>In June 2013, DuckDuckGo indicated that it had seen a significant traffic increase; according to the website's <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account, on Monday, June 17, 2013, it had three million daily direct searches. On average during May 2013, it had 1.8 million daily direct searches. Some<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> relate this claim to the exposure of <a href="/wiki/PRISM_(surveillance_program)" title="PRISM (surveillance program)">PRISM</a> and to the fact that other programs operated by the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Agency" title="National Security Agency">National Security Agency</a> (NSA) were leaked by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden" title="Edward Snowden">Edward Snowden</a>. <a href="/wiki/Danny_Sullivan_(technologist)" title="Danny Sullivan (technologist)">Danny Sullivan</a> wrote on <i>Search Engine Land</i> that despite the search engine's growth "it's not grown anywhere near the amount to reflect any substantial or even mildly notable switching by the searching public" for reasons due to privacy, and he concluded "No One Cares About "Private" Search".<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> In response, Caleb Garling of the <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> argued: "I think this thesis suffers from a few key failures in logic" because a traffic increase had occurred and because there was a lack of widespread awareness of the existence of DuckDuckGo.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Later in September 2013, the search engine hit 4 million searches per day.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of August 2018, DDG was receiving 23 million direct queries per day.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
