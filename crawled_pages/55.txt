<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Overture</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Overture_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Overture (disambiguation)">Overture (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Overture</b> (from <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> <i>ouverture</i>, <abbr title="literally">lit.</abbr> "opening") in <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> is the term originally applied to the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental" title="Instrumental">instrumental</a> introduction to an <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Blom_1954_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blom_1954-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> During the early <a href="/wiki/Romantic_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantic era">Romantic era</a>, composers such as <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Beethoven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Mendelssohn</a> began to use the term to refer to independent, self-existing instrumental, programmatic works that presaged genres such as the <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_poem" title="Symphonic poem">symphonic poem</a>. These were "at first undoubtedly intended to be played at the head of a programme".<sup id="cite_ref-Blom_1954_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blom_1954-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="17th_century">17th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 17th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The idea of an instrumental opening to <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a> existed during the 17th century. <a href="/wiki/Jacopo_Peri" title="Jacopo Peri">Peri</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Euridice_(opera)" class="mw-redirect" title="Euridice (opera)">Euridice</a></i> opens with a brief instrumental <a href="/wiki/Ritornello" title="Ritornello">ritornello</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Claudio_Monteverdi" title="Claudio Monteverdi">Monteverdi</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/L%27Orfeo" title="L&#39;Orfeo">L'Orfeo</a></i> (1607) opens with a <a href="/wiki/Toccata" title="Toccata">toccata</a>, in this case a <a href="/wiki/Fanfare" title="Fanfare">fanfare</a> for muted <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpets</a>. More important, however, was the prologue, which comprised sung dialogue between <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorical</a> characters which introduced the overarching themes of the stories depicted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="French_overture">French overture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: French overture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As a <a href="/wiki/Musical_form" title="Musical form">musical form</a>, however, the <a href="/wiki/French_overture" title="French overture">French overture</a> first appears in the court ballet and operatic overtures of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lully" title="Jean-Baptiste Lully">Jean-Baptiste Lully</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> which he elaborated from a similar, two-section form called <i>Ouverture</i>, found in the French <a href="/wiki/Ballets_de_cour" title="Ballets de cour">ballets de cour</a> as early as 1640.<sup id="cite_ref-Temperley_2001_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Temperley_2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This French overture consists of a slow introduction in a marked "dotted rhythm" (i.e., exaggerated <a href="/wiki/Iamb_(foot)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iamb (foot)">iambic</a>, if the first chord is disregarded), followed by a lively movement in <a href="/wiki/Fugato" class="mw-redirect" title="Fugato">fugato</a> style. The overture was frequently followed by a series of dance tunes before the curtain rose, and would often<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (February 2013)">vague</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> return following the Prologue to introduce the action proper. This ouverture style was also used in English opera, most notably in <a href="/wiki/Henry_Purcell" title="Henry Purcell">Henry Purcell</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dido_and_%C3%86neas" class="mw-redirect" title="Dido and Æneas">Dido and Æneas</a></i>. Its distinctive rhythmic profile and function thus led to the French overture style as found in the works of late Baroque composers such as <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_Friedrich_H%C3%A4ndel" class="mw-redirect" title="Georg Friedrich Händel">Georg Friedrich Händel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georg_Philipp_Telemann" title="Georg Philipp Telemann">Georg Philipp Telemann</a>. The style is most often used in <a href="/wiki/Prelude_(music)" title="Prelude (music)">preludes</a> to <a href="/wiki/Suite_(music)" title="Suite (music)">suites</a>, and can be found in non-staged vocal works such as <a href="/wiki/Cantata" title="Cantata">cantatas</a>, for example in the opening chorus of Bach's <a href="/wiki/Bach_cantata" title="Bach cantata">cantata</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Nun_komm,_der_Heiden_Heiland,_BWV_61" title="Nun komm, der Heiden Heiland, BWV 61">Nun komm, der Heiden Heiland, BWV 61</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">Handel</a> also uses the French overture form in some of his Italian operas such as <i><a href="/wiki/Giulio_Cesare" title="Giulio Cesare">Giulio Cesare</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_overture">Italian overture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Italian overture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_overture" title="Italian overture">Italian overture</a></div>
<p>In Italy, a distinct form called "overture" arose in the 1680s, and became established particularly through the operas of <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Scarlatti" title="Alessandro Scarlatti">Alessandro Scarlatti</a>, and spread throughout Europe, supplanting the French form as the standard operatic overture by the mid-18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_2001_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_2001-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Its stereotypical form is in three generally <a href="/wiki/Homophony" title="Homophony">homophonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Movement_(music)" title="Movement (music)">movements</a>: fast–slow–fast. The opening movement was normally in duple metre and in a major key; the slow movement in earlier examples was usually quite<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="Who says this? (February 2013)">weasel&#160;words</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> short, and could be in a contrasting key; the concluding movement was dance-like, most often with rhythms of the <a href="/wiki/Gigue" title="Gigue">gigue</a> or <a href="/wiki/Minuet" title="Minuet">minuet</a>, and returned to the key of the opening section. As the form evolved, the first movement often<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (February 2013)">vague</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> incorporated fanfare-like elements and took on the pattern of so-called "sonatina form" (<a href="/wiki/Sonata_form" title="Sonata form">sonata form</a> without a development section), and the slow section became more extended and lyrical.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisher_2001_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisher_2001-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Italian overtures were often detached from their operas and played as independent concert pieces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (February 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In this context, they became important in the early history of the <a href="/wiki/Symphony" title="Symphony">symphony</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century">18th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 18th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to the 18th century, the symphony and the overture were almost interchangeable, with overtures being extracted from operas to serve as stand alone instrumental works, and symphonies were tagged to the front of operas as overtures.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> With the reform of <i>opera seria,</i> the overture began to distinguish itself from the symphony, and composers began to link the content of overtures to their operas dramatically and emotionally.  Elements from the opera are foreshadowed in the overture, following the reform ideology that the music and every other element on stages serves to enhance the plot.  One such overture was that of <i><a href="/wiki/La_Magnifique" class="mw-redirect" title="La Magnifique">La Magnifique</a></i> by André-Ernest-Modeste Grétry, in which several of the arias are quoted.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th-century_opera">19th-century opera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 19th-century opera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 19th-century opera the overture, <i>Vorspiel</i>, <i>Einleitung,</i> Introduction, or whatever else it may be called, is generally nothing more definite than that portion of the music which takes place before the curtain rises. <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Richard Wagner</a>'s <i>Vorspiel</i> to <i><a href="/wiki/Lohengrin_(opera)" title="Lohengrin (opera)">Lohengrin</a></i> is a short self-contained movement founded on the music of the Grail.
</p><p>In Italian opera after about 1800, the "overture" became known as the <i>sinfonia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FISH_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FISH-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  Fisher also notes the term <i>Sinfonia avanti l'opera</i> (literally, the "symphony before the opera") was "an early term for a sinfonia used to begin an opera, that is, as an overture as opposed to one serving to begin a later section of the work".<sup id="cite_ref-FISH_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FISH-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concert_overture">Concert overture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Concert overture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_19th_century">Early 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although by the end of the eighteenth century opera overtures were already beginning to be performed as separate items in the concert hall,  the "concert overture", intended specifically as an individual concert piece without reference to stage performance and generally based  on some literary theme, began to appear early in the <a href="/wiki/Romantic_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantic era">Romantic era</a>. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Maria_von_Weber" title="Carl Maria von Weber">Carl Maria von Weber</a> wrote two concert overtures, <i>Der Beherrscher der Geister</i> ('The Ruler of the Spirits', 1811, a revision of the overture to his unfinished opera <i><a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCbezahl" title="Rübezahl">Rübezahl</a></i> of 1805), and <i>Jubel-Ouvertüre</i> ('Jubilee Overture', 1818, incorporating <i><a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_King" class="mw-redirect" title="God Save the King">God Save the King</a></i> at its climax). However, the overture <i><a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream_(Mendelssohn)" title="A Midsummer Night&#39;s Dream (Mendelssohn)">A Midsummer Night's Dream</a></i> (1826) by <a href="/wiki/Felix_Mendelssohn" title="Felix Mendelssohn">Felix Mendelssohn</a> is generally regarded as the first concert overture (Temperley 2001). Mendelssohn's other contributions to this genre include his <i><a href="/wiki/Calm_Sea_and_Prosperous_Voyage_(Mendelssohn)" title="Calm Sea and Prosperous Voyage (Mendelssohn)">Calm Sea and Prosperous Voyage</a></i> overture (1828), his overture <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hebrides_(overture)" title="The Hebrides (overture)">The Hebrides</a></i> (1830; also known as <i>Fingal's Cave</i>) and the overtures <i>Die schöne Melusine</i> (<i>The Fair Melusine</i>, 1834) and <i>Ruy Blas</i> (1839).  Other notable early concert overtures were written by <a href="/wiki/Hector_Berlioz" title="Hector Berlioz">Hector Berlioz</a> (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Les_francs-juges" title="Les francs-juges">Les Francs juges</a></i> (1826), and <i><a href="/wiki/Overtures_by_Hector_Berlioz#Le_corsaire" title="Overtures by Hector Berlioz">Le corsaire</a></i> (1828)).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_19th_century">Later 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Later 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1850s the concert overture began to be supplanted by the <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_poem" title="Symphonic poem">symphonic poem</a>, a form devised by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Liszt" title="Franz Liszt">Franz Liszt</a> in several works that began as dramatic overtures. The distinction between the two genres was the freedom to mould the musical form according to external <a href="/wiki/Program_music" title="Program music">programmatic</a> requirements (Temperley 2001). The symphonic poem became the preferred form for the more "progressive" composers, such as <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Franck" title="César Franck">César Franck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strauss" title="Richard Strauss">Richard Strauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Scriabin" title="Alexander Scriabin">Alexander Scriabin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>, while more conservative composers like <a href="/wiki/Anton_Rubinstein" title="Anton Rubinstein">Anton Rubinstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Tchaikovsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Brahms" title="Johannes Brahms">Johannes Brahms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schumann" title="Robert Schumann">Robert Schumann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Sullivan" title="Arthur Sullivan">Arthur Sullivan</a> remained faithful to the overture.<sup id="cite_ref-Temperley_2001_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Temperley_2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="999.144" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky_-_1812_overture.ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/04/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky_-_1812_overture.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (138 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="137751" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/0/04/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky_-_1812_overture.ogg/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky_-_1812_overture.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="186744" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky_-_1812_overture.ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tchaikovsky's <i>1812 Overture</i></div></div></div>
<p>In the age when the symphonic poem had already become popular, Brahms wrote his <i><a href="/wiki/Academic_Festival_Overture" title="Academic Festival Overture">Academic Festival Overture</a></i>, Op. 80, as well as his <i><a href="/wiki/Tragic_Overture" class="mw-redirect" title="Tragic Overture">Tragic Overture</a></i>, Op. 81. An example clearly influenced by the symphonic poem is  Tchaikovsky's <i><a href="/wiki/1812_Overture" title="1812 Overture">1812 Overture</a></i>. His equally well-known <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet_(Tchaikovsky)" title="Romeo and Juliet (Tchaikovsky)">Romeo and Juliet</a></i> is also labelled a 'fantasy-overture'.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In European music after 1900, an example of an overture displaying a connection with the traditional form is <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Shostakovich" title="Dmitri Shostakovich">Dmitri Shostakovich</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Festive_Overture_(Shostakovich)" title="Festive Overture (Shostakovich)">Festive Overture</a></i>, Op. 96 (1954), which is in two linked sections, "Allegretto" and "Presto" (Temperely 2001).  <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Arnold" title="Malcolm Arnold">Malcolm Arnold</a>'s <i>A Grand, Grand Overture</i>, Op. 57 (1956), is a 20th-century parody of the late 19th century concert overture, scored for an enormous orchestra with organ, additional <a href="/wiki/Brass_instrument" title="Brass instrument">brass instruments</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Obbligato" title="Obbligato">obbligato</a> parts for four <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifles</a>, three <a href="/wiki/The_Hoover_Company" title="The Hoover Company">Hoover</a> <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_cleaner" title="Vacuum cleaner">vacuum cleaners</a> (two uprights in B<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>, one horizontal with detachable sucker in C), and an electric <a href="/wiki/Floor_polisher" class="mw-redirect" title="Floor polisher">floor polisher</a> in E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span class="music-flat">&#x266d;</span></span>; it is dedicated "to <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">President Hoover</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Motion_pictures" class="mw-redirect" title="Motion pictures">motion pictures</a>, an overture is a piece of music setting the mood for the film before the <a href="/wiki/Opening_credits" title="Opening credits">opening credits</a> start. For a comprehensive list, see the <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_with_overtures" title="List of films with overtures">list of films with overtures</a>.
</p>
