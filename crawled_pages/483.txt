<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland</b> (<b>HSC</b>) is the designation of the <a href="/wiki/Publicly-funded_health_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Publicly-funded health care">publicly funded</a> service which provides public health and other social care services in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> through its <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Health_(Northern_Ireland)" title="Department of Health (Northern Ireland)">Department of Health</a> is responsible for its funding, while the <b>Public Health Agency</b> is the executive agency responsible for the provision of health and social care services across Northern Ireland. It is free of charge to all citizens of Northern Ireland and the rest of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>For services such as A&amp;E, patients simply walk in, state their name and date of birth, are given treatment and then leave. Patients are unaware of costs incurred by them using the service. It is sometimes called the "<a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a>", as in England, Scotland and Wales, but differs from the NHS in England and Wales in that it provides not only health care but social care too (the NHS in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> also includes social care). Social services are provided by local councils. The Health and Social Care service was created by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Parliament of Northern Ireland">Parliament of Northern Ireland</a> in 1948 after the <a href="/wiki/Beveridge_Report" title="Beveridge Report">Beveridge Report</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hospitals in the region were managed by the Northern Ireland Hospitals Authority and Hospital Management Committees from 1948 to 1974, and then transferred to four health and social services boards, along with responsibility for social care.  The pattern of local government in the region was of 26 single-tier local authorities which, apart from <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, covered small populations ranging from 13,000 to 90,000 and were not considered an adequate base for the provision of personal social services.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Health and Social Care (Reform) Act (Northern Ireland) 2009<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> led to a reorganisation of health and social care delivery in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, reducing the number of organisations involved. This Act established the <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_Board" title="Health and Social Care Board">Health and Social Care Board</a> and five Health and Social Care Trusts which are responsible for the delivery of primary, secondary and community health care. The act also established five local commissioning groups which work in parallel with the health and social care trusts.
</p><p>The Department of Health is now organised under a <a href="/wiki/Permanent_Secretary" class="mw-redirect" title="Permanent Secretary">Permanent Secretary</a> into several groups and one agency. These are the Planning and Resources Group, Strategic Planning and Modernisation Group and Primary, Secondary and Community Care Group and the 5 Professional Groups. The Department's executive agency responsible for the oversight, coordination and overall provision of health and social care services is the Public Health Agency.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The five professional groups are
</p>
<ul><li>Medical and Allied Services<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Social Services Inspectorate<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Nursing and Midwifery Advisory Group<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Dental Services<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Pharmaceutical Advice and Services<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trusts">Trusts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trusts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trusts are the statutory bodies responsible for the management of staff, health and social care services on the ground and have control their own budgets. The 19 Health and Social Services Trusts were merged into six Health and Social Care Trusts which became operational on 1 April 2007.
</p><p>The 5 regional trusts are:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Belfast_Health_and_Social_Care_Trust" title="Belfast Health and Social Care Trust">Belfast Health and Social Care Trust</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Northern_Health_and_Social_Care_Trust" title="Northern Health and Social Care Trust">Northern Health and Social Care Trust</a></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Eastern_Health_and_Social_Care_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="South Eastern Health and Social Care Trust (page does not exist)">South Eastern Health and Social Care Trust</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Southern_Health_and_Social_Care_Trust" title="Southern Health and Social Care Trust">Southern Health and Social Care Trust</a></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Health_and_Social_Care_Trust&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Western Health and Social Care Trust (page does not exist)">Western Health and Social Care Trust</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambulance_Service">Ambulance Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ambulance Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Northern Ireland also has its own dedicated <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Ambulance_Service" title="Northern Ireland Ambulance Service">Ambulance Service</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_Care">Primary Care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Primary Care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are about 350 GP Practices in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016 <a href="/wiki/Michelle_O%27Neill" title="Michelle O&#39;Neill">Michelle O'Neill</a> produced a plan to install a named district nurse, health visitor and social worker in every practice.  54 pharmacists are to start in practices in December 2016 with one in every practice by 2021.  The number of GP training places is to increase to 111.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Medical_Association" title="British Medical Association">British Medical Association</a> voted to start collecting undated resignations from Northern Ireland GPs in January 2017 in protest against the underfunding of general practice in the province. Less than 60% were collected. There was a decline in partners take-home pay in 2016/17, to £90,500. An investment package of £22 million was announced in 2018. £12.3 million is for pharmacists in practices.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In May 2018 a £100 million Health and Social Care transformation funding package was announced, including £5 million for Multi Disciplinary Teams in two areas initially, each of about 100,000 people, which will involve the establishment of practice-based physiotherapists, mental health specialists and social workers working alongside doctors and nurses.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first community hub for primary care in the province is to be established in <a href="/wiki/Cookstown" title="Cookstown">Cookstown</a>, backed by four local GP practices and the health board.  It is to incorporate scanning facilities, a minor surgery suite, a pharmacy, out-of-hours consultations and community healthcare partnerships, with the possibility of developing supported living accommodation for older people.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prescription_charges">Prescription charges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prescription charges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 29 September 2008 Health minister <a href="/wiki/Michael_McGimpsey" title="Michael McGimpsey">Michael McGimpsey</a> announced that <a href="/wiki/Prescription_charges" title="Prescription charges">Prescription charges</a> were to be phased out by April 2010, being first reduced to £3.00 in January 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This was widely accepted by the four main parties of the Northern Ireland Executive plus the Alliance Party of Northern Ireland. The move brought Northern Ireland in line with the Scottish and Welsh system's which had already abolished charges.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Northern Ireland health and social care have been part of the same structure since 1974 but according to <a href="/wiki/Terry_Bamford" title="Terry Bamford">Terry Bamford</a> "integration has failed to address a reliance on hospitals and institutional care which is significantly greater than elsewhere in the UK." He says that there are various reasons for this. It is difficult to get resources out of acute care without closing buildings, which is a political problem particularly in rural areas. Information technology systems may not be compatible and patient confidentiality hinders the sharing of information. Health services are free but social care is means tested.  But, he says, "the greatest difficulties lie in the different cultures and values of health and social care." <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until 2014 the <a href="/wiki/Kingsbridge_private_hospital" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingsbridge private hospital">Kingsbridge private hospital</a> in Belfast was used to reduce waiting lists for routine surgery.  In September 2015 the Northern Ireland Health and Social Care Board admitted that waiting lists for surgery had grown and that they did not have the money to pay either NHS providers or the private sector to bring them down.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The NHS waiting list target in Northern Ireland is 52 weeks, not 18 as in England, and it isn't met. 10% of healthcare provided in the region is paid for privately.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A report by the <a href="/wiki/Nuffield_Trust" title="Nuffield Trust">Nuffield Trust</a> in 2017 showed that though spending per head in the province at £2,200 a year was much the same as the rest of the UK the performance of the system was much worse.  Using the <a href="/wiki/NHS_targets" title="NHS targets">NHS targets</a> more than 20% of patients waited more than 4 hours in A&amp;E departments, and sometimes 30%. About 16% of the population were on a waiting list, compared with around 7% for the rest of the UK. More than 64,000 of them, around a quarter, had been waiting over a year for their first outpatient appointment.  In the last 16 years there had been 7 government reports calling for a move to more stress on prevention and away from hospital based care.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
