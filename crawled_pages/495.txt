<p><b>Irreligion in the United Kingdom</b> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnosticism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nontheism" title="Nontheism">nontheism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularism</a>, or, more generally, the lack of a <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> in the country. Historically, the growth of irreligion in the UK has followed a European-wide pattern of <a href="/wiki/Secularisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secularisation">secularisation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first British census to gather data on religion, <a href="/wiki/2001_United_Kingdom_census" class="mw-redirect" title="2001 United Kingdom census">in 2001</a>, showed that there were 7.7 million irreligious people in the country. The wording of the question was criticised by humanist groups, and a revised question in the next census in 2011 counted 14.1 million irreligious people. This made irreligion the second-largest viewpoint on religion after Christianity, larger than the combined populations of non-Christian religions in the UK, and also the largest growth between the two censuses. <a href="/wiki/Humanists_UK" title="Humanists UK">Humanists UK</a> is the charity that provides services for this community and lobbies government on their behalf.
</p><p>Freedom to be irreligious had been limited by the country's <a href="/wiki/Blasphemy_law_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Blasphemy law in the United Kingdom">blasphemy law</a>, which punished criticism and mockery of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, particularly the state <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. The last conviction was in <a href="/wiki/Whitehouse_v_Lemon" title="Whitehouse v Lemon">1977</a>, with the law being abolished in England and Wales in 2008. Similar offences remain in statute in Scotland and Northern Ireland, and remain theoretically incompatible with the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span id="1700.E2.80.931850"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1700–1850">1700–1850</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irreligion_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 1700–1850">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Organized activism for irreligion in the United Kingdom derived its roots from the legacy of British <a href="/wiki/Nonconformism" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonconformism">nonconformists</a>. The <a href="/wiki/South_Place_Ethical_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="South Place Ethical Society">South Place Religious Society</a>, which would later become associated with the Ethical movement, was founded in 1793 as an organization of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphians" title="Philadelphians">Philadelphians</a> or Universalists.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Oracle_of_Reason" title="The Oracle of Reason">The Oracle of Reason</a></i>, the first avowedly-atheist periodical publication in British history, was published from 1841 to 1843 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Southwell" title="Charles Southwell">Charles Southwell</a>. It suffered from numerous imprisonments of its staff, including Southwell, George Holyoake and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Paterson_(publisher)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas Paterson (publisher) (page does not exist)">Thomas Paterson</a>, for missives deemed "blasphemous" by the authorities (Holyoake was the last person in Britain convicted of blasphemy in a public lecture). Holyoake took to publishing <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Movement_(magazine)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Movement (magazine) (page does not exist)">The Movement</a></i> (1842-1845) following his six-month sentence, which later became <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Reasoner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Reasoner (page does not exist)">The Reasoner</a></i> (1845-1860) and shifted to a larger focus on social issues facing the British working class, increasing the publication's readership. It was during this time that Holyoake developed his idea for the replacement of Christianity with an ethical system based upon science and reason, terming his proposal "secularism".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="1850.E2.80.931900"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1850–1900">1850–1900</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irreligion_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1850–1900">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Bradlaugh.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Charles_Bradlaugh.jpg/200px-Charles_Bradlaugh.jpg" width="200" height="273" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Charles_Bradlaugh.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="258" data-file-height="352" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Bradlaugh.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_Bradlaugh" title="Charles Bradlaugh">Charles Bradlaugh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northampton_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Northampton (UK Parliament constituency)">Member of Parliament for Northampton</a> and founder of the <a href="/wiki/National_Secular_Society" title="National Secular Society">National Secular Society</a> in 1866.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/George_Holyoake" title="George Holyoake">George Holyoake</a>'s coining of the word <i><a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularism</a></i> in 1851 offered the English-speaking world the clarification of the nascent movement for separation of religion and state. The <a href="/wiki/National_Secular_Society" title="National Secular Society">National Secular Society</a>, founded in 1866 by politician <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bradlaugh" title="Charles Bradlaugh">Charles Bradlaugh</a>, spearheaded the advocacy for freeing citizens from absolute government requirements involving religious observances; the <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Secular_Society" title="Leicester Secular Society">Leicester Secular Society</a> was founded in 1851. Bradlaugh's 1880 election to Parliament brought on a decade-long dispute over the demanded right to affirm declarations of office rather than swear oaths, as he was denied his seat for five years by a ruling that he had no right to affirm and resolutions preventing him from swearing an oath. When Bradlaugh was ultimately admitted in 1886, he took up the issue and saw the <a href="/wiki/Oaths_Act_1888" title="Oaths Act 1888">Oaths Act 1888</a> passed, which confirmed the right to optionally affirm declarations for inaugurations to office and offering testimony to government bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In 1881, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Freethinker_(journal)" title="The Freethinker (journal)">The Freethinker</a></i> began circulation as Britain's longest-running humanist periodical. In 1891, the <a href="/wiki/British_Humanist_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="British Humanist Association">Union of Ethical Societies</a> was formed in the United Kingdom by American <a href="/wiki/Stanton_Coit" title="Stanton Coit">Stanton Coit</a> as a union of British <a href="/wiki/Ethical_movement" title="Ethical movement">Ethical movement</a> societies; this group would later become known as the Ethical Union and the British Humanist Association.<sup id="cite_ref-humanism.org.uk_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humanism.org.uk-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1899, the <a href="/wiki/Rationalist_Association" title="Rationalist Association">Rational Press Association</a> was formed by a group of free-thinkers including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Albert_Watts" title="Charles Albert Watts">Charles Albert Watts</a> and George Holyoake.
</p><p>Meanwhile, the South Place Religious Society became further aligned with organized secularist advocacy during the tenure of <a href="/wiki/Moncure_D._Conway" title="Moncure D. Conway">Moncure D. Conway</a> as minister of the congregation; Conway, an American Unitarian minister who served from 1864–1885 and 1892–1897, moved the congregation further away from doctrinal Unitarianism, and spent the break in his tenure (during which Stanton Coit served in his stead) writing a biography of American revolutionary ideologue <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Paine" title="Thomas Paine">Thomas Paine</a>. In 1888, the South Place Religious Society became the South Place Ethical Society, now known as the <a href="/wiki/Conway_Hall_Ethical_Society" title="Conway Hall Ethical Society">Conway Hall Ethical Society</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irreligion_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg/200px-Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg" width="200" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg/300px-Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg/400px-Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1438" data-file-height="1798" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Richard_Dawkins_Cooper_Union_Shankbone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a> has been a significant figure in irreligion since the 1970s</div></div></div> 
<p>The 1960s were a significant time for irreligion, as the Ethical Union rebranded as the British Humanist Association, which went on to co-found the <a href="/wiki/International_Humanist_and_Ethical_Union" title="International Humanist and Ethical Union">International Humanist and Ethical Union</a> and create a symbol for <a href="/wiki/Secular_humanism" title="Secular humanism">humanism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Human" title="Happy Human">Happy Human</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-humanism.org.uk_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-humanism.org.uk-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Broadcasters such as <a href="/wiki/Margaret_K._Knight" title="Margaret K. Knight">Margaret K. Knight</a> sensationalised Britain with open advocacy of non-religious values and secular education.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Senior figures in the British humanist movement went out to take on leading roles in institutions such as <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>, the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Health Organisation">World Health Organisation</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Agriculture Organisation">Food and Agriculture Organisation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_William_Gott" title="John William Gott">John William Gott</a>, a working man of <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, attacked religion, especially <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, seeing it as reducing the opportunity for a <a href="/wiki/Socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist">socialist</a> revolution. His lectures on rationalism and scepticism, and anti-Christian pamphlets, saw him jailed for blasphemy in 1911. Liberal Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a> was one of a group of Members of Parliament who proposed an ultimately unsuccessful piece of legislation to abolish blasphemy offences. Gott was jailed again ten years later for a pamphlet showing <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Clown" title="Clown">clown</a>, and died in 1922 soon after his nine-month sentence which included hard labour despite his worsening physical condition. There was a public backlash against his sentence.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gott was the last Briton jailed for blasphemy, but the offence remained a technical crime through <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> until being abolished in the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Justice_and_Immigration_Act_2008" title="Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008">Criminal Justice and Immigration Act 2008</a>.
</p><p>Evolutionary biologist <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" title="Richard Dawkins">Richard Dawkins</a>, who first came to prominence in 1976 following the release of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Selfish_Gene" title="The Selfish Gene">The Selfish Gene</a></i>, increasingly figured in British irreligion with the release of his 1986 work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Blind_Watchmaker" title="The Blind Watchmaker">The Blind Watchmaker</a></i>, in which he argued in favour of evolutionary natural selection as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_design" title="Intelligent design">intelligent design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Creationism" title="Creationism">creationism</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irreligion_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:West_from_castle_mound.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/West_from_castle_mound.jpg/200px-West_from_castle_mound.jpg" width="200" height="301" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/West_from_castle_mound.jpg/300px-West_from_castle_mound.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/West_from_castle_mound.jpg/400px-West_from_castle_mound.jpg 2x" data-file-width="681" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:West_from_castle_mound.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The City of <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a>, according to the United Kingdom's 2011 census, is the most irreligious local authority in England, with 42.5% professing no religious affiliation.</div></div></div>
<p>In the 21st century, <a href="/wiki/New_Atheism" title="New Atheism">New Atheism</a> became a popular topic of debate, support and critique in the United Kingdom. Dawkins' 2006 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_God_Delusion" title="The God Delusion">The God Delusion</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hitchens" title="Christopher Hitchens">Christopher Hitchens</a>' 2007 book <i><a href="/wiki/God_Is_Not_Great" title="God Is Not Great">God Is Not Great</a></i> were considered emblematic works of the era among British authors, and Dawkins advocated for the <a href="/wiki/Brights_movement" title="Brights movement">Brights movement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Atheist_Bus_Campaign" title="Atheist Bus Campaign">Atheist Bus Campaign</a> was inaugurated during this time, in which advertisements on double-decker buses were purchased by the <a href="/wiki/British_Humanist_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="British Humanist Association">British Humanist Association</a> in order to advocate non-belief in the supernatural; the campaign caused controversy and complaints to authorities, but soon spread to other countries and continents, taking root in the United States as a variety of <a href="/wiki/Atheist_billboard" title="Atheist billboard">atheist billboard</a> campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Atheism is expected to be the dominant position on the existence of God by 2038.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A 2009 survey of 1,000 teenagers aged 13 to 18 reports that two-thirds of British teenagers do not believe in God.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rise in irreligion was confirmed in the UK's <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 census</a>, which saw irreligion rise from 7.7 million in 2001 to 14.1 million, a rise of 10.3 percentage points. The local authority with the highest level of irreligion was <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a>, the county town of <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>, where the level was 42.5%.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Religion has the least influence on youth.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> According to the 2011 census, 25% of England has no religion, 7% of Northern Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> one third in Scotland and one-third of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/YouGov" title="YouGov">YouGov</a>, Christianity is perceived to be on the decline.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Mori Polls have shown that British Christians support a secular state.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Britons are amongst the most skeptical about religion.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Irreligious_British_politicians">Irreligious British politicians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irreligion_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Irreligious British politicians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Non-religious people have risen to the highest ranks of UK politics, including at least three Prime Ministers, one Deputy Prime Minister, and several Leaders of the Opposition. <a href="/wiki/Clement_Attlee" title="Clement Attlee">Clement Attlee</a>, Prime Minister for the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> from 1945 to 1951, rejected organised religion and claims of the supernatural his whole life, despite growing up in a Christian household.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a>, also of Labour, was Prime Minister from 1976 to 1979. He left the <a href="/wiki/Baptist_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist Church">Baptist Church</a> as a young man when he became more involved in politics.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> (1940&#8211;1945 and 1951&#8211;1955) was an atheist, saying  "I do not accept the Christian or any other form of religious belief".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Past Opposition <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> leaders <a href="/wiki/Michael_Foot" title="Michael Foot">Michael Foot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neil_Kinnock" title="Neil Kinnock">Neil Kinnock</a> were also atheists and humanists. Foot, much like Neil and <a href="/wiki/Glenys_Kinnock" class="mw-redirect" title="Glenys Kinnock">Glenys Kinnock</a>, was a patron of <a href="/wiki/Humanists_UK" title="Humanists UK">Humanists UK</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_Morgan" title="Rhodri Morgan">Rhodri Morgan</a>, former MP and <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Wales" title="First Minister of Wales">First Minister of Wales</a> from 2000 to 2009, was an atheist and a humanist.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other influential atheists and humanists in British history include NHS founder <a href="/wiki/Nye_Bevan" class="mw-redirect" title="Nye Bevan">Nye Bevan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and "father of the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>" <a href="/wiki/William_Beveridge" title="William Beveridge">William Beveridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris1-323_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris1-323-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Contemporary British atheist politicians include <a href="/wiki/Ken_Livingstone" title="Ken Livingstone">Ken Livingstone</a> of Labour, who was <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>'s first mayor between 2000 and 2008. He left Christianity at the age of 11.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Former <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Prime Minister">Deputy Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Nick_Clegg" title="Nick Clegg">Nick Clegg</a> (2010&#8211;2015) is an <a href="/wiki/Agnostic_atheist" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnostic atheist">agnostic atheist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-typepad1_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-typepad1-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a>, a former leader of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> from 2010 to 2015, is also a <a href="/wiki/Jewish_atheist" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish atheist">Jewish atheist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Outside of elected politicians, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street_Director_of_Communications" title="Downing Street Director of Communications">Director of Communications</a> <a href="/wiki/Alastair_Campbell" title="Alastair Campbell">Alastair Campbell</a> is an atheist and was infamous during his time in post for once telling a journalist "We don't do god".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Over 100 Parliamentarians in the UK are members of the <a href="/wiki/All-Party_Parliamentary_Humanist_Group" title="All-Party Parliamentary Humanist Group">All-Party Parliamentary Humanist Group</a>, which means the non-religious have substantial representation among MPs and Lords.<sup id="cite_ref-BHA-APPHG_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BHA-APPHG-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
