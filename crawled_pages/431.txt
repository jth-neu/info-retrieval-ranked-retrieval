<p><b>Garret Desmond FitzGerald</b> (9 February 1926&#160;&#8211;&#32;19 May 2011) was an Irish <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a> politician who served as <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> from 1981 to 1982 and 1982 to 1987, <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_Fine_Gael" title="Leader of Fine Gael">Leader of Fine Gael</a> from 1977 to 1987, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Irish_Leaders_of_the_Opposition" title="List of Irish Leaders of the Opposition">Leader of the Opposition</a> from 1977 to 1981 and March 1982 to December 1982 and <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs and Trade">Minister for Foreign Affairs</a> from 1973 to 1977. He served as a <a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta Dála">Teachta Dála</a> (TD) from 1969 to 1992. He was a <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Senator</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_and_Commercial_Panel" title="Industrial and Commercial Panel">Industrial and Commercial Panel</a> from 1965 to 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oireachtas_db_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oireachtas_db-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was the son of <a href="/wiki/Desmond_FitzGerald_(politician)" title="Desmond FitzGerald (politician)">Desmond FitzGerald</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs and Trade">Minister for External Affairs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>. At the time of his death, FitzGerald was the President of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_International_and_European_Affairs" title="Institute of International and European Affairs">Institute of International and European Affairs</a>, had a column in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Times" title="The Irish Times">The Irish Times</a></i> and made occasional appearances on television programmes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Garret FitzGerald was born in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> in 1926, into a very politically active family. His mother <a href="/wiki/Mabel_McConnell_Fitzgerald" title="Mabel McConnell Fitzgerald">Mabel McConnell Fitzgerald</a> was involved in politics and it was through her that his father became political.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Desmond_FitzGerald_(politician)" title="Desmond FitzGerald (politician)">Desmond FitzGerald</a> was born in London and raised. He was Minister for External Affairs at the time of his son's birth.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-obit_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-obit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> FitzGerald senior, whose father had emigrated as a labourer, from <a href="/wiki/Skeheenarinky" title="Skeheenarinky">Skeheenarinky</a> in County Tipperary, had joined the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Volunteers" title="Irish Volunteers">Irish Volunteers</a> in 1914 and fought during the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">1916 Easter Rising</a>. Desmond FitzGerald had been active in <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>, and had been one of the founders of <a href="/wiki/Cumann_na_nGaedheal" title="Cumann na nGaedheal">Cumann na nGaedheal</a>. The party was formed to support the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> of 1921, which created the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although a senior figure on the pro-treaty side of Ireland's political divide, Desmond FitzGerald had remained friendly with anti-Treaty <a href="/wiki/Irish_republicanism" title="Irish republicanism">republicans</a>, such as Belfast man <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_MacEntee" title="Seán MacEntee">Seán MacEntee</a>, a Minister, in <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="Éamon de Valera">Éamon de Valera</a>'s government, and father-in-law of <a href="/wiki/Conor_Cruise_O%27Brien" title="Conor Cruise O&#39;Brien">Conor Cruise O'Brien</a>. The families of <a href="/wiki/Patrick_McGilligan" title="Patrick McGilligan">Patrick McGilligan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Blythe" title="Ernest Blythe">Ernest Blythe</a>, were also frequent visitors to the FitzGerald household. FitzGerald's mother, the former Mabel Washington McConnell, was a <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">nationalist</a> and republican of <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Ulster Protestant</a> descent, although some sources<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2010)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> indicate that she became a Catholic on her marriage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Her son would later describe his political objective as the creation of a pluralist Ireland where the northern Protestants of his mother's family tradition and the southern Catholics of his father's could feel equally at home.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>FitzGerald was educated at the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuit</a> <a href="/wiki/Belvedere_College" title="Belvedere College">Belvedere College</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_College_Dublin" title="University College Dublin">University College Dublin</a> (UCD), from which he graduated with a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1946, later returning to complete a PhD which he obtained in 1968. He was deeply interested in the politics of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. A bright student who counted among his contemporaries in UCD, his future political rival, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Haughey" title="Charles Haughey">Charles Haughey</a>, who also knew Joan O'Farrell (the Liverpool-born daughter of a British army officer, Richard O'Farrell) a fellow student, whom FitzGerald married in 1947. Their children were <a href="/wiki/John_D._FitzGerald" title="John D. FitzGerald">John</a>, Mary, Mark and Desmond.<sup id="cite_ref-Tel_obit_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tel_obit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following his university education, in 1947, he started working with <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a>, the state airline of Ireland, and became an authority on the strategic economic planning of transport. During this time he wrote many newspaper articles, was the Irish correspondent for British magazine <a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and was encouraged to write on National Accounts and economics by the Features Editor in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Times" title="The Irish Times">The Irish Times</a></i>. He remained in Aer Lingus until 1959, when after undertaking a study of the economics of Irish Industry in <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Dublin" class="mw-redirect" title="Trinity College, Dublin">Trinity College, Dublin</a>, he became a lecturer in economics at UCD.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Fitzgerald qualified as a barrister, from the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Inn" class="mw-redirect" title="King&#39;s Inn">King's Inn</a> of Ireland<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and spoke French fluently.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_political_life">Early political life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early political life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FitzGerald was eager to enter politics, and it was suggested by several members of <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Charles_Haughey" title="Charles Haughey">Charles Haughey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Yeats" title="Michael Yeats">Michael Yeats</a>, that he should join that party.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, FitzGerald, made his entry into party politics under the banner of Fine Gael. He attached himself to the liberal wing of Fine Gael, which rallied around the Just Society programme written by <a href="/wiki/Declan_Costello" title="Declan Costello">Declan Costello</a>. FitzGerald was elected to Seanad Éireann, for the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_and_Commercial_Panel" title="Industrial and Commercial Panel">Industrial and Commercial Panel</a> in 1965 and soon built up his political profile. FitzGerald was elected to Dáil Éireann at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1969" title="Irish general election, 1969">1969 general election</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_South-East_(D%C3%A1il_constituency)" title="Dublin South-East (Dáil constituency)">Dublin South-East</a> constituency,<sup id="cite_ref-elecs_irl_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elecs_irl-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the same year he obtained his PhD for a thesis later published under the title "Planning in Ireland". He became an important figure almost immediately in the parliamentary party and his liberal ideas were seen as a counterweight to the conservative leader, <a href="/wiki/Liam_Cosgrave" title="Liam Cosgrave">Liam Cosgrave</a>. Difference in political outlook, and FitzGerald's ambitions for the Fine Gael leadership resulted in profound tensions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> between the two men. In his leadership address to the 1972 Fine Gael ard fheis in Cork,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Cosgrave referred to the 'mongrel foxes' who should be rooted out of the party, a reference seen by many as an attack on FitzGerald's efforts to unseat him as leader.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs">Minister for Foreign Affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Minister for Foreign Affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1973" title="Irish general election, 1973">1973 general election</a>, Fine Gael came to power in a coalition government with the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Ireland)" title="Labour Party (Ireland)">Labour Party</a>, with Liam Cosgrave as Taoiseach. FitzGerald hoped<sup id="cite_ref-Independent-Obit_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-Obit-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> that he would take over as Minister for Finance, particularly after a good performance in a pre-election debate with the then Minister for Finance <a href="/wiki/George_Colley" title="George Colley">George Colley</a>. However the position went to <a href="/wiki/Richie_Ryan_(politician)" title="Richie Ryan (politician)">Richie Ryan</a>, with FitzGerald becoming <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs and Trade">Minister for Foreign Affairs</a>. It was a case of history repeating itself as FitzGerald's father had held that post in a government led by Liam Cosgrave's father <a href="/wiki/W._T._Cosgrave" title="W. T. Cosgrave">W. T. Cosgrave</a>, fifty years earlier. His appointment to <a href="/wiki/Iveagh_House" title="Iveagh House">Iveagh House</a> (the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade_(Ireland)" title="Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (Ireland)">Department of Foreign Affairs</a>) would have a huge effect on FitzGerald's own career and the future of Fine Gael. Cosgrave was suspicious of FitzGerald's liberal ideas and believed that he had designs on the leadership. During his period at Foreign Affairs, Fitzgerald developed a good relationship with <a href="/wiki/Liam_Cosgrave" title="Liam Cosgrave">Liam Cosgrave</a> and all the tension that had existed between them in opposition disappeared.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg/220px-US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg/330px-US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg/440px-US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4518" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Pres_Ford_%26_Taoiseach_Cosgrave-Patricks_Day_1976_.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Garret FitzGerald (l-r) with <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/Liam_Cosgrave" title="Liam Cosgrave">Liam Cosgrave</a>, <a href="/wiki/US_President" class="mw-redirect" title="US President">US President</a> <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oval_Office" title="Oval Office">Oval Office</a> St Patrick's Day 1976</div></div></div>
<p>The Minister's role had changed substantially since his father's day. Ireland was no longer a member of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a>, but had in 1973, joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a> (EEC), the organisation which would later become the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU). FitzGerald, firmly ensconced as Foreign Minister, was free from any blame due to other Ministers' mishandling of the economy. If anything, his tenure at the Department of Foreign Affairs helped him to eventually achieve the leadership of the party. His innovative views, energy and fluency in French won him – and through him, Ireland – a status in European affairs far exceeding the country's size and ensured that the first Irish Presidency of the European Council in 1975 was a noted success.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>FitzGerald's policy towards <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, however, brought him into confrontation with the Roman Catholic church, whose "special position" in the Republic had been enshrined in the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Fifth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">Referendum</a> of December 1972. FitzGerald in 1973 met <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Secretary_of_State" title="Cardinal Secretary of State">Cardinal Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Agostino_Casaroli" title="Agostino Casaroli">Agostino Casaroli</a>, and proposed to further modify the Republic's Constitution to remove laws with overtly Catholic foundations, such as the bans on divorce and contraception, as well as to relax the public stigmas in Northern Ireland towards mixed religious marriages and integrated education. Casaroli at first seemed receptive, and the Government formally submitted the proposal to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a>. FitzGerald's vision caused great consternation among the church's hierarchy, however, and in 1977, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Pope Paul VI</a> personally met with FitzGerald to tell him that "Ireland was a Catholic country – perhaps the only one left – and it should stay that way. Laws should not be changed in any way that would make the country less Catholic."<sup id="cite_ref-newhistory_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newhistory-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leader_of_Fine_Gael">Leader of Fine Gael</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Leader of Fine Gael">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1977, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_20th_D%C3%A1il" title="Government of the 20th Dáil">National Coalition</a> of Fine Gael and Labour suffered a disastrous electoral defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1977" title="Irish general election, 1977">general election</a>. Liam Cosgrave resigned as party leader and FitzGerald was chosen by acclamation to succeed him.<sup id="cite_ref-Independent-Obit_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-Obit-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In his new role as <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Leader of the Opposition (Ireland)">Leader of the Opposition</a> and party leader, he set about modernising and revitalising Fine Gael. He immediately appointed a General-Secretary to oversee all of this, a tactic copied from Fianna Fáil. Under FitzGerald, Fine Gael experienced a rapid rise in support and popularity. After the November 1982 election it held only five seats fewer than Fianna Fáil (their closest ever margin until 2011; at times Fianna Fáil was nearly twice as large), with Fine Gael in the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> <i>bigger</i> than Fianna Fáil, who had been the dominant force in Irish politics for 40 years.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Taoiseach_1981.E2.80.9382"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Taoiseach_1981–82">Taoiseach 1981–82</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Taoiseach 1981–82">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1981" title="Irish general election, 1981">1981 general election</a>, Fine Gael had a party machine that could easily match Fianna Fáil's.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The party won 65 seats and formed a minority coalition government with the Labour Party and the support of a number of Independent TDs. FitzGerald was elected Taoiseach, on 30 June 1981. To the surprise of many Fitzgerald excluded Richie Ryan, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burke_(politician)" title="Richard Burke (politician)">Richard Burke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_O%27Donnell" title="Tom O&#39;Donnell">Tom O'Donnell</a>, former Fine Gael stalwarts, from the cabinet.
</p><p>Two fundamental problems faced FitzGerald during his first period: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and the worsening economic situation. A protest march in support of the <a href="/wiki/Maze_(HM_Prison)" class="mw-redirect" title="Maze (HM Prison)">H-Block</a> <a href="/wiki/1981_Irish_hunger_strike" title="1981 Irish hunger strike">hunger strikers</a> in July 1981, was harshly dealt with by FitzGerald. On one occasion where he met with relatives of the hunger strikers, he refused to meet the family of <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Sands" title="Bobby Sands">Bobby Sands</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> for <a href="/wiki/Fermanagh_and_South_Tyrone_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Fermanagh and South Tyrone (UK Parliament constituency)">Fermanagh and South Tyrone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Officer_Commanding" class="mw-redirect" title="Officer Commanding">O/C</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_IRA" class="mw-redirect" title="Provisional IRA">Provisional IRA</a> hunger strikers, and the first to die on this strike, along with the sister of <a href="/wiki/Raymond_McCreesh" title="Raymond McCreesh">Raymond McCreesh</a>, who had died on 21 May. During the meeting, two of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_McElwee" title="Thomas McElwee">Thomas McElwee</a>'s sisters, Mary and Nora, broke down and left the meeting. Mary McElwee stated to the media outside that "he's doing nothing, he's asking for suggestions". Fitzgerald then ordered Gardaí to remove the families from the meeting. Fitzgerald's response was, in the words of Eamonn Sweeney, to "lay all the blame for the hunger strikers on the republican movement and to suggest an immediate unilateral end to their military campaign".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The economic crisis was also a lot worse than FitzGerald had feared. Fine Gael had to jettison its plans for tax cuts in the run-up to the election and a draconian mid-year budget was introduced almost immediately. The July Budget seemed exceptionally austere for a government dependent on Independent TDs support.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, the second budget introduced by John Bruton led to the Government's shock defeat in Dáil Éireann on the evening of 27 January 1982.
</p><p>In light of this <a href="/wiki/Loss_of_supply" title="Loss of supply">loss of supply</a>, FitzGerald went to <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ras_an_Uachtar%C3%A1in" title="Áras an Uachtaráin">Áras an Uachtaráin</a>, to request an immediate dissolution of the Dáil, from the President, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Hillery" title="Patrick Hillery">Patrick Hillery</a>. When he got there, he was informed that a series of telephone calls had been made by senior opposition figures (and some Independent TDs), including the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Irish_Leaders_of_the_Opposition" title="List of Irish Leaders of the Opposition">Leader of the Opposition</a> (and ex-Taoiseach) Charles Haughey, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Lenihan_Snr" title="Brian Lenihan Snr">Brian Lenihan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_Barrett" title="Sylvester Barrett">Sylvester Barrett</a> demanding that the President refuse the dissolution, as he was constitutionally allowed to do when it was advised by a Taoiseach who has "ceased to retain the support of a majority in Dáil Éireann." Had Hillery done so, it would have forced FitzGerald's resignation as Taoiseach and enabled the Dáil to nominate someone else for the post--presumably Haughey. Hillery is said to have angrily rejected such pressure, regarding it as gross misconduct. He granted FitzGerald the dissolution.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_February_1982" title="Irish general election, February 1982">general election</a> in February 1982, Fine Gael lost only two seats but were out of power. However, a third <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_November_1982" title="Irish general election, November 1982">general election</a> within eighteen months in November 1982, resulted in FitzGerald being returned as Taoiseach for a second time, heading a Fine Gael-Labour coalition with a working majority.
</p>
<h2><span id="Taoiseach_1982.E2.80.9387"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Taoiseach_1982–87">Taoiseach 1982–87</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Taoiseach 1982–87">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Deep economic recession dominated FitzGerald's second term as well as his first. The pursuit of 'fiscal rectitude' to reduce a high national debt required a firmer control of public spending than Labour found easy to accept. The harmonious relationship the Taoiseach developed with <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1naiste" title="Tánaiste">Tánaiste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dick_Spring" title="Dick Spring">Dick Spring</a> successfully avoided a collapse of the coalition for more than four years, despite tensions between other Ministers, and enabled the Government to survive. Fine Gael wanted to revive the economy by controlling public spending and imposing cutbacks to reduce the public budget deficit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The measures proposed by FitzGerald's Minister for Finance, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Dukes" title="Alan Dukes">Alan Dukes</a>, were completely unacceptable to the Labour Party which was under enormous pressure from its support base to maintain public services. The two parties in Government found themselves in a stalemate position. They stopped the financial crisis from worsening but could not take the decisive action that would generate economic growth. With negligible economic growth and large scale unemployment, the FitzGerald Government was deeply unpopular with the public. The Fianna Fáil opposition added to the woes of the Government by taking a decidedly opportunistic and populist line in opposing every suggested reform and cutback.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>When FitzGerald attended a meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Bilderberg_Group" title="Bilderberg Group">Bilderberg Group</a> in 1985, his rival Charles Haughey suggested it had links with <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>, thus contravening Ireland's official position of neutrality.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It was not his only participation. He took part also in 1975, 1977, 1984, 1986 and 1987. With 6 participations in total he is thus the third Irishmen after <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sutherland" title="Peter Sutherland">Peter Sutherland</a> (24 conferences) and <a href="/wiki/Dermot_Gleeson" title="Dermot Gleeson">Dermot Gleeson</a> with 8 conferences. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_reform">Constitutional reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Constitutional reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Taoiseach for a second time, FitzGerald advocated a liberalisation of Irish society, to create what he called the non-sectarian nation of "<a href="/wiki/Theobald_Wolfe_Tone" class="mw-redirect" title="Theobald Wolfe Tone">Tone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Osborne_Davis_(Irish_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Osborne Davis (Irish politician)">Davis</a>". His attempt to introduce divorce was defeated in a referendum, although he did liberalise Ireland's contraception laws.<sup id="cite_ref-Independent-Obit_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-Obit-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A controversial <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">'Pro-Life Amendment'</a> (anti-abortion clause), which was stated to recognise the 'Right to Life of the Unborn, with due regard to the Equal Right to Life of the Mother' was added to the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Irish constitution</a>, against FitzGerald's advice, in a national referendum.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>FitzGerald set up The <a href="/wiki/New_Ireland_Forum" title="New Ireland Forum">New Ireland Forum</a> in 1983, which brought together representatives of the constitutional political parties in the Republic and the nationalist <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">SDLP</a> from Northern Ireland. Although the Unionist parties declined his invitation to join, and the Forum's conclusions proposing various forms of association between Northern Ireland and the Republic were rejected outright by <a href="/wiki/British_Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="British Prime Minister">British Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, the Forum provided the impetus for the resumption of serious negotiations between the Irish and <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Government">British governments</a>, which culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a> of November 1985. This agreement provided for a mechanism by which the Republic of Ireland could be consulted by the British Government under Margaret Thatcher regarding the governance of Northern Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-obit_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-obit-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and was bitterly opposed by Unionists in Northern Ireland, whose MPs all resigned their seats in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British Parliament</a> in protest. New elections were required to be held in Northern Ireland, in which the unionists lost the seat of (<a href="/wiki/Newry_and_Armagh_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Newry and Armagh (UK Parliament constituency)">Newry and Armagh</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Seamus_Mallon" title="Seamus Mallon">Seamus Mallon</a> of the SDLP. During this period, on 15 March 1984, he was also invited to address a joint session of the US Congress, the fourth Irish leader to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His government had also passed the Extradition Act of 1987, which ended the long-standing defence against <a href="/wiki/Extradition" title="Extradition">extradition</a> of suspects who could plead that an act of violence in Northern Ireland or Britain was a political offence.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the Agreement was repudiated and condemned by Unionists, it was said to become the basis for developing trust and common action between the governments, which in time would ultimately bring about the Downing Street Declaration of 1993, and the subsequent republican and <a href="/wiki/Ulster_loyalism" title="Ulster loyalism">loyalist</a> cease-fires.<sup id="cite_ref-Independent-Obit_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-Obit-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infighting_and_declining_support">Infighting and declining support</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Infighting and declining support">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1986, FitzGerald attempted to reshuffle his cabinet but certain Ministers, including notably <a href="/wiki/Barry_Desmond" title="Barry Desmond">Barry Desmond</a>, refused to move from his Health and Social Welfare portfolio. The eventual outcome of the cabinet changes further undermined FitzGerald's authority. The new <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Democrats" title="Progressive Democrats">Progressive Democrats</a> party was launched at the same time by Desmond O'Malley, out of the divisions within Fianna Fáil. Ironically, it struck an immediate chord with many disenchanted Fine Gael supporters who had tired of the failure to fully address the economic crisis and who yearned for a coherent rightwing policy from FitzGerald. Seeing its support base under attack from the right only strengthened the resolve of FitzGerald's Fine Gael colleagues to break with the Labour Party approach, despite their leader's close empathy with that party.
</p><p>Stymied by economic crisis, FitzGerald tried to rescue some of his ambitions to reform the State and he proposed, in the middle of 1986, a referendum to change the Constitution to allow for divorce. The proposed amendment was mired in controversy and the many accompanying legal changes needed were not clearly presented. Haughey skilfully opposed the referendum along with the Roman Catholic Church and landed interests worried about property rights. The defeat of the referendum sealed the fate of the Government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In January 1987, the Labour Party members of the government withdrew from the government over disagreements due to budget proposals. FitzGerald continued as Taoiseach, heading a minority Fine Gael government and proposed the stringent budgetary cutbacks that Labour had blocked for some four years. Fianna Fáil returned to power in March 1987, after Fine Gael were heavily defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1987" title="Irish general election, 1987">1987 general election</a>. The Progressive Democrats won some 14 seats mainly from <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a>. Although Haughey did not have an overall majority when it came to a vote the Independent Socialist TD <a href="/wiki/Tony_Gregory" title="Tony Gregory">Tony Gregory</a> voted against Fitzgerald but abstained on Haughey, seeing Haughey as the "lesser of two evils" . The reason for this was Gregory's opposition to the Anglo-Irish agreement along with his strong personal dislike for Fitzgerald. Haughey was elected Taoiseach on the casting vote of the <a href="/wiki/Ceann_Comhairle" title="Ceann Comhairle">Ceann Comhairle</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Taoiseach_period">Post-Taoiseach period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Post-Taoiseach period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FitzGerald retired as leader of Fine Gael immediately after the election by the Dáil of Charles Haughey as Taoiseach,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> to be replaced by Alan Dukes. His autobiography <i>All in a Life</i> appeared in 1991, immediately becoming a best-seller. He retired completely from politics at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1992" title="Irish general election, 1992">1992 general election</a>. His wife, Joan, predeceased him, dying in 1999<sup id="cite_ref-Tel_obit_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tel_obit-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> after a long illness.
</p><p>
After that FitzGerald wrote a weekly column every Saturday in <i>The Irish Times</i>, and lectured widely at home and abroad on public affairs. In a leading article on his death the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Times">Irish Times</a> said that </p><blockquote><p><i>He was an extraordinary Irishman who fashioned our future in so many ways.</i></p></blockquote><p> and that he was the papers longest-serving contributor and columnist, for over 57 years.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He came out of retirement to campaign for a "yes" vote in the second Irish referendum on the EU's <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Nice" title="Treaty of Nice">Treaty of Nice</a>, held in 2002. He held the post of Chancellor of the <a href="/wiki/National_University_of_Ireland" title="National University of Ireland">National University of Ireland</a> from 1997 to 2009. In March 2000, Fitzgerald was on the board of directors of Election.com, when it conducted the world's first public election ever held over the Internet, which was the Arizona Democratic Primary, which was won by <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>; in that primary, voter turnout increased more than 500% over the 1996 primary.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>FitzGerald took a leading part in the campaign for the second referendum on the EU's <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> in 2009. He argued for Ireland to continue with European integration. FitzGerald has been scathing of the record of the Fianna Fáil led Government since 1997, on the economy and the national finances. He was a frequent, critic in his column in <i>The Irish Times</i>, of the loss of competitiveness that occurred and the inflation caused by the tax cuts and excessive public spending increases of the Celtic Tiger era. In 2009, FitzGerald received a new ministerial car, the first and only one to be purchased by the state since an economic recession hit the country in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, FitzGerald appeared on RTÉ's "<a href="/wiki/Ireland%27s_Greatest" title="Ireland&#39;s Greatest">Top 40 Irishmen</a>" list.
</p><p>He was Vice-President of the <a href="/wiki/Railway_Preservation_Society_of_Ireland" title="Railway Preservation Society of Ireland">Railway Preservation Society of Ireland</a> for his last 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Finances">Finances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Finances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early 1999, it was revealed that some six years earlier, <a href="/wiki/Allied_Irish_Banks" title="Allied Irish Banks">AIB</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ansbacher_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Ansbacher Bank">Ansbacher Banks</a> wrote off debts of almost IR£200,000 owed by FitzGerald, following the collapse of the aircraft leasing company, <a href="/wiki/Guinness_Peat_Aviation" title="Guinness Peat Aviation">Guinness Peat Aviation</a>, in which he was a shareholder.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Chairman of AIB at the time, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sutherland" title="Peter Sutherland">Peter Sutherland</a>, was also a former director of GPA and had served as <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Ireland" title="Attorney General of Ireland">Attorney General</a> under FitzGerald, prior to FitzGerald appointing him as Ireland's member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Moriarty_Tribunal" title="Moriarty Tribunal">Moriarty Tribunal</a> investigated this matter, and compared the treatment by AIB of FitzGerald, with their treatment of Charles Haughey. They found no evidence of any wrongdoing, indeed the tribunal heard evidence as to the considerable hardship that FitzGerald went to – to the extent of selling of his family home – to repay the debt to the best of his ability.
</p><p>The Tribunal concluded in their report:
</p>
<blockquote><p>In summary it would appear that in compromising his indebtedness with the Bank, Dr. Fitzgerald disposed of his only substantial asset, namely, his family home at Palmerston Road, a property which would now be worth a considerable sum of money. As in Mr. Haughey's case, there was a substantial discounting or forbearance shown in Dr. Fitzgerald's case. However, in contrast with Mr. Haughey's case, Dr. Fitzgerald's case involved the effective exhaustion of his assets in order to achieve a settlement whereas Mr. Haughey's assets were retained virtually intact.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garret_FitzGerald&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 5 May 2011, it was reported that FitzGerald was seriously ill in a Dublin hospital. The Taoiseach, <a href="/wiki/Enda_Kenny" title="Enda Kenny">Enda Kenny</a> sent his regards and called him an "institution".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He was put on a ventilator.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> On 19 May, he died, aged 85, in the <a href="/wiki/Mater_Private_Hospital" title="Mater Private Hospital">Mater Private Hospital</a> in Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_McAleese" title="Mary McAleese">Mary McAleese</a> described him as a man steeped in the history of the State who constantly strove to make Ireland a better place for all its people. </p><blockquote><p><i>His thoughtful writing, distinctive voice and probing intellect all combined to make him one of our national treasures. Above all, Garret Fitzgerald was a true public servant...</i> <i>Long after he departed active politics, Garret continued to contribute to public life through his voluminous writing and scholarship. His weekly columns in </i><a href="/wiki/The_Irish_Times" title="The Irish Times">The Irish Times</a><i> were essential reading for those who sought enlightenment on the issues and debates of the day.</i><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enda_Kenny" title="Enda Kenny">Enda Kenny</a> said Dr. FitzGerald was; </p><blockquote><p><i>a truly remarkable man who made a truly remarkable contribution to Ireland</i> </p></blockquote><p>and that; </p><blockquote><p><i>his towering intellect and enthusiasm for life will be missed by everybody. </i> <i>He had an eternal optimism for what could be achieved in politics. You could not tire him out and his belief that politics and democracy would work for peace.</i></p></blockquote><p> former Taoiseach and Fine Gael leader, <a href="/wiki/John_Bruton" title="John Bruton">John Bruton</a> said FitzGerald would; </p><blockquote><p><i>stand out as a man who changed Ireland</i> and that he had changed attitudes to in the Republic to Northern Ireland and to Europe and that he saw that <i>Ireland could do best in Europe if it contributed creatively to goals and ambitions of other members.</i><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Former US Secretary of State, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kissinger" title="Henry Kissinger">Henry Kissinger</a> described FitzGerald as an intelligent and amusing man who was dedicated to his country.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
His death occurred during the third day of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II%27s_visit_to_the_Republic_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II&#39;s visit to the Republic of Ireland">the state visit of Queen Elizabeth II to Ireland</a>, an event designed to mark the completion of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">the peace process</a>, that FitzGerald began with the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> In response to his death, the Queen said of FitzGerald, </p><blockquote><p><i>I was saddened to hear this morning's news of the death of Garret FitzGerald, a true statesman. He made a lasting contribution to peace and will be greatly missed.</i><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote> <p><a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">British Prime Minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a>, who was also in Ireland, said of him:
</p><blockquote><p><i>I watched him as a student of politics, rather than someone involved in politics, and he always struck me as someone who was a statesman as well as a politician, someone who was in politics for all the right reasons, and someone who made a huge contribution to the peace process bringing reconciliation for all that had happened in the past. And I think that today of all days with the state visit and the warm relationship between Britain and Ireland that he can see that some of his work being completed.</i><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>
On his visit to Dublin, US President, <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> offered condolences on the former Taoiseach's death, describing Dr FitzGerald as; </p><blockquote><p><i>someone who believed in the power of education, someone who believed in the potential of youth, someone who believed in the potential of peace and who lived to see that peace realised</i></p></blockquote><p> He was buried at  Shanganagh Cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>British politician <a href="/wiki/Roy_Jenkins" title="Roy Jenkins">Roy Jenkins</a> remembered FitzGerald speaking fluent <a href="/wiki/French_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="French (language)">French</a> at the opening of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>.
</p>
<blockquote><p><i>There, I thought, spoke the Ireland of <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">Joyce</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._M._Synge" class="mw-redirect" title="J. M. Synge">Synge</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Countess_Markiewicz" class="mw-redirect" title="Countess Markiewicz">Countess Markiewicz</a> ... It was he who made me feel provincial.</i><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>There has been a call for <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a>'s Terminal 2 to be renamed the <i>Garret FitzGerald Terminal</i> after the former Taoiseach in light of his early career and lifelong interest in aviation.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2012, <a href="/wiki/Young_Fine_Gael" title="Young Fine Gael">Young Fine Gael</a> announced that its annual Summer School would be renamed the Garret FitzGerald YFG Summer School.
</p>
