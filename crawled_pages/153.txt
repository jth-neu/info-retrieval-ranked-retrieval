<p>A <b>ranking</b> is a relationship between a set of items such that, for any two items, the first is either 'ranked higher than', 'ranked lower than' or 'ranked equal to' the second.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
In <a href="/wiki/Order_theory" title="Order theory">mathematics</a>, this is known as a <a href="/wiki/Strict_weak_ordering#Total_preorders" class="mw-redirect" title="Strict weak ordering">weak order or total preorder</a> of objects. It is not necessarily a <a href="/wiki/Total_order" title="Total order">total order</a> of objects because two different objects can have the same ranking. The rankings themselves are totally ordered. For example, materials are totally preordered by <a href="/wiki/Hardness_(materials_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hardness (materials science)">hardness</a>, while degrees of hardness are totally ordered. If two items are the same in rank it is considered a tie.
</p><p>By reducing detailed measures to a sequence of <a href="/wiki/Ordinal_numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordinal numbers">ordinal numbers</a>, rankings make it possible to evaluate complex information according to certain criteria.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Thus, for example, an Internet search engine may rank the pages it finds according to an estimation of their <a href="/wiki/Relevance" title="Relevance">relevance</a>, making it possible for the user quickly to select the pages they are likely to want to see.
</p><p>Analysis of data obtained by ranking commonly requires <a href="/wiki/Non-parametric_statistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-parametric statistics">non-parametric statistics</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strategies_for_assigning_rankings">Strategies for assigning rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Strategies for assigning rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is not always possible to assign rankings uniquely. For example, in a race or competition two (or more) entrants might tie for a place in the ranking.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> When computing an <a href="/wiki/Ordinal_measurement" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordinal measurement">ordinal measurement</a>, two (or more) of the quantities being ranked might measure equal. In these cases, one of the strategies shown below for assigning the rankings may be adopted. 
A common shorthand way to distinguish these ranking strategies is by the ranking numbers that would be produced for four items, with the first item ranked ahead of the second and third (which compare equal) which are both ranked ahead of the fourth. These names are also shown below.
</p>
<h3><span id="Standard_competition_ranking_.28.221224.22_ranking.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_competition_ranking_(&quot;1224&quot;_ranking)">Standard competition ranking ("1224" ranking)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standard competition ranking (&quot;1224&quot; ranking)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In competition ranking, items that compare equal receive the same ranking number, and then a gap is left in the ranking numbers. The number of ranking numbers that are left out in this gap is one less than the number of items that compared equal. Equivalently, each item's ranking number is 1 plus the number of items ranked above it. This ranking strategy is frequently adopted for competitions, as it means that if two (or more) competitors tie for a position in the ranking, the position of all those ranked below them is unaffected (i.e., a competitor only comes second if exactly one person scores better than them, third if exactly two people score better than them, fourth if exactly three people score better than them, etc.).
</p><p>Thus if A ranks ahead of B and C (which compare equal) which are both ranked ahead of D, then A gets ranking number 1 ("first"), B gets ranking number 2 ("joint second"), C also gets ranking number 2 ("joint second") and D gets ranking number 4 ("fourth").
</p>
<h3><span id="Modified_competition_ranking_.28.221334.22_ranking.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Modified_competition_ranking_(&quot;1334&quot;_ranking)">Modified competition ranking ("1334" ranking)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modified competition ranking (&quot;1334&quot; ranking)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sometimes, competition ranking is done by leaving the gaps in the ranking numbers <i>before</i> the sets of equal-ranking items (rather than after them as in standard competition ranking).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (July 2016)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The number of ranking numbers that are left out in this gap remains one less than the number of items that compared equal. Equivalently, each item's ranking number is equal to the number of items ranked equal to it or above it. This ranking ensures that a competitor only comes second if they score higher than all but one of their opponents, third if they score higher than all but two of their opponents, etc.
</p><p>Thus if A ranks ahead of B and C (which compare equal) which are both ranked head of D, then A gets ranking number 1 ("first"), B gets ranking number 3 ("joint third"), C also gets ranking number 3 ("joint third") and D gets ranking number 4 ("fourth"). In this case, nobody would get ranking number 2 ("second") and that would be left as a gap.
</p>
<h3><span id="Dense_ranking_.28.221223.22_ranking.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dense_ranking_(&quot;1223&quot;_ranking)">Dense ranking ("1223" ranking)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dense ranking (&quot;1223&quot; ranking)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In dense ranking, items that compare equally receive the same ranking number, and the next item(s) receive the immediately following ranking number. Equivalently, each item's ranking number is 1 plus the number of items ranked above it that are distinct with respect to the ranking order.
</p><p>Thus if A ranks ahead of B and C (which compare equal) which are both ranked ahead of D, then A gets ranking number 1 ("first"), B gets ranking number 2 ("joint second"), C also gets ranking number 2 ("joint second") and D gets ranking number 3 ("Third").
</p>
<h3><span id="Ordinal_ranking_.28.221234.22_ranking.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordinal_ranking_(&quot;1234&quot;_ranking)">Ordinal ranking ("1234" ranking)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ordinal ranking (&quot;1234&quot; ranking)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In ordinal ranking, all items receive distinct ordinal numbers, including items that compare equal. The assignment of distinct ordinal numbers to items that compare equal can be done at random, or arbitrarily, but it is generally preferable to use a system that is arbitrary but consistent, as this gives stable results if the ranking is done multiple times. An example of an arbitrary but consistent system would be to incorporate other attributes into the ranking order (such as alphabetical ordering of the competitor's name) to ensure that no two items exactly match.
</p><p>With this strategy, if A ranks ahead of B and C (which compare equal) which are both ranked ahead of D, then A gets ranking number 1 ("first") and D gets ranking number 4 ("fourth"), and <b>either</b> B gets ranking number 2 ("second") and C gets ranking number 3 ("third") <b>or</b> C gets ranking number 2 ("second") and B gets ranking number 3 ("third").
</p><p>In computer data processing, ordinal ranking is also referred to as "row numbering".
</p>
<h3><span id="Fractional_ranking_.28.221_2.5_2.5_4.22_ranking.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fractional_ranking_(&quot;1_2.5_2.5_4&quot;_ranking)">Fractional ranking ("1 2.5 2.5 4" ranking)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fractional ranking (&quot;1 2.5 2.5 4&quot; ranking)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Items that compare equal receive the same ranking number, which is the <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">mean</a> of what they would have under ordinal rankings. Equivalently, the ranking number of 1 plus the number of items ranked above it plus half the number of items equal to it. This strategy has the property that the sum of the ranking numbers is the same as under ordinal ranking. For this reason, it is used in computing <a href="/wiki/Borda_count" title="Borda count">Borda counts</a> and in statistical tests (see below).
</p><p>Thus if A ranks ahead of B and C (which compare equal) which are both ranked ahead of D, then A gets ranking number 1 ("first"), B and C each get ranking number 2.5 (average of "joint second/third") and D gets ranking number 4 ("fourth").
</p><p>Here is an example: 
Suppose you have the data set 1.0, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 3.0, 4.0, 5.0, 5.0, 5.0.
</p><p>The ordinal ranks are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9.
</p><p>For v = 1.0, the fractional rank is the average of the ordinal ranks: (1 + 2) / 2 = 1.5.
In a similar manner, for v = 5.0, the fractional rank is (7 + 8 + 9) / 3 = 8.0.
</p><p>Thus the fractional ranks are: 1.5, 1.5, 3.0, 4.5, 4.5, 6.0, 8.0, 8.0, 8.0
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ranking_in_statistics">Ranking in statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ranking in statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a>, "ranking" refers to the <a href="/wiki/Data_transformation_(statistics)" title="Data transformation (statistics)">data transformation</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">numerical</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ordinal_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordinal scale">ordinal</a> values are replaced by their rank when the data are sorted. For example, the numerical data 3.4, 5.1, 2.6, 7.3 are observed, the ranks of these data items would be 2, 3, 1 and 4 respectively. For example, the ordinal data hot, cold, warm would be replaced by 3, 1, 2. In these examples, the ranks are assigned to values in ascending order. (In some other cases, descending ranks are used.) Ranks are related to the indexed list of <a href="/wiki/Order_statistics" class="mw-redirect" title="Order statistics">order statistics</a>, which consists of the original dataset rearranged into ascending order.
</p><p>Some kinds of <a href="/wiki/Statistical_test" class="mw-redirect" title="Statistical test">statistical tests</a> employ calculations based on ranks. Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Friedman_test" title="Friedman test">Friedman test</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kruskal%E2%80%93Wallis_one-way_analysis_of_variance" title="Kruskal–Wallis one-way analysis of variance">Kruskal–Wallis test</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rank_product" title="Rank product">Rank products</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Spearman%27s_rank_correlation_coefficient" title="Spearman&#39;s rank correlation coefficient">Spearman's rank correlation coefficient</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wilcoxon_rank-sum_test" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilcoxon rank-sum test">Wilcoxon rank-sum test</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wilcoxon_signed-rank_test" title="Wilcoxon signed-rank test">Wilcoxon signed-rank test</a></li></ul>
<p>The distribution of values in decreasing order of rank is often of interest when values vary widely in scale; this is the <a href="/wiki/Rank-size_distribution" title="Rank-size distribution">rank-size distribution</a> (or rank-frequency distribution), for example for city sizes or word frequencies. These often follow a <a href="/wiki/Power_law" title="Power law">power law</a>.
</p><p>Some ranks can have non-integer values for tied data values. For example, when there is an even number of copies of the same data value, the above described <a href="#Fractional_ranking_(&quot;1_2.5_2.5_4&quot;_ranking)">fractional statistical rank</a> of the tied data ends in ½.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rank_function_in_Excel">Rank function in Excel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rank function in Excel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Excel" title="Microsoft Excel">Microsoft Excel</a> provides two ranking functions, the <b>Rank.EQ</b> function which assigns competition ranks ("1224") and the <b>Rank.AVG</b> function which assigns fractional ranks ("1 2.5 2.5 4") as described above.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_rankings">Comparison of rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comparison of rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Rank_correlation" title="Rank correlation">rank correlation</a> can be used to compare two rankings for the same set of objects. For example, <a href="/wiki/Spearman%27s_rank_correlation_coefficient" title="Spearman&#39;s rank correlation coefficient">Spearman's rank correlation coefficient</a> is useful to measure the statistical dependence between the rankings of athletes in two tournaments. Another example is the "Rank–rank hypergeometric overlap" approach<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>, which is designed to compare ranking of the genes that are at the "top" of two ordered lists of differentially expressed genes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_ranking">Examples of ranking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ranking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Examples of ranking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, rankings focus on the comparison of economic, social, environmental and governance performance of countries, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_international_rankings" title="List of international rankings">List of international rankings</a>.</li>
<li>In many <a href="/wiki/Sport" title="Sport">sports</a>, individuals or teams are given rankings, generally by the sport's <a href="/wiki/Sports_governing_body" title="Sports governing body">governing body</a>.
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> (soccer), national teams are ranked in the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Rankings" title="FIFA World Rankings">FIFA World Rankings</a>, the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_Women%27s_World_Rankings" title="FIFA Women&#39;s World Rankings">Women's World Rankings</a> and, unofficially, in the <a href="/wiki/World_Football_Elo_Ratings" title="World Football Elo Ratings">World Football Elo Ratings</a>.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a>, each member country (<a href="/wiki/National_Olympic_Committee" title="National Olympic Committee">NOC</a>) is ranked based upon gold, silver and bronze medal counts in the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_medal_table" title="Olympic medal table">Olympic medal rankings</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a>, national teams are ranked in the <a href="/wiki/FIBA_World_Rankings" title="FIBA World Rankings">FIBA World Rankings</a> and the <a href="/wiki/FIBA_Women%27s_World_Ranking" title="FIBA Women&#39;s World Ranking">Women's World Rankings</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">baseball</a> and <a href="/wiki/Softball" title="Softball">softball</a>, national teams are ranked in the <a href="/wiki/WBSC_World_Rankings" title="WBSC World Rankings">WBSC World Rankings</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a>, national teams are ranked in the <a href="/wiki/IIHF_World_Ranking" title="IIHF World Ranking">IIHF World Ranking</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>, the top male golfers are ranked using the <a href="/wiki/Official_World_Golf_Rankings" class="mw-redirect" title="Official World Golf Rankings">Official World Golf Rankings</a>, and the top female golfers are ranked using the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_World_Golf_Rankings" title="Women&#39;s World Golf Rankings">Women's World Golf Rankings</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Snooker" title="Snooker">snooker</a>, players are ranked using the <a href="/wiki/Snooker_world_rankings" title="Snooker world rankings">Snooker world rankings</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a>, male and female players are ranked using the <a href="/wiki/ATP_Rankings" title="ATP Rankings">ATP Rankings</a> and <a href="/wiki/WTA_Rankings" title="WTA Rankings">WTA Rankings</a> respectively, whilst the <a href="/wiki/ITF_Rankings" title="ITF Rankings">ITF Rankings</a> are used for national <a href="/wiki/Davis_Cup" title="Davis Cup">Davis Cup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fed_Cup" title="Fed Cup">Fed Cup</a> teams.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Road_bicycle_racing" title="Road bicycle racing">road bicycle racing</a>, male cyclists have been ranked using the <a href="/wiki/UCI_World_Ranking" title="UCI World Ranking">UCI World Ranking</a> from 2016, having previously been ranked using the <a href="/wiki/UCI_Road_World_Rankings" title="UCI Road World Rankings">UCI Road World Rankings</a> from 1984 to 2004. Female cyclists have been ranked using the <a href="/wiki/UCI_Women%27s_Road_World_Rankings" title="UCI Women&#39;s Road World Rankings">UCI Women's Road World Rankings</a> since 1994.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Track_cycling" title="Track cycling">track cycling</a> riders and nations are ranked using the <a href="/wiki/UCI_Track_Cycling_World_Ranking" title="UCI Track Cycling World Ranking">UCI Track Cycling World Ranking</a></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>, players are ranked using the <a href="/wiki/FIDE_World_Rankings" class="mw-redirect" title="FIDE World Rankings">FIDE World Rankings</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Sailing" title="Sailing">sailing</a>, boats are scored directly using the sum of the ranking.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bridge (game)">bridge</a>, matchpoint scoring uses fractional ranking to assign the score.</li></ul></li>
<li>In relation to <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a> standing, the ranking of a security refers to where that particular security would stand in a <a href="/wiki/Liquidation" title="Liquidation">wind up</a> of the issuing company, i.e., its <a href="/wiki/Seniority_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Seniority (finance)">seniority</a> in the company's <a href="/wiki/Capital_structure" title="Capital structure">capital structure</a>. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Capital_note" title="Capital note">capital notes</a> are subordinated securities; they would rank behind senior debt in a wind up. In other words, the holders of <a href="/wiki/Senior_debt" title="Senior debt">senior debt</a> would be paid out before <a href="/wiki/Subordinated_debt" title="Subordinated debt">subordinated debt</a> holders received any funds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">Search engines</a> rank web pages by their expected <a href="/wiki/Relevance" title="Relevance">relevance</a> to a user's query using a combination of query-dependent and query-independent methods. Query-independent methods attempt to measure the estimated importance of a page, independent of any consideration of how well it matches the specific query. Query-independent ranking is usually based on link analysis; examples include the <a href="/wiki/HITS_algorithm" title="HITS algorithm">HITS algorithm</a>, <a href="/wiki/PageRank" title="PageRank">PageRank</a> and <a href="/wiki/TrustRank" title="TrustRank">TrustRank</a>. Query-dependent methods attempt to measure the degree to which a page matches a specific query, independent of the importance of the page. Query-dependent ranking is usually based on <a href="/wiki/Heuristic" title="Heuristic">heuristics</a> that consider the number and locations of matches of the various query words on the page itself, in the <a href="/wiki/URL" title="URL">URL</a> or in any <a href="/wiki/Anchor_text" title="Anchor text">anchor text</a> referring to the page.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Webometrics" title="Webometrics">Webometrics</a> it is possible to rank institutions according to their presence in the web (number of webpages) and the impact of these contents (external inlinks=site citations), such as the <a href="/wiki/Webometrics_Ranking_of_World_Universities" title="Webometrics Ranking of World Universities">Webometrics Ranking of World Universities</a></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Video_gaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Video gaming">video gaming</a>, players may be given a ranking. To "<a href="/wiki/Rank_up" title="Rank up">rank up</a>" is to achieve a higher ranking relative to other players, especially with strategies that do not depend on the player's skill.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/TrueSkill" title="TrueSkill">TrueSkill</a> ranking system is a skill based ranking system for Xbox Live developed at Microsoft Research</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Bibliogram" title="Bibliogram">bibliogram</a> ranks common noun phrases in a piece of text.</li>
<li>In language, the status of an item (usually through what is known as "downranking" or "rank-shifting") in relation to the uppermost rank in a clause; for example, in the sentence "I want to eat the cake you made today", "eat" is on the uppermost rank, but "made" is downranked as part of the nominal group "the cake you made today"; this nominal group behaves as though it were a single noun (i.e., I want to eat <i>it</i>), and thus the verb within it ("made") is ranked differently from "eat".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Academic_journal#Ranking" title="Academic journal">Academic journals</a> are sometimes ranked according to <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a>; the number of later articles that cite articles in a given journal.</li></ul>
