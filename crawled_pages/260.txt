<p><b>Lycos, Inc.</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">web search engine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">web portal</a> established in 1995, spun out of <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University</a>. Lycos also encompasses a network of email, webhosting, social networking, and entertainment websites.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_history">Corporate history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lycos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Corporate history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lycos is a <a href="/wiki/University_spin-off" title="University spin-off">university spin-off</a> that began as a research project by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Loren_Mauldin" title="Michael Loren Mauldin">Michael Loren Mauldin</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Mellon_University" title="Carnegie Mellon University">Carnegie Mellon University's</a> main <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a> campus in 1994. <b>Lycos Inc.</b> was formed with approximately US$2 million in <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> funding from <a href="/wiki/CMGI" class="mw-redirect" title="CMGI">CMGI</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bob_Davis_(businessman)" title="Bob Davis (businessman)">Bob Davis</a> became the CEO and first employee of the new company in 1995, and concentrated on building the company into an advertising-supported <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">web portal</a>. Lycos enjoyed several years of growth during the 1990s and became the most visited online destination in the world in 1999, with a global presence in more than 40 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1996, the company completed the fastest <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> from inception to offering in <a href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> (LCOS) history. In 1997, it became one of the first profitable internet businesses in the world. In 1998, Lycos paid $58 million for <a href="/wiki/Tripod.com" title="Tripod.com">Tripod.com</a> in an attempt to "break into the portal market."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Over the course of the next few years, Lycos acquired nearly two dozen internet brands including <a href="/wiki/Gamesville" title="Gamesville">Gamesville</a>, WhoWhere, <a href="/wiki/Wired_News" class="mw-redirect" title="Wired News">Wired News</a> (eventually sold to <a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired</a>), <a href="/wiki/Quote.com" title="Quote.com">Quote.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angelfire" title="Angelfire">Angelfire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matchmaker.com" title="Matchmaker.com">Matchmaker.com</a>, and <a href="/wiki/RagingBull.com" title="RagingBull.com">RagingBull.com</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lycos_Europe" title="Lycos Europe">Lycos Europe</a> was a joint venture between Lycos and the <a href="/wiki/Bertelsmann" title="Bertelsmann">Bertelsmann</a> transnational media corporation, but it has always been a distinct corporate entity. Although Lycos Europe remains the largest of Lycos's overseas ventures, several other Lycos subsidiaries also entered into joint venture agreements including Lycos Canada, Lycos Korea and Lycos Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Near the peak of the <a href="/wiki/Internet_bubble" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet bubble">internet bubble</a> on May 16, 2000, Lycos announced its intent to be acquired by <a href="/wiki/Terra_Networks" class="mw-redirect" title="Terra Networks">Terra Networks</a>, the internet arm of the Spanish telecommunications giant <a href="/wiki/Telef%C3%B3nica" title="Telefónica">Telefónica</a>, for $12.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The acquisition price represented a return of nearly 3,000 times the company's initial venture capital investment and about 20 times its initial public offering valuation. The transaction closed in October 2000 and the merged company was renamed Terra Lycos, although the Lycos brand continued to be used in the United States. Overseas, the company continued to be known as Terra Networks.
</p><p>On August 2, 2004, Terra announced that it was selling Lycos to <a href="/wiki/Seoul" title="Seoul">Seoul</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>-based Daum Communications Corporation, now <a href="/wiki/Kakao" title="Kakao">Kakao</a>, for $95.4 million in cash, less than 2% of Terra's initial multibillion-dollar investment. In October 2004, the transaction closed for sale of half of the business and the company name was changed back to Lycos Inc. The remaining Terra half was reacquired by Telefónica.
</p><p>Under new ownership, Lycos began to refocus its strategy. In 2005, the company moved away from a search-centric portal and toward a community destination for broadband entertainment content. With a new management team in place, Lycos also began divesting properties that were not core to its new strategy. In July 2006, <a href="/wiki/Wired_News" class="mw-redirect" title="Wired News">Wired News</a>, which had been part of Lycos since the purchase of Wired Digital in 1998, was sold to <a href="/wiki/Cond%C3%A9_Nast_Publications" class="mw-redirect" title="Condé Nast Publications">Condé Nast Publications</a> and re-merged with <i><a href="/wiki/Wired_(magazine)" title="Wired (magazine)">Wired Magazine</a></i>. The Lycos Finance division, best known for <a href="/wiki/Quote.com" title="Quote.com">Quote.com</a> and <a href="/wiki/RagingBull.com" title="RagingBull.com">RagingBull.com</a>, was sold to FT Interactive Data Corporation in February 2006, while its online dating site, <a href="/wiki/Matchmaker.com" title="Matchmaker.com">Matchmaker.com</a>, was sold to Date.com. In 2006, Lycos regained ownership of the Lycos trademark from Carnegie Mellon University.
</p><p>During 2006, Lycos introduced several media services, including Lycos Phone which combined video chat, real-time video on demand, and an MP3 player.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In November 2006, Lycos began to roll out applications centered on social media, including its video application, Lycos Cinema, that featured simultaneous watch and chat functionality.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In February 2007, Lycos MIX was launched, allowing users to pull video clips from <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Google_Video" title="Google Video">Google Video</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Video" class="mw-redirect" title="Yahoo! Video">Yahoo! Video</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=MySpace_Video&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MySpace Video (page does not exist)">MySpace Video</a>. Lycos MIX also allowed users to create playlists where other users could add video comments and chat in real-time.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of a corporate restructuring to focus on mobile, social networks and <a href="/wiki/Location-based_service" title="Location-based service">location-based services</a>, Daum sold Lycos for $36 million in August 2010 to <a href="/wiki/Ybrant_Digital" title="Ybrant Digital">Ybrant Digital</a>, an internet marketing company based in <a href="/wiki/Hyderabad,_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyderabad, India">Hyderabad, India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-marketwatch1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marketwatch1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Ybrant Digital paid $20 million at signing and there has been a legal dispute over magnitude of the second installment between Ybrant and Daum. In 2018, a New York court ruled in favor of Daum and appointed Daum as receiver of Ybrant's 56% ownership interest in Lycos.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2012 Lycos announced the appointment of former employee Rob Balazy as CEO of Media division of Lycos.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2014 Ed Noel was appointed in place of Rob and manages the operations under the title of General Manager of Lycos Media.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
