<p>The <b>Corinthian helmet</b> originated in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> and took its name from the <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Corinth" title="Ancient Corinth">Corinth</a>. It was a <a href="/wiki/Helmet" title="Helmet">helmet</a> made of <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> which in its later styles covered the entire head and neck, with slits for the eyes and mouth.  A large curved projection protected the nape of the neck. 
</p><p>Out of combat, a Greek <a href="/wiki/Hoplite" title="Hoplite">hoplite</a> would wear the helmet tipped upward for comfort. This practice gave rise to a series of variant forms in Italy, where the slits were almost closed, since the helmet was no longer pulled over the face but worn cap-like. Although the classical Corinthian helmet fell out of use among the Greeks in favour of more open types, the Italo-Corinthian types remained in use until the 1st century AD, being used, among others, by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">Roman army</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_evidence">Physical evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Corinthian_helmet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Getty_villa_1526_(6571555293).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Getty_villa_1526_%286571555293%29.jpg/250px-Getty_villa_1526_%286571555293%29.jpg" width="250" height="376" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Getty_villa_1526_%286571555293%29.jpg/375px-Getty_villa_1526_%286571555293%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Getty_villa_1526_%286571555293%29.jpg/500px-Getty_villa_1526_%286571555293%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3056" data-file-height="4592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Getty_villa_1526_(6571555293).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Italo-Corinthian helmet, <a href="/wiki/Getty_Villa" title="Getty Villa">Getty Villa</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MetapontumDrachmRutter1576Obverse.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/MetapontumDrachmRutter1576Obverse.jpg/250px-MetapontumDrachmRutter1576Obverse.jpg" width="250" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/MetapontumDrachmRutter1576Obverse.jpg/375px-MetapontumDrachmRutter1576Obverse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/MetapontumDrachmRutter1576Obverse.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MetapontumDrachmRutter1576Obverse.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Leucippus" title="Leucippus">Leucippus</a> on a coin, with Corinthian helmet</div></div></div>
<p>Apparently (judging from artistic and <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> evidence) the most popular helmet during the Archaic and early Classical periods, the style gradually gave way to the more open <a href="/wiki/Thracian_helmet" class="mw-redirect" title="Thracian helmet">Thracian helmet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chalcidian_helmet" title="Chalcidian helmet">Chalcidian helmet</a> and the much simpler <a href="/wiki/Pilos" class="mw-redirect" title="Pilos">pilos</a> type, which was less expensive to manufacture and did not obstruct the wearer's critical senses of <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">vision</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hearing (sense)">hearing</a> as the Corinthian helmet did. Numerous examples of Corinthian helmets have been excavated, and they are frequently depicted on <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>.
</p><p>The Corinthian helmet was depicted on more sculpture than any other helmet; it seems the Greeks romantically associated it with glory and the past. The Romans also revered it, from copies of Greek originals to sculpture of their own. Based on the sparse pictorial evidence of the republican Roman army, in Italy the Corinthian helmet evolved into a jockey-cap style helmet called the Italo-Corinthian, Etrusco-Corinthian or Apulo-Corinthian helmet, with the characteristic nose guard and eye slits becoming mere decorations on its face<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Given many Roman appropriations of ancient Greek ideas, this change was probably inspired by the "over the forehead" position common in Greek art. This helmet remained in use well into the 1st century AD.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere,_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella,_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei,_9249,_02.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere%2C_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella%2C_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei%2C_9249%2C_02.JPG/220px-Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere%2C_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella%2C_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei%2C_9249%2C_02.JPG" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere%2C_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella%2C_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei%2C_9249%2C_02.JPG/330px-Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere%2C_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella%2C_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei%2C_9249%2C_02.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere%2C_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella%2C_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei%2C_9249%2C_02.JPG/440px-Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere%2C_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella%2C_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei%2C_9249%2C_02.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3344" data-file-height="2728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seduzione_tra_marte_e_venere,_alla_presenza_di_un_amorino_e_ancella,_da_casa_dell%27amore_punito_a_pompei,_9249,_02.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Venus_(mythology)" title="Venus (mythology)">Venus</a> being seduced by <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a> (wearing a plumed Corinthian helmet), fresco from <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a>, 1st century AD</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a> mentions the Corinthian helmet in his <i>Histories</i> when writing of the <a href="/wiki/Machlyes" title="Machlyes">Machlyes</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auseans&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Auseans (page does not exist)">Auseans</a>, two tribes living along the River <a href="/w/index.php?title=Triton_River&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Triton River (page does not exist)">Triton</a> in ancient <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a> (the portion of ancient Libya he describes is most likely in modern <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>).  The tribes chose annually two teams of the fairest <a href="/wiki/Virgin" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin">maidens</a> who fought each other ceremonially with sticks and stones.  They were dressed in the finest Greek <a href="/wiki/Panoply" title="Panoply">panoply</a> topped off with a Corinthian helmet. The <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> fight was part of a festival honoring the virgin goddess <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>.  Young women who succumbed to their wounds during the ordeal were thought to have been punished by the goddess for lying about their virginity (<i>Histories, 4.180</i>).
</p>
