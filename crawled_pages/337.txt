<p><b>Sir Timothy John Berners-Lee</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_Order_of_Merit" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of the Order of Merit">OM</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Knight_Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight Commander of the Order of the British Empire">KBE</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering">FREng</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Arts" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Arts">FRSA</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Computer_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellow of the British Computer Society">FBCS</a></span> (born 8 June 1955),<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> also known as <b>TimBL</b>, is an English <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer scientist</a>, best known as the inventor of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>. He is currently a professor of Computer Science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a> (MIT).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  He made a proposal for an information management system in March 1989,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and he implemented the first successful communication between a <a href="/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol" title="Hypertext Transfer Protocol">Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP)</a> client and <a href="/wiki/Server_(computing)" title="Server (computing)">server</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> in mid-November the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-weavingtheweb_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weavingtheweb-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Berners-Lee is the director of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Consortium" title="World Wide Web Consortium">World Wide Web Consortium</a> (W3C), which oversees the continued development of the Web. He is also the founder of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Foundation" title="World Wide Web Foundation">World Wide Web Foundation</a> and is a senior researcher and holder of the 3Com <a href="/wiki/Professors_in_the_United_States#Special_academic_ranks_(tenured)" title="Professors in the United States">founders chair</a> at the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Computer_Science_and_Artificial_Intelligence_Laboratory" title="MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory">MIT Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory</a> (CSAIL).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
He is a director of the <a href="/wiki/Web_Science_Research_Initiative" class="mw-redirect" title="Web Science Research Initiative">Web Science Research Initiative</a> (WSRI),<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and a member of the advisory board of the <a href="/wiki/MIT_Center_for_Collective_Intelligence" title="MIT Center for Collective Intelligence">MIT Center for Collective Intelligence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, he was named as a member of the board of trustees of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> He is a founder and president of the <a href="/wiki/Open_Data_Institute" title="Open Data Institute">Open Data Institute</a>.
</p><p>In 2004, Berners-Lee was <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">knighted</a> by Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> for his pioneering work.<sup id="cite_ref-tecb_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tecb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-knighted_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knighted-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  In April 2009, he was elected a foreign associate of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">United States National Academy of Sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Named in <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine's list of the <a href="/wiki/Time_100:_The_Most_Important_People_of_the_Century" title="Time 100: The Most Important People of the Century">100 Most Important People of the 20th century</a>, Berners-Lee has received a <a href="/wiki/Awards_and_honours_presented_to_Tim_Berners-Lee" class="mw-redirect" title="Awards and honours presented to Tim Berners-Lee">number of other accolades</a> for his invention.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> He was honoured as the "Inventor of the World Wide Web" during the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics_opening_ceremony" title="2012 Summer Olympics opening ceremony">2012 Summer Olympics opening ceremony</a>, in which he appeared in person, working with a vintage <a href="/wiki/NeXT_Computer" title="NeXT Computer">NeXT Computer</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Stadium_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Olympic Stadium (London)">London Olympic Stadium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Friar_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friar-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> He <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">tweeted</a> "This is for everyone",<sup id="cite_ref-OlympicsTweet_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OlympicsTweet-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> which instantly was spelled out in <a href="/wiki/LCD" class="mw-redirect" title="LCD">LCD</a> lights attached to the chairs of the 80,000 people in the audience.<sup id="cite_ref-Friar_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Friar-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Berners-Lee received the 2016 <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> "for inventing the World Wide Web, the first web browser, and the fundamental protocols and algorithms allowing the Web to scale".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span></h2>
<p>Berners-Lee was born in London, England, United Kingdom,<sup id="cite_ref-W3Bio_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3Bio-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> one of four children born to <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lee_Woods" title="Mary Lee Woods">Mary Lee Woods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conway_Berners-Lee" title="Conway Berners-Lee">Conway Berners-Lee</a>. His parents worked on the first commercially built computer, the <a href="/wiki/Ferranti_Mark_1" title="Ferranti Mark 1">Ferranti Mark 1</a>. He attended Sheen Mount Primary School, and then went on to attend south west London's <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_School" title="Emanuel School">Emanuel School</a> from 1969 to 1973, at the time a <a href="/wiki/Direct_grant_grammar_school" title="Direct grant grammar school">direct grant grammar school</a>, which became an <a href="/wiki/Independent_school" title="Independent school">independent school</a> in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tecb_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tecb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A keen <a href="/wiki/Trainspotter" class="mw-redirect" title="Trainspotter">trainspotter</a> as a child, he learnt about electronics from tinkering with a model railway.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He studied at <a href="/wiki/The_Queen%27s_College,_Oxford" title="The Queen&#39;s College, Oxford">The Queen's College, Oxford</a>, from 1973 to 1976, where he received a <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification#First-class_honours" title="British undergraduate degree classification">first-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Bachelor of arts">bachelor of arts</a> degree in physics.<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-W3Bio_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3Bio-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> While he was at college, Berners-Lee made a computer out of an old <a href="/wiki/Television_set" title="Television set">television set</a>, which he bought from a repair shop.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tim_Berners-Lee.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Tim_Berners-Lee.jpg/220px-Tim_Berners-Lee.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Tim_Berners-Lee.jpg/330px-Tim_Berners-Lee.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f8/Tim_Berners-Lee.jpg 2x" data-file-width="425" data-file-height="425" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tim_Berners-Lee.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Berners-Lee, 2005</div></div></div>
<p>After graduation, Berners-Lee worked as an engineer at the telecommunications company <a href="/wiki/Plessey" title="Plessey">Plessey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a>, Dorset.<sup id="cite_ref-W3Bio_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3Bio-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In 1978, he joined D. G. Nash in <a href="/wiki/Ferndown" title="Ferndown">Ferndown</a>, Dorset, where he helped create type-setting software for printers.<sup id="cite_ref-W3Bio_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W3Bio-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Berners-Lee worked as an <a href="/wiki/Independent_contractor" title="Independent contractor">independent contractor</a> at <a href="/wiki/CERN" title="CERN">CERN</a> from June to December 1980. While in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, he proposed a project based on the concept of <a href="/wiki/Hypertext" title="Hypertext">hypertext</a>, to facilitate sharing and updating information among researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> To demonstrate it, he built a prototype system named <a href="/wiki/ENQUIRE" title="ENQUIRE">ENQUIRE</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-linvinginternet_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linvinginternet-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After leaving CERN in late 1980, he went to work at John Poole's Image Computer Systems, Ltd, in Bournemouth, Dorset.<sup id="cite_ref-faq_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> He ran the company's technical side for three years.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The project he worked on was a "<a href="/wiki/Real-time_computing" title="Real-time computing">real-time</a> <a href="/wiki/Remote_procedure_call" title="Remote procedure call">remote procedure call</a>" which gave him experience in <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer networking</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-faq_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faq-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In 1984, he returned to CERN as a fellow.<sup id="cite_ref-linvinginternet_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linvinginternet-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1989, CERN was the largest internet node in Europe, and Berners-Lee saw an opportunity to join hypertext with the internet:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>I just had to take the hypertext idea and connect it to the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">Transmission Control Protocol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Domain name system">domain name system</a> ideas and—ta-da!—the World Wide Web<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> ... Creating the web was really an act of desperation, because the situation without it was very difficult when I was working at CERN later. Most of the technology involved in the web, like the hypertext, like the internet, multifont text objects, had all been designed already. I just had to put them together. It was a step of generalising, going to a higher level of abstraction, thinking about all the documentation systems out there as being possibly part of a larger imaginary documentation system.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:First_Web_Server.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/First_Web_Server.jpg/220px-First_Web_Server.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/First_Web_Server.jpg/330px-First_Web_Server.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/First_Web_Server.jpg/440px-First_Web_Server.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:First_Web_Server.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This <a href="/wiki/NeXT_Computer" title="NeXT Computer">NeXT Computer</a> was used by Berners-Lee at CERN and became the world's first web server</div></div></div>
<p>Berners-Lee wrote his proposal in March 1989 and, in 1990, redistributed it. It then was accepted by his manager, Mike Sendall.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> He used similar ideas to those underlying the ENQUIRE system to create the World Wide Web, for which he designed and built the first <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">Web browser</a>. His software also functioned as an editor (called <a href="/wiki/WorldWideWeb" title="WorldWideWeb">WorldWideWeb</a>, running on the <a href="/wiki/NeXTSTEP" title="NeXTSTEP">NeXTSTEP</a> operating system), and the first Web server, <a href="/wiki/CERN_HTTPd" class="mw-redirect" title="CERN HTTPd">CERN HTTPd</a> (short for Hypertext Transfer Protocol <a href="/wiki/Daemon_(computer_software)" class="mw-redirect" title="Daemon (computer software)">daemon</a>).
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Mike Sendall buys a NeXT cube for evaluation, and gives it to Tim [Berners-Lee]. Tim's prototype implementation on NeXTStep is made in the space of a few months, thanks to the qualities of the NeXTStep software development system. This prototype offers <a href="/wiki/WYSIWYG" title="WYSIWYG">WYSIWYG</a> browsing/authoring! Current Web browsers used in 'surfing the internet' are mere passive windows, depriving the user of the possibility to contribute. During some sessions in the CERN cafeteria, Tim and I try to find a catching name for the system. I was determined that the name should not yet again be taken from Greek mythology..... Tim proposes 'World-Wide Web'. I like this very much, except that it is difficult to pronounce in French... by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cailliau" title="Robert Cailliau">Robert Cailliau</a>, 2 November 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-Ben_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ben-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> 
</p></blockquote>
<p>The first website was built at CERN. Despite this being an international organisation hosted by Switzerland, the office that Berners-Lee used was just across the border in France.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The website was put online on 6 August 1991 for the first time:<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><b>info.cern.ch</b> was the address of the world's first-ever web site and web server, running on a NeXT computer at CERN. The first web page address was <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://info.cern.ch/hypertext/WWW/TheProject.html">http://info.cern.ch/hypertext/WWW/TheProject.html</a>, which centred on information regarding the WWW project. Visitors could learn more about hypertext, technical details for creating their own webpage, and even an explanation on how to search the Web for information. There are no screenshots of this original page and, in any case, changes were made daily to the information available on the page as the WWW project developed. You may find a later copy (1992) on the World Wide Web Consortium website.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>It provided an explanation of what the World Wide Web was, and how one could use a browser and set up a web server, as well as how to get started with your own website.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In a list of 80 cultural moments that shaped the world, chosen by a panel of 25 eminent scientists, academics, writers, and world leaders, the invention of the World Wide Web was ranked number one, with the entry stating, "The fastest growing communications medium of all time, the internet has changed the shape of modern life forever. We can connect with each other instantly, all over the world".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, Berners-Lee founded the W3C at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>. It comprised various companies that were willing to create standards and recommendations to improve the quality of the Web. Berners-Lee made his idea available freely, with no patent and no royalties due. The World Wide Web Consortium decided that its standards should be based on royalty-free technology, so that they easily could be adopted by anyone.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, Berners-Lee became a patron of the East Dorset Heritage Trust, having previously lived in <a href="/wiki/Colehill" title="Colehill">Colehill</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wimborne_Minster" title="Wimborne Minster">Wimborne</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Dorset" title="East Dorset">East Dorset</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In December 2004, he accepted a chair in computer science at the School of Electronics and Computer Science, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southampton" title="University of Southampton">University of Southampton</a>, Hampshire, to work on the <a href="/wiki/Semantic_Web" title="Semantic Web">Semantic Web</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">Times</a></i> article in October 2009, Berners-Lee admitted that <a href="/wiki/URI_scheme#Syntax" class="mw-redirect" title="URI scheme">the initial pair of slashes</a> ("//") in a web address were "unnecessary". He told the newspaper that he easily could have designed web addresses without the slashes. "There you go, it seemed like a good idea at the time", he said in his lighthearted apology.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_work">Recent work</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Timbernerslee.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Timbernerslee.jpg/220px-Timbernerslee.jpg" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Timbernerslee.jpg/330px-Timbernerslee.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Timbernerslee.jpg/440px-Timbernerslee.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="862" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Timbernerslee.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tim Berners-Lee at the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a>, London, on 11 March 2010</div></div></div>
<p>In June 2009, then-British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> announced Berners-Lee would work with the <a href="/wiki/UK_government" class="mw-redirect" title="UK government">UK government</a> to help make data more open and accessible on the Web, building on the work of the Power of Information Task Force.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Berners-Lee and Professor <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Shadbolt" title="Nigel Shadbolt">Nigel Shadbolt</a> are the two key figures behind <a href="/wiki/Data.gov.uk" title="Data.gov.uk">data.gov.uk</a>, a UK government project to open up almost all data acquired for official purposes for free re-use. Commenting on the opening up of <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> data in April 2010, Berners-Lee said that: "The changes signal a wider cultural change in government based on an assumption that information should be in the public domain unless there is a good reason not to—not the other way around." He went on to say: "Greater openness, accountability and transparency in Government will give people greater choice and make it easier for individuals to get more directly involved in issues that matter to them."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg/220px-Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg/330px-Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg/440px-Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg 2x" data-file-width="864" data-file-height="576" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Berners-Lee_announcing_W3F.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Berners-Lee speaking at the launch of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Foundation" title="World Wide Web Foundation">World Wide Web Foundation</a></div></div></div>
<p>In November 2009, Berners-Lee launched the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Foundation" title="World Wide Web Foundation">World Wide Web Foundation</a> in order to "advance the Web to empower humanity by launching transformative programs that build local capacity to leverage the Web as a medium for positive change."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Berners-Lee is one of the pioneer voices in favour of <a href="/wiki/Net_neutrality" title="Net neutrality">net neutrality</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> and has expressed the view that <a href="/wiki/ISP" class="mw-redirect" title="ISP">ISPs</a> should supply "connectivity with no strings attached", and should neither control nor monitor the browsing activities of customers without their expressed consent.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> He advocates the idea that net neutrality is a kind of human network right: "Threats to the internet, such as companies or governments that interfere with or snoop on internet traffic, compromise basic human network rights."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Berners-Lee participated in an open letter to the US Federal Communications Commission (FCC). He and 20 other Internet pioneers urged the FCC to cancel a vote on 14 December 2017 to uphold net neutrality. The letter was addressed to Senator Roger Wicker, Senator Brian Schatz, Representative Marsha Blackburn and Representative Michael F. Doyle.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:This_is_for_Everyone.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/This_is_for_Everyone.jpg/220px-This_is_for_Everyone.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/This_is_for_Everyone.jpg/330px-This_is_for_Everyone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/This_is_for_Everyone.jpg/440px-This_is_for_Everyone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:This_is_for_Everyone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Berners-Lee's tweet, "This is for everyone",<sup id="cite_ref-OlympicsTweet_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OlympicsTweet-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics_opening_ceremony#Frankie_and_June_say...thanks_Tim_(21:52–22:09)" title="2012 Summer Olympics opening ceremony">2012 Summer Olympic Games</a> in London</div></div></div>
<p>Berners-Lee joined the board of advisors of start-up <a href="/wiki/State_(website)" title="State (website)">State.com</a>, based in London.<sup id="cite_ref-state_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-state-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> As of May 2012, Berners-Lee is president of the <a href="/wiki/Open_Data_Institute" title="Open Data Institute">Open Data Institute</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> which he co-founded with <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Shadbolt" title="Nigel Shadbolt">Nigel Shadbolt</a> in 2012.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Alliance_for_Affordable_Internet" title="Alliance for Affordable Internet">Alliance for Affordable Internet</a> (A4AI) was launched in October 2013 and Berners-Lee is leading the coalition of public and private organisations that includes <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>, <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>. The A4AI seeks to make internet access more affordable so that access is broadened in the developing world, where only 31% of people are online. Berners-Lee will work with those aiming to decrease internet access prices so that they fall below the <a href="/wiki/Broadband_Commission_for_Digital_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadband Commission for Digital Development">UN Broadband Commission</a>'s worldwide target of 5% of monthly income.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Berners-Lee holds the founders chair in Computer Science at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, where he heads the Decentralized Information Group and is leading <a href="/wiki/Solid_(web_decentralization_project)" title="Solid (web decentralization project)">Solid</a>, a joint project with the <a href="/wiki/Qatar_Computing_Research_Institute" title="Qatar Computing Research Institute">Qatar Computing Research Institute</a> that aims to radically change the way Web applications work today, resulting in true data ownership as well as improved privacy.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> In October 2016, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Computer_Science,_University_of_Oxford" title="Department of Computer Science, University of Oxford">Department of Computer Science</a> at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> as a professorial research fellow<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> and as a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_(college)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellow (college)">fellow</a> of <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church,_Oxford" title="Christ Church, Oxford">Christ Church</a>, one of the Oxford colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 30, 2018, Berners-Lee announced a new application made by <a href="/wiki/Open-source_model" title="Open-source model">open-source</a> startup Inrupt called Solid, which aims to give users more control over their personal data and lets users choose where the data goes, who's allowed to see certain elements and which apps are allowed to see that data.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h2>
<p>Berners-Lee married Nancy Carlson, an American computer programmer, in 1990; she was also working in Switzerland, at the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Health Organisation">World Health Organisation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> They had two children and divorced in 2011. 
</p><p>He formed a relationship with Rosemary Leith, a Canadian internet and banking entrepreneur. Leith studied business at <a href="/wiki/Queen%E2%80%99s_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen’s University">Queen’s University</a>, then moved to Britain to pursue a career in investment and analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> She worked in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> as a principal investor prior to 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> She was married to <a href="/wiki/Mark_Opzoomer" title="Mark Opzoomer">Mark Opzoomer</a>, later the CEO of <a href="/wiki/Rambler_Media" class="mw-redirect" title="Rambler Media">Rambler Media</a>; the couple had three children, after which she left the financial sector, co-founding a start-up during the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Haymarket_Media_Group#Haymarket_Business_Media" title="Haymarket Media Group">Management Today</a></i> described her <a href="/wiki/Online_magazine" title="Online magazine">webzine</a> (as it was called then) as "the first web site devoted entirely to those women struggling to strike a healthy balance between work and home life".<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> More recent projects span both finance and internet. In 2011 she chaired the <a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Forum" title="World Economic Forum">World Economic Forum</a> Global Agenda Council for the Future of Internet Security<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> and from 2015 she has been on the board of <a href="/wiki/YouGov" title="YouGov">YouGov</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> She is on the advisory board for funding platform <a href="/wiki/AllBright" class="mw-redirect" title="AllBright">AllBright</a>, to support "female entrepreneurs from start-ups to established businesses"<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>, and provided funding to <a href="/wiki/Netwealth_Investments_Ltd" title="Netwealth Investments Ltd">Netwealth Investments Ltd</a><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>. Leith is a fellow at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Berkman_Klein_Center_for_Internet_%26_Society" title="Berkman Klein Center for Internet &amp; Society">Berkman Klein Center for Internet &amp; Society</a><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014 Berners-Lee and Leith married at the <a href="/wiki/Chapel_Royal" title="Chapel Royal">Chapel Royal</a>, <a href="/wiki/St._James%27s_Palace" class="mw-redirect" title="St. James&#39;s Palace">St. James's Palace</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The couple collaborate on projects such as <a href="/wiki/Venture_capital" title="Venture capital">venture capital</a> to support <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> companies<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>. Leith is a founding director of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web_Foundation" title="World Wide Web Foundation">World Wide Web Foundation</a><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>, a non-profit Berners-Lee launched.
</p><p>Berners-Lee was raised as an <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a>, but in his youth, he turned away from religion. After he became a parent, he became a <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Unitarian Universalist">Unitarian Universalist</a> (UU).<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> He has stated: "Like many people, I had a religious upbringing which I rejected as a teenager... Like many people, I came back to religion when we had children".<sup id="cite_ref-auto_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> He and his wife wanted to teach spirituality to their children, and after hearing a Unitarian minister and visiting the UU Church, they opted for it.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> He is an active member of that church,<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> to which he adheres because he perceives it as a tolerant and liberal belief. He has said: "I believe that much of the philosophy of life associated with many religions is much more sound than the dogma which comes along with it. So I do respect them."<sup id="cite_ref-auto_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinctions">Distinctions</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_awards_and_honours_received_by_Tim_Berners-Lee" title="List of awards and honours received by Tim Berners-Lee">List of awards and honours received by Tim Berners-Lee</a></div>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:30%;&#32;;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303512">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">"He wove the World Wide Web and created a mass medium for the 21st century. The World Wide Web is Berners-Lee's alone. He designed it. He loosed it on the world. And he more than anyone else has fought to keep it open, nonproprietary and free."</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">—Tim Berners-Lee's entry in <i>Time</i> magazine's list of the <a href="/wiki/Time_100:_The_Most_Important_People_of_the_Century" title="Time 100: The Most Important People of the Century">100 Most Important People of the 20th century</a>, March 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Berners-Lee has received many awards and honours. He was <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">knighted</a> by Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2004_New_Year_Honours" title="2004 New Year Honours">2004 New Year Honours</a> "for services to the global development of the internet", and was invested formally on 16 July 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-tecb_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tecb-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-knighted_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knighted-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 13 June 2007, he was appointed to the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit" title="Order of Merit">Order of Merit</a> (OM), an order restricted to 24 (living) members.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Bestowing membership of the Order of Merit is within the personal purview of the Queen, and does not require recommendation by ministers or the Prime Minister. He was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_2001" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 2001">Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 2001</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-frs_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frs-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He has been conferred honorary degrees from a number of Universities around the world, including <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">Manchester</a> (his parents worked on the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Mark_1" title="Manchester Mark 1">Manchester Mark 1</a> in the 1940s), <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, Berners-Lee was among the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Culture of the United Kingdom">British cultural icons</a> selected by artist Sir <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Peter Blake</a> to appear in a new version of his most famous artwork – the Beatles' <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band" title="Sgt. Pepper&#39;s Lonely Hearts Club Band">Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band</a></i> album cover – to celebrate the British cultural figures of his life that he most admires to mark his 80th birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, he was awarded the inaugural <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_Prize_for_Engineering" title="Queen Elizabeth Prize for Engineering">Queen Elizabeth Prize for Engineering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> On 4 April 2017, he received the 2016 ACM <a href="/wiki/Turing_Award" title="Turing Award">Turing Award</a> "for inventing the World Wide Web, the first web browser, and the fundamental protocols and algorithms allowing the Web to scale".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
