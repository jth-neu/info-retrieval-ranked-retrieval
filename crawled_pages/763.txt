<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Informal empire</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Informal_Empire&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Informal Empire">Informal Empire</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Informal empire</b> describes the <a href="/wiki/Spheres_of_influence" class="mw-redirect" title="Spheres of influence">spheres of influence</a> which an <a href="/wiki/Empire" title="Empire">empire</a> may develop that  translate into a degree of influence over a region or country, which is not a formal colony in the empire,  as a result of the extension of commercial, strategic or military interests of the empire.
</p><p>In a 2010 article, Gregory Barton and Brett Bennett defined informal empire as:
</p>
<dl><dd>a willing and successful attempt by commercial and political elites to control a foreign region, resource, or people. The means of control included the enforcement of extra-territorial privileges and the threat of economic and political sanctions, often coupled with the attempt to keep other would-be imperial powers at bay. For the term "informal empire" to be applicable, we argue, historians have to show that one nation’s elite or government exerted extraterritorial legal control, de facto economic domination, and was able to strongly influence policies in a foreign country critical to the more powerful country’s interests.</dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Empire">British Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Informal_empire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: British Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is most commonly associated with the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, where it is used to describe the extensive reach of British interests into regions and nations which were not formal parts of the Empire, in that they were not <a href="/wiki/Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonies">colonies</a> and were not directly ruled by the British.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Among the most notable elements of the British informal empire was China, which played an important, if unwilling, role in British imperial trade, and South America, which was a significant region for British commercial interests and investments.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Informal empire, like many imperial relationships, is difficult to classify and reduce to a prescriptive definition.  In the instance of the British informal empire, the character of the relationship varied widely.  Chinese inclusion in the sphere of British informal empire was unwilling, and was maintained by the use of direct military coercion – the <a href="/wiki/First_Opium_War" title="First Opium War">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Opium_War" title="Second Opium War">Second Opium Wars</a> – and through the exertion of great diplomatic pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  South American governments were often willing partners in the extension of British commercial ventures, but force was sometimes used against those who tried to apply <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionist</a> policies (see for example, the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-French_blockade_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Anglo-French blockade of the Río de la Plata">Anglo-French blockade of the River Plate</a>).
</p><p>Informal empire is an important concept required to adequately explain the reach and influence of empire, and in the case of the British Empire, is vital to any holistic account of the British Imperial experience and intrinsic to describing the interests and purposes of the Empire as a whole.  Informal empire, far from being distinctive and separate from formal empire, is often bound up with formal imperial interests.  For example, British informal empire in China was a product of British formal control of India, resulting from the need to finance the activities of the British <a href="/wiki/East_India_Company" title="East India Company">East India Company</a> through the sale of <a href="/wiki/Opium" title="Opium">opium</a> to the Chinese.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the economic sphere, British informal empire was driven by the <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free trade</a> economic system of the Empire.  In the so-called "<a href="/wiki/The_Imperialism_of_Free_Trade" title="The Imperialism of Free Trade">Imperialism of Free Trade</a>" thesis, as articulated by historians <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Robinson" title="Ronald Robinson">Ronald Robinson</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Andrew_Gallagher" title="John Andrew Gallagher">John Gallagher</a>, the British Empire expanded as much by the growth of informal empire as it did by acquiring formal dominion over colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Furthermore, British investment in empire was to be found not only in the formal Empire, but also in the informal empire – and, by Robinson and Gallagher's account, was indeed predominantly located in the informal empire.  It is estimated that between 1815 and 1880, £1,187,000,000 in credit had accumulated abroad, but no more than one-sixth was placed in the formal empire. Even by 1913, less than half of the £3,975,000,000 of foreign investment lay inside the formal Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Challenges to British dominance in China from other Europeans, as well as by the US, led to a shift from informal to formal imperialism at the turn of the century.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>British <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> <a href="/wiki/David_Reynolds_(British_historian)" title="David Reynolds (British historian)">David Reynolds</a> claims that during the process of <a href="/wiki/Decolonization" title="Decolonization">decolonisation</a>, it was hoped that as an alternative to the declining formal empire, informal influence, sealed by economic ties and defense treaties would be swayed over the former colonies. The <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> is suggested to have been an attempt by Britain to maintain some indirect influence over the newly independent countries.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Informal empire may assume a primarily economic guise.  However, this is not the exclusive definition or requirement of the term.  Strategic considerations or other concerns may bring about the creation of an imperial influence over a region not formally a component of the empire.
</p>
