<p><b>Mark Perry McCahill</b> (born February 7, 1956) is an American <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer scientist</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Internet_pioneers" title="List of Internet pioneers">Internet pioneer</a>. He has been involved in developing and popularizing a number of <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> technologies since the late 1980s.
</p><p>McCahill led the development of the <a href="/wiki/Gopher_(protocol)" title="Gopher (protocol)">Gopher</a> protocol, the effective predecessor of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>, he was involved in creating and codifying the standard for <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locators" class="mw-redirect" title="Uniform Resource Locators">Uniform Resource Locators</a> (URLs) and he led the development of <a href="/wiki/POPmail" title="POPmail">POPmail</a>, one of the first e-mail clients which had a foundational influence on later e-mail clients and the popularization of graphical user interfaces in Internet technologies more broadly. He also coined the phrase "surfing the Internet."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-netmom_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-netmom-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He currently works at the Office of Information Technology at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> as an architect of 3-D learning and <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_software" title="Collaborative software">collaborative systems</a>. A major focus of his later work has been <a href="/wiki/Virtual_worlds" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtual worlds">virtual worlds</a>, and he was one of six principal architects of the <a href="/wiki/Croquet_Project" title="Croquet Project">Croquet Project</a>. He is also a noted writer on virtual worlds as his "tabloid reporter" <a href="/wiki/Avatar" title="Avatar">avatar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> "Pixeleen Mistral."
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_P._McCahill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mark McCahill received a BA in Chemistry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> in 1979, spent one year doing analytical environmental chemistry, and then joined the University of Minnesota Computer Center's microcomputer support group as an <a href="/wiki/Apple_II" title="Apple II">Apple II</a> and <a href="/wiki/CDC_Cyber" title="CDC Cyber">CDC Cyber</a> programmer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_pioneer">Internet pioneer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_P._McCahill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Internet pioneer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, McCahill led the team at the University of Minnesota that developed one of the first popular Internet e-mail clients, <a href="/wiki/POPmail" title="POPmail">POPmail</a>, for the Macintosh (and later the PC). The usage of graphical user interface clients for Internet standards-based protocols proved to be one of the dominant themes in the popularization of the Internet. At about the same time as POPmail was being developed, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Dorner" title="Steve Dorner">Steve Dorner</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign</a> developed <a href="/wiki/Eudora_(e-mail_client)" class="mw-redirect" title="Eudora (e-mail client)">Eudora</a>, and the user interface conventions found in these early efforts continue to be present in modern-day e-mail clients.
</p><p>In 1991, McCahill led the original <a href="/wiki/Gopher_(protocol)" title="Gopher (protocol)">Gopher</a> development team<sup id="cite_ref-tri_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tri-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (Farhad Anklesaria, Paul Lindner, Daniel  Torrey, Bob Alberti), which invented a simple way to navigate distributed information resources on the Internet. Gopher's menu-based <a href="/wiki/Hypermedia" title="Hypermedia">hypermedia</a> combined with full-text <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">search engines</a> paved the way for the popularization of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> and was the <i>de facto</i> standard for Internet information systems in the early to mid 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Working with other pioneers such as <a href="/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee" title="Tim Berners-Lee">Tim Berners-Lee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Andreessen" title="Marc Andreessen">Marc Andreessen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Emtage" title="Alan Emtage">Alan Emtage</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_J._Deutsch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Peter J. Deutsch (page does not exist)">Peter J. Deutsch</a> (creators of <a href="/wiki/Archie_search_engine" title="Archie search engine">Archie</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Jon_Postel" title="Jon Postel">Jon Postel</a>, McCahill was involved in creating and codifying the standard for <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Resource_Locators" class="mw-redirect" title="Uniform Resource Locators">Uniform Resource Locators</a> (URLs).
</p><p>In 1994-95 McCahill's team developed <a href="/wiki/GopherVR" title="GopherVR">GopherVR</a>, a 3D user interface for the Gopher protocol to explore how spatial metaphors could be used to organize information and create social spaces. While there was significant interest in the mid-1990s in 3D Internet-enabled information/social spaces (see <a href="/wiki/VRML" title="VRML">VRML</a>), the limitied capabilities of mainstream hardware resulted in little uptake of these technologies. Mark McCahill was involved in the <a href="/wiki/Croquet_project" class="mw-redirect" title="Croquet project">Croquet project</a> along with <a href="/wiki/David_P._Reed" title="David P. Reed">David P. Reed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Raab" class="mw-redirect" title="Andreas Raab">Andreas Raab</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_A._Smith_(computer_scientist)" title="David A. Smith (computer scientist)">David A Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julian_Lombardi" title="Julian Lombardi">Julian Lombardi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Alan_Kay" title="Alan Kay">Alan Kay</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_work">Later work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_P._McCahill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2007, McCahill left the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> to join the Office of Information Technology at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> as an architect of 3-D learning and collaborative systems.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_worlds">Virtual worlds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_P._McCahill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Virtual worlds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2010, Mark McCahill was revealed by the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ludlow" title="Peter Ludlow">Peter Ludlow</a> (also known by the pseudonym Urizenus Sklar) to be the Internet persona Pixeleen Mistral, a noted "tabloid reporter" covering <a href="/wiki/Virtual_worlds" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtual worlds">virtual worlds</a> who was the editor of Ludlow's newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alphaville_Herald" title="The Alphaville Herald">The Alphaville Herald</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In a 2016 interview with <a href="/wiki/Leo_Laporte" title="Leo Laporte">Leo Laporte</a>, McCahill said that his involvement with developing the <a href="/wiki/Croquet_Project" title="Croquet Project">Croquet Project</a> had led him into contact with <a href="/wiki/Second_Life" title="Second Life">Second Life</a> and that he had become interested in the <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> of virtual worlds. As Pixeleen Mistral, he was a prominent reporter on Second Life, and a celebrity inside the game, although his real identity was not known by anyone for many years.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
