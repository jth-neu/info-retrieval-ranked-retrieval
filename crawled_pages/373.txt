<p><b>Paid inclusion</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_marketing" title="Search engine marketing">search engine marketing</a> product where the <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">search engine</a> company charges fees related to inclusion of <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">websites</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Search_index" class="mw-redirect" title="Search index">search index</a>. The use of paid inclusion is controversial and paid inclusion's popularity has decreased over time among search engines.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_of_paid_inclusion">Definition of paid inclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paid_inclusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition of paid inclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">FTC</a> defined paid inclusion as "Paid inclusion can take many forms. Examples of paid inclusion include programs where the only sites listed are those that have paid; where paid sites are intermingled among non-paid sites; and where companies pay to have their Web sites or URLs reviewed more quickly, or for more frequent spidering of their Web sites or URLs, or for the review or inclusion of deeper levels of their Web sites, than is the case with non-paid sites."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Note that paid inclusion is different from <a href="/wiki/Pay_for_placement" title="Pay for placement">paid placement</a>. With paid placement, companies pay search engines to rank higher than they would have ranked if relevancy was the only ranking factor. Paid placement also gives companies guaranteed top rankings if they pay for it. With paid inclusion, top rankings are not guaranteed and only inclusion within the search engine is. Therefore, a company who paid for inclusion within a search engine will still have its rankings determined by relevancy.<sup id="cite_ref-evo_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_paid_inclusion">History of paid inclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paid_inclusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of paid inclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early days of search, paid inclusion was a convenient way for search engines, such as <a href="/wiki/Inktomi" title="Inktomi">Inktomi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Microsoft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ask.com" title="Ask.com">Ask</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overture" title="Overture">Overture</a>, <a href="/wiki/AltaVista" title="AltaVista">AltaVista</a>, and FAST, to obtain revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-evo_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evo-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dino_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dino-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Unlike the other major search engines, <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> decided to avoid paid inclusion and, instead, pursue higher relevancy using <a href="/wiki/AdSense" title="AdSense">AdSense</a> as its revenue source. As time went by, search engines such as <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Microsoft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ask.com" title="Ask.com">Ask</a> moved away from paid inclusion—both search engines ended paid inclusion programs in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Google.27s_incorporation_of_paid_inclusion"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Google's_incorporation_of_paid_inclusion">Google's incorporation of paid inclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paid_inclusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Google&#039;s incorporation of paid inclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2012, Google re-incorporated paid inclusion within its search, though in a different form. <a href="/wiki/Google_Flights" title="Google Flights">Google Flights</a>, Google Hotel Finder, and <a href="/wiki/Google_Shopping" title="Google Shopping">Google Shopping</a> all have new forms of paid inclusion programs. Some critics, such as Danny Sullivan, founder of <a href="/wiki/Search_Engine_Watch" title="Search Engine Watch">Search Engine Watch</a>, criticize this move as a step away from the Founders Letter that was a part of Google's IPO.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Aaron Wall of the popular SEO site SEOBook.com criticize Google's use of paid inclusion as a way to push almost all organic rankings <a href="/wiki/Above_the_fold" title="Above the fold">below the fold</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_views_on_paid_inclusion">Mixed views on paid inclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paid_inclusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mixed views on paid inclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Paid inclusion has its advantages and drawbacks. The advantage of a paid inclusion search engine is that spam is reduced while relevancy improves.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, detractors of paid inclusion allege that it causes searches to return results based more on the <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic</a> standing of the interests of a web site, and less on the relevancy of that site to <a href="/wiki/End-user" class="mw-redirect" title="End-user">end-users</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dino_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dino-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ask_Jeeves" class="mw-redirect" title="Ask Jeeves">Ask Jeeves</a> reported that paid inclusion reduced relevancy and, in 2004, ended its paid inclusion program.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Guidelines_for_paid_inclusion">Guidelines for paid inclusion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paid_inclusion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Guidelines for paid inclusion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The FTC has advised search engines to clearly mark paid placement and paid inclusion in accordance with Section 5 of the <a href="/wiki/FTC_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="FTC Act">FTC Act</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> For this is only a guideline and not a law, companies with search algorithms such as Nextag and Google are not legally bound to follow it.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
