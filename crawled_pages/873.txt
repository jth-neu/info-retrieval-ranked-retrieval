<p><b>Monetarism</b> is a <a href="/wiki/School_of_thought" title="School of thought">school of thought</a> in <a href="/wiki/Monetary_economics" title="Monetary economics">monetary economics</a> that emphasizes the role of governments in controlling the amount of <a href="/wiki/Money_in_circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Money in circulation">money in circulation</a>. Monetarist theory asserts that variations in the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> have major influences on <a href="/wiki/Measures_of_national_income_and_output" title="Measures of national income and output">national output</a> in the short run and on <a href="/wiki/Price_level" title="Price level">price levels</a> over longer periods. Monetarists assert that the objectives of <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a> are best met by targeting the growth rate of the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> rather than by engaging in <a href="/wiki/Discretionary_policy" title="Discretionary policy">discretionary monetary policy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cagan_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cagan-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Monetarism today is mainly associated with the work of <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>, who was among the generation of economists to accept <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a> and then criticise Keynes's theory of fighting economic downturns using fiscal policy (government spending). Friedman and <a href="/wiki/Anna_Schwartz" title="Anna Schwartz">Anna Schwartz</a> wrote an influential book, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Monetary_History_of_the_United_States" title="A Monetary History of the United States">A Monetary History of the United States, 1867–1960</a></i>, and argued "<a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a> is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." 
</p><p> Though he opposed the existence of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" title="Federal Reserve System">Federal Reserve</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-reason_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reason-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Friedman advocated, given its existence, a <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a> policy aimed at keeping the growth of the money supply at a rate commensurate with the growth in productivity and <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">demand for goods</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monetarism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Monetarism is an economic theory that focuses on the <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomic</a> effects of the supply of money and central banking. Formulated by <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>, it argues that excessive expansion of the money supply is inherently <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflationary</a>, and that monetary authorities should focus solely on maintaining <a href="/wiki/Price_stability" title="Price stability">price stability</a>.
</p><p>This theory draws its roots from two historically antagonistic schools of thought: the hard money policies that dominated monetary thinking in the late 19th century, and the monetary theories of <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a>, who, working in the inter-war period during the failure of the restored <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>, proposed a demand-driven model for money.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While Keynes had focused on the stability of a currency’s value, with panics based on an insufficient money supply leading to the use of an alternate currency and collapse of the monetary system, Friedman focused on price stability.
</p><p>The result was summarised in a historical analysis of monetary policy, <i>Monetary History of the United States 1867–1960</i>, which Friedman coauthored with <a href="/wiki/Anna_Schwartz" title="Anna Schwartz">Anna Schwartz</a>. The book attributed inflation to excess money supply generated by a central bank. It attributed deflationary spirals to the reverse effect of a failure of a central bank to support the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> during a <a href="/wiki/Liquidity" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquidity">liquidity</a> crunch.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Friedman originally proposed a fixed <i>monetary rule</i>, called <a href="/wiki/Friedman%27s_k-percent_rule" title="Friedman&#39;s k-percent rule">Friedman's k-percent rule</a>, where the money supply would be automatically increased by a fixed percentage per year. Under this rule, there would be no leeway for the central reserve bank, as money supply increases could be determined "by a computer", and business could anticipate all money supply changes.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> With other monetarists he believed that the active manipulation of the money supply or its growth rate is more likely to destabilise than stabilise the economy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_the_gold_standard">Opposition to the gold standard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monetarism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Opposition to the gold standard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most monetarists oppose the <a href="/wiki/Gold_standard" title="Gold standard">gold standard</a>. Friedman, for example, viewed a pure gold standard as impractical.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> For example, whereas one of the benefits of the gold standard is that the intrinsic limitations to the growth of the money supply by the use of gold would prevent inflation, if the growth of population or increase in trade outpaces the money supply, there would be no way to counteract deflation and reduced liquidity (and any attendant recession) except for the mining of more gold.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise">Rise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monetarism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Clark_Warburton" title="Clark Warburton">Clark Warburton</a> is credited with making the first solid empirical case for the monetarist interpretation of <a href="/wiki/Business_fluctuation" class="mw-redirect" title="Business fluctuation">business fluctuations</a> in a series of papers from 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-Cagan_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cagan-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup>p.&#160;493</sup> Within mainstream economics, the rise of monetarism accelerated from <a href="/wiki/Milton_Friedman" title="Milton Friedman">Milton Friedman</a>'s 1956 restatement of the <a href="/wiki/Quantity_theory_of_money" title="Quantity theory of money">quantity theory of money</a>. Friedman argued that the <a href="/wiki/Demand_for_money" title="Demand for money">demand for money</a> could be described as depending on a small number of economic variables.<sup id="cite_ref-hilbertcorporation_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilbertcorporation-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thus, where the <a href="/wiki/Money_supply" title="Money supply">money supply</a> expanded, people would not simply wish to hold the extra money in idle money balances; i.e., if they were in equilibrium before the increase, they were already holding money balances to suit their requirements, and thus after the increase they would have money balances surplus to their requirements. These excess money balances would therefore be spent and hence <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_demand" title="Aggregate demand">aggregate demand</a> would rise. Similarly, if the money supply were reduced people would want to replenish their holdings of money by reducing their spending. In this, Friedman challenged a simplification attributed to Keynes suggesting that "money does not matter."<sup id="cite_ref-hilbertcorporation_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hilbertcorporation-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Thus the word 'monetarist' was coined.
</p><p>The rise of the popularity of monetarism also picked up in political circles when Keynesian economics seemed unable to explain or cure the seemingly contradictory problems of rising <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inflation_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inflation (economics)">inflation</a> in response to the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system" title="Bretton Woods system">Bretton Woods system</a> in 1972 and the <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">oil shocks of 1973</a>. On the one hand, higher unemployment seemed to call for Keynesian <a href="/wiki/Reflation" title="Reflation">reflation</a>, but on the other hand rising inflation seemed to call for Keynesian <a href="/wiki/Disinflation_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Disinflation (economics)">disinflation</a>.
</p><p>In 1979, President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> appointed as Federal Reserve chief <a href="/wiki/Paul_Volcker" title="Paul Volcker">Paul Volcker</a>, who made fighting inflation his primary objective, and who restricted the money supply (in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Friedman_rule" title="Friedman rule">Friedman rule</a>) to tame inflation in the economy. The result was a major rise in interest rates, not only in the United States; but worldwide. The "Volcker shock" continued from 1979 to the summer of 1982, dramatically both decreasing inflation and increasing <a href="/wiki/Unemployment" title="Unemployment">unemployment</a>. Monetarist economists never recognized that the policy implemented by the Federal Reserve from 1979 was a monetarist policy. Nevertheless, the influence of monetarism on the Federal Reserve was twofold: a direct influence, by the adherence of some members of the Federal Open Market Committee to monetarist ideas; and an indirect influence, because monetarist views were taken into account in the determination of US monetary policy: even the members of the FOMC who were not monetarists took monetarist influence into strong consideration.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png/400px-Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png" width="400" height="194" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png/600px-Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png/800px-Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png 2x" data-file-width="3036" data-file-height="1473" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Money_supply_during_the_great_depression_era.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Money supply decreased significantly between <a href="/wiki/Black_Tuesday" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Tuesday">Black Tuesday</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Banking_Act" title="Emergency Banking Act">Bank Holiday in March 1933</a> in the wake of massive <a href="/wiki/Bank_runs" class="mw-redirect" title="Bank runs">bank runs</a> across the United States.</div></div></div>
<p>Monetarists not only sought to explain present problems; they also interpreted historical ones. Milton Friedman and <a href="/wiki/Anna_Schwartz" title="Anna Schwartz">Anna Schwartz</a> in their book <i>A Monetary History of the United States, 1867–1960</i> argued that the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> of 1930 was caused by a massive contraction of the money supply and not by the lack of investment Keynes had argued. They also maintained that post-war inflation was caused by an over-expansion of the money supply.
</p><p>They made famous the assertion of monetarism that "inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon". Many Keynesian economists initially believed that the Keynesian vs. monetarist debate was solely about whether fiscal or <a href="/wiki/Monetary_policy" title="Monetary policy">monetary policy</a> was the more effective tool of demand management. By the mid-1970s, however, the debate had moved on to other issues as monetarists began presenting a fundamental challenge to Keynesianism.
</p><p>Many monetarists sought to resurrect the pre-Keynesian view that market economies are inherently stable in the absence of major unexpected fluctuations in the money supply. Because of this belief in the stability of free-market economies they asserted that active demand management (e.g. by the means of increasing government spending) is unnecessary and indeed likely to be harmful. The basis of this argument is a relationship between "stimulus" fiscal spending and future interest rates. In effect, Friedman's model argues that current fiscal spending creates as much of a drag on the economy by increased interest rates as it creates present consumption: that it has no real effect on total demand, merely that of shifting demand from the investment sector to the consumer sector.
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, Leader of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, won the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">1979 general election</a> defeating the sitting <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Government</a> led by <a href="/wiki/James_Callaghan" title="James Callaghan">James Callaghan</a>, the UK had endured several years of severe <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>, which was rarely below the 10% mark and by the time of the May 1979 general election, stood at 15.4%.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Thatcher implemented monetarism as the weapon in her battle against inflation, and succeeded at reducing it to 4.6% by 1983.
</p><p>Callaghan had adopted anti-inflation policies during his tenure as Prime Minister from 1976-79, reducing public spending in response to high inflation and national debt. He initially had some success, as inflation was below 10% by the summer of 1978, although unemployment now stood at 1,500,000. However, by the time of his election defeat barely a year later, inflation had risen to 15.4%.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_state">Current state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monetarism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1990, the classical form of monetarism has been questioned. This is because of events that many economists interpreted as being inexplicable in monetarist terms: the disconnection of the money supply growth from inflation in the 1990s and the failure of pure monetary policy to stimulate the economy in the 2001–2003 period. Greenspan argued that the 1990s decoupling was explained by a <a href="/wiki/Virtuous_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtuous cycle">virtuous cycle</a> of productivity and investment on one hand, and a certain degree of "<a href="/wiki/Irrational_exuberance" title="Irrational exuberance">irrational exuberance</a>" in the investment sector on the other.
</p><p>There are also arguments that monetarism is a special case of Keynesian theory. The central test case over the validity of these theories would be the possibility of a <a href="/wiki/Liquidity_trap" title="Liquidity trap">liquidity trap</a>, like that experienced by Japan. <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bernanke" title="Ben Bernanke">Ben Bernanke</a>, Princeton professor and another former chairman of the U.S. Federal Reserve, argued that monetary policy could respond to zero interest rate conditions by direct expansion of the money supply. In his words, "We have the keys to the printing press, and we are not afraid to use them." Progressive economist <a href="/wiki/Paul_Krugman" title="Paul Krugman">Paul Krugman</a> has advanced the counterargument that this would have a corresponding devaluationary effect, like the sustained low interest rates of 2001–2004 produced against world currencies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>These disagreements—along with the role of monetary policies in trade liberalisation, international investment, and central bank policy—remain lively topics of investigation and argument.
</p>
