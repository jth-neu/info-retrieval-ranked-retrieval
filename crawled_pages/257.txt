<p><b>WebCrawler</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Metasearch_engine" title="Metasearch engine">metasearch engine</a> that blends the top search results from <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google Search</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search" title="Yahoo! Search">Yahoo! Search</a>. WebCrawler also provides users the option to search for images, audio, video, news, yellow pages and white pages. WebCrawler is a registered trademark of <a href="/wiki/InfoSpace" class="mw-redirect" title="InfoSpace">InfoSpace</a>, Inc. It went live on April 20, 1994, and was created by Brian Pinkerton at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-before_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-before-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WebCrawler&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WebCrawler was the first <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">Web search engine</a> to provide <a href="/wiki/Full_text_search" class="mw-redirect" title="Full text search">full text search</a>. It was bought by <a href="/wiki/America_Online" class="mw-redirect" title="America Online">America Online</a> on June 1, 1995, and sold to <a href="/wiki/Excite" title="Excite">Excite</a> on April 1, 1997. WebCrawler was acquired by <a href="/wiki/InfoSpace" class="mw-redirect" title="InfoSpace">InfoSpace</a> in 2001 after <a href="/wiki/Excite" title="Excite">Excite</a> (which was then called <a href="/wiki/Excite@Home" class="mw-redirect" title="Excite@Home">Excite@Home</a>) went bankrupt. InfoSpace also owns and operates the metasearch engines <a href="/wiki/Dogpile" title="Dogpile">Dogpile</a> and <a href="/wiki/MetaCrawler" title="MetaCrawler">MetaCrawler</a>.
</p><p>WebCrawler was originally a separate search engine with its own database, and displayed advertising results in separate areas of the page.  More recently it has been repositioned as a metasearch engine, providing a composite of separately identified sponsored and non-sponsored search results from most of the popular search engines.
</p><p>WebCrawler also changed its image in early 2008, scrapping its classic spider <a href="/wiki/Mascot" title="Mascot">mascot</a>.
</p><p>In July 2010, WebCrawler was ranked the 753rd most popular website in the U.S., and 2994th most popular in the world by Alexa.  Quantcast estimated 1.7 million unique U.S. visitors a month, while Compete estimated 7,015,395—a difference so large that at least one of the companies has faulty methods, according to Alexa.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>WebCrawler formerly fetched results from <a href="/wiki/Bing_Search" class="mw-redirect" title="Bing Search">Bing Search</a> (formerly <a href="/wiki/MSN_Search" class="mw-redirect" title="MSN Search">MSN Search</a> and <a href="/wiki/Live_Search" class="mw-redirect" title="Live Search">Live Search</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ask.com" title="Ask.com">Ask.com</a>, <a href="/wiki/About.com" class="mw-redirect" title="About.com">About.com</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Miva_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Miva (company) (page does not exist)">MIVA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/LookSmart" title="LookSmart">LookSmart</a>.
</p><p>Pinkerton later led the Amazon <a href="/wiki/A9.com" title="A9.com">A9.com</a> search division.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-before_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-before-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2016, Blucora announced the sale of its Infospace business to OpenMail for $45 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> OpenMail was later renamed System1.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
