<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Spell checker</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>, a <b>spell checker</b> (or <b>spell check</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">application program</a> that flags words in a document that may not be spelled correctly. Spell checkers may be stand-alone,  capable of operating on a block of text, or as part of a larger application, such as a <a href="/wiki/Word_processor" title="Word processor">word processor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Email_client" title="Email client">email client</a>, electronic <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">dictionary</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">search engine</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner noresize" style="width:254px;"><div class="thumbimage" style="width:252px;"><div style="font-family: arial; font-size: 12px; font-weight: bold; background-color: #ffffff" class="poem">
<p>Eye have a spelling chequer,<br />
It came with my Pea Sea.<br />
It plane lee marks four my revue<br />
Miss Steaks I can knot sea.<br />
<br />
Eye strike the quays and type a whirred <br />
And weight four it two say <br />
Weather eye am write oar wrong <br />
It tells me straight a weigh.<br />
<br />
Eye ran this poem threw it,<br />
Your shore real glad two no.<br />
Its vary polished in its weigh.<br />
My chequer tolled me sew.<br />
<br />
A chequer is a bless thing,<br />
It freeze yew lodes of thyme.<br />
It helps me right all stiles of righting,<br />
And aides me when eye rime.<br />
<br />
Each frays come posed up on my screen<br />
Eye trussed too bee a joule.<br />
The chequer pours o'er every word<br />
Two cheque sum spelling rule.
</p>
</div></div><div class="thumbcaption">The original version of this poem was written by Jerrold H. Zar in 1992.  An unsophisticated spell checker will find little or no fault with this poem because it checks words in isolation.  A more sophisticated spell checker will make use of a <a href="/wiki/Language_model" title="Language model">language model</a> to consider the context in which a word occurs.</div></div></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spell_checker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A basic spell checker carries out the following processes:
</p>
<ul><li>It scans the text and extracts the words contained in it.</li>
<li>It then compares each word with a known list of correctly spelled words (i.e. a dictionary). This might contain just a list of words, or it might also contain additional information, such as hyphenation points or lexical and grammatical attributes.</li>
<li>An additional step is a language-dependent algorithm for handling <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>. Even for a lightly inflected language like <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, the spell-checker will need to consider different forms of the same word, such as plurals, verbal forms, <a href="/wiki/Contraction_(grammar)" title="Contraction (grammar)">contractions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Possessive_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Possessive (linguistics)">possessives</a>. For many other languages, such as those featuring agglutination and more complex declension and conjugation, this part of the process is more complicated.</li></ul>
<p>It is unclear whether morphological analysis&#8212;allowing for many different forms of a word depending on its grammatical role&#8212;provides a significant benefit for English, though its benefits for highly <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">synthetic languages</a> such as German, Hungarian or Turkish are clear.
</p><p>As an adjunct to these components, the program's <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a> will allow users to approve or reject replacements and modify the program's operation.
</p><p>An alternative type of spell checker uses solely statistical information, such as <a href="/wiki/N-gram" title="N-gram">n-grams</a>, to recognize errors instead of correctly-spelled words. This approach usually requires a lot of effort to obtain sufficient statistical information. Key advantages include needing less runtime storage and the ability to correct errors in words that are not included in a dictionary.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In some cases spell checkers use a fixed list of misspellings and suggestions for those misspellings; this less flexible approach is often used in paper-based correction methods, such as the <i>see also</i> entries of encyclopedias.
</p><p>Clustering algorithms have also been used for spell checking<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> combined with phonetic information.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spell_checker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1961, <a href="/wiki/Les_Earnest" title="Les Earnest">Les Earnest</a>, who headed the research on this budding technology, saw it necessary to include the first spell checker that accessed a list of 10,000 acceptable words.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Ralph Gorin, a graduate student under Earnest at the time, created the first true spelling checker program written as an applications program (rather than research) for general English text: Spell for the DEC PDP-10 at Stanford University's Artificial Intelligence Laboratory, in February 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Gorin wrote SPELL in assembly language, for faster action; he made the first spelling corrector by searching the word list for plausible correct spellings that differ by a single letter or adjacent letter transpositions and presenting them to the user.  Gorin made SPELL publicly accessible, as was done with most SAIL (Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory) programs, and it soon spread around the world via the new ARPAnet, about ten years before personal computers came into general use.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>   Spell, its algorithms and data structures inspired the Unix ispell program.
</p><p>The first spell checkers were widely available on mainframe computers in the late 1970s.  A group of six linguists from <a href="/wiki/Georgetown_University" title="Georgetown University">Georgetown University</a> developed the first spell-check system for the IBM corporation.<sup id="cite_ref-cled.georgetown.edu_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cled.georgetown.edu-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first spell checkers for personal computers appeared for <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a> and <a href="/wiki/TRS-80" title="TRS-80">TRS-80</a> computers in 1980, followed by packages for the <a href="/wiki/IBM_PC" class="mw-redirect" title="IBM PC">IBM PC</a> after it was introduced in 1981. Developers such as Maria Mariani,<sup id="cite_ref-cled.georgetown.edu_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cled.georgetown.edu-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-pc198211_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pc198211-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Soft-Art, Microlytics, Proximity, Circle Noetics, and Reference Software<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> rushed <a href="/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer" title="Original equipment manufacturer">OEM</a> packages or end-user products into the rapidly expanding software market, primarily for the PC but also for <a href="/wiki/Apple_Macintosh" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Macintosh">Apple Macintosh</a>, <a href="/wiki/VAX" title="VAX">VAX</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a>. On the PCs, these spell checkers were standalone programs, many of which could be run in <a href="/wiki/Terminate_and_Stay_Resident" class="mw-redirect" title="Terminate and Stay Resident">TSR</a> mode from within word-processing packages on PCs with sufficient memory.
</p><p>However, the market for standalone packages was short-lived, as by the mid-1980s developers of popular word-processing packages like <a href="/wiki/WordStar" title="WordStar">WordStar</a> and <a href="/wiki/WordPerfect" title="WordPerfect">WordPerfect</a> had incorporated spell checkers in their packages, mostly licensed from the above companies, who quickly expanded support from just <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and eventually even <a href="/wiki/Asian_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian language">Asian languages</a>. However, this required increasing sophistication in the morphology routines of the software, particularly with regard to heavily-<a href="/wiki/Agglutinative" class="mw-redirect" title="Agglutinative">agglutinative</a> languages like <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>. Although the size of the word-processing market in a country like <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> might not have justified the investment of implementing a spell checker, companies like WordPerfect nonetheless strove to localize their software for as many national markets as possible as part of their global <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a> strategy.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Firefox" title="Firefox">Firefox</a> 2.0, a <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a>, has spell check support for user-written content, such as when editing Wikitext, writing on many <a href="/wiki/Webmail" title="Webmail">webmail</a> sites, <a href="/wiki/Blogs" class="mw-redirect" title="Blogs">blogs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_networking" class="mw-redirect" title="Social networking">social networking</a> websites. The web browsers <a href="/wiki/Google_Chrome" title="Google Chrome">Google Chrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konqueror" title="Konqueror">Konqueror</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Opera_(web_browser)" title="Opera (web browser)">Opera</a>, the email client <a href="/wiki/Kmail" class="mw-redirect" title="Kmail">Kmail</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a> <a href="/wiki/Client_(computing)" title="Client (computing)">client</a> <a href="/wiki/Pidgin_(software)" title="Pidgin (software)">Pidgin</a> also offer spell checking support, transparently using previously <a href="/wiki/GNU_Aspell" title="GNU Aspell">GNU Aspell</a> and currently <a href="/wiki/Hunspell" title="Hunspell">Hunspell</a> as their engine. <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a> now has spell check system-wide, extending the service to virtually all bundled and third party applications.
</p><p>Some spell checkers have separate support for medical dictionaries to help prevent medical errors.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functionality">Functionality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spell_checker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Functionality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first spell checkers were "verifiers" instead of "correctors." They offered no suggestions for incorrectly spelled words. This was helpful for <a href="/wiki/Typos" class="mw-redirect" title="Typos">typos</a> but it was not so helpful for logical or phonetic errors. The challenge the developers faced was the difficulty in offering useful suggestions for misspelled words. This requires reducing words to a skeletal form and applying pattern-matching algorithms.
</p><p>It might seem logical that where spell-checking dictionaries are concerned, "the bigger, the better," so that correct words are not marked as incorrect. In practice, however, an optimal size for English appears to be around 90,000 entries. If there are more than this, incorrectly spelled words may be skipped because they are mistaken for others. For example, a linguist might determine on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Corpus_linguistics" title="Corpus linguistics">corpus linguistics</a> that the word <i><a href="/wiki/Baht" class="mw-redirect" title="Baht">baht</a></i> is more frequently a misspelling of <i>bath</i> or <i>bat</i> than a reference to the Thai currency. Hence, it would typically be more useful if a few people who write about Thai currency were slightly inconvenienced than if the spelling errors of the many more people who discuss baths were overlooked.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spell_check.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Spell_check.PNG/220px-Spell_check.PNG" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Spell_check.PNG/330px-Spell_check.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Spell_check.PNG/440px-Spell_check.PNG 2x" data-file-width="452" data-file-height="390" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spell_check.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A screenshot of <a href="/wiki/Enchant_(software)" title="Enchant (software)">Enchant</a>, the <a href="/wiki/AbiWord" title="AbiWord">AbiWord</a> spell checker.</div></div></div>
<p>The first MS-DOS spell checkers were mostly used in proofing mode from within word processing packages. After preparing a document, a user scanned the text looking for misspellings. Later, however, batch processing was offered in such packages as <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle</a>'s short-lived CoAuthor and allowed a user to view the results after a document was processed and correct only the words that were known to be wrong. When memory and processing power became abundant, spell checking was performed in the background in an interactive way, such as has been the case with the Sector Software produced Spellbound program released in 1987 and <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">Microsoft Word</a> since Word 95.
</p><p>In recent years, spell checkers have become increasingly sophisticated; some are now capable of recognizing simple <a href="/wiki/Grammatical" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical">grammatical</a> errors. However, even at their best, they rarely catch all the errors in a text (such as <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophone</a> errors) and will flag <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologisms</a> and foreign words as misspellings. Nonetheless, spell checkers can be considered as a type of <a href="/wiki/Foreign_language_writing_aid" title="Foreign language writing aid">foreign language writing aid</a> that non-native language learners can rely on to detect and correct their misspellings in the target language.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spell-checking_non-English_languages">Spell-checking non-English languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spell_checker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spell-checking non-English languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>English is unusual in that most words used in formal writing have a single spelling that can be found in a typical dictionary, with the exception of some jargon and modified words. In many languages, words are often <a href="/wiki/Concatenation" title="Concatenation">concatenated</a> into new combinations of words. In German, compound nouns are frequently coined from other existing nouns. Some scripts do not clearly separate one word from another, requiring word-splitting algorithms. Each of these presents unique challenges to non-English language spell checkers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Context-sensitive_spell_checkers">Context-sensitive spell checkers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spell_checker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Context-sensitive spell checkers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ol><li>Recently, research has focused on developing algorithms that are capable of recognizing a misspelled word, even if the word itself is in the vocabulary, based on the context of the surrounding words. Not only does this allow words such as those in the poem above to be caught, but it mitigates the detrimental effect of enlarging dictionaries, allowing more words to be recognized. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Baht" class="mw-redirect" title="Baht">baht</a></i> in the same paragraph as <i>Thai</i> or <i>Thailand</i> would not be recognized as a misspelling of <i>bath</i>. The most common example of errors caught by such a system are <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophone</a> errors, such as the bold words in the following sentence:</li></ol>
<dl><dd><b>Their</b> coming <b>too</b> <b>sea</b> if <b>its</b> <b>reel</b>.</dd></dl>
<p>The most successful algorithm to date is Andrew Golding and Dan Roth's "<a href="/wiki/Winnow_(algorithm)" title="Winnow (algorithm)">Winnow</a>-based  spelling correction algorithm",<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> published in 1999, which is able to recognize about 96% of context-sensitive spelling errors, in addition to ordinary non-word spelling errors. A <a href="/wiki/Context_sensitive_user_interface" class="mw-redirect" title="Context sensitive user interface">context-sensitive</a> spell checker appears in <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Office_2007" title="Microsoft Office 2007">Microsoft Office 2007</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>, <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Google_Wave" class="mw-redirect" title="Google Wave">Google Wave</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and in Ghotit Dyslexia Software<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> context spell checker tuned for people with dyslexia.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spell_checker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2010)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> of technology and computers have attempted to link spell checkers to a trend of skill losses in writing, reading, and speaking. They claim that computers have made people lazy, often not proofreading written work except for a simple pass through a spell checker. Supporters<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2010)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> claim that these changes may actually be beneficial to society, by making writing and learning new languages more accessible to ordinary people. They claim that the skills lost by the use of automated spell checkers are replaced by better skills, such as faster and more efficient research skills.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Other supporters of technology point out that these skills are not being lost by people who use written language frequently in their work, such as authors and journalists.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An example of the folly of relying completely on spell checkers is shown in the Spell-checker Poem<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> above. It was originally composed by Dr Jerrold H. Zar<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> in 1991, assisted by Mark Eckman,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> with an original length of 225 words, and containing 123 incorrectly used words. According to most unsophisticated spell checkers, the poem is valid, but most people can tell at a simple glance that most words are used incorrectly. As a result, spell checkers are sometimes derided as <b>spilling chuckers</b> or similar, slightly misspelled names.
</p><p>Not all critics are opponents of technological progress, however. An article based on research by Galletta et al.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> reports that higher verbal skills are needed for highest performance when using a spell checker.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The theory suggested that only writers with higher verbal skills could recognize and ignore false positives or incorrect suggestions. However, it was found that those with the higher skills lost their unaided performance advantage in multiple categories of errors, performing as poorly as the low verbals with the spell-checkers turned on. The conclusion points to some evidence of a loss of skill.
</p>
