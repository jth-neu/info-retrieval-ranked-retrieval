<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG/220px-HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG/330px-HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG/440px-HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2736" data-file-height="3648" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HBF_from_Conic_Hill.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View along the Highland Boundary Fault from <a href="/wiki/Conic_Hill" title="Conic Hill">Conic Hill</a> – the topographic ridge is mainly due to the presence of <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> age conglomerates on the southwestern side of the fault and can be traced across <a href="/wiki/Loch_Lomond" title="Loch Lomond">Loch Lomond</a> on the islands of <a href="/wiki/Inchcailloch" title="Inchcailloch">Inchcailloch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Torrinch" title="Torrinch">Torrinch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creinch" title="Creinch">Creinch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inchmurrin" title="Inchmurrin">Inchmurrin</a>, to Ben Bowie on the western shore</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Highland Boundary Fault</b> is a major <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">fault zone</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> that traverses Scotland from <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Arran" title="Isle of Arran">Arran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helensburgh" title="Helensburgh">Helensburgh</a> on the west coast to <a href="/wiki/Stonehaven" title="Stonehaven">Stonehaven</a> in the east. It separates two distinctly different physiographic and geological terrains: the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Highlands</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Lowlands" title="Scottish Lowlands">Lowlands</a>, and in most places it is recognisable as a change in topography. Where rivers cross the fault, they often pass through gorges, and the associated waterfalls can be a barrier to <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> migration.<sup id="cite_ref-ast_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ast-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fault is believed to have formed in conjunction with the Strathmore <a href="/wiki/Syncline" title="Syncline">syncline</a> to the south-east during the <a href="/wiki/Acadian_orogeny" title="Acadian orogeny">Acadian orogeny</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Transpression" title="Transpression">transpressive</a> regime that caused the uplift of the Grampian block and a small <a href="/wiki/Sinistral_fault#Strike-slip_faults" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinistral fault">sinistral movement</a> on the Highland Boundary Fault.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ff7728_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ff7728-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png/220px-BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png/330px-BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png/440px-BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png 2x" data-file-width="3039" data-file-height="1880" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BarrowHighlandBoundaryFault.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map published in 1912 by <a href="/wiki/George_Barrow_(geologist)" title="George Barrow (geologist)">George Barrow</a><sup id="cite_ref-barrow_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barrow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> showing what became known as the Highland Boundary Fault</div></div></div>
<p>One of the earliest and most prominent references to the Highland Boundary Fault was by <a href="/wiki/George_Barrow_(geologist)" title="George Barrow (geologist)">George Barrow</a> in 1912, ʻOn the Geology of Lower Dee-side and the Southern Highland Borderʼ, which highlights the nature of the rocks accompanying the Highland Border and describes the outlining mineral zones associated with metamorphism.<sup id="cite_ref-barrow_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-barrow-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In the same publication, Barrow also outlines the ʻHighland Faultʼ and the areas where he believes there are planes of overthrust. Barrowʼs description of the structural nature of the rocks along the Highland Border suggests that rocks along both ends of the fault plane are indistinguishable from one another, with no <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">brecciation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent_of_fault">Extent of fault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extent of fault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Aligned southwest to northeast from <a href="/wiki/Lochranza" title="Lochranza">Lochranza</a> on Arran, the Highland Boundary Fault bisects <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Bute" title="Isle of Bute">Bute</a> and crosses the southeastern parts of the <a href="/wiki/Cowal" title="Cowal">Cowal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosneath" title="Rosneath">Rosneath</a> Peninsulas, as it passes up the <a href="/wiki/Firth_of_Clyde" title="Firth of Clyde">Firth of Clyde</a>. It comes ashore near Helensburgh, then continues through <a href="/wiki/Loch_Lomond" title="Loch Lomond">Loch Lomond</a>. The loch islands of <a href="/wiki/Inchmurrin" title="Inchmurrin">Inchmurrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creinch" title="Creinch">Creinch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Torrinch" title="Torrinch">Torrinch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Inchcailloch" title="Inchcailloch">Inchcailloch</a> all form part of the Highland Boundary Fault.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
From Loch Lomond the Highland Boundary Fault continues to <a href="/wiki/Aberfoyle,_Stirling" title="Aberfoyle, Stirling">Aberfoyle</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Callander" title="Callander">Callander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Comrie" title="Comrie">Comrie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crieff" title="Crieff">Crieff</a>. It then forms the northern boundary of Strathmore and reaches the North Sea immediately north of Stonehaven near the ruined <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_St._Mary_and_St._Nathalan" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapel of St. Mary and St. Nathalan">Chapel of St. Mary and St. Nathalan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ff7728_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ff7728-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stonehaven_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stonehaven-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
Aeromagnetic maps of Great Britain and Northern Ireland show that the Highland Boundary Fault can be traced from <a href="/wiki/Ireland_(island)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ireland (island)">Ireland</a> to the region of <a href="/wiki/Greenock" title="Greenock">Greenock</a>. In these areas, the Highland Boundary Fault is seen to be dividing a northerly low area from a southerly high area.<sup id="cite_ref-max_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-max-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
In 1970, Hall and Dagley identified the Highland Boundary Fault as coincident with a regional magnetic feature dividing a string of negative anomalies in the north from positive ones in the south.<sup id="cite_ref-max_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-max-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> On discovering this, Hall and Dagley concluded that the observed trend, which followed the length of the Dalradian trough transition from positive to negative anomalies. This linear feature of magnetic anomalies has since been referred to as the Fair Head–Clew Bay line.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tectonic_controversy">Tectonic controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tectonic controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At present, it is believed that the Highland Boundary Fault was active during two main orogenic events associated with the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">Caledonian orogeny</a>&#160;: the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny#Grampian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">Grampian orogeny</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Ordovician" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Ordovician">Early Ordovician</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Acadian_orogeny" title="Acadian orogeny">Acadian orogeny</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Devonian" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Devonian">Middle Devonian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The fault allowed the <a href="/wiki/Central_Lowlands" title="Central Lowlands">Midland Valley</a> to descend as a major rift by up to 4,000&#160;m (13,000&#160;ft) and there was subsequent vertical movement. This earlier vertical movement was later replaced by a horizontal shear. A complementary fault, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Uplands_Fault" title="Southern Uplands Fault">Southern Uplands Fault</a>, forms the southern boundary for the Central Lowlands.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ff7728_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ff7728-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
The age of the Highland Boundary Fault has been inferred to be between Ordovician to middle Devonian and through several generations it has been interpreted as a <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">graben</a>-bounding normal fault, a major sinistral <a href="/wiki/Strike-slip_fault" class="mw-redirect" title="Strike-slip fault">strike-slip fault</a>, a northwest-dipping <a href="/wiki/Reverse_fault" class="mw-redirect" title="Reverse fault">reverse fault</a> or <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">terrane</a> boundary.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The reason the precise nature of the fault is still unknown is because there is little evidence of a continuous fault plane on the surface. More recently, seismic activities marking the fault line have been analysed to show that the 2003 Aberfoyle earthquake had a <a href="/wiki/Hypocentre" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypocentre">hypocentre</a> at 4&#160;km (2.5 miles) depth and was caused by an oblique sinistral strike-slip fault with <a href="/wiki/Normal_fault" class="mw-redirect" title="Normal fault">normal movement</a>. The fault plane was estimated to be dipping at 65°&#160;NW.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_lithology"><span id="Highland_Border_Complex"></span>Associated lithology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Associated lithology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To the north and west of the Highland Boundary Fault lie hard Precambrian and Cambrian metamorphic rocks: marine deposits metamorphosed to <a href="/wiki/Schist" title="Schist">schists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phyllite" title="Phyllite">phyllites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slate" title="Slate">slates</a>, namely the Dalradian Supergroup and the Highland Border <a href="/wiki/Ophiolite" title="Ophiolite">Ophiolite</a> suite.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> To the south and east are <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a> conglomerates and sandstones: softer, sedimentary rocks of the Devonian and Carboniferous periods.<sup id="cite_ref-ff7728_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ff7728-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Between these areas lie the quite different rocks of the <b>Highland Border Complex</b> (at one time called the Highland Boundary Complex<sup id="cite_ref-bgshbx_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bgshbx-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>), a weakly metamorphosed sedimentary sequence of sandstones, lavas, limestones, mudstones and conglomerates. These make up a zone which is found discontinuously along the line of the fault and which is up to       1.2 kilometres (0.75&#160;mi) in width.<sup id="cite_ref-complex_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-complex-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dalradian_Supergroup">Dalradian Supergroup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dalradian Supergroup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Dalradian Supergroup consists of <a href="/wiki/Metasediment" class="mw-redirect" title="Metasediment">metasedimentary</a> rocks which underwent polyphase deformation and metamorphism during the Precambrian and early Paleozoic.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ff7728_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ff7728-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They were the earliest <a href="/wiki/Grampian_Group" title="Grampian Group">Grampian Group</a> rocks and were deformed and metamorphosed around 750&#160;Ma. The deposition of younger Dalradian sediments continued until 590&#160;Ma, where the sediments underwent transformation to the <a href="/wiki/Greenschist" title="Greenschist">greenschist</a> facies during the Proterozoic and Ordovician.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Highland_Border_Ophiolitic_Rocks">Highland Border Ophiolitic Rocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Highland Border Ophiolitic Rocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Modeling of gravity and magnetic data along the fault has confirmed the presence of an extensive <a href="/wiki/Ophiolite" title="Ophiolite">ophiolite</a> suite. The Dalradian metasedimentary rocks are overlain by an <a href="/wiki/Obduction" title="Obduction">obducted</a> <a href="/wiki/Ophiolite" title="Ophiolite">ophiolite</a> that is continuous for at least several kilometers on either side of the Highland Boundary Fault. The models generated from magnetic data suggest that the ophiolite is only slightly displaced vertically by the fault.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Red_Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Old Red Sandstone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Red_Sandstone" title="Old Red Sandstone">Old Red Sandstone</a> is a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnafacies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Magnafacies (page does not exist)">magnafacies</a> of red beds and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lacustrine_deposit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lacustrine deposit (page does not exist)">lacustrine deposits</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Late_Silurian" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Silurian">Late Silurian</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a>. The NE segment of the Highland Boundary Fault is marked by an abrupt change in the dip of the Old Red Sandstone from around 20° to near-vertical and subsequently exposes the Old Red Sandstone basement.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Displacement_along_fault">Displacement along fault</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Highland_Boundary_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Displacement along fault">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is currently believed that there were two main displacement events along the Highland Boundary Fault: the Acadian, and the post-Acadian.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dempster_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dempster-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ramsay_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Evidence for the Acadian displacement event is based on the geochemical study of detrital garnets in the Lower Old Red Sandstone on the Northern limb of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Strathmore_Basin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Strathmore Basin (page does not exist)">Strathmore Basin</a>. These garnets were linked to those in isolated Dalradian sediments in the northwest, providing evidence for post-Early Devonian (Acadian) movement to be only few tens of kilometers.<sup id="cite_ref-dempster_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dempster-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ramsay_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ramsay-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lintrathen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lintrathen (page does not exist)">Lintrathen</a> <a href="/wiki/Ignimbrite" title="Ignimbrite">ignimbrite</a>, which is present at the base of the Lower Devonian sequence was traced along the fault and it was found that the displacement was both short and lateral.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dempster_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dempster-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The post-Acadian movements are highlighted in the stratigraphy of the region. The Lower Old Red Sandstone is unconformably overlain by Upper Old Red Sandstone, where the Upper Old Red Sandstone is tilted close to the Highland Boundary Fault.<sup id="cite_ref-sg_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sg-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
