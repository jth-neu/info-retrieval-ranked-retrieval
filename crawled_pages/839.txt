<p>The <b>Supreme Governor of the Church of England</b> is a title held by the <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">British monarch</a> that signifies titular leadership over the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-monarchtoday_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-monarchtoday-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Although the monarch's authority over the Church of England is largely ceremonial, the position is still very relevant to the church and is mostly observed in a symbolic capacity. The Supreme Governor formally appoints high-ranking members of the church on the advice of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister of the United Kingdom</a>, who is in turn advised by church leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-monarchtoday_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-monarchtoday-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Governor_of_the_Church_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By 1536, <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a> had broken with <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Rome</a>, seized the Church's assets in England and declared the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Established_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Established church">established church</a> with himself as its head. The <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Supremacy_1534" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Supremacy 1534">Act of Supremacy 1534</a> confirmed the King's status as having supremacy over the church and required the <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">peers</a> to swear an oath recognising Henry's supremacy.<sup id="cite_ref-Catholic_Encyclopedia:_Henry_Viii_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic_Encyclopedia:_Henry_Viii-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Henry's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Mary_I_of_England" title="Mary I of England">Mary I</a>, attempted to restore the English Church's allegiance to the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> and repealed the Act of Supremacy in 1555.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> ascended to the throne in 1558 and the next year <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a> passed the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Supremacy_1558" title="Act of Supremacy 1558">Act of Supremacy 1558</a> that restored the original act.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> To placate critics, the <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_Supremacy" title="Oath of Supremacy">Oath of Supremacy</a> which peers were required to swear, gave the monarch's title as Supreme Governor rather than <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Head_of_the_Church_of_England" title="Supreme Head of the Church of England">Supreme Head</a> of the church. This wording avoided the charge that the monarchy was claiming divinity or usurping <a href="/wiki/Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Christ">Christ</a>, whom the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> explicitly identifies as <a href="/wiki/Head_of_the_Church" title="Head of the Church">Head of the Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Defender of the Faith" (<i><a href="/wiki/Fidei_Defensor" class="mw-redirect" title="Fidei Defensor">Fidei Defensor</a></i>) has been part of the English (and since the Union of Scotland and England, British) monarch's title since Henry VIII was granted it by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">Pope Leo X</a> in 1521 in recognition of Henry's role in opposing the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Catholic_Encyclopedia:_Henry_Viii_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catholic_Encyclopedia:_Henry_Viii-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The pope withdrew the title, but it was later reconferred by Parliament in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VI">Edward VI</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thirty-Nine_Articles"><i>Thirty-Nine Articles</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Governor_of_the_Church_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thirty-Nine Articles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This royal role is acknowledged in the <i>Preface to the <a href="/wiki/Thirty-Nine_Articles" class="mw-redirect" title="Thirty-Nine Articles">Thirty-Nine Articles</a></i> of 1562. It states that:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Being by God's Ordinance, according to Our just Title, Defender of the Faith and Supreme Governor of the Church, within these Our Dominions, We hold it most agreeable to this Our Kingly Office, and Our own religious zeal, to conserve and maintain the Church committed to Our Charge, in Unity of true Religion, and in the Bond of Peace ... We have therefore, upon mature Deliberation, and with the Advice of so many of Our Bishops as might conveniently be called together, thought fit to make this Declaration following ... That We are Supreme Governor of the Church of England ... "
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Article 37 makes this claim to <a href="/wiki/Royal_supremacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal supremacy">royal supremacy</a> more explicit:
</p>
<blockquote><p>
"The Queen's Majesty hath the chief power in this Realm of England, and other her Dominions, unto whom the chief Government of all Estates of this Realm, whether they be Ecclesiastical or Civil, in all causes doth appertain, and is not, nor ought to be, subject to any foreign Jurisdiction. ...[W]e give not to our Princes the ministering either of God's Word, or of the Sacraments...but only that prerogative, which we see to have been given always to all godly Princes in holy Scriptures by God himself; that is, that they should rule all estates and degrees committed to their charge by God, whether they be Ecclesiastical or Temporal, and restrain with the civil sword the stubborn and evildoers. The Bishop of Rome hath no jurisdiction in this Realm of England."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_of_Scotland">Church of Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Governor_of_the_Church_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Church of Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The British monarch vows to uphold the constitution of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian">Presbyterian</a> <a href="/wiki/National_church" title="National church">national church</a>), but does not hold a leadership position in it. Nevertheless, the monarch appoints the <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Commissioner_to_the_General_Assembly_of_the_Church_of_Scotland" title="Lord High Commissioner to the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland">Lord High Commissioner to the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland</a> as his or her personal representative, with a ceremonial role. The Queen on occasion has filled the role personally, as when she opened the General Assembly in 1977 and 2002 (her <a href="/wiki/Silver_Jubilee_of_Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Silver Jubilee of Queen Elizabeth II">Silver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_Jubilee_of_Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Jubilee of Queen Elizabeth II">Golden Jubilee</a> years).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
