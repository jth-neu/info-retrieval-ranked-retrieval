<p><b>Not proven</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Verdict" title="Verdict">verdict</a> available to a <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Scotland" title="Courts of Scotland">court</a> in Scotland. Under <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scots law</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Criminal_procedure" title="Criminal procedure">criminal trial</a> may end in one of three verdicts: one of <a href="/wiki/Conviction" title="Conviction">conviction</a> ("guilty") and two of <a href="/wiki/Acquittal" title="Acquittal">acquittal</a> ("not proven" and "not guilty").<sup id="cite_ref-Duff_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duff-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-In3V_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In3V-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, the two verdicts available to Scots <a href="/wiki/Jury" title="Jury">juries</a> were that the case had been "proven" or "not proven". However, in a 1728 murder trial, the jury asserted "its ancient right" to declare a defendant "not guilty".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Over time, the "not guilty" verdict gained wide acceptance and use amongst Scots juries, a trend that was encouraged by defence lawyers. It eventually displaced "not proven" as the primary verdict of acquittal.
</p><p>The result is the modern perception that the "not proven" verdict is an <a href="/wiki/Acquittal" title="Acquittal">acquittal</a> used when the <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jury" title="Jury">jury</a> does not have enough <a href="/wiki/Evidence_(law)" title="Evidence (law)">evidence</a> to <a href="/wiki/Conviction" title="Conviction">convict</a> but is not sufficiently convinced of the accused person's innocence to bring in a "not guilty" verdict. Essentially, the judge or jury is unconvinced that the suspect is innocent, but guilt has not been proven "beyond <a href="/wiki/Reasonable_doubt" title="Reasonable doubt">reasonable doubt</a>". There is no difference between the legal effects of a "not guilty" and "not proven" verdict, which both acquit the defendant.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Out of the country, the "not proven" verdict may be referred to as the <b>Scottish verdict</b>, and in Scotland itself it may be referred to colloquially as the <b>bastard verdict</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> which was a term coined by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Scott" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Walter Scott">Sir Walter Scott</a>, who was sheriff in the court of <a href="/wiki/Selkirk,_Scottish_Borders" title="Selkirk, Scottish Borders">Selkirk</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Not_proven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three verdict system was established in Scots law by 1728 (since then juries have been able to pass a <i><a href="/wiki/Acquittal" title="Acquittal">not guilty</a></i> verdict).  For some time before this, there were just two verdicts: <i>Proven</i> and <i>Not proven</i>; scholars dispute the origins of this system.
</p><p>On one account, advanced two hundred years ago by the historians <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">Hume</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Arnot" title="Hugo Arnot">Arnot</a>, the older distinctively Scottish two verdict system was rooted in religious oppression. The Crown persecuted the <a href="/wiki/Covenanters" class="mw-redirect" title="Covenanters">Covenanters</a> but popular support made it impossible to convict them in a jury trial. To pare the power of the jury, the Scottish judges began restricting the jury's role: no longer would the jury announce whether the accused was "guilty" or "not guilty"; instead it would decide whether specific factual allegations were "proven" or "not proven"; and the judge would then decide whether to convict. Some historians, however, such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ian_Douglas_Willock&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ian Douglas Willock (page does not exist)">Ian Douglas Willock</a>, have rejected the traditional account.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Not_guilty">Not guilty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Not_proven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Not guilty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a notable trial in 1728, a defence lawyer (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Dundas,_of_Arniston,_the_elder" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Dundas, of Arniston, the elder">Robert Dundas</a>) persuaded a jury to reassert its ancient right of acquitting, of finding an accused "not guilty", in spite of the facts being proven. The case involved the trial of <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_of_Finhaven" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnegie of Finhaven">Carnegie of Finhaven</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyon,_6th_Earl_of_Strathmore_and_Kinghorne" title="Charles Lyon, 6th Earl of Strathmore and Kinghorne">Earl of Strathmore</a>. The law (as it stood) required the jury merely to look at the facts and pass a verdict of "proven" or "not proven" depending on whether they believed the evidence proved that the accused had killed the Earl. Carnegie had undoubtedly killed the Earl, but had also clearly not intended to do so (<a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">manslaughter</a>). If the jury brought in a "proven" verdict they would in effect constrain the judge to find Carnegie guilty of murder, for which the punishment was <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanging</a>. To avert this outcome, the jury decided to assert what it believed to be their "ancient right" to judge the whole case and not just the facts, and brought in the verdict of "not guilty".
</p><p>The (re)introduction of the "not guilty" verdict was part of a wider movement during the 17th and 18th century which saw a gradual increase in the power of juries, such as the trial of <a href="/wiki/William_Penn" title="William Penn">William Penn</a> in 1670, in which an English jury first gained the right to pass a verdict contrary to the law (known as <a href="/wiki/Jury_nullification" title="Jury nullification">jury nullification</a>), and the trial of <a href="/wiki/John_Peter_Zenger" title="John Peter Zenger">John Peter Zenger</a> in New York in 1735 in which jury nullification is credited with establishing <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press_in_the_United_States" title="Freedom of the press in the United States">freedom of the press</a> as firm right in what would become the United States.
</p><p>Although jurors continued to use both "not guilty" and "not proven", jurors tended to favour the "not guilty" verdict over the "not proven" and the interpretation changed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Not_proven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Scotland, a criminal case may be decided either <i>"in solemn procedure"</i> by a jury (instructed by the judge), or <i>"in summary procedure"</i> by the judge alone (with no jury appointed). There are various rules for when the one or the other procedure may or must be employed; in general, juries are employed for the more severe accusations, while petty crimes and offences are treated summarily. A criminal case jury consists of 15 jurors, who make their decision by a simple majority vote: eight votes are necessary and sufficient for the verdict <i>"guilty"</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Duff_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duff-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> which has replaced the older verdict <i>"proven"</i>.
</p><p>Approximately one third of all acquittal verdicts by Scottish juries use the formulation <i>Not proven</i>; the others use <i>Not guilty</i>. The verdict <i>"Not proven"</i> also is available for judges in the summary procedure, and is employed in about a fifth of the "summary" acquittals.<sup id="cite_ref-Duff_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duff-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The proportion of <i>Not proven</i> acquittals in general is higher in the more severe cases; but so then are the proportion of acquittals <i>versus</i> convictions. This might have many different reasons, e.g., that in average it might be factually  more complicated to establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt in the more severe cases.<sup id="cite_ref-In3V_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In3V-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both in the "solemn" and the "summary" acquittals, <i>not proven</i> is interpreted as indicating that the jury or judge, respectively, is not convinced of the innocence of the accused; in fact, they may be morally convinced that the accused is guilty, but do not find the proofs sufficient for a conviction.  One reason for this is the rule that in such cases the evidence for the prosecution must be <a href="/wiki/Corroboration_in_Scots_law" title="Corroboration in Scots law">corroborated</a> in order to permit a conviction.  Thus, there might be a single plaintiff or witness for the prosecution, which the jury or judge believes is both truthful and trustworthy, but no other witness or circumstances against the accused. By Scottish law, the accused then should be acquitted, but often will be so by the verdict <i>"not proven"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duff_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duff-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Not_proven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years there have been repeated calls for reform, most arguing for a move to only two verdicts.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> However, there are several issues and no consensus. Some of the arguments against the third verdict are<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Many favour the "proven" verdict as it directs the jury to look at the evidence and to err on the side of the accused if there is any doubt.</li>
<li>Because the "not proven" verdict carries with it an implication of guilt but no formal conviction, the accused is often seen as morally guilty without the option of a retrial to clear their name.</li>
<li>Many Scottish jurors (through TV, etc.) are more familiar with the English/US verdicts of "guilty"/"not guilty" than "proven"/"not guilty"/"not proven".</li></ul>
<p>However, the perceived advantages in retaining the three verdict system have outweighed the disadvantages. Many people believe that if only <i>"guilty"</i> and <i>"not guilty"</i> were available, then some people who are currently acquitted by a "not proven" verdict would instead be convicted.<sup id="cite_ref-In3V_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In3V-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In cases where the public found the evidence of guilt overwhelming, but the jury returned the verdict "not proven", critics have argued that the jury probably would have given a guilty verdict if they did not have the third alternative.  Proponents of retaining the third option argue that people acquitted with a "not proven" were not found guilty beyond a reasonable doubt; but with the removal of the third option, they might have been found guilty anyway. This would mean that the presumption of innocence until sufficient proof of guilt is established would be weakened.
</p><p>Another argument for retaining the three verdict system was brought up by Scottish rape crisis centres, who lobbied for this among the members of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish parliament">Scottish parliament</a>. The number of <i>"not proven"</i> verdicts in sexual assault cases is proportionally large; about three out of ten of these cases end with an acquittal, and a third of these acquittals (a tenth of all verdicts in such cases) are judged <i>"not proven"</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-In3V_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In3V-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This is related to the fact that fairly often there is not sufficient corrobating evidence, and the victim's testimony alone does not constitute a legal ground for a conviction. Now, the rape crisis centres argued, if the <i>"not proven"</i> verdict is removed, a smaller number of assaulted women will dare to press for a trial. If either the first trial, or a successful appeal to the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court</a>, ends in a <i>"not guilty"</i> verdict, the woman may feel stigmatised in the eyes of the public, which often treats the trial as if there were two accused: the alleged attacker and the victim. On the other hand, a <i>"not proven"</i> verdict is not at all considered as damaging for "the credibility, honesty and reliability of women" (footnote 65 in <sup id="cite_ref-In3V_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In3V-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>), since it indicates that the jurors do not disbelieve them, even if the total evidence is not sufficient for a conviction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_other_jurisdictions">Use in other jurisdictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Not_proven&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use in other jurisdictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1989, in the Italian juridical system, there are <a href="/wiki/Italian_Code_of_Criminal_Procedure#Verdicts" title="Italian Code of Criminal Procedure">five modes of acquittal</a> available. Two of these correspond roughly to the Scottish <i>Not guilty</i> and <i>Not proven</i>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, the Scottish verdict has not been permanently adopted outside its home country, but it was sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial Canada">colonial Canada</a>, especially by some judges in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> . Its most famous use in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> came when <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Arlen_Specter" title="Arlen Specter">Arlen Specter</a> tried to vote "not proven" on the two articles of <a href="/wiki/Impeachment_of_Bill_Clinton" title="Impeachment of Bill Clinton">impeachment of Bill Clinton</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-arlen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arlen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> his votes were recorded as "not guilty"<sup id="cite_ref-In3V_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In3V-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and when, at the <a href="/wiki/O.J._Simpson_murder_case" class="mw-redirect" title="O.J. Simpson murder case">O.J. Simpson murder case</a>, various reformers, including Fred Goldman, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Goldman" title="Ron Goldman">Ron Goldman</a>'s father, pushed for a change to "not proven" because of what they felt was an incorrect <a href="/wiki/Presumption_of_innocence" title="Presumption of innocence">presumption of innocence</a> on the part of Simpson.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, a proposal was made in the <i><a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Law_Review" title="University of Chicago Law Review">University of Chicago Law Review</a></i> to introduce the not proven verdict into the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-In3V_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-In3V-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
