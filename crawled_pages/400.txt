<p><b>Rachel Whiteread</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">CBE</a> (born 20 April 1963) is an English artist who primarily produces sculptures, which typically take the form of casts. She was the first woman to win the annual <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> in 1993.
</p><p>Whiteread was one of the <a href="/wiki/Young_British_Artists" title="Young British Artists">Young British Artists</a> who exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Sensation_exhibition" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensation exhibition">Sensation</a></i> exhibition in 1997. Among her most renowned works are <i><a href="/wiki/House_(sculpture)" title="House (sculpture)">House</a></i>, a large concrete cast of the inside of an entire Victorian house; the <a href="/wiki/Judenplatz_Holocaust_Memorial" title="Judenplatz Holocaust Memorial">Judenplatz Holocaust Memorial</a> in Vienna, resembling  the shelves of a library with the pages turned outwards; and <i>Untitled Monument</i>, her <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a> sculpture for the empty <a href="/wiki/Fourth_plinth" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourth plinth">fourth plinth</a> in London's <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whiteread was born in 1963 in <a href="/wiki/Ilford" title="Ilford">Ilford</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and raised in the <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> countryside until age seven when the family moved to London.<sup id="cite_ref-Wroe2013_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wroe2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Her mother, Patricia Whiteread (née Lancaster), who was also an artist, died in 2003 at the age of 72.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Her father, Thomas Whiteread, was a geography teacher, <a href="/wiki/Polytechnic_(United_Kingdom)" title="Polytechnic (United Kingdom)">polytechnic</a> <a href="/wiki/Academic_administration" title="Academic administration">administrator</a> and lifelong supporter of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, who died when Whiteread was studying at art school in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> She is the third of three sisters – the older two being <a href="/wiki/Identical_twins" class="mw-redirect" title="Identical twins">identical twins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wroe2013_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wroe2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>She took a workshop on casting with the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Richard_Wilson_(sculptor)" title="Richard Wilson (sculptor)">Richard Wilson</a> and began to realize the possibilities in casting objects.<sup id="cite_ref-Wroe2013_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wroe2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She was briefly at the <a href="/wiki/Cyprus_College_of_Art" title="Cyprus College of Art">Cyprus College of Art</a>. From 1985 to 1987 she studied sculpture at <a href="/wiki/Slade_School_of_Art" class="mw-redirect" title="Slade School of Art">Slade School of Art</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_College,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="University College, London">University College, London</a>, graduating with an MA in 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-Wroe2013_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wroe2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>For a time she worked in <a href="/wiki/Highgate_Cemetery" title="Highgate Cemetery">Highgate Cemetery</a> fixing lids back onto time-damaged coffins. She began to exhibit in 1987, with her first solo exhibition coming in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-Zelevansky1994_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zelevansky1994-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> She lives and works in a former synagogue in east London with long-term partner and fellow sculptor Marcus Taylor. They have two sons.<sup id="cite_ref-Barber2005_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2005-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Barber2001_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2001-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of Whiteread's works are casts of ordinary domestic objects and, in numerous cases, their so-called <a href="/wiki/Negative_space" title="Negative space">negative space</a>. For example, she is known for making solid casts of the open space in and around pieces of furniture such as tables and chairs, architectural details and even entire rooms and buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> She says the casts carry "the residue of years and years of use."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Whiteread mainly focuses on the line and the form for her pieces.
</p><p>While still at the Slade, Whiteread cast domestic objects and created her first sculpture, <i>Closet</i>. She made a plaster cast of the interior of a wooden wardrobe and covered it with black felt. It was based on comforting childhood memories of hiding in a dark closet.<sup id="cite_ref-Wroe2013_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wroe2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> After she graduated she rented space for a studio using the <a href="/wiki/Enterprise_Allowance_Scheme" title="Enterprise Allowance Scheme">Enterprise Allowance Scheme</a>. She created <i>Shallow Breath</i> (1988), the cast of the underside of a bed, made not long after her father died.<sup id="cite_ref-Wroe2013_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wroe2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Both sculptures were exhibited in her first solo show in 1988 along with casts of other domestic pieces. The work all sold and allowed her to apply for grants to find funding for larger sculptures.<sup id="cite_ref-Wroe2013_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wroe2013-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ghost"><i>Ghost</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ghost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After her first solo exhibition, Whiteread decided to cast the space that her domestic objects could have inhabited. She applied for grants, describing the project as "mummifying the air in a room."<sup id="cite_ref-Tusa2004_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tusa2004-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> She completed <i>Ghost</i> in 1990. It was cast from a room in a house on Archway Road in north London, much like the house she grew up in.<sup id="cite_ref-Burn2005_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burn2005-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The road was being widened, and the house was torn down. She used plaster to cast the parlor walls and ceiling in sections and assembled them on a metal frame.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Ghost</i> was first shown at the nonprofit <a href="/wiki/Chisenhale_Gallery" title="Chisenhale Gallery">Chisenhale Gallery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> It was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Saatchi" title="Charles Saatchi">Charles Saatchi</a> and included with other works by Whiteread in his first "Young British Art" show in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-KentCork1999_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KentCork1999-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In May 2004 a fire in a <a href="/wiki/Momart" title="Momart">Momart</a> storage warehouse destroyed many works from the Saatchi collection, including, it is believed, some by Whiteread. However, <i>Ghost</i> had recently been moved from the warehouse to the new <a href="/wiki/Gagosian_Gallery" title="Gagosian Gallery">Gagosian Gallery</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The work was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Art" title="National Gallery of Art">National Gallery of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> in the autumn of 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="House_and_the_Turner_Prize"><i>House</i> and the Turner Prize</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: House and the Turner Prize">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 1993 Whiteread completed <i><a href="/wiki/House_(sculpture)" title="House (sculpture)">House</a></i>, the cast of a Victorian <a href="/wiki/Terraced_house" title="Terraced house">terrace house</a>. She had begun considering casting an entire house in 1991. She and <a href="/wiki/James_Lingwood" class="mw-redirect" title="James Lingwood">James Lingwood</a> of <a href="/wiki/Artangel" title="Artangel">Artangel</a> looked at houses to be torn down in North and East London in 1992, but without success in securing one.<sup id="cite_ref-Lingwood1995_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lingwood1995-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> During this period in 1992 and 1993, Whiteread had an artist residency in Berlin with a scholarship from the <a href="/wiki/German_Academic_Exchange_Service" title="German Academic Exchange Service">DAAD</a> <a href="/wiki/DAAD_Artists-in-Berlin_Program" title="DAAD Artists-in-Berlin Program">Artist's Programme</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> While in Berlin, she created <i>Untitled (Room)</i>, the cast of a generic, anonymous room that she built herself. She finished the interior of a room-sized box with wallpaper, windows, and door before casting.<sup id="cite_ref-Zelevansky1994_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zelevansky1994-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The sculpture is in the collection of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>House</i>, perhaps her best-known work, was a concrete cast of the inside of an entire Victorian terraced house completed in autumn 1993, exhibited at the location of the original house – 193 Grove Road – in East London (all the houses in the street had earlier been knocked down by the council). It drew mixed responses, winning her both the <a href="/wiki/Turner_Prize" title="Turner Prize">Turner Prize</a> for the best young British artist in 1993 and the <a href="/wiki/K_Foundation_art_award" title="K Foundation art award">K Foundation art award</a> for the worst British artist.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> She was the first woman to win a Turner Prize.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Tower_Hamlets" title="London Borough of Tower Hamlets">Tower Hamlets</a> London Borough Council demolished <i>House</i> on 11 January 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> a decision which caused some controversy itself.
</p>

This work can be seen as a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Nauman" title="Bruce Nauman">Bruce Nauman</a>'s concrete cast of the area under his chair of 1965.

They look like a regulated graveyard or a series of futuristic standing stones with a passing resemblance to television sets."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<dl><dd>— Andrew Lambirth, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator" title="The Spectator">The Spectator</a></i>, 12 October 1996.</dd></dl>
<blockquote>

</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_Tower"><i>Water Tower</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Water Tower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1998, Whiteread made <i>Water Tower</i> as part of a grant for New York City's <a href="/wiki/Public_Art_Fund" title="Public Art Fund">Public Art Fund</a>. The piece, which is 12' 2" and 9' in diameter, was a translucent resin cast of a <a href="/wiki/Water_tower" title="Water tower">water tower</a> installed on a rooftop in New York City's <a href="/wiki/SoHo" class="mw-redirect" title="SoHo">SoHo</a> district.<sup id="cite_ref-Neri1999_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neri1999-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It has been called "an extremely beautiful object, which changes colour with the sky, and also a very appropriate one, celebrating one of the most idiosyncratic and charming features of the New York skyline."<sup id="cite_ref-Barber2001_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2001-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The piece is now in the permanent collection of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> (MoMA).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Just as <i>Ghost</i> led on to the larger and better known <i>House</i>, so <i>Water Tower</i> led to the more public Trafalgar Square plinth work three years later.<sup id="cite_ref-Barber2001_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2001-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Holocaust_Monument_a.k.a._Nameless_Library_.282000.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Holocaust_Monument_a.k.a._Nameless_Library_(2000)"><i>Holocaust Monument</i> a.k.a. <i>Nameless Library</i> (2000)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Holocaust Monument a.k.a. Nameless Library (2000)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg/220px-Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg/330px-Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg/440px-Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rachel_whitereadwien_holocaust_mahnmal_wien_judenplatz.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Holocaust Monument</i> (2000) <a href="/wiki/Judenplatz" title="Judenplatz">Judenplatz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a></div></div></div>
<p>During the Holocaust, 65,000 Austrian Jews were executed, and in memory, <i>Monument to the Victims of Fascism</i> was a monument erected to commemorate these lost lives, however, this piece was seen as unsatisfying, so Simon Wiesenthal proposed the idea for a new memorial to the mayor of Vienna. With the condition that this memorial could not be figurative and needed to represent all 65,000 lives and the camps they were executed at, Rachel Whiteread was chosen out of ten artists to create this monument. Her monument <i>Nameless Library</i> was erected in Judenplatz Square in Vienna and appears to be an inside-out library. This structure was built from positively casted cement books which are placed with their spines facing inward. The inability to read these books allude to the lost lives of the 65,000 Austrian Jews whose stories are unable to be told leaving the viewer with a sense of loss and absence. These books have also been seen as referring to the Nazi book burnings. The sculpture also does not include corners or bookshelves which further symbolizes the lack of structure and support.    
</p><p><i>Nameless Library</i> also is constructed on the excavation grounds of Vienna's oldest synagogues which caused a lot of criticism towards the piece as many citizens felt that the grounds sufficed for the memorial itself. Some critics even accused her of stereotyping the Jewish people as "the people of the book" considering that Jewish memorials were traditionally written. This monument also questions the architectural concepts of interior and exterior as the building surrounding the square form walls, and the streets leading into it like doorways. In addition, the inverted rose ceiling works as a drainage point to the interior of the sculpture.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Untitled_Monument_.282001.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Untitled_Monument_(2001)"><i>Untitled Monument</i> (2001)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Untitled Monument (2001)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2001_Stuckist_Trafalgar_Square_demo_(1).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/2001_Stuckist_Trafalgar_Square_demo_%281%29.jpg/220px-2001_Stuckist_Trafalgar_Square_demo_%281%29.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/2001_Stuckist_Trafalgar_Square_demo_%281%29.jpg/330px-2001_Stuckist_Trafalgar_Square_demo_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8d/2001_Stuckist_Trafalgar_Square_demo_%281%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2001_Stuckist_Trafalgar_Square_demo_(1).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stuckism" title="Stuckism">Stuckist</a> demonstration against the unveiling of <i>Untitled Monument</i>, 4 June 2001.</div></div></div>
<p>With <i>Untitled Monument</i> (2001), (also variously known as <i>Plinth</i> or <i>Inverted Plinth</i>), Whiteread became the third artist to provide a sculpture for the empty <a href="/wiki/Fourth_plinth,_Trafalgar_Square" title="Fourth plinth, Trafalgar Square">Fourth plinth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Trafalgar_Square" title="Trafalgar Square">Trafalgar Square</a>. Her sculpture was an 11-ton resin cast of the plinth itself, made by Mike Smith Studio, London,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> which stood upside down, creating a sort of mirror-image of the plinth. It was said to be the most massive object ever made out of resin, taking eight attempts to produce due to the resin cracking.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The work was produced in two halves, and surface blisters of the cast were repaired by picking them off and filling the small craters with a syringe of resin.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Unusually for a public work, she raised funds for the piece herself by selling maquettes (small preparatory models); this was no small gesture with the mold alone costing over £100,000 and the total cost estimated at £225,000<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The critical response included:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"This dazzling anti-monument monument looks like a glass coffin, but its watery transparency relates to the large fountain that dominates the Trafalgar plaza. Following the aquatic theme, Whiteread's Monument evokes the scene of the 1805 naval battle for which the square is named."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<dl><dd>— David Ebony, <i>Artnet</i></dd></dl>
<blockquote>
<p>"It's a simple trick, but an effective one, and the associations it conjures – heaviness and lightness, earth and heaven, death and life – are thought-provoking and manifold [...] Whiteread's Monument, as light and gleaming as the plinth is dark and squat, is the only one of the four commissioned pieces to allude directly to the plinth's defining emptiness. She sees it not as a space to be filled, but as an absence to be acknowledged, and she does it well."
</p>
</blockquote>
<dl><dd>— Ned Denny, <i><a href="/wiki/New_Statesman" title="New Statesman">New Statesman</a></i>, 9 July 2001.</dd></dl>
<h3><span id="Snow_Show_.282004.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Snow_Show_(2004)"><i>Snow Show</i> (2004)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Snow Show (2004)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<blockquote>
<p>"The challenge has been to work in collaboration with an architect [Finnish architect <a href="/wiki/Juhani_Pallasmaa" title="Juhani Pallasmaa">Juhani Pallasmaa</a>] in a completely unfamiliar material. At this point, there is a 3-dimensional model of an actual stairwell space in East London, electronic imagery and a wooden mould that is being constructed in Rovaniemi, Finland. I know that the piece will be made from snow and will have a feeling of solidity; the viewer will be able to walk into it. The form is based upon a simple stairwell space that has been turned by 90 degrees. The exterior of the piece is a pragmatic solution simply reflecting the complex geometry of the interior. The new space should feel familiar and domestic. I hope that it will disorientate the viewer and make one think of other places."<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>— Rachel Whiteread</dd></dl></blockquote>

</p>
<dl><dd>— Rachel Whiteread</dd></dl>
<h3><span id="Embankment_.282005.E2.80.932006.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Embankment_(2005–2006)"><i>Embankment</i> (2005–2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Embankment (2005–2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Whiteread_tate_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Whiteread_tate_1.jpg/220px-Whiteread_tate_1.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Whiteread_tate_1.jpg/330px-Whiteread_tate_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Whiteread_tate_1.jpg/440px-Whiteread_tate_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Whiteread_tate_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Embankment</i></div></div></div>
<p>In spring 2004, she was offered the annual <a href="/wiki/Unilever" title="Unilever">Unilever</a> series commission to produce a piece for <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a>'s vast Turbine Hall, delaying acceptance for five to six months until she was confident she could conceive of a work to fill the space.<sup id="cite_ref-Barber2005_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2005-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the latter half of September 2005 and mid-way through October her work <i>Embankment</i> was installed and was made public on 10 October. 
</p><p>It consists of some 14,000 translucent, white <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> boxes (themselves casts of the inside of cardboard boxes) stacked in various ways; some in very tall mountain-like peaks and others in lower (though still over human height), rectangular, more leveled arrangements. They are fixed in position with an adhesive. She cited the end scenes of both <i><a href="/wiki/Raiders_of_the_Lost_Ark" title="Raiders of the Lost Ark">Raiders of the Lost Ark</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">Citizen Kane</a></i> as visual precursors; she also spoke of the death of her mother and a period of upheaval which involved packing and moving comparable boxes.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>

The boxes were manufactured from casts of ten plain cardboard boxes by a company that produces grit bins and traffic bollards.<sup id="cite_ref-Barber2005_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barber2005-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>

</blockquote>

<p>"Everything feels surprisingly domestic in scale, the intimidating vistas of the Turbine Hall shrunk down to irregular paths and byways. From atop the walkway, it looks like a storage depot that is steadily losing the plot; from inside, as you thread your way between the mounds of blocks, it feels more like an icy maze."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>

<h3><span id="The_Gran_Boathouse_.282010.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Gran_Boathouse_(2010)"><i>The Gran Boathouse</i> (2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The Gran Boathouse (2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://skulpturstopp.no/en/gran">The Gran Boathouse</a></i> is located on the waters edge in Gran <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>. From a distance, it looks like any other boathouse, but closer inspection reveals that this is a work of art in concrete. The work is a cast of the interior of an old boathouse. Whiteread turns the boathouse inside out thereby capturing a moment in time. In this way, she encourages us to reflect on what we see around us. "I have mummified the air inside the boathouse," says Rachel Whiteread. "I wanted to make a shy sculpture, a sculpture that would stand there peaceful and noble." The boathouse and its interior had all the qualities that she was looking for. It represented the history of the place. The sculpture is preserving what would otherwise have been lost.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Work_since_2012">Work since 2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Work since 2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cast from generic wooden sheds, <i>Detached 1</i>, <i>Detached 2</i>, and <i>Detached 3</i> (2012) render the empty interior of a garden shed in concrete and steel. <i>Circa 1665 (I)</i> (2012), <i>LOOK, LOOK, LOOK</i> (2012) and <i>Loom</i> (2012) belong to a series cast from doors and windows in shades of rose, eau-de-nil, or steely resin. Propped against or affixed to walls, the sculptures glow with absorbed and reflected light.<sup id="cite_ref-gagosian.com_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gagosian.com-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other works like <i>Untitled (Amber)</i> (2012) and <i>Untitled (Green)</i> (2012) are diminutive cardboard constructions mounted on graphite-marked notepaper, painted with silver leaf and complete with celluloid "windows" that refer to the resin sculptures.<sup id="cite_ref-gagosian.com_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gagosian.com-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Cabin_.282016.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cabin_(2016)"><i>Cabin</i> (2016)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rachel_Whiteread&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cabin (2016)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Cabin</i> is a concrete reverse cast of a wooden shed. It has been located on Discovery Hill on <a href="/wiki/Governors_Island" title="Governors Island">Governors Island</a> in New York Harbour since 2016. Whiteread uses this idea in order to produce a negative space that had existed but no longer does. Since <i>Cabin</i> is away from the noisy city, it creates a peaceful scene and a quiet sense. <i>Cabin</i> is said to be her first public commission in the United State that is installed permanently on the island.<sup id="cite_ref-LAug_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LAug-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
With his work, Whiteread wanted to "blur the notion of space even further by allowing the booming nature of the park to and hide the installation." Therefore even though the city that is so advanced with technology and is polluted by gasoline, "nature is still present."<sup id="cite_ref-Sleek_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sleek-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"What an extraordinary site, and what an honor to be asked to put something there," Whiteread states. 'I tried to imagine what one could sit there with some kind of dignity, to create a place of remembrance." She would like "to make a piece that was evocative without wanting to make a memorial to the World Trade Center."
</p>
</blockquote>
<dl><dd>- Rachel Whiteread<sup id="cite_ref-CBurns_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CBurns-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
