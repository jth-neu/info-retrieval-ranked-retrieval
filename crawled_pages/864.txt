<p><b>Greenwich Mean Time</b> (<b>GMT</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Local_mean_time" title="Local mean time">mean</a> <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">solar time</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">Royal Observatory</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, reckoned from midnight. At different times in the past, it has been calculated in different ways, including being calculated from noon;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> as a consequence, it cannot be used to specify a precise time unless a context is given.
</p><p>English speakers often use GMT as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">Coordinated Universal Time</a> (UTC).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> For navigation, it is considered equivalent to <a href="/wiki/UT1" class="mw-redirect" title="UT1">UT1</a> (the modern form of mean solar time at 0° longitude); but this meaning can differ from UTC by up to 0.9<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>s. The term GMT should not thus be used for technical purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of Earth's uneven speed in its elliptical <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a>, noon (12:00:00) GMT is rarely the exact moment the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> crosses the <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_meridian" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenwich meridian">Greenwich meridian</a> and reaches its highest point in the sky there. This event may occur up to 16 minutes before or after noon GMT, a discrepancy calculated by the <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_time" title="Equation of time">equation of time</a>. Noon GMT is the annual average (i.e. "mean") moment of this event, which accounts for the word "mean" in "Greenwich Mean Time".
</p><p>Originally, astronomers considered a GMT day to start at noon while for almost everyone else it started at midnight. To avoid confusion, the name <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">Universal Time</a> was introduced to denote GMT as counted from midnight.<sup id="cite_ref-McCarthySeidelmann2009_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthySeidelmann2009-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Astronomers preferred the old convention to simplify their observational data, so that each night was logged under a single calendar date. Today Universal Time usually refers to UTC or UT1.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "GMT" is especially used by bodies connected with the United Kingdom, such as the <a href="/wiki/BBC_World_Service" title="BBC World Service">BBC World Service</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Met_Office" title="Met Office">Met Office</a> and others particularly in Arab countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East_Broadcasting_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle East Broadcasting Centre">Middle East Broadcasting Centre</a> and <a href="/wiki/OSN" title="OSN">OSN</a>. It is a term commonly used in the United Kingdom and countries of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a>, including Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, India, Pakistan, Bangladesh and Malaysia; and in many other countries of the eastern hemisphere. In some countries (the United Kingdom for example) Greenwich Mean Time is the legal time in the winter and the population uses the term. See GMT in legislation, below, for further explanation.
</p>

<p>As the United Kingdom grew into an advanced <a href="/wiki/Maritime_nation" title="Maritime nation">maritime nation</a>, British mariners kept at least one <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">chronometer</a> on GMT to calculate their <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">Greenwich meridian</a>, which was by convention considered to have longitude zero degrees, internationally adopted in the <a href="/wiki/International_Meridian_Conference" title="International Meridian Conference">International Meridian Conference</a> of 1884. Synchronisation of the chronometer on GMT did not affect shipboard time, which was still solar time. But this practice, combined with mariners from other nations drawing from <a href="/wiki/Nevil_Maskelyne" title="Nevil Maskelyne">Nevil Maskelyne</a>'s method of <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(navigation)" title="Lunar distance (navigation)">lunar distances</a> based on observations at Greenwich, led to GMT being used worldwide as a standard time independent of location. Most <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">time zones</a> were based upon GMT, as an offset of a number of hours (and possibly half or quarter hours) "ahead of GMT" or "behind GMT".
</p><p>Greenwich Mean Time was adopted across the island of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Railway_Clearing_House" title="Railway Clearing House">Railway Clearing House</a> in 1847, and by almost all railway companies by the following year, from which the term "<a href="/wiki/Railway_time" title="Railway time">railway time</a>" is derived. It was gradually adopted for other purposes, but a legal case in 1858 held "<a href="/wiki/Local_mean_time" title="Local mean time">local mean time</a>" to be the official time.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> On 14 May 1880, a letter signed by 'Clerk to Justices' appeared in 'The Times', stating that "Greenwich time is now kept almost throughout England, but it appears that Greenwich time is not legal time. For example, our polling booths were opened, say, at 8 13 and closed at 4 13 p.m."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This was changed later in 1880, when Greenwich Mean Time was legally adopted throughout the island of Great Britain. GMT was adopted on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> in 1883, <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> in 1898 and <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> in 1913. Ireland adopted GMT in 1916, supplanting <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Mean_Time" class="mw-redirect" title="Dublin Mean Time">Dublin Mean Time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Hourly <a href="/wiki/Time_signal" title="Time signal">time signals</a> from Greenwich Observatory were first broadcast on 5 February 1924, rendering the <a href="/wiki/Time_ball" title="Time ball">time ball</a> at the observatory redundant.
</p><p>The daily rotation of the Earth is irregular (see <a href="/wiki/%CE%94T" title="ΔT">ΔT</a>) and constantly slows; therefore the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">atomic clocks</a> constitute a much more stable timebase. On 1 January 1972, GMT was superseded as the international civil time standard by Coordinated Universal Time, maintained by an ensemble of atomic clocks around the world. <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">Universal Time (UT)</a>, a term introduced in 1928, initially represented mean time at Greenwich determined in the traditional way to accord with the originally defined <a href="/wiki/International_Meridian_Conference" title="International Meridian Conference">universal day</a>; from 1 January 1956 (as decided by the <a href="/wiki/IAU" class="mw-redirect" title="IAU">IAU</a> at Dublin, 1955, at the initiative of <a href="/wiki/William_Markowitz" title="William Markowitz">William Markowitz</a>) this "raw" form of UT was re-labelled UT0 and effectively superseded by refined forms UT1 (UT0 equalised for the effects of polar wandering)<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and UT2 (UT1 further equalised for annual seasonal variations in earth rotation rate).
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p> Indeed, even the Greenwich meridian itself is not quite what it used to be—defined by "the centre of the transit instrument at the Observatory at Greenwich". Although that instrument still survives in working order, it is no longer in use and now the meridian of origin of the world's longitude and time is not strictly defined in material form but from a statistical solution resulting from observations of all time-determination stations which the <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">BIPM</a> takes into account when co-ordinating the world's time signals. Nevertheless, the line in the old observatory's courtyard today differs no more than a few metres from that imaginary line which is now the prime meridian of the world. </p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Howse, D. (1997). <i>Greenwich time and the longitude</i>. London: Philip Wilson.</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambiguity_in_the_definition_of_GMT">Ambiguity in the definition of GMT</span></h2>
<p>Historically GMT has been used with two different conventions for numbering hours. The long-standing astronomical convention dating from the work of <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">Ptolemy</a>, was to refer to <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">noon</a> as zero hours (see <a href="/wiki/Julian_day" title="Julian day">Julian day</a>). This contrasted with the civil convention of referring to <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">midnight</a> as zero hours dating from the Romans. The latter convention was adopted on and after 1 January 1925 for astronomical purposes, resulting in a discontinuity of 12 hours, or half a day. The instant that was designated 'December 31.5 GMT' in 1924 almanacs became 'January 1.0 GMT' in 1925 almanacs. The term Greenwich Mean Astronomical Time (GMAT) was introduced to unambiguously refer to the previous noon-based astronomical convention for GMT.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The more specific terms UT and UTC do not share this ambiguity, always referring to midnight as zero hours.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GMT_in_legislation">GMT in legislation</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span></h3>
<p>Legally, the civil time used in the UK is called still "Greenwich mean time" (without capitalisation), according to the <a href="/wiki/Interpretation_Act_1978" title="Interpretation Act 1978">Interpretation Act 1978</a>, with an exception made for those periods when the Summer Time Act 1972 orders an hour's shift for daylight saving. The Interpretation Act 1978, section 9, provides that whenever an expression of time occurs in an Act, the time referred to shall (unless otherwise specifically stated) be held to be Greenwich mean time. Under subsection 23(3), the same rule applies to deeds and other instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-Myers_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Myers-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the experiment of 1968-1971, when the British Isles did not revert to Greenwich Mean Time during the winter, the all-year <a href="/wiki/British_Summer_Time" title="British Summer Time">British Summer Time</a> was called British Standard Time (BST).
</p><p>In the UK, UTC+0 is disseminated to the general public in winter and UTC+1 in summer.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McCarthySeidelmann2009_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McCarthySeidelmann2009-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>BBC radio stations broadcast the "six pips" of the <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Time_Signal" title="Greenwich Time Signal">Greenwich Time Signal</a>. It is named from its original generation at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">Royal Greenwich Observatory</a>, is aligned to Coordinated Universal Time, and called either Greenwich Mean Time or British Summer Time as appropriate for the time of year.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">Other countries</span></h3>
<p>Several countries define their local time by reference to Greenwich Mean Time.<sup id="cite_ref-AMANO_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMANO-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Seago_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seago-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Some examples are:
</p>
<ul><li>Belgium: Decrees of 1946 and 1947 set legal time as one hour ahead of GMT.<sup id="cite_ref-AMANO_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMANO-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Ireland: Standard Time (Amendment) Act, 1971, section 1, and Interpretation Act 2005, part iv, section 18(i).</li>
<li>Canada: Interpretation Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. I-21, section 35(1). This refers to 'standard time' for the several provinces, defining each in relation to 'Greenwich time', but does not use the expression 'Greenwich mean time'. Several provinces, such as <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a> (Time Definition Act. R.S., c. 469, s. 1), have their own legislation which specifically mentions either "Greenwich Mean Time" or "Greenwich mean solar time".</li></ul>

<p>Since legal, political, social and economic criteria in addition to physical or geographical criteria are used in the drawing of <a href="/wiki/Time_zones" class="mw-redirect" title="Time zones">time zones</a>, actual time zones do not precisely adhere to meridian lines. The 'GMT' time zone, were it drawn by purely <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geographical terms</a>, would consist of the area between meridians 7°30'W and 7°30'E. As a result, there are European locales that despite lying in an area with a 'physical' UTC time use another time zone (<a href="/wiki/UTC%2B1" class="mw-redirect" title="UTC+1">UTC+1</a> in particular); conversely, there are European areas that use UTC, even though their 'physical' time zone is <a href="/wiki/UTC-1" class="mw-redirect" title="UTC-1">UTC−1</a> (e.g., most of <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>), or UTC−2 (the westernmost part of <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>). Because the UTC time zone in Europe is 'shifted' to the west, <a href="/wiki/Lowestoft" title="Lowestoft">Lowestoft</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> at only 1°45'E is the easternmost settlement in Europe in which UTC is applied. Following is a list of the 'incongruencies':
</p>
<dl><dt>Countries (or parts thereof) west of 22°30'W ("physical" UTC−2) that use UTC</dt></dl>
<ul><li>The westernmost part of Iceland, including the northwest peninsula and its main town of <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Dsafj%C3%B6r%C3%B0ur" title="Ísafjörður">Ísafjörður</a>, which is west of 22°30'W, uses UTC. <a href="/wiki/Bjargtangar" title="Bjargtangar">Bjargtangar</a>, Iceland is the westernmost point in which UTC is applied.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Countries (or parts thereof) west of 7°30'W ("physical" UTC−1) that use UTC</dt></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">Canary Islands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>)</li>
<li>Most of Portugal, including <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aveiro,_Portugal" title="Aveiro, Portugal">Aveiro</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a>. (Only the easternmost part, including cities such as <a href="/wiki/Bragan%C3%A7a_(Portugal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bragança (Portugal)">Bragança</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guarda,_Portugal" title="Guarda, Portugal">Guarda</a>, lies east of 7°30'W.) Since the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Windsor_(1386)" title="Treaty of Windsor (1386)">Treaty of Windsor</a> in 1386 (the world's oldest diplomatic alliance), Portugal has maintained close ties to the UK, which possibly explains its choice of UTC. <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">Madeira</a>, even further to the west, also employs UTC. A more likely explanation is that during the mid-1970s, when Portugal was on Central European Time all year round, it did not begin to get light in Lisbon in winter until 08:30.</li>
<li>Western part of <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, including the cities of <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limerick" title="Limerick">Limerick</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a>.</li>
<li>Westernmost tip of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, including the county town of <a href="/wiki/County_Fermanagh" title="County Fermanagh">County Fermanagh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enniskillen" title="Enniskillen">Enniskillen</a></li>
<li>Extreme westerly portion of the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Hebrides" title="Outer Hebrides">Outer Hebrides</a>, west of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>; for instance, <a href="/wiki/Vatersay" title="Vatersay">Vatersay</a>, an inhabited island and the westernmost settlement of the island of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, lies at 7°54'W. If <a href="/wiki/Uninhabited_island" class="mw-redirect" title="Uninhabited island">uninhabited islands</a> or rocks are taken into account <a href="/wiki/St_Kilda,_Scotland" title="St Kilda, Scotland">St Kilda</a>, west of the Outer Hebrides, at 8°58'W, and <a href="/wiki/Rockall" title="Rockall">Rockall</a>, at 13°41'W, should be included.</li>
<li>Westernmost island of the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> (autonomous region of the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish Kingdom</a>), <a href="/wiki/Mykines,_Faroe_Islands" title="Mykines, Faroe Islands">Mykines</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a></li>
<li>Northeastern part of <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Danmarkshavn" title="Danmarkshavn">Danmarkshavn</a></li></ul>

<dl><dt>Countries (mostly) between meridians 7°30'W and 7°30'E ("physical" UTC) that use UTC+1</dt></dl>
<ul><li>Spain (except for the Canary Islands, which use UTC). Parts of <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> lie west of 7°30'W ('physical' UTC−1), whereas there is no Spanish territory east of 7°30'E ('physical' UTC+1). Spain's time is the direct result of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">Franco</a>'s presidential order (published in <a href="/wiki/Bolet%C3%ADn_Oficial_del_Estado" title="Boletín Oficial del Estado">Boletín Oficial del Estado</a> of 8 March 1940)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> abandoning Greenwich Mean Time and advancing clocks one hour effective 23:00 16 March 1940. This is an excellent example of political criteria used in the drawing of time zones: the time change was passed "in consideration of the convenience from the national time marching in step according to that of other European countries".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BOE68p1676_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOE68p1676-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The presidential order (most likely enacted to be in synchrony with <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, with which the Franco regime was unofficially allied) included in its 5th article a provision for its future phase out,<sup id="cite_ref-BOE68p1676_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOE68p1676-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> which never took place. Due to this political decision Spain is two hours ahead of its <a href="/wiki/Local_mean_time" title="Local mean time">local mean time</a> during the summer, one hour ahead in winter, which possibly explains the notoriously late schedule for which the country is known.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In Portugal, which is a mere one hour behind Spain, the timetable is quite different.</li>
<li>Most of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, including the cities of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>. Only small parts of <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorraine (region)">Lorraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> are east of 7°30'E ("physical" UTC+1).</li>
