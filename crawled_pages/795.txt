<p><b>British rock</b> describes a wide variety of forms of music made in the United Kingdom. Since around 1964, with the "<a href="/wiki/British_Invasion" title="British Invasion">British Invasion</a>" of the United States spearheaded by <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>, British rock music has had a considerable impact on the development of American music and rock music across the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002BI_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002BI-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Initial attempts to emulate American <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> took place in Britain in the mid-1950s, but the terms "rock music" and "rock" usually refer to the music derived from the <a href="/wiki/Blues-rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Blues-rock">blues-rock</a> and other genres that emerged during the 1960s. The term is often used in combination with other terms to describe a variety of hybrids or subgenres, and is often contrasted with pop music, with which it shares many structures and instrumentation.  Rock music has tended to be more orientated toward the albums market, putting an emphasis on innovation, virtuosity, performance and song writing by the performers.<sup id="cite_ref-Firth2001pp93-108_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001pp93-108-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although much too diverse to be a genre in itself, British rock has produced many of the most significant groups and performers in rock music internationally, and has initiated or significantly developed many of the most influential subgenres, including <a href="/wiki/Beat_music" title="Beat music">beat music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art_rock" title="Art rock">art rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">post-punk</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Romantic" title="New Romantic">New Romantic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a>.
</p>

<p>In the 1950s, Britain was well placed to receive American rock and roll music and culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Unterberger_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unterberger-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It shared a common language, had been exposed to American culture through the stationing of troops in the country, and shared many social developments, including the emergence of distinct youth sub-cultures, which in Britain included the <a href="/wiki/Teddy_Boys" class="mw-redirect" title="Teddy Boys">Teddy Boys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Trad_Jazz" class="mw-redirect" title="Trad Jazz">Trad Jazz</a> became popular, and many of its musicians were influenced by related American styles, including <a href="/wiki/Boogie_Woogie" class="mw-redirect" title="Boogie Woogie">Boogie Woogie</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Blues" class="mw-redirect" title="The Blues">the Blues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Skiffle" title="Skiffle">skiffle</a> craze, led by <a href="/wiki/Lonnie_Donegan" title="Lonnie Donegan">Lonnie Donegan</a>, utilised mostly amateurish versions mainly of American folk songs and encouraged many of the subsequent generation of rock and roll, folk, R&amp;B and beat musicians to start performing.<sup id="cite_ref-Broken2003_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Broken2003-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> At the same time British audiences were beginning to encounter American rock and roll, initially through films including <i><a href="/wiki/Blackboard_Jungle" title="Blackboard Jungle">Blackboard Jungle</a></i> (1955) and <i><a href="/wiki/Rock_Around_the_Clock_(film)" title="Rock Around the Clock (film)">Rock Around the Clock</a></i> (1955).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Both films contained the <a href="/wiki/Bill_Haley_%26_His_Comets" title="Bill Haley &amp; His Comets">Bill Haley &amp; His Comets</a> hit "<a href="/wiki/Rock_Around_the_Clock" title="Rock Around the Clock">Rock Around the Clock</a>", which first entered the British charts in early 1955 - four months before it reached the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">US pop charts</a> - topped the British charts later that year and again in 1956, and helped identify rock and roll with teenage delinquency.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> American rock and roll acts such as <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Little_Richard" title="Little Richard">Little Richard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buddy_Holly" title="Buddy Holly">Buddy Holly</a> thereafter became major forces in the British charts.
</p><p>The initial response of the British music industry was to attempt to produce copies of American records, recorded with session musicians and often fronted by teen idols.<sup id="cite_ref-Unterberger_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unterberger-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> British rock and rollers soon began to appear, including <a href="/wiki/Wee_Willie_Harris" title="Wee Willie Harris">Wee Willie Harris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Steele" title="Tommy Steele">Tommy Steele</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Unterberger_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unterberger-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The bland or wholly imitative form of much British rock and roll in this period meant that the American product remained dominant. However, in 1958 Britain produced its first "authentic" rock and roll song and star, when <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Richard" title="Cliff Richard">Cliff Richard</a> reached number 2 in the charts with "<a href="/wiki/Move_It" title="Move It">Move It</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> British impresario <a href="/wiki/Larry_Parnes" title="Larry Parnes">Larry Parnes</a> fashioned young singers to the new trend, giving them corny names such as <a href="/wiki/Billy_Fury" title="Billy Fury">Billy Fury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marty_Wilde" title="Marty Wilde">Marty Wilde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vince_Eager" title="Vince Eager">Vince Eager</a>.  At the same time, TV shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/Six-Five_Special" title="Six-Five Special">Six-Five Special</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Oh_Boy!_(TV_series)" title="Oh Boy! (TV series)">Oh Boy!</a></i>, both produced by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Good_(producer)" title="Jack Good (producer)">Jack Good</a>, promoted the careers of British rock and rollers like Marty Wilde and <a href="/wiki/Adam_Faith" title="Adam Faith">Adam Faith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Unterberger_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unterberger-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Cliff Richard and his backing band The Drifters, who quickly changed their name to <a href="/wiki/The_Shadows" title="The Shadows">The Shadows</a>, were the most successful home grown rock and roll based acts of the era.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Other leading acts included <a href="/wiki/Joe_Brown_(singer)" title="Joe Brown (singer)">Joe Brown</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Kidd_%26_The_Pirates" class="mw-redirect" title="Johnny Kidd &amp; The Pirates">Johnny Kidd &amp; The Pirates</a>, whose 1960 hit song "<a href="/wiki/Shakin%27_All_Over" title="Shakin&#39; All Over">Shakin' All Over</a>" became a rock and roll standard.<sup id="cite_ref-Unterberger_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unterberger-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The first American rock and roll artist to hit British stages and appear on television was <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Gracie" title="Charlie Gracie">Charlie Gracie</a>, quickly followed by <a href="/wiki/Gene_Vincent" title="Gene Vincent">Gene Vincent</a> in December 1959, soon joined on tour by his friend <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cochran" title="Eddie Cochran">Eddie Cochran</a>. The producer <a href="/wiki/Joe_Meek" title="Joe Meek">Joe Meek</a> was the first to produce sizeable rock hits in England, culminating with <a href="/wiki/The_Tornados" title="The Tornados">The Tornados</a>' instrumental "<a href="/wiki/Telstar_(song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Telstar (song)">Telstar</a>", which went to number one in both the UK and USA.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_in_the_1960s_and_early_1970s">Development in the 1960s and early 1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development in the 1960s and early 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beat_music">Beat music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beat music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beat_music" title="Beat music">Beat music</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG/220px-Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG/330px-Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG/440px-Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG 2x" data-file-width="579" data-file-height="551" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dave_Clark_Five_1966.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Dave_Clark_Five" title="The Dave Clark Five">The Dave Clark Five</a> shown in 1966</div></div></div>
<p>In late 1950s Britain a flourishing culture of groups began to emerge, often out of the declining <a href="/wiki/Skiffle" title="Skiffle">skiffle</a> scene, in major urban centres in the UK like <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and London. This was particularly true in Liverpool, where it has been estimated that there were around 350 different bands active, often playing ballrooms, concert halls and clubs.<sup id="cite_ref-Harry_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harry-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Van_Morrison_(1972).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Van_Morrison_%281972%29.png/220px-Van_Morrison_%281972%29.png" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Van_Morrison_%281972%29.png/330px-Van_Morrison_%281972%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/Van_Morrison_%281972%29.png 2x" data-file-width="353" data-file-height="354" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Van_Morrison_(1972).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a> in 1972</div></div></div>
<p>These beat bands were heavily influenced by American groups of the era, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Crickets" title="The Crickets">Buddy Holly and the Crickets</a> (from which groups <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Hollies" title="The Hollies">The Hollies</a> derived their names), as well as earlier British groups such as The Shadows.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> After the national success of the Beatles in Britain from 1962, a number of Liverpool performers were able to follow them into the charts, including <a href="/wiki/Gerry_%26_The_Pacemakers" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerry &amp; The Pacemakers">Gerry &amp; The Pacemakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Searchers_(band)" title="The Searchers (band)">The Searchers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cilla_Black" title="Cilla Black">Cilla Black</a>. Among the most successful beat acts from Birmingham were <a href="/wiki/The_Spencer_Davis_Group" title="The Spencer Davis Group">The Spencer Davis Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Moody_Blues" title="The Moody Blues">The Moody Blues</a>; <a href="/wiki/The_Animals" title="The Animals">The Animals</a> came from <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Them_(band)" title="Them (band)">Them</a>, featuring <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>. From London, the term <i>Tottenham Sound</i> was largely based around <a href="/wiki/The_Dave_Clark_Five" title="The Dave Clark Five">The Dave Clark Five</a>, but other London bands that benefited from the beat boom of this era included the <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolling Stones">Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">The Kinks</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Yardbirds" title="The Yardbirds">The Yardbirds</a>. The first non-<a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, non-<a href="/wiki/Brian_Epstein" title="Brian Epstein">Brian Epstein</a>-managed band to break through in the UK were <a href="/wiki/Freddie_and_the_Dreamers" title="Freddie and the Dreamers">Freddie and the Dreamers</a>, who were based in Manchester,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> as were <a href="/wiki/Herman%27s_Hermits" title="Herman&#39;s Hermits">Herman's Hermits</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Hollies" title="The Hollies">The Hollies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The beat movement provided most of the bands responsible for the <a href="/wiki/British_invasion" class="mw-redirect" title="British invasion">British invasion</a> of the American pop charts in the period after 1964, and furnished the model for many important developments in pop and rock music, particularly through their small group format - typically lead guitar, rhythm guitar, bass guitar, and drums, sometimes replacing the rhythm guitar with keyboards, either with a lead singer or with one of the musicians taking lead vocals and the others providing vocal harmonies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_blues_boom">British blues boom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: British blues boom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_blues" title="British blues">British blues</a></div>
<p>In parallel with beat music, in the late 1950s and early 1960s a British blues scene was developing recreating the sounds of American <a href="/wiki/R%26B" class="mw-redirect" title="R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> and later particularly the sounds of bluesmen <a href="/wiki/Robert_Johnson" title="Robert Johnson">Robert Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Howlin%27_Wolf" title="Howlin&#39; Wolf">Howlin' Wolf</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muddy_Waters" title="Muddy Waters">Muddy Waters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic700_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic700-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Initially led by purist blues followers such as <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Korner" title="Alexis Korner">Alexis Korner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyril_Davies" title="Cyril Davies">Cyril Davies</a>, it reached its height of mainstream popularity in the 1960s, when it developed a distinctive and influential style dominated by electric guitar and made international stars of several proponents of the genre including The Rolling Stones, <a href="/wiki/The_Yardbirds" title="The Yardbirds">The Yardbirds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eric_Clapton" title="Eric Clapton">Eric Clapton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cream_(band)" title="Cream (band)">Cream</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fleetwood_Mac" title="Fleetwood Mac">Fleetwood Mac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a> (who morphed out of The Yardbirds). A number of these moved through <a href="/wiki/Blues-rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Blues-rock">Blues-rock</a> to different forms of rock music, with increasing emphasis on technical virtuosity and improvisational skills.  As a result, British blues helped to form many of the subgenres of rock, including <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal music</a>. Since then direct interest in the blues in Britain has declined, but many of the key performers have returned to it in recent years, new acts have emerged and there has been a renewed interest in the genre.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusic700_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusic700-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="The_Beatles_and_the_.22British_Invasion.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Beatles_and_the_&quot;British_Invasion&quot;">The Beatles and the "British Invasion"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Beatles and the &quot;British Invasion&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_invasion" class="mw-redirect" title="British invasion">British invasion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Beatles_in_America.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/The_Beatles_in_America.JPG/220px-The_Beatles_in_America.JPG" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/The_Beatles_in_America.JPG/330px-The_Beatles_in_America.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/The_Beatles_in_America.JPG/440px-The_Beatles_in_America.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3578" data-file-height="2526" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Beatles_in_America.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The arrival of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> in the U.S., and subsequent appearance on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">The Ed Sullivan Show</a></i>, marked the start of the British Invasion.</div></div></div>
<p>The Beatles themselves were less influenced by blues music than the music of later American genres such as <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a>.  Their popular success in Britain in the early 1960s was matched by their new and highly influential emphases on their own song writing, and on technical production values, some of which were shared by other British beat groups.  On 7 February 1964, the <a href="/wiki/CBS_Evening_News" title="CBS Evening News">CBS Evening News</a> with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Cronkite" title="Walter Cronkite">Walter Cronkite</a> ran a story about <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' United States arrival in which the correspondent said "The British Invasion this time goes by the code name <a href="/wiki/Beatlemania" title="Beatlemania">Beatlemania</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A few days later, they appeared on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">The Ed Sullivan Show</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Seventy five percent of Americans watching television that night viewed their appearance thus "launching"<sup id="cite_ref-BeatlesArrive_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeatlesArrive-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> the invasion with a massive wave of chart success that would continue until the Beatles broke up in 1970. On 4 April 1964, the Beatles held the top 5 positions on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> singles chart, the only time to date that any act has accomplished this.<sup id="cite_ref-BeatlesArrive_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeatlesArrive-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBCharts_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCharts-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> During the next two years, <a href="/wiki/Peter_and_Gordon" title="Peter and Gordon">Peter and Gordon</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Animals" title="The Animals">The Animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Mann" title="Manfred Mann">Manfred Mann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petula_Clark" title="Petula Clark">Petula Clark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freddie_and_the_Dreamers" title="Freddie and the Dreamers">Freddie and the Dreamers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Fontana_and_the_Mindbenders" class="mw-redirect" title="Wayne Fontana and the Mindbenders">Wayne Fontana and the Mindbenders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herman%27s_Hermits" title="Herman&#39;s Hermits">Herman's Hermits</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Troggs" title="The Troggs">The Troggs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Donovan" title="Donovan">Donovan</a> would have one or more number one singles in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Other acts that were part of the "invasion" included <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">The Kinks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Dave_Clark_Five" title="The Dave Clark Five">The Dave Clark Five</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-BeatlesArrive_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeatlesArrive-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> these acts were also successful within the UK, although clearly the term "British Invasion" itself was not applied there except as a description of what was happening in the USA.  So-called "British Invasion" acts influenced fashion, haircuts and manners of the 1960s of what was to be known as the "<a href="/wiki/Counterculture" title="Counterculture">Counterculture</a>". In particular, the Beatles' movie <i><a href="/wiki/A_Hard_Day%27s_Night_(film)" title="A Hard Day&#39;s Night (film)">A Hard Day's Night</a></i> and fashions from <a href="/wiki/Carnaby_Street" title="Carnaby Street">Carnaby Street</a> led American media to proclaim England as the centre of the music and fashion world.<sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The success of British acts of the time, particularly that of the Beatles themselves, has been seen as revitalising rock music in the US and influenced many American bands to develop their sound and style.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002BI_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002BI-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The growth of the British music industry itself, and its increasingly prominent global role in the forefront of changing popular culture, also enabled it to discover and first establish the success of new rock artists from elsewhere in the world, notably <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> and, in the early 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Firth2001pp81&amp;194-6_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Firth2001pp81&amp;194-6-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychedelic_rock">Psychedelic rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Psychedelic rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">Psychedelic rock</a></div>
<p>Psychedelic music is a style of music that is inspired or influenced by <a href="/wiki/Psychedelia" title="Psychedelia">psychedelic</a> culture and attempts to replicate and enhance the mind-altering experiences of <a href="/wiki/Psychedelics,_dissociatives_and_deliriants" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychedelics, dissociatives and deliriants">hallucinogenic drugs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It particularly grew out of blues-rock and <a href="/wiki/Progressive_folk_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Progressive folk music">progressive folk music</a> and drew on non-Western sources such as Indian music's <a href="/wiki/Raga" title="Raga">ragas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sitar" title="Sitar">sitars</a> as well as studio effects and long instrumental passages and surreal lyrics. It emerged during the mid-1960s among <a href="/wiki/Progressive_folk" title="Progressive folk">progressive folk</a> acts in Britain such as <a href="/wiki/The_Incredible_String_Band" title="The Incredible String Band">The Incredible String Band</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donovan" title="Donovan">Donovan</a>, as well as in the United States, and rapidly moved into rock and pop music being taken up by acts including the Beatles, The Yardbirds, The Moody Blues, <a href="/wiki/Small_Faces" title="Small Faces">Small Faces</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Move" title="The Move">The Move</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traffic_(band)" title="Traffic (band)">Traffic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cream_(band)" title="Cream (band)">Cream</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">Psychedelic rock</a> bridged the transition from early <a href="/wiki/Blues-rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Blues-rock">blues-rock</a> to <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Art_rock" title="Art rock">art rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Experimental_rock" title="Experimental rock">experimental rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a> and eventually <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> that would become major genres in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Shock_rock" title="Shock rock">Shock rock</a> pioneer <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Brown_(musician)" title="Arthur Brown (musician)">Arthur Brown</a> performed his 1968 hit song "Fire" wearing black and white makeup (<a href="/wiki/Corpse_paint" title="Corpse paint">corpse paint</a>) and a burning headpiece.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He has been a significant influence on extreme acts that have followed.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainstream_and_global_success">Mainstream and global success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mainstream and global success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:David_bowie_05061978_01_150.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/David_bowie_05061978_01_150.jpg" width="150" height="221" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="150" data-file-height="221" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:David_bowie_05061978_01_150.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>David Bowie, <a href="/wiki/Ekeberghallen" title="Ekeberghallen">Ekeberghallen</a>, Oslo, Norway, 1978</div></div></div>
<p>By the early 1970s, rock music had become more mainstream, and internationalised, with many British acts becoming massively successful in the United States and globally. Some of the most successful artists, such as the individual members of The Beatles, <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rod_Stewart" title="Rod Stewart">Rod Stewart</a> performed their own songs (and in some cases those written by others) in an eclectic variety of styles, in which the presentation of the performance itself became increasingly important.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> By way of contrast, the former psychedelic-pop act, The <a href="/wiki/Status_Quo_(band)" title="Status Quo (band)">Status Quo</a>, dropped the <a href="/wiki/Definite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Definite article">definite article</a> from their name and became one of the most successful British rock acts by presenting an apparently unsophisticated style of <a href="/wiki/Boogie" title="Boogie">boogie</a>-based rock music;<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Van_Morrison" title="Van Morrison">Van Morrison</a> gained international critical acclaim through a blend of rock, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> styles.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Some well-established British bands that began their careers in the British Invasion, notably <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">The Kinks</a>, also developed their own particular styles and expanded their international fan base during that period, but would be joined by new acts in new styles and subgenres.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_subgenres_in_the_1970s">New subgenres in the 1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: New subgenres in the 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_folk_rock">British folk rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: British folk rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_folk_rock" title="British folk rock">British folk rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png/220px-Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png" width="220" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png/330px-Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png/440px-Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="498" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fairport_Convention_-_TopPop_1972_7.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Fairport_Convention" title="Fairport Convention">Fairport Convention</a> in a Dutch television show in 1972</div></div></div>
<p>British folk rock developed in Britain during the mid to late 1960s by the bands <a href="/wiki/Fairport_Convention" title="Fairport Convention">Fairport Convention</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pentangle" title="Pentangle">Pentangle</a> which built on elements of American <a href="/wiki/Folk_rock" title="Folk rock">folk rock</a>, and on the second <a href="/wiki/British_folk_revival" title="British folk revival">British folk revival</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brocken2003_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brocken2003-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Using traditional English music as its basis, these bands drew heavily on the <a href="/wiki/Child_Ballads" title="Child Ballads">Child Ballads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ballads" class="mw-redirect" title="Ballads">ballads</a> of the British Isles from the later medieval period until the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> An early success was Fairport Convention's 1969 album <i><a href="/wiki/Liege_and_Lief" class="mw-redirect" title="Liege and Lief">Liege and Lief</a></i>, but it became more significant in the 1970s, when it was taken up by groups such as <a href="/wiki/Pentangle" title="Pentangle">Pentangle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steeleye_Span" title="Steeleye Span">Steeleye Span</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Albion_Band" class="mw-redirect" title="Albion Band">Albion Band</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> It was rapidly adopted and developed in the surrounding Celtic cultures of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>, where it was pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Stivell" title="Alan Stivell">Alan Stivell</a> and bands like <a href="/wiki/Malicorne_(band)" title="Malicorne (band)">Malicorne</a>; in Ireland by groups such as <a href="/wiki/Horslips" title="Horslips">Horslips</a>; and also in Scotland, Wales and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, to produce <a href="/wiki/Celtic_rock" title="Celtic rock">Celtic rock</a> and its derivatives.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> It was also influential in those parts of the world with close cultural connections to the UK, such as the US and Canada and gave rise to the subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Medieval_folk_rock" title="Medieval folk rock">Medieval folk rock</a> and the fusion genres of <a href="/wiki/Folk_punk" title="Folk punk">folk punk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_metal" title="Folk metal">folk metal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> By the end of the 1970s the genre was in steep decline in popularity, as other forms of music, including punk and electronic began to be established.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Progressive_rock">Progressive rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Progressive rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">Progressive rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yes_concert.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Yes_concert.jpg/220px-Yes_concert.jpg" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Yes_concert.jpg/330px-Yes_concert.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Yes_concert.jpg/440px-Yes_concert.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1514" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yes_concert.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a> performing in <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a> in 1977.</div></div></div>
<p>Progressive or prog rock developed out of late 1960s <a href="/wiki/Blues-rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Blues-rock">blues-rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a>. Dominated by British bands, it was part of an attempt to elevate rock music to new levels of artistic credibility.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusic1_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusic1-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Progressive rock bands attempted to push the technical and compositional boundaries of rock by going beyond the standard verse-<a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">chorus</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Song_structure_(popular_music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Song structure (popular music)">song structures</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Arrangement" title="Arrangement">arrangements</a> often incorporated elements drawn from <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, and international sources later called "<a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world music</a>". <a href="/wiki/Instrumental" title="Instrumental">Instrumentals</a> were common, while songs with lyrics were sometimes conceptual, abstract, or based in fantasy. Progressive rock bands sometimes used <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept albums</a> that made unified statements, usually telling an epic story or tackling a grand overarching theme.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusic1_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusic1-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/King_Crimson" title="King Crimson">King Crimson</a>'s 1969 début album, <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Court_of_the_Crimson_King" title="In the Court of the Crimson King">In the Court of the Crimson King</a></i>, which mixed powerful guitar riffs and <a href="/wiki/Mellotron" title="Mellotron">mellotron</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Symphonic_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Symphonic music">symphonic music</a>, is often taken as the key recording in progressive rock, helping the widespread adoption of the genre in the early 1970s among existing blues-rock and psychedelic bands, as well as newly formed acts. The term was applied to the music of bands such as <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jethro_Tull_(band)" title="Jethro Tull (band)">Jethro Tull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soft_Machine" title="Soft Machine">Soft Machine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_Light_Orchestra" title="Electric Light Orchestra">Electric Light Orchestra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Procol_Harum" title="Procol Harum">Procol Harum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawkwind" title="Hawkwind">Hawkwind</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer" title="Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer">Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusic1_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusic1-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> It reached its peak of popularity in the mid-1970s, but had mixed critical acclaim and the punk movement can be seen as a reaction against its musicality and perceived pomposity.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Many bands broke up, but some, including Genesis, ELP, Yes, and Pink Floyd, regularly scored Top Ten albums with successful accompanying worldwide tours.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glam_rock">Glam rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Glam rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">Glam rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg/170px-Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg" width="170" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg/255px-Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg/340px-Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg 2x" data-file-width="855" data-file-height="1137" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Marc_Bolan_In_Concert_1973.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Marc_Bolan" title="Marc Bolan">Marc Bolan</a> of <a href="/wiki/T._Rex_(band)" title="T. Rex (band)">T. Rex</a> performing in 1973</div></div></div>
<p>Glam or glitter rock developed in the UK in the post-<a href="/wiki/Hippie" title="Hippie">hippie</a> early 1970s. It was characterised by "outrageous" clothes, makeup, hairstyles, and platform-soled boots.<sup id="cite_ref-Encarta_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encarta-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The flamboyant lyrics, costumes, and visual styles of glam performers were a <a href="/wiki/Camp_(style)" title="Camp (style)">campy</a>, playing with categories of sexuality in a theatrical blend of <a href="/wiki/Nostalgia" title="Nostalgia">nostalgic</a> references to science fiction and old movies, all over a guitar-driven <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a> sound.<sup id="cite_ref-AllmusicGR_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllmusicGR-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Pioneers of the genre included <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music" title="Roxy Music">Roxy Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mott_the_Hoople" title="Mott the Hoople">Mott the Hoople</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Bolan" title="Marc Bolan">Marc Bolan</a> and <a href="/wiki/T._Rex_(band)" title="T. Rex (band)">T. Rex</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AllmusicGR_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllmusicGR-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> These, and many other acts straddled the divide between pop and rock music, managing to maintain a level of respectability with rock audiences, while enjoying success in the UK singles chart, including <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>. Other performers aimed much more directly for the popular music market, where they were the dominant groups of their era, including <a href="/wiki/Slade" title="Slade">Slade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wizzard" title="Wizzard">Wizzard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mud_(band)" title="Mud (band)">Mud</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Sweet" title="The Sweet">Sweet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AllmusicGR_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllmusicGR-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The glitter image was pushed to its limits by <a href="/wiki/Gary_Glitter" title="Gary Glitter">Gary Glitter</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Glitter_Band" title="The Glitter Band">The Glitter Band</a>. Largely confined to the British music scene where it originated, glam rock peaked during the mid-1970s, before it declined in the face of punk rock and new wave trends.<sup id="cite_ref-AllmusicGR_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllmusicGR-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> It has had a direct influence on acts that rose to prominence later.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_metal">Heavy metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Heavy metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">Heavy metal music</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_wave_of_British_heavy_metal" title="New wave of British heavy metal">New wave of British heavy metal</a></div>
<p>With roots in <a href="/wiki/Blues-rock" class="mw-redirect" title="Blues-rock">blues-rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Garage_rock" title="Garage rock">garage rock</a> the bands that created heavy metal developed a thick, powerful sound, characterised by overt rhythmic basslines, highly amplified <a href="/wiki/Distortion_(guitar)" class="mw-redirect" title="Distortion (guitar)">distortion</a>, extended guitar solos, emphatic beats, and overall loudness. Heavy metal lyrics and performance styles often incorporated elements of fantasy and science fiction, and are generally associated with masculinity and <a href="/wiki/Machismo" title="Machismo">machismo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The three pioneering heavy metal bands, <a href="/wiki/Led_Zeppelin" title="Led Zeppelin">Led Zeppelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a>, were all British and, while gaining little critical acclaim, they and the next generation of metal groups, which included American, Australian and continental bands beside British acts <a href="/wiki/Judas_Priest" title="Judas Priest">Judas Priest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mot%C3%B6rhead" title="Motörhead">Motörhead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(English_band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainbow (English band)">Rainbow</a>, attracted large audiences and record sales.<sup id="cite_ref-Walser1993_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walser1993-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Rainbow moved heavy metal into <a href="/wiki/Arena_rock" title="Arena rock">stadium rock</a> while <a href="/wiki/Mot%C3%B6rhead" title="Motörhead">Motörhead</a> introduced a <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> sensibility and an increasing emphasis on speed. After a decline in popularity in the late 1970s <a href="/wiki/Judas_Priest" title="Judas Priest">Judas Priest</a> discarded most of the genre's <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> influences, particularly on their 1980 album <i><a href="/wiki/British_Steel_(album)" title="British Steel (album)">British Steel</a></i>, which opened the door for the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_of_British_heavy_metal" title="New wave of British heavy metal">new wave of British heavy metal</a> (NWOBHM) including <a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vardis" title="Vardis">Vardis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxon_(band)" title="Saxon (band)">Saxon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Def_Leppard" title="Def Leppard">Def Leppard</a>, and a return to popularity in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Walser1993_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walser1993-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:217px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_(France).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_%28France%29.jpg/215px-Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_%28France%29.jpg" width="215" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_%28France%29.jpg/323px-Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_%28France%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_%28France%29.jpg/430px-Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_%28France%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2527" data-file-height="1726" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Iron_Maiden_in_the_Palais_Omnisports_of_Paris-Bercy_(France).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a> were leaders of the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_of_British_heavy_metal" title="New wave of British heavy metal">new wave of British heavy metal</a></div></div></div>
<p>Although NWOBHM inspired many new bands, in the late 1980s much of the creative impetus in the genre shifted towards America and continental Europe (particularly Germany and Scandinavia), which produced most of the major new subgenres of metal, which were then taken up by British acts. These included <a href="/wiki/Thrash_metal" title="Thrash metal">thrash metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a>, both developed in the USA; <a href="/wiki/Black_metal" title="Black metal">black metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_metal" title="Power metal">power metal</a>, both developed in continental Europe, but influenced by the British band <a href="/wiki/Venom_(band)" title="Venom (band)">Venom</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Doom_metal" title="Doom metal">doom</a>, which was developed in the USA but which soon had a number of bands from England, including <a href="/wiki/Pagan_Altar" title="Pagan Altar">Pagan Altar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Witchfinder_General_(band)" title="Witchfinder General (band)">Witchfinder General</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> There's also a large British influence in the doom/gothic metal scene, pioneered by such bands as <a href="/wiki/Paradise_Lost_(band)" title="Paradise Lost (band)">Paradise Lost</a>, <a href="/wiki/My_Dying_Bride" title="My Dying Bride">My Dying Bride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anathema_(band)" title="Anathema (band)">Anathema</a>. <a href="/wiki/Grindcore" title="Grindcore">Grindcore</a>, or simply grind, was a hybrid of death metal and hardcore punk, characterized by heavily <a href="/wiki/Guitar_distortion" class="mw-redirect" title="Guitar distortion">distorted</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guitar_tunings#Rock_music_tunings" title="Guitar tunings">down-tuned guitars</a>, high speed <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blast_beat" title="Blast beat">blast beats</a>, songs often lasting no more than two minutes (some are seconds long), and vocals which consist of <a href="/wiki/Death_growl" title="Death growl">growls</a> and high-pitched screams. Pioneers, the British band <a href="/wiki/Napalm_Death" title="Napalm Death">Napalm Death</a> inspired other British grindcore groups in the 1980s, among them <a href="/wiki/Extreme_Noise_Terror" title="Extreme Noise Terror">Extreme Noise Terror</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carcass_(band)" title="Carcass (band)">Carcass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sore_Throat_(grindcore_band)" title="Sore Throat (grindcore band)">Sore Throat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vonhavoc_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vonhavoc-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Probably the most successful British metal band since the days of <a href="/wiki/NWOBHM" class="mw-redirect" title="NWOBHM">NWOBHM</a> were <a href="/wiki/Cradle_of_Filth" title="Cradle of Filth">Cradle of Filth</a>, formed in 1991, and pursuing a form of extreme metal that is difficult to categorise.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The term "retro-metal" has been applied to such bands as <a href="/wiki/The_Darkness_(band)" title="The Darkness (band)">The Darkness</a>, whose mix of <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">glam rock</a> and heavy riffs earned them a string of singles hits and a quintuple platinum album with <i><a href="/wiki/One_Way_Ticket_to_Hell..._and_Back" title="One Way Ticket to Hell... and Back">One Way Ticket to Hell... and Back</a></i> (2005), which reached number 11 in the UK charts.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bullet_for_My_Valentine" title="Bullet for My Valentine">Bullet for My Valentine</a>, from Wales, broke into the top 5 in both the U.S. and British charts with their metalcore, a mixture of metal and hardcore, with <i><a href="/wiki/Scream_Aim_Fire" title="Scream Aim Fire">Scream Aim Fire</a></i> (2008).<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Proto-punk.2C_punk_and_new_wave"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-punk,_punk_and_new_wave">Proto-punk, punk and new wave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Proto-punk, punk and new wave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pub_rock">Pub rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pub rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pub_rock_(United_Kingdom)" title="Pub rock (United Kingdom)">Pub rock (United Kingdom)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pub_rock_(United_Kingdom)" title="Pub rock (United Kingdom)">Pub rock</a> was a short-lived trend that left a lasting influence on the British music scene, especially in <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a>. It was a back-to-basics movement that reacted against the glittery <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">glam rock</a> of <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gary_Glitter" title="Gary Glitter">Gary Glitter</a>, and peaked in the mid-1970s. Pub rock developed in large north London pubs.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> It is said to have begun in May 1971 with <a href="/wiki/Eggs_over_Easy" title="Eggs over Easy">Eggs over Easy</a>, an American band, playing in the Tally Ho! in <a href="/wiki/Kentish_Town" title="Kentish Town">Kentish Town</a>. A group of musicians who had been playing in blues and R&amp;B bands during the 1960s and early 70s soon formed influential bands like <a href="/wiki/Brinsley_Schwarz" title="Brinsley Schwarz">Brinsley Schwarz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ducks_Deluxe" title="Ducks Deluxe">Ducks Deluxe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bees_Make_Honey" title="Bees Make Honey">Bees Make Honey</a>. Brinsley Schwarz was probably the most influential group, achieving some mainstream success both in the UK and in the States.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The second wave of pub rock included <a href="/wiki/Kilburn_and_the_High_Roads" title="Kilburn and the High Roads">Kilburn and the High Roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ace_(band)" title="Ace (band)">Ace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chilli_Willi_and_the_Red_Hot_Peppers" title="Chilli Willi and the Red Hot Peppers">Chilli Willi and the Red Hot Peppers</a>; these were followed by the third and final wave of pub rock, including <a href="/wiki/Dr._Feelgood_(band)" title="Dr. Feelgood (band)">Dr. Feelgood</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Winkies" title="The Winkies">The Winkies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sniff_%27n%27_the_Tears" title="Sniff &#39;n&#39; the Tears">Sniff 'n' the Tears</a>. Several pub rock musicians joined the new wave acts such as <a href="/wiki/Graham_Parker" title="Graham Parker">Graham Parker</a>'s backing band, <a href="/wiki/The_Rumour" title="The Rumour">The Rumour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello_%26_the_Attractions" class="mw-redirect" title="Elvis Costello &amp; the Attractions">Elvis Costello &amp; the Attractions</a> and even <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Punk_rock">Punk rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Punk rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">Punk rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg/220px-Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg/330px-Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg/440px-Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="468" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clash_21051980_12_800.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a> performing in 1980.</div></div></div>
<p>Punk rock developed between 1974 and 1976, originally in the United States, where it was rooted in <a href="/wiki/Garage_rock" title="Garage rock">garage rock</a>, and other forms of what is now known as <a href="/wiki/Protopunk" class="mw-redirect" title="Protopunk">protopunk</a> music.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The first punk band is usually thought to be the <a href="/wiki/Ramones" title="Ramones">Ramones</a> from 1976. This was taken up in Britain by bands also influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Pub_rock_(United_Kingdom)" title="Pub rock (United Kingdom)">pub rock</a> scene, like the <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Damned_(band)" title="The Damned (band)">The Damned</a>, particularly in London, who became the vanguard of a new musical and cultural movement, blending simple aggressive sounds and lyrics with <a href="/wiki/Punk_fashion" title="Punk fashion">clothing styles</a> and a variety of <a href="/wiki/Punk_ideologies" title="Punk ideologies">anti-authoritarian ideologies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicPunk_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicPunk-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Punk rock bands eschewed the perceived excesses of mainstream 1970s rock, creating fast, hard-edged music, typically with short songs, stripped-down instrumentation, and often political, <a href="/wiki/Anti-establishment" title="Anti-establishment">anti-establishment</a> lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicPunk_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicPunk-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Punk embraced a <a href="/wiki/DIY_ethic" title="DIY ethic">DIY</a> (do it yourself) ethic, with many bands self-producing their recordings and distributing them through informal channels.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicPunk_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicPunk-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> 1977 saw punk rock spreading around the world, and it became a major international cultural phenomenon. However, by 1978, the initial impulse had subsided and punk had morphed into the wider and more diverse new wave and post-punk movements.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicPunk_53-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicPunk-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_wave">New wave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: New wave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">New wave music</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ThePolice_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/ThePolice_2007.jpg/220px-ThePolice_2007.jpg" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/ThePolice_2007.jpg/330px-ThePolice_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/ThePolice_2007.jpg/440px-ThePolice_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="563" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ThePolice_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">The Police</a> (pictured in 2007) had a string of UK number one albums</div></div></div>
<p>As the initial punk impulse began to subside, with the major punk bands either disbanding or taking on new influences, the term "New Wave" began to be used to describe particularly British bands that emerged in the later 1970s with mainstream appeal. These included pop bands like <a href="/wiki/XTC" title="XTC">XTC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squeeze_(band)" title="Squeeze (band)">Squeeze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nick_Lowe" title="Nick Lowe">Nick Lowe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_rock" title="Electronic rock">electronic rock</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a> as well as songwriters like <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a>, rock &amp; roll influenced bands like the Pretenders, the reggae influenced music of bands like <a href="/wiki/The_Police" title="The Police">The Police</a>, as well as bands of the <a href="/wiki/Ska" title="Ska">ska</a> revival like <a href="/wiki/The_Specials" title="The Specials">The Specials</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madness_(band)" title="Madness (band)">Madness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> By the end of the decade many of these bands, most obviously the Police, were beginning to make an impact in American and world markets.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-punk">Post-punk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Post-punk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">Post-punk</a></div>
<p>Beside the development of mainstream new wave, there were also less commercial, darker and sub-culture acts, often classified as post-punk. Like new wave they incorporated a range of influences, including electronic music, Jamaican <a href="/wiki/Dub_music" title="Dub music">dub music</a> (specifically in bass guitar), and American <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a>. Examples of post-punk outfits in Britain included <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">The Smiths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_Juice_(band)" title="Orange Juice (band)">Orange Juice</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Psychedelic_Furs" title="The Psychedelic Furs">The Psychedelic Furs</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Fall_(band)" title="The Fall (band)">The Fall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siouxsie_and_the_Banshees" title="Siouxsie and the Banshees">Siouxsie and the Banshees</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Lords_of_the_New_Church" title="The Lords of the New Church">The Lords of the New Church</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joy_Division" title="Joy Division">Joy Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/Killing_Joke" title="Killing Joke">Killing Joke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echo_%26_the_Bunnymen" title="Echo &amp; the Bunnymen">Echo &amp; the Bunnymen</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">The Cure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus_(band)" title="Bauhaus (band)">Bauhaus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(band)" title="Magazine (band)">Magazine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wire_(band)" title="Wire (band)">Wire</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Jesus_and_Mary_Chain" title="The Jesus and Mary Chain">The Jesus and Mary Chain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tubeway_Army" title="Tubeway Army">Tubeway Army</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Post-punk would be a major element in the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gothic_rock" title="Gothic rock">gothic rock</a> genres.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_punk">Folk punk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Folk punk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Folk_punk" title="Folk punk">Folk punk</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Levelersi115.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Levelersi115.JPG/220px-Levelersi115.JPG" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Levelersi115.JPG/330px-Levelersi115.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Levelersi115.JPG/440px-Levelersi115.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1853" data-file-height="1293" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Levelersi115.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Levellers in Prague, 2006</div></div></div>
<p>Folk punk or rogue folk is a fusion of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a>, or occasionally other genres, which was pioneered by the London-based Irish band <a href="/wiki/The_Pogues" title="The Pogues">The Pogues</a> in the 1980s. It achieved some mainstream success in the 1980s and, particularly as the subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Celtic_punk" title="Celtic punk">Celtic punk</a>, has been widely adopted in areas of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_diaspora" title="Irish diaspora">Celtic diaspora</a> in North America and Australia and by many bands in continental central and eastern Europe. Unlike earlier <a href="/wiki/Celtic_rock" title="Celtic rock">Celtic rock</a> and electric folk groups, folk punk groups tend to include relatively little <a href="/wiki/Traditional_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional music">traditional music</a> in their repertoire, but instead usually performed their own compositions, often following the form of punk rock, using additional folk instrumentation, including, <a href="/wiki/Mandolin" title="Mandolin">mandolin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banjo" title="Banjo">banjo</a> and particularly violin.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005pp197-8_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005pp197-8-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Other bands adopted some traditional forms of music, including <a href="/wiki/Sea_shanties" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea shanties">sea shanties</a> and eastern European <a href="/wiki/Gypsy_style" title="Gypsy style">gypsy music</a>. Among the most successful performers were <a href="/wiki/The_Men_They_Couldn%27t_Hang" title="The Men They Couldn&#39;t Hang">The Men They Couldn't Hang</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Model_Army_(band)" title="New Model Army (band)">New Model Army</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oysterband" title="Oysterband">Oysterband</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Levellers_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Levellers (band)">The Levellers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Sweers2005_33-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweers2005-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Billy_Bragg" title="Billy Bragg">Billy Bragg</a>, who enjoyed a series of hits in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_rock_in_the_early_1980s">Electronic rock in the early 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Electronic rock in the early 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synth_rock">Synth rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Synth rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electronic_rock" title="Electronic rock">Electronic rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tierecke_dm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Tierecke_dm.jpg/220px-Tierecke_dm.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Tierecke_dm.jpg/330px-Tierecke_dm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Tierecke_dm.jpg/440px-Tierecke_dm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="962" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tierecke_dm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Depeche_Mode" title="Depeche Mode">Depeche Mode</a> in concert, 2006</div></div></div>
<p>Many progressive rock bands had incorporated synthesizers into their sound, including <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yes_(band)" title="Yes (band)">Yes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genesis_(band)" title="Genesis (band)">Genesis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> In 1977, <a href="/wiki/Ultravox" title="Ultravox">Ultravox</a> member <a href="/wiki/Warren_Cann" title="Warren Cann">Warren Cann</a> purchased a <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland</a> TR-77 drum machine, which was first featured in their October 1977 single release "Hiroshima Mon Amour".<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The ballad arrangement, metronome-like percussion and heavy use of the <a href="/wiki/ARP_Odyssey" title="ARP Odyssey">ARP Odyssey</a> synthesizer was effectively a prototype for nearly all <a href="/wiki/Synthpop" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthpop">synthpop</a> and rock bands that were to follow. In 1978, the first incarnation of <a href="/wiki/The_Human_League" title="The Human League">The Human League</a> released their début single "<a href="/wiki/Being_Boiled" title="Being Boiled">Being Boiled</a>". Others were soon to follow, including <a href="/wiki/Tubeway_Army" title="Tubeway Army">Tubeway Army</a>, a little known outfit from West London, who dropped their <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> image and jumped on the band wagon, topping the UK charts in the summer of 1979 with the single "<a href="/wiki/Are_Friends_Electric%3F" class="mw-redirect" title="Are Friends Electric?">Are Friends Electric?</a>". This prompted the singer, <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a> to go solo and in the same year he release the <a href="/wiki/Kraftwerk" title="Kraftwerk">Kraftwerk</a> inspired album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pleasure_Principle_(Gary_Numan_album)" title="The Pleasure Principle (Gary Numan album)">The Pleasure Principle</a></i> and again topped the charts for the second time with the single "<a href="/wiki/Cars_(song)" title="Cars (song)">Cars</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Particularly through its adoption by <a href="/wiki/New_Romantic" title="New Romantic">New Romantics</a>, synthesizers came to dominate the pop and rock music of the early 80s. Albums such as <a href="/wiki/Visage_(band)" title="Visage (band)">Visage</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Visage_(Visage_album)" title="Visage (Visage album)">Visage</a></i> (1980), <a href="/wiki/John_Foxx" title="John Foxx">John Foxx</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Metamatic" title="Metamatic">Metamatic</a></i> (1980), <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Telekon" title="Telekon">Telekon</a></i> (1980), <a href="/wiki/Ultravox" title="Ultravox">Ultravox</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Vienna_(album)" title="Vienna (album)">Vienna</a></i> (1980), <a href="/wiki/The_Human_League" title="The Human League">The Human League</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dare_(album)" title="Dare (album)">Dare</a></i> (1981) and <a href="/wiki/Depeche_Mode" title="Depeche Mode">Depeche Mode</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Speak_and_Spell_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Speak and Spell (album)">Speak and Spell</a></i> (1981), established a sound that influenced most mainstream pop and rock bands, until it began to fall from popularity in the mid-1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Romantics">New Romantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: New Romantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Romantic" title="New Romantic">New Romantic</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg/220px-Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg/330px-Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg/440px-Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg 2x" data-file-width="824" data-file-height="705" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boy_George_At_Ronnie_Scotts.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Boy_George" title="Boy George">Boy George</a>, lead singer of <a href="/wiki/Culture_Club" title="Culture Club">Culture Club</a>, performing at <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Scott%27s_Jazz_Club" title="Ronnie Scott&#39;s Jazz Club">Ronnie Scott's Jazz Club</a> in London</div></div></div>
<p>New Romantic emerged as part of the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave music</a> movement in London nightclubs including Billy's and The <a href="/wiki/Blitz_Kids" title="Blitz Kids">Blitz Club</a> towards the end of the 1970s. Influenced by <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music" title="Roxy Music">Roxy Music</a>, it developed <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">glam rock</a> fashions, gaining its name from the frilly <a href="/wiki/Fop" title="Fop">fop</a> <a href="/wiki/Poet_shirt" title="Poet shirt">shirts</a> of early <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>. New Romantic music often made extensive use of synthesizers. Pioneers included <a href="/wiki/Visage_(band)" title="Visage (band)">Visage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan_(band)" title="Japan (band)">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ultravox" title="Ultravox">Ultravox</a> and among the commercially most successful acts associated with the movement were <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_the_Ants" title="Adam and the Ants">Adam and the Ants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Culture_Club" title="Culture Club">Culture Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Human_League" title="The Human League">The Human League</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spandau_Ballet" title="Spandau Ballet">Spandau Ballet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Duran_Duran" title="Duran Duran">Duran Duran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NRTL_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRTL-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> By about 1983 the original movement had dissolved, with surviving acts dropping most of the fashion elements to pursue mainstream careers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_second_British_invasion">The second British invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: The second British invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Second_British_invasion" class="mw-redirect" title="Second British invasion">Second British invasion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg/220px-Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg/330px-Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg/440px-Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="523" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dire_straits_22101985_23_800.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a> Playing in Norway in October 1985</div></div></div>
<p>From its inception in 1981, the cable music channel MTV featured a disproportionate amount of music videos from image conscious British acts.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> British acts, who had been accustomed to using music videos for half a decade, featured heavily on the channel.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VVMTV_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VVMTV-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Buggles" title="The Buggles">The Buggles</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Video_Killed_the_Radio_Star" title="Video Killed the Radio Star">Video Killed the Radio Star</a>" was the <a href="/wiki/First_music_videos_aired_on_MTV" class="mw-redirect" title="First music videos aired on MTV">first music video shown on MTV</a>. In late 1982, "<a href="/wiki/I_Ran_(So_Far_Away)" title="I Ran (So Far Away)">I Ran (So Far Away)</a>" by <a href="/wiki/A_Flock_of_Seagulls" title="A Flock of Seagulls">A Flock of Seagulls</a> entered the Billboard Top Ten, arguably the first successful song that owed almost everything to video.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> They would be followed by bands like <a href="/wiki/Duran_Duran" title="Duran Duran">Duran Duran</a> whose glossy videos would come to symbolise the power of MTV.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Money_for_Nothing_(song)" title="Money for Nothing (song)">Money for Nothing</a>" gently poked fun at MTV which had helped make them international rock stars.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> In 1983, 30% of the record sales were from British acts. 18 of the Top 40 and 6 of the Top 10 singles on 18 July were by British artists. Overall record sales would rise by 10% from 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> <i>Newsweek</i> featured <a href="/wiki/Annie_Lennox" title="Annie Lennox">Annie Lennox</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eurythmics" title="Eurythmics">Eurythmics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boy_George" title="Boy George">Boy George</a> of <a href="/wiki/Culture_Club" title="Culture Club">Culture Club</a> on the cover of one of its issues, while <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> would release an "England Swings" issue.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> In April 1984, 40 of the Top 100 singles were from British acts while 8 of the Top 10 singles in a May 1985 survey were of British origin.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Veteran music journalist <a href="/wiki/Simon_Reynolds" title="Simon Reynolds">Simon Reynolds</a> theorised that similar to the first British Invasion the use of black American influences by the British acts helped to spur success.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Commentators in the mainstream media credited MTV and the British acts with bringing colour and energy back to pop music while rock journalists were generally hostile to the phenomenon because they felt it represented image over content.<sup id="cite_ref-Reynolds_64-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reynolds-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indie_rock">Indie rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Indie rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">Indie rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">Alternative rock</a></div>
<p>Indie or independent rock, particularly in America often known as alternative rock, was a scene that emerged from post-punk and new wave in the 1980s, eschewing the <a href="/wiki/Music_industry" title="Music industry">major record labels</a> for control of their own music and relying on local scenes or national sub-cultures to provide an audience. Having enjoyed some success a number of indie acts were able to move into the mainstream, including early indie bands <a href="/wiki/Aztec_Camera" title="Aztec Camera">Aztec Camera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_Juice_(band)" title="Orange Juice (band)">Orange Juice</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">The Smiths</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/The_Housemartins" title="The Housemartins">The Housemartins</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_(band)" title="James (band)">James</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Other forms of alternative rock developed in the UK during the 1980s. <a href="/wiki/The_Jesus_and_Mary_Chain" title="The Jesus and Mary Chain">The Jesus and Mary Chain</a> wrapped their pop melodies in walls of guitar noise, while <a href="/wiki/New_Order_(band)" title="New Order (band)">New Order</a> emerged from the demise of post-punk band <a href="/wiki/Joy_Division" title="Joy Division">Joy Division</a> and experimented with <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">house music</a>, forging the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_dance" title="Alternative dance">alternative dance</a> style. The Mary Chain, along with Dinosaur Jr and the <a href="/wiki/Dream_pop" title="Dream pop">dream pop</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cocteau_Twins" title="Cocteau Twins">Cocteau Twins</a>, were the influences for the <a href="/wiki/Shoegazing" title="Shoegazing">shoegazing</a> movement of the late 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gothic_rock">Gothic rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Gothic rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Gothic_rock" title="Gothic rock">Gothic rock</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg/220px-The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg/330px-The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg/440px-The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Cure_Live_in_Singapore_2-_1st_August_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">The Cure</a> on stage in 2007</div></div></div>
<p>Gothic rock, often shortened to goth, developed out of the post-punk scene in the later 1970s. It combines dark, often keyboard-heavy music with introspective and depressing lyrics. Notable early gothic rock bands include <a href="/wiki/Bauhaus_(band)" title="Bauhaus (band)">Bauhaus</a> (whose "<a href="/wiki/Bela_Lugosi%27s_Dead" title="Bela Lugosi&#39;s Dead">Bela Lugosi's Dead</a>" is often cited as the first goth record), <a href="/wiki/Siouxsie_and_the_Banshees" title="Siouxsie and the Banshees">Siouxsie and the Banshees</a> (who may have coined the term), <a href="/wiki/The_Cure" title="The Cure">The Cure</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Sisters_of_Mercy" title="The Sisters of Mercy">The Sisters of Mercy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fields_of_the_Nephilim" title="Fields of the Nephilim">Fields of the Nephilim</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Gothic rock gave rise to a broader <a href="/wiki/Goth_subculture" title="Goth subculture">goth subculture</a> that included clubs, <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fashion" title="Gothic fashion">various fashion trends</a> and numerous publications that grew in popularity in the 1980s, gaining notoriety by being associated by several <a href="/wiki/Moral_panic" title="Moral panic">moral panics</a> over suicide and Satanism.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Madchester">Madchester</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Madchester">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Madchester" title="Madchester">Madchester</a></div>
<p>The independent rock scene that had developed in Manchester in the second half of the 1980s, based in <a href="/wiki/The_Ha%C3%A7ienda" title="The Haçienda">The Haçienda</a> nightclub and <a href="/wiki/Factory_Records" title="Factory Records">Factory Records</a> and dubbed Madchester, came to national prominence at the end of the decade, with the <a href="/wiki/Happy_Mondays" title="Happy Mondays">Happy Mondays</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Inspiral_Carpets" title="Inspiral Carpets">Inspiral Carpets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Stone_Roses" title="The Stone Roses">Stone Roses</a> charting late in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The scene became the centre of media attention for independent rock in the early 1990s, with bands like <a href="/wiki/World_of_Twist" title="World of Twist">World of Twist</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Fast_Automatic_Daffodils" title="New Fast Automatic Daffodils">New Fast Automatic Daffodils</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_High" title="The High">The High</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northside_(band)" title="Northside (band)">Northside</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris_Angels" title="Paris Angels">Paris Angels</a>, and Intastella also gaining national attention.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The period of dominance was relatively short lived with The Stone Roses beginning to retreat from public performance while engaged in contractual disputes, the Happy Mondays having difficulty in producing a second album and <a href="/wiki/Factory_Records" title="Factory Records">Factory Records</a> going bankrupt in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Local bands catching the tail-end of Madchester, such as <a href="/wiki/The_Mock_Turtles" title="The Mock Turtles">The Mock Turtles</a>, became part of a wider <a href="/wiki/Baggy" title="Baggy">baggy</a> scene. The music press in the UK began to place more focus on <a href="/wiki/Shoegazing" title="Shoegazing">shoegazing</a> bands from the <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">south of England</a> and bands emerging through US <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dream_pop_and_shoegazing">Dream pop and shoegazing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Dream pop and shoegazing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Dream_pop" title="Dream pop">Dream pop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shoegazing" title="Shoegazing">Shoegazing</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg/220px-My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg/330px-My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg/440px-My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:My_Bloody_Valentine-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>My Bloody Valentine, 2008.</div></div></div>
<p>Dream pop had developed out of the indie rock scene of the 1980s, when bands like <a href="/wiki/Cocteau_Twins" title="Cocteau Twins">Cocteau Twins</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Chameleons" title="The Chameleons">The Chameleons</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Passions_(UK_band)" title="The Passions (UK band)">The Passions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dif_Juz" title="Dif Juz">Dif Juz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lowlife_(band)" title="Lowlife (band)">Lowlife</a> and <a href="/wiki/A.R._Kane" title="A.R. Kane">A.R. Kane</a> began fusing <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">post-punk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethereal_Wave" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethereal Wave">ethereal</a> experiments with bittersweet pop melodies into sensual, sonically ambitious soundscapes.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The 4AD record label is the one most associated with dream pop, though others such as <a href="/wiki/Creation_Records" title="Creation Records">Creation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Projekt_Records" title="Projekt Records">Projekt</a>, Fontana, <a href="/wiki/Bedazzled_Records" title="Bedazzled Records">Bedazzled</a>, Vernon Yard, and <a href="/wiki/Slumberland_Records" title="Slumberland Records">Slumberland</a> also released significant records in the genre. A louder, more aggressive strain of dream pop came to be known as <a href="/wiki/Shoegazing" title="Shoegazing">shoegazing</a>; key bands of this style were <a href="/wiki/Lush_(band)" title="Lush (band)">Lush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slowdive" title="Slowdive">Slowdive</a>, <a href="/wiki/My_Bloody_Valentine_(band)" title="My Bloody Valentine (band)">My Bloody Valentine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ride_(band)" title="Ride (band)">Ride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alison%27s_Halo" title="Alison&#39;s Halo">Alison's Halo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chapterhouse" title="Chapterhouse">Chapterhouse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Curve_(band)" title="Curve (band)">Curve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Levitation_(band)" title="Levitation (band)">Levitation</a>. These bands kept the atmospheric qualities of dream pop, but added the intensity of post-punk-influenced bands such as <a href="/wiki/The_Chameleons" title="The Chameleons">The Chameleons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sonic_Youth" title="Sonic Youth">Sonic Youth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-rock">Post-rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Post-rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post-rock" title="Post-rock">Post-rock</a></div>
<p>Post-rock originated in the release of <a href="/wiki/Talk_Talk" title="Talk Talk">Talk Talk</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Laughing_Stock_(album)" title="Laughing Stock (album)">Laughing Stock</a></i> and US band <a href="/wiki/Slint" title="Slint">Slint</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Spiderland" title="Spiderland">Spiderland</a></i>, both in 1991, which produced experimental work influenced by sources as varied as <a href="/wiki/Electronica" title="Electronica">electronica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, and minimalist classical music, often abandoning the traditional song format in favour of instrumental and ambient music.<sup id="cite_ref-AMpostrock_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMpostrock-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> The term was first used to describe the band <a href="/wiki/Bark_Psychosis" title="Bark Psychosis">Bark Psychosis</a> and their album <i><a href="/wiki/Hex_(album)" title="Hex (album)">Hex</a></i> (1994), but was soon employed for bands such as <a href="/wiki/Stereolab" title="Stereolab">Stereolab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laika_(band)" title="Laika (band)">Laika</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disco_Inferno_(band)" title="Disco Inferno (band)">Disco Inferno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pram_(band)" title="Pram (band)">Pram</a> and other acts in America and Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-AMpostrock_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMpostrock-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Scottish group <a href="/wiki/Mogwai" title="Mogwai">Mogwai</a> are one of the influential post-rock groups to arise at the turn of the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indie_pop">Indie pop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Indie pop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indie_pop" title="Indie pop">Indie pop</a></div>
<p>Initially dubbed as '<a href="/wiki/C86_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="C86 (music)">C86</a>' after the 1986 <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> tape, and also known as "cutie", "shambling bands" and later as "twee pop",<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> indie pop was characterised by jangling guitars, a love of sixties pop and often fey, innocent lyrics.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> It was also inspired by the DIY scene of punk and there was a thriving fanzine, label and club and gig circuit. Early bands included <a href="/wiki/The_Pastels" title="The Pastels">The Pastels</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Shop_Assistants" title="The Shop Assistants">The Shop Assistants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Primal_Scream" title="Primal Scream">Primal Scream</a>. Scenes later developed in the United States particularly around labels such as <a href="/wiki/K_Records" title="K Records">K Records</a>. Genres such as <a href="/wiki/Riot_Grrrl" class="mw-redirect" title="Riot Grrrl">Riot Grrrl</a> and bands as diverse as <a href="/wiki/Nirvana_(band)" title="Nirvana (band)">Nirvana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manic_Street_Preachers" title="Manic Street Preachers">Manic Street Preachers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Belle_and_Sebastian" title="Belle and Sebastian">Belle and Sebastian</a> have all acknowledged its influence.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Britpop">Britpop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Britpop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg/220px-Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg/330px-Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg/440px-Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="492" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oasis_Noel_and_Liam_WF.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Oasis_(band)" title="Oasis (band)">Oasis</a> performing in 2005.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Britpop" title="Britpop">Britpop</a></div>
<p>Britpop emerged from the British <a href="/wiki/Independent_music" title="Independent music">independent music</a> scene of the early 1990s and was characterised by bands influenced by British guitar pop music of the 1960s and 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The movement developed as a reaction against various musical and cultural trends in the late 1980s and early 1990s, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a> phenomenon from the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> New British groups such as <a href="/wiki/Suede_(band)" title="Suede (band)">Suede</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blur_(band)" title="Blur (band)">Blur</a> launched the movement by positioning themselves as opposing musical forces, referencing British guitar music of the past and writing about uniquely British topics and concerns. These bands were soon joined by others including <a href="/wiki/Oasis_(band)" title="Oasis (band)">Oasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulp_(band)" title="Pulp (band)">Pulp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supergrass" title="Supergrass">Supergrass</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Boo_Radleys" title="The Boo Radleys">The Boo Radleys</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kula_Shaker" title="Kula Shaker">Kula Shaker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ash_(band)" title="Ash (band)">Ash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Colour_Scene" title="Ocean Colour Scene">Ocean Colour Scene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elastica" title="Elastica">Elastica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Britpop groups brought British alternative rock into the mainstream and formed the backbone of a larger British cultural movement called <a href="/wiki/Cool_Britannia" title="Cool Britannia">Cool Britannia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Although its more popular bands were able to spread their commercial success overseas, especially to the United States, the movement largely fell apart by the end of the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-Allmusicrockindie_73-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Allmusicrockindie-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Britpop">Post-Britpop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Post-Britpop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post-Britpop" title="Post-Britpop">Post-Britpop</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thom_Yorke.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Thom_Yorke.jpg/170px-Thom_Yorke.jpg" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Thom_Yorke.jpg/255px-Thom_Yorke.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Thom_Yorke.jpg/340px-Thom_Yorke.jpg 2x" data-file-width="410" data-file-height="614" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thom_Yorke.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Thom_Yorke" title="Thom Yorke">Thom Yorke</a> of <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>From about 1997, as dissatisfaction grew with the concept of Cool Britannia, and Britpop as a movement began to dissolve, emerging bands began to avoid the Britpop label while still producing music derived from it.<sup id="cite_ref-Harris2004_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harris2004-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> Many of these bands tended to mix elements of British traditional rock (or British trad rock),<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> particularly the Beatles, Rolling Stones and <a href="/wiki/Small_Faces" title="Small Faces">Small Faces</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Patridis2004_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Patridis2004-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> with American influences, including post-grunge.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicTravis_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicTravis-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Placebo_(band)" title="Placebo (band)">Placebo</a> and Post-Britpop bands like <a href="/wiki/The_Verve" title="The Verve">The Verve</a>, <a href="/wiki/Travis_(band)" title="Travis (band)">Travis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stereophonics" title="Stereophonics">Stereophonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feeder_(band)" title="Feeder (band)">Feeder</a>, and particularly <a href="/wiki/Coldplay" title="Coldplay">Coldplay</a>, achieved much wider international success than most of the Britpop groups that had preceded them, and were some of the most commercially successful acts of the late 1990s and early 2000s.<sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicTravis_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicTravis-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicStereophonics_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicStereophonics-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AllMusicColdplay_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AllMusicColdplay-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Garage_rock_revival.2FPost-punk_revival"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Garage_rock_revival/Post-punk_revival">Garage rock revival/Post-punk revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Garage rock revival/Post-punk revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Garage_rock_revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Garage rock revival">Garage rock revival</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post-punk_revival" title="Post-punk revival">Post-punk revival</a></div>
<p>In the 2000s British indie rock experienced a resurgence. Like modern American <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a>, many British indie bands such as <a href="/wiki/Franz_Ferdinand_(band)" title="Franz Ferdinand (band)">Franz Ferdinand</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Libertines" title="The Libertines">The Libertines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bloc_Party" title="Bloc Party">Bloc Party</a> drew influences from post-punk groups such as <a href="/wiki/Joy_Division" title="Joy Division">Joy Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wire_(band)" title="Wire (band)">Wire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gang_of_Four_(band)" title="Gang of Four (band)">Gang of Four</a>. Other prominent independent rock bands in the 2000s include: <a href="/wiki/Editors_(band)" title="Editors (band)">Editors</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Fratellis" title="The Fratellis">The Fratellis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lostprophets" title="Lostprophets">Lostprophets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Razorlight" title="Razorlight">Razorlight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keane_(band)" title="Keane (band)">Keane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kaiser_Chiefs" title="Kaiser Chiefs">Kaiser Chiefs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">Muse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kasabian" title="Kasabian">Kasabian</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Cribs" title="The Cribs">The Cribs</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Maccabees_(band)" title="The Maccabees (band)">The Maccabees</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Kooks" title="The Kooks">The Kooks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Monkeys" title="Arctic Monkeys">Arctic Monkeys</a><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> (the last being the most prominent act to gain their initial fan base through the use of <a href="/wiki/Internet_social_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet social network">internet social networking</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_rave">New rave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_rock_music&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: New rave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_rave" title="New rave">New rave</a></div>
<p>With developments in computer technology and <a href="/wiki/Music_software" title="Music software">music software</a> advanced, it became possible to create high quality music using little more than a single laptop computer.<sup id="cite_ref-S._Emmerson,_2007_pp._80-1_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S._Emmerson,_2007_pp._80-1-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> This resulted in a massive increase in the amount of home-produced electronic music available to the general public via the expanding internet,<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> and new forms of performance such as <a href="/wiki/Laptronica" class="mw-redirect" title="Laptronica">laptronica</a><sup id="cite_ref-S._Emmerson,_2007_pp._80-1_92-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S._Emmerson,_2007_pp._80-1-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Live_coding" title="Live coding">live coding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> In Britain the combination of indie with American pioneered <a href="/wiki/Dance-punk" title="Dance-punk">dance-punk</a> was dubbed <a href="/wiki/New_rave" title="New rave">new rave</a> in publicity for <a href="/wiki/Klaxons" title="Klaxons">Klaxons</a> and the term was picked up and applied by the <a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a> to a number of bands,<sup id="cite_ref-rousing_rave_from_the_grave_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rousing_rave_from_the_grave-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Trash_Fashion" title="Trash Fashion">Trash Fashion</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Here_we_glo_again_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Here_we_glo_again-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/New_Young_Pony_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="New Young Pony Club">New Young Pony Club</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-new_rave?_old_rubbish_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_rave?_old_rubbish-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hadouken!" title="Hadouken!">Hadouken!</a>, <a href="/wiki/Late_of_the_Pier" title="Late of the Pier">Late of the Pier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Test_Icicles" title="Test Icicles">Test Icicles</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Rave_on,_just_don&#39;t_call_it_&#39;new_rave&#39;_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rave_on,_just_don&#39;t_call_it_&#39;new_rave&#39;-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Shitdisco" title="Shitdisco">Shitdisco</a><sup id="cite_ref-rousing_rave_from_the_grave_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rousing_rave_from_the_grave-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> forming a scene with a similar visual aesthetic to earlier <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">raves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rousing_rave_from_the_grave_95-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rousing_rave_from_the_grave-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-the_future&#39;s_bright..._99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-the_future&#39;s_bright...-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
