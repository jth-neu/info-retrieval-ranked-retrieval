<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Indian subcontinent</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>Indian subcontinent</b> is a southern <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">region</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, mostly situated on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Plate" title="Indian Plate">Indian Plate</a> and projecting southwards into the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geologically</a>, the Indian subcontinent is related to the land mass that rifted from <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> and merged with the <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_Plate" title="Eurasian Plate">Eurasian plate</a> nearly 55 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones2011p267_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2011p267-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">Geographically</a>, it is the <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsular</a> region in south-central Asia delineated by the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> in the north, the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a> in the west, and the <a href="/wiki/Arakan_Mountains" title="Arakan Mountains">Arakanese</a> in the east.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapman,_Graham_P._p._10_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman,_Graham_P._p._10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Politically, the Indian subcontinent includes <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dkumar889_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dkumar889-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pirbhai14_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pirbhai14-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sometimes, the geographical term 'Indian subcontinent' is used interchangeably with '<a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>',<sup id="cite_ref-mcleodplus_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcleodplus-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> although that last term is used typically as a political term and is also used to include <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> as well.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Which countries should be included in either of these remains the subject of debate.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson5_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson5-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mmann_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mmann-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jona_Razzaque_2004_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jona_Razzaque_2004-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_subcontinent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>, the term "subcontinent" signifies a "subdivision of a continent which has a distinct geographical, political, or cultural identity" and also a "large land mass somewhat smaller than a continent". It is first attested in 1845 to refer to the <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South Americas</a>, before they were regarded as separate continents. Its use to refer to the Indian subcontinent is seen from the early twentieth century. It was especially convenient for referring to the region comprising both <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Princely_state" title="Princely state">princely states</a> under <a href="/wiki/British_Paramountcy" class="mw-redirect" title="British Paramountcy">British Paramountcy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term Indian subcontinent also has a geological significance. Similar to various continents, it was a part of the supercontinent of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>. A series of tectonic splits caused formation of various basins, each drifting in various directions. The geological region called "Greater India" once included <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and Austrolasia along with the Indian subcontinent basin. As a geological term, Indian subcontinent has meant that region formed from the collision of the Indian basin with Eurasia nearly 55 million years ago, towards the end of <a href="/wiki/Paleocene" title="Paleocene">Paleocene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones2011p267_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2011p267-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hinsbergen1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hinsbergen1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Indian subcontinent has been a term particularly common in the British Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-mcleodplus_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcleodplus-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2018)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The geographical region has historically simply been known as "India" (in antiquity referring to the <a href="/wiki/Indus_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Indus Valley">Indus Valley</a> region, not the entire subcontinent).  Other related terms are <a href="/wiki/Greater_India" title="Greater India">Greater India</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mittal_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mittal-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kathleen_M_page_10_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kathleen_M_page_10-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  
And the terms "Indian subcontinent" and "South Asia" are sometimes used interchangeably.<sup id="cite_ref-mcleodplus_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcleodplus-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There is no globally accepted definition on which countries are a part of South Asia or the Indian subcontinent.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson5_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson5-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jona_Razzaque_2004_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jona_Razzaque_2004-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mmann_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mmann-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
The less common term "South Asian subcontinent" has seen occasional use since the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_subcontinent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_subcontinent_(orthographic_projection).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Indian_subcontinent_%28orthographic_projection%29.png/220px-Indian_subcontinent_%28orthographic_projection%29.png" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Indian_subcontinent_%28orthographic_projection%29.png/330px-Indian_subcontinent_%28orthographic_projection%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Indian_subcontinent_%28orthographic_projection%29.png/440px-Indian_subcontinent_%28orthographic_projection%29.png 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indian_subcontinent_(orthographic_projection).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Orthographic projection of the Indian subcontinent</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_subcontinent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geologically, the Indian subcontinent was first a part of so-called "Greater India",<sup id="cite_ref-hinsbergen1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hinsbergen1-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> a region of <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> that drifted away from <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a> about 160 million years ago, around the Middle Jurassic period.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones2011p267_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2011p267-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The region experienced high volcanic activity and plate subdivisions, creating Madagascar, Seychelles, Antarctica, Austrolasia and the Indian subcontinent basin. The Indian subcontinent drifted northeastwards, colliding with the Eurasian plate nearly 55 million years ago, towards the end of Paleocene. This geological region largely includes Bangladesh, Bhutan, India, Maldives, Nepal, Pakistan and Sri Lanka.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones2011p267_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones2011p267-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The zone where the Eurasian and Indian subcontinent plates meet remains one of the geologically active areas, prone to major earthquakes.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English term "subcontinent" mainly continues to refer to the Indian subcontinent.<sup id="cite_ref-McLeod_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLeod-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">Physiographically</a>, it is a <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsular</a> region in south-central Asia delineated by the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a> in the north, the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a> in the west, and the <a href="/wiki/Arakan_Mountains" title="Arakan Mountains">Arakanese</a> in the east.<sup id="cite_ref-Chapman,_Graham_P._p._10_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chapman,_Graham_P._p._10-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It extends southward into the Indian Ocean with the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a> to the southwest and the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> to the southeast.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-McLeod_p1_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLeod_p1-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Most of this region rests on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Plate" title="Indian Plate">Indian Plate</a> and is isolated from the rest of Asia by large mountain barriers.<sup id="cite_ref-EB_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EB-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Using the more expansive definition – counting India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Nepal, Bhutan and Maldives as the constituent countries – the Indian subcontinent covers about 4.4 million km<sup>2</sup> (1.7 million sq&#160;mi), which is 10% of the Asian continent or 3.3% of the world's land surface area.<sup id="cite_ref-JJCO_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJCO-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Overall, it accounts for about 45% of Asia's population (or over 25% of the world's population) and is home to a vast array of peoples.<sup id="cite_ref-JJCO_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JJCO-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Britannica_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britannica-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socio-cultural_sphere">Socio-cultural sphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_subcontinent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Socio-cultural sphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Buddhist_Expansion.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Buddhist_Expansion.svg/300px-Buddhist_Expansion.svg.png" width="300" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Buddhist_Expansion.svg/450px-Buddhist_Expansion.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Buddhist_Expansion.svg/600px-Buddhist_Expansion.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="474" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Buddhist_Expansion.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historical transmission routes of Buddhism from India to Central Asia, East Asia and Southeast Asia.</div></div></div>

<p>The Indian subcontinent is a natural physical landmass in South Asia, geologically the dry-land portion of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Plate" title="Indian Plate">Indian Plate</a>, which  has been relatively isolated from the rest of Eurasia.<sup id="cite_ref-AsherTalbot2006_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AsherTalbot2006-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> 
Given the difficulty of passage through the Himalayas, the sociocultural, religious and political interaction of the Indian subcontinent has largely been through the valleys of Afghanistan in its northwest,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> the valleys of <a href="/wiki/Manipur" title="Manipur">Manipur</a> in its east, and by maritime routes.<sup id="cite_ref-AsherTalbot2006_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AsherTalbot2006-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> More difficult but historically important interaction has also occurred through passages pioneered by the <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_people" title="Tibetan people">Tibetans</a>. These routes and interactions have led to the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road_transmission_of_Buddhism" title="Silk Road transmission of Buddhism">spread of Buddhism</a>  out of the Indian subcontinent into other parts of Asia. And the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_conquests_in_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic conquests in India">Islamic expansion</a> arrived into the Indian subcontinent in two ways, through Afghanistan on land and to Indian coast through the maritime routes on the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AsherTalbot2006_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AsherTalbot2006-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whether called the Indian subcontinent or South Asia, the definition of the geographical extent of this region varies. <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">Geopolitically</a>, it had formed the whole territory of <a href="/wiki/Greater_India" title="Greater India">Greater India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mittal_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mittal-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kathleen_M_page_10_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kathleen_M_page_10-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  
In terms of modern  geopolitical boundaries, the Indian subcontinent comprises the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of India">Republic of India</a>,
<a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, besides, by convention, the island nation of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and other islands of the Indian Ocean,<sup id="cite_ref-dkumar889_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dkumar889-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> such as the <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pirbhai14_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pirbhai14-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
The term "Indian continent" is first introduced in the early 20th century, when most of the territory was  part of <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Kush" title="Hindu Kush">Hindu Kush</a>, centered on eastern  <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, is the boundary connecting the Indian subcontinent with <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> to the northwest, and the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Plateau" class="mw-redirect" title="Persian Plateau">Persian Plateau</a> to the west. The socio-religious history of Afghanistan are related to the Turkish-influenced <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a> and northwestern parts of the Indian subcontinent, now known as Pakistan.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Others state Afghanistan being a part of Central Asia is not an accepted practice, and it is "clearly not part of the Indian subcontinent".<sup id="cite_ref-anderson5_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson5-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The precise definition of an "Indian subcontinent" vs. "South Asia" in geopolitical context is somewhat contested.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson5_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson5-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jona_Razzaque_2004_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jona_Razzaque_2004-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
