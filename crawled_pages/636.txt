<p>The <b>British Film Institute</b> (<b>BFI</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charitable_organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Charitable organisation">charitable organisation</a> which promotes and preserves <a href="/wiki/Filmmaking" title="Filmmaking">filmmaking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> in the United Kingdom.
</p><p>It was established by <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a> to:
</p>
<dl><dd>Encourage the development of the arts of film, television and the moving image throughout the United Kingdom, to promote their use as a record of contemporary life and manners, to promote education about film, television and the moving image generally, and their impact on society, to promote access to and appreciation of the widest possible range of British and <a href="/wiki/World_cinema" title="World cinema">world cinema</a> and to establish, care for and develop collections reflecting the moving image history and heritage of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BFI_activities">BFI activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: BFI activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Archive">Archive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Archive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BFI maintains the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Film_archive" class="mw-redirect" title="Film archive">film archive</a>, the <a href="/wiki/BFI_National_Archive" title="BFI National Archive">BFI National Archive</a>, previously called <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="National Film Library">National Film Library</a> (1935–1955), National Film Archive (1955–1992), and National Film and Television Archive (1993–2006). The archive contains more than 50,000 fiction films, over 100,000 non-fiction titles, and around 625,000 television programmes. The majority of the collection is British material but it also features internationally significant holdings from around the world. The Archive also collects films which feature key British actors and the work of British directors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinemas">Cinemas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cinemas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg/220px-BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg/330px-BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg/440px-BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BFI_London_IMAX_at_night.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/London_IMAX" class="mw-redirect" title="London IMAX">London IMAX</a> cinema</div></div></div>
<p>The BFI runs the <a href="/wiki/BFI_Southbank" title="BFI Southbank">BFI Southbank</a> (formerly the National Film Theatre (NFT)) and <a href="/wiki/London_IMAX" class="mw-redirect" title="London IMAX">London IMAX</a> cinema, both located on the south bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The IMAX has the largest cinema screen in the UK and shows popular recent releases and short films showcasing its technology, which includes 3D screenings and 11,600 watts of digital surround sound.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> BFI Southbank (the National Film Theatre screens and the Studio) shows films from all over the world, particularly critically acclaimed historical &amp; specialised films that may not otherwise get a cinema showing. The BFI also distributes archival and cultural cinema to other venues – each year to more than 800 venues all across the UK, as well as to a substantial number of overseas venues.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BFI offers a range of education initiatives, in particular to support the teaching of film and media studies in schools.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In late 2012, the BFI received money from the <a href="/wiki/Department_For_Education" class="mw-redirect" title="Department For Education">Department For Education</a> to create the BFI Film Academy Network.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BFI runs the annual <a href="/wiki/London_Film_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="London Film Festival">London Film Festival</a> along with <a href="/wiki/BFI_Flare:_London_LGBT_Film_Festival" title="BFI Flare: London LGBT Film Festival">BFI Flare: London LGBT Film Festival</a> and the youth-orientated Future Film Festival.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_activities">Other activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BFI publishes the monthly <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> magazine as well as films on <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a>, <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> and books. It runs the BFI National Library (a reference library), and maintains the <a href="/wiki/BFI_Film_%26_TV_Database" title="BFI Film &amp; TV Database">BFI Film &amp; TV Database</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Summary_of_Information_on_Film_and_Television&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Summary of Information on Film and Television (page does not exist)">Summary of Information on Film and Television</a> (SIFT), which are databases of credits, synopses and other information about film and television productions. SIFT has a collection of about 7 million <a href="/wiki/Still_frame" class="mw-redirect" title="Still frame">still frames</a> from film and television.
</p><p>The BFI has co-produced a number of television series featuring footage from the BFI National Archive, in partnership with the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_of_Mitchell_%26_Kenyon" title="The Lost World of Mitchell &amp; Kenyon">The Lost World of Mitchell &amp; Kenyon</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_of_Friese-Greene" title="The Lost World of Friese-Greene">The Lost World of Friese-Greene</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_of_Tibet" title="The Lost World of Tibet">The Lost World of Tibet</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NFT.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/NFT.jpg/220px-NFT.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/NFT.jpg/330px-NFT.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/NFT.jpg/440px-NFT.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="531" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NFT.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/BFI_Southbank" title="BFI Southbank">National Film Theatre</a></div></div></div>
<p>The institute was founded in 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Despite its foundation resulting from a recommendation in a report on <i>Film in National Life</i>, at that time the institute was a private company, though it has received public money throughout its history—from the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a> and Treasury until 1965 and the various culture departments since then.
</p><p>The institute was restructured following the Radcliffe Report of 1948 which recommended that it should concentrate on developing the appreciation of filmic art, rather than creating film itself. Thus control of educational film production passed to the National Committee for Visual Aids in Education and the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">British Film Academy</a> assumed control for promoting production. From 1952–2000, the BFI provided funding for new and experimental filmmakers via the <a href="/wiki/BFI_Production_Board" title="BFI Production Board">BFI Production Board</a>.
</p><p>The institute received a Royal Charter in 1983. This was updated in 2000, and in the same year the newly established <a href="/wiki/UK_Film_Council" title="UK Film Council">UK Film Council</a> took responsibility for providing the BFI's annual grant-in-aid (government subsidy). As an independent registered charity, the BFI is regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Charity_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Charity Commission">Charity Commission</a> and the Privy Council.
</p><p>In 1988, the BFI opened the London <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_the_Moving_Image_(London)" title="Museum of the Moving Image (London)">Museum of the Moving Image</a> (MOMI) on the <a href="/wiki/South_Bank" title="South Bank">South Bank</a>. MOMI was acclaimed internationally and set new standards for education through entertainment, but subsequently it did not receive the high levels of continuing investment that might have enabled it to keep pace with technological developments and ever-rising audience expectations.  The Museum was "temporarily" closed in 1999 when the BFI stated that it would be re-sited. This did not happen, and MOMI's closure became permanent in 2002 when it was decided to redevelop the South Bank site. This redevelopment was itself then further delayed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Film_Institute&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BFI is currently managed on a day-to-day basis by its chief executive, Amanda Nevill. Supreme decision-making authority rests with a chair and a board of up to 14 governors. The current chair is Josh Berger, who took up the post in February 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He succeeded <a href="/wiki/Greg_Dyke" title="Greg Dyke">Greg Dyke</a>, who took office on 1 March 2008. Dyke succeeded the late <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Minghella" title="Anthony Minghella">Anthony Minghella</a> (film director), who was chair from 2003 until 31 December 2007. The chair of the board is appointed by the BFI's own Board of Governors but requires the consent of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Culture,_Media_and_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport">Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport</a>. Other Governors are co-opted by existing board members when required (but if one of these is appointed Deputy Chair, that appointment is subject to ratification by the Secretary of State).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The BFI operates with three sources of income. The largest is public money allocated by the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Culture,_Media_and_Sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Culture, Media and Sport">Department for Culture, Media and Sport</a>. In 2011–12, this funding amounted to approximately £20m.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The second largest source is commercial activity such as receipts from ticket sales at BFI Southbank or the BFI London IMAX theatre (£5m in 2007), sales of DVDs, etc. Thirdly, grants and sponsorship of around £5m are obtained from various sources, including <a href="/wiki/National_Lottery_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Lottery (United Kingdom)">National Lottery</a> funding grants, private sponsors and through donations (<a href="/wiki/Paul_Getty" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Getty">J. Paul Getty, Jr.</a> donated around £1m in his will following his death in 2003). The BFI is also the distributor for all Lottery funds for film (in 2011–12 this amounted to c.£25m).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As well as its work on film, the BFI also devotes a large amount of its time to the preservation and study of British <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> programming and its history. In 2000, it published a high-profile list of the <a href="/wiki/100_Greatest_British_Television_Programmes" class="mw-redirect" title="100 Greatest British Television Programmes">100 Greatest British Television Programmes</a>, as voted for by a range of industry figures.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The delayed redevelopment of the National Film Theatre finally took place in 2007, creating in the rebranded "BFI Southbank" new education spaces, a gallery, and a pioneering mediatheque which for the first time enabled the public to gain access, free of charge, to some of the otherwise inaccessible treasures in the National Film &amp; Television Archive. The mediatheque has proved to be the most successful element of this redevelopment, and there are plans to roll out a network of them across the UK.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>An announcement of a £25 million capital investment in the Strategy for UK Screen Heritage was made by Secretary of State for Culture Media and Sport at the opening night of the 2007 London Film Festival. The bulk of this money paid for long overdue development of the BFI National Archive facilities in Hertfordshire and Warwickshire.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>During 2009 the <a href="/wiki/UK_Film_Council" title="UK Film Council">UK Film Council</a> persuaded the government that there should only be one main public-funded body for film, and that body should be the UKFC while the BFI should be abolished. During 2010 the government announced that there would be a single body for film. Despite intensive lobbying (including controversially using public funding to pay public relations agencies to put its case) the UKFC failed to persuade the government that it should have that role and, instead, the BFI took over most of the UKFC's functions and funding from 1 April 2011, and the UKFC was subsequently abolished. Since then, the BFI has been responsible for all Lottery funding for film—originally in excess of £25m p.a., and currently in excess of £40m p.a.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The BFI Film Academy forms part of the BFI's overall 5-19 Education Scheme. The programme is being supported by the Department for Education in England who have committed £1m per annum funding from April 2012 and 31 March 2015. It is also funded through the <a href="/wiki/National_Lottery_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Lottery (United Kingdom)">National Lottery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creative_Scotland" title="Creative Scotland">Creative Scotland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Screen" title="Northern Ireland Screen">Northern Ireland Screen</a>.
</p><p>On 29 November 2016 BFI announced that over 100,000 television programmes are to be digitised before the video tapes, which currently have an estimated five-to-six-year shelf life, become unusable. BFI aim to make sure that the television archive is still there in 200 years' time.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
