<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">WHOIS</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Whois&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Whois">Whois</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about a method for checking information about ownership of a <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a>. For an <a href="/wiki/IRC" class="mw-redirect" title="IRC">IRC</a> command called WHOIS, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Internet_Relay_Chat_commands#WHOIS" title="List of Internet Relay Chat commands">List of Internet Relay Chat commands §&#160;WHOIS</a>.</div>
<p><b>WHOIS</b> (pronounced as the phrase "who is") is a query and response <a href="/wiki/Communications_protocol" class="mw-redirect" title="Communications protocol">protocol</a> that is widely used for querying <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">databases</a> that store the registered users or assignees of an <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> resource, such as a <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a>, an <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a> block or an <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_system_(Internet)" title="Autonomous system (Internet)">autonomous system</a>, but is also used for a wider range of other information. The protocol stores and delivers database content in a human-readable format.<sup id="cite_ref-rfc3912_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rfc3912-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The WHOIS protocol is documented in <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc3912">RFC 3912</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>When the Internet was emerging out of the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Research_Project_Agency_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Research Project Agency Network">ARPANET</a>, there was only one organization that handled all domain registrations, which was <a href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">DARPA</a> itself. The process of registration was established in RFC 920. WHOIS was standardized in the early 1980s to look up domains, people and other resources related to domain and number registrations. As all registration was done by one organization at that time, one centralized server was used for WHOIS queries. This made looking up such information very easy.
</p><p>Responsibility of domain registration remained with DARPA as the ARPANET became the Internet during the 1980s. <a href="/wiki/UUNET" title="UUNET">UUNET</a> began offering domain registration service; however they simply handled the paperwork which they forwarded to the DARPA Network Information Center (NIC). Then the <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> directed that management of Internet domain registration would be handled by commercial, third-party entities. <a href="/wiki/InterNIC" title="InterNIC">InterNIC</a> was formed in 1993 under contract with the NSF, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Network_Solutions" title="Network Solutions">Network Solutions, Inc.</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_Atomics" title="General Atomics">General Atomics</a> and <a href="/wiki/AT%26T" title="AT&amp;T">AT&amp;T</a>. The General Atomics contract was canceled after several years due to performance issues.
</p><p>20th century WHOIS servers were highly permissive and would allow wild-card searches. A WHOIS query of a person's last name would yield all individuals with that name. A query with a given keyword returned all registered domains containing that keyword. A query for a given administrative contact returned all domains the administrator was associated with. Since the advent of the commercialized Internet, <a href="/wiki/Domain_Registrars" class="mw-redirect" title="Domain Registrars">multiple registrars</a> and unethical spammers, such permissive searching is no longer available.
</p><p>On December 1, 1999, management of the <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domains</a> (TLDs) <tt>com</tt>, <tt>net</tt>, and <tt>org</tt> was assigned to <a href="/wiki/Internet_Corporation_for_Assigned_Names_and_Numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers">ICANN</a>. At the time, these TLDs were converted to a thin WHOIS model. Existing WHOIS clients stopped working at that time. A month later, it had self-detecting <a href="/wiki/Common_Gateway_Interface" title="Common Gateway Interface">Common Gateway Interface</a> support so that the same program could operate a web-based WHOIS lookup, and an external TLD table to support multiple WHOIS servers based on the TLD of the request. This eventually became the model of the modern WHOIS client.
</p><p>By 2005, there were many more generic top-level domains than there had been in the early 1980s. There are also many more country-code top-level domains. This has led to a complex network of <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_registrar" title="Domain name registrar">domain name registrars</a> and registrar associations, especially as the management of Internet infrastructure has become more internationalized. As such, performing a WHOIS query on a domain requires knowing the correct, authoritative WHOIS server to use. Tools to do WHOIS proxy searches have become common.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CRISP_and_IRIS">CRISP and IRIS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CRISP and IRIS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="WEIRDS_and_RDAP">WEIRDS and RDAP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: WEIRDS and RDAP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Registration_Data_Access_Protocol" title="Registration Data Access Protocol">Registration Data Access Protocol</a></div>
<p>In 2013, the IETF acknowledged that IRIS had not been a successful replacement for WHOIS. The primary technical reason for that appeared to be the complexity of IRIS. Further, non-technical reasons were deemed to lie in areas upon which the IETF does not pass judgment. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protocol">Protocol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protocol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The WHOIS protocol had its origin in the ARPANET <i>NICNAME protocol</i> and was based on the <a href="/wiki/Finger_protocol" title="Finger protocol"><i>NAME/FINGER Protocol</i></a>, described in RFC 742 (1977). The NICNAME/WHOIS protocol was first described in RFC 812 in 1982 by Ken Harrenstien and Vic White of the Network Information Center at <a href="/wiki/SRI_International" title="SRI International">SRI International</a>.
</p><p>WHOIS was originally implemented on the <a href="/wiki/Network_Control_Program" title="Network Control Program">Network Control Program</a> (NCP) but found its major use when the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Protocol_Suite" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Protocol Suite">TCP/IP</a> suite was standardized across the ARPANET and later the Internet.
</p><p>The protocol specification is the following (original quote):<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><pre>Connect to the service host
   TCP: service port 43 decimal
   NCP: ICP to socket 43 decimal, establishing two 8-bit connections
Send a single "command line", ending with &lt;CRLF&gt;.
Receive information in response to the command line.  The
server closes its connections as soon as the output is
finished.</pre></blockquote>
<p>The <i>command line</i> server query is normally a single name specification. i.e. the name of a resource. However, servers accept a query, consisting of only the question mark (?) to return a description of acceptable command line formats. Substitution or <i>wild-card</i> formats also exist, e.g., appending a <a href="/wiki/Full-stop" class="mw-redirect" title="Full-stop">full-stop</a> (period) to the query name returns all entries beginning with the query name.
</p><p>On the modern Internet, WHOIS services are typically communicated using the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">Transmission Control Protocol</a> (TCP). Servers listen to requests on the well-known <a href="/wiki/Port_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Port number">port number</a> 43. Clients are simple applications that establish a communications channel to the server, transmit a text record with the name of the resource to be queried and await the response in form of a sequence of text records found in the database. This simplicity of the protocol also permits an application, and a command line interface user, to query a WHOIS server using the <a href="/wiki/Telnet" title="Telnet">Telnet</a> protocol.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WHOIS lookups were traditionally performed with a <a href="/wiki/Command_line_interface" class="mw-redirect" title="Command line interface">command line interface</a> application, but now many alternative web-based tools exist. WHOIS has a sister protocol called <i>Referral Whois</i> (<a href="/wiki/RWhois" class="mw-redirect" title="RWhois">RWhois</a>).
</p><p>A WHOIS database consists of a set of text records for each resource. These text records consists of various items of information about the resource itself, and any associated information of assignees, registrants, administrative information, such as creation and expiration dates.
</p><p>Two data models exist for storing resource information in a WHOIS database, the <i>thick</i> and the <i>thin</i> model.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thin_and_thick_lookups">Thin and thick lookups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Thin and thick lookups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>WHOIS information can be stored and looked up according to either a <i>thick</i> or a <i>thin</i> data model:
</p>
<dl><dt>Thick</dt>
<dd>A Thick WHOIS server stores the complete WHOIS information from all the registrars for the particular set of data (so that one WHOIS server can respond with WHOIS information on all <a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">.org</a> domains, for example).</dd>
<dt>Thin</dt>
<dd>A Thin WHOIS server stores only the name of the WHOIS server of the registrar of a domain, which in turn has the full details on the data being looked up (such as the <a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">.com</a> WHOIS servers, which refer the WHOIS query to the registrar where the domain was registered).</dd></dl>
<p>The thick model usually ensures consistent data and slightly faster queries, since only one WHOIS server needs to be contacted. If a registrar goes out of business, a thick registry contains all important information (if the registrant entered correct data, and privacy features were not used to obscure the data) and registration information can be retained. But with a thin registry, the contact information might not be available, and it could be difficult for the rightful registrant to retain control of the domain.<sup id="cite_ref-circleid.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-circleid.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>If a WHOIS client did not understand how to deal with this situation, it would display the full information from the registrar. Unfortunately, the WHOIS protocol has no standard for determining how to distinguish the thin model from the thick model.
</p><p>Specific details of which records are stored vary among <a href="/wiki/Domain_name_registry" title="Domain name registry">domain name registries</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domains</a>, including <tt><a href="/wiki/.com" title=".com">com</a></tt> and <tt><a href="/wiki/.net" title=".net">net</a></tt>, operate a thin WHOIS, requiring domain registrars to maintain their own customers' data. The other global top-level registries, including <tt><a href="/wiki/.org" title=".org">org</a></tt>, operate a thick model.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Each country-code top-level registry has its own national rules.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software">Software</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Software">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first applications written for the WHOIS information system were <a href="/wiki/Command_line_interface" class="mw-redirect" title="Command line interface">command line interface</a> tools for <a href="/wiki/Unix" title="Unix">Unix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unix-like" title="Unix-like">Unix-like</a> operating systems (i.e. Solaris, Linux etc.). WHOIS client and server software is distributed as free <a href="/wiki/Open-source_software" title="Open-source software">open-source software</a> and binary distributions are included with all <a href="/wiki/Unix-like" title="Unix-like">Unix-like</a> systems. Various commercial Unix implementations may use a proprietary implementation (for example, <a href="/wiki/Solaris_(operating_system)" title="Solaris (operating system)">Solaris</a> 7).
</p><p>A WHOIS command line client passes a phrase given as an argument directly to the WHOIS server. Various free open source examples can still be found on sites such as sourceforge.net. However, most modern WHOIS tools implement command line flags or options, such as the -h option to access a specific server host, but default servers are preconfigured. Additional options may allow control of the <a href="/wiki/Port_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Port number">port number</a> to connect on, displaying additional debugging data, or changing recursion/referral behavior.
</p><p>Like most TCP/IP <a href="/wiki/Client-server" class="mw-redirect" title="Client-server">client-server</a> applications, a WHOIS client takes the user input and then opens an <a href="/wiki/Internet_socket" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet socket">Internet socket</a> to its destination server. The WHOIS protocol manages the transmission of the query and reception of results.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Web">Web</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Web">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the advent of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a> and especially the loosening up of the <a href="/wiki/Network_Solutions" title="Network Solutions">Network Solutions</a> monopoly, looking up WHOIS information via the web has become quite common. At present, popular web-based WHOIS-queries may be conducted from <a href="/wiki/ARIN" class="mw-redirect" title="ARIN">ARIN</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/RIPE" title="RIPE">RIPE</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/APNIC" class="mw-redirect" title="APNIC">APNIC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Most early web-based WHOIS clients were merely front-ends to a command-line client, where the resulting output just gets displayed on a web page with little, if any, clean-up or formatting.
</p><p>Currently, web based WHOIS clients usually perform the WHOIS queries directly and then format the results for display. Many such clients are proprietary, authored by domain name registrars.
</p><p>The need for web-based clients came from the fact that command-line WHOIS clients largely existed only in the Unix and large computing worlds. <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> and Macintosh computers had no WHOIS clients installed by default, so registrars had to find a way to provide access to WHOIS data for potential customers. Many end-users still rely on such clients, even though command line and graphical clients exist now for most home PC platforms. Microsoft provides the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://technet.microsoft.com/en-us/sysinternals/bb842062">Sysinternals Suite</a> that includes a whois client at no cost.
</p><p>There are also many sites not owned by registrars or Internet-related companies. These support most of main <a href="/wiki/TLD" class="mw-redirect" title="TLD">TLD</a> and remain free. But most of web-based whois sites are incomplete and do not support all TLD nor IP search.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/CPAN" title="CPAN">CPAN</a> has several <a href="/wiki/Perl" title="Perl">Perl</a> modules available that work with WHOIS servers. Many of them are not current and do not fully function with the current (2005) WHOIS server infrastructure. However, there is still much useful functionality to derive including looking up <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_System_(Internet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous System (Internet)">AS numbers</a> and registrant contacts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Servers">Servers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Servers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_Internet_registries">Regional Internet registries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regional Internet registries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg/220px-Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg.png" width="220" height="97" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg/330px-Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg/440px-Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="940" data-file-height="415" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Regional_Internet_Registries_world_map.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Regional Internet registries</div></div></div>
<p>WHOIS servers operated by <a href="/wiki/Regional_Internet_registries" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Internet registries">regional Internet registries</a> (RIR) can be queried directly to determine the <a href="/wiki/Internet_service_provider" title="Internet service provider">Internet service provider</a> responsible for a particular resource.
</p><p>The records of each of these registries are cross-referenced, so that a query to <a href="/wiki/ARIN" class="mw-redirect" title="ARIN">ARIN</a> for a record which belongs to <a href="/wiki/RIPE" title="RIPE">RIPE</a> will return a placeholder pointing to the RIPE WHOIS server. This lets the WHOIS user making the query know that the detailed information resides on the RIPE server. In addition to the RIRs servers, commercial services exist, such as the <a href="/wiki/Routing_Assets_Database" title="Routing Assets Database">Routing Assets Database</a> used by some large networks (e.g., large Internet providers that acquired other ISPs in several RIR areas).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Server_discovery">Server discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Server discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is currently no standard for determining the responsible WHOIS server for a DNS domain, though a number of methods are in common use for <a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">top-level domains</a> (TLDs). Some WHOIS lookups require searching the procuring domain registrar to display domain owner details.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Query_example">Query example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Query example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Normally the contact information of the resources assignee is returned. However, some registrars offer private registration, in which case the contact information of the registrar is shown instead.
</p><p>Some registry operators are wholesalers, meaning that they typically provide domain name services to a large number of retail registrars, who in turn offer them to consumers. For private registration, only the identity of the wholesale registrar may be returned. In this case, the identity of the individual as well as the retail registrar may be hidden.
</p><p>Below is an example of WHOIS data returned for an individual resource holder. This is the result of a WHOIS query of <a href="/wiki/Example.com" title="Example.com">example.com</a>:
</p>
<div class="mw-highlight mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr"><pre>whois example.com
[Querying whois.verisign-grs.com]
[Redirected to whois.iana.org]
[Querying whois.iana.org]
[whois.iana.org]
% IANA WHOIS server
% for more information on IANA, visit http://www.iana.org
% This query returned 1 object
domain:       EXAMPLE.COM
organisation: Internet Assigned Numbers Authority
created:      1992-01-01
source:       IANA</pre></div>
<p><span id="RWhois"></span>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Referral_Whois">Referral Whois</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Referral Whois">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Referral Whois</b> (<b>RWhois</b>) is an extension of the original Whois protocol and service. RWhois extends the concepts of Whois in a <a href="/wiki/Scalability" title="Scalability">scalable</a>, hierarchical fashion, potentially creating a system with a tree-like architecture. Queries are deterministically routed to servers based on hierarchical labels, reducing a query to the primary repository of information.<sup id="cite_ref-rfc1714_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rfc1714-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lookups of IP address allocations are often limited to the larger <a href="/wiki/Classless_Inter-Domain_Routing" title="Classless Inter-Domain Routing">Classless Inter-Domain Routing</a> (CIDR) blocks (e.g., /24, /22, /16), because usually only the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Internet_registry" title="Regional Internet registry">regional Internet registries</a> (RIRs) and <a href="/wiki/Domain_registrars" class="mw-redirect" title="Domain registrars">domain registrars</a> run RWhois or Whois servers, although RWhois is intended to be run by even smaller <a href="/wiki/Local_Internet_Registry" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Internet Registry">local Internet registries</a>, to provide more granular information about IP address assignment.
</p><p>RWhois is intended to replace Whois, providing an organized hierarchy of referral services where one could connect to any RWhois server, request a look-up and be automatically re-directed to the correct server(s). However, while the technical functionality is in place, adoption of the RWhois standard has been weak.
</p><p>RWhois services are typically communicated using the <a href="/wiki/Transmission_Control_Protocol" title="Transmission Control Protocol">Transmission Control Protocol</a> (TCP). Servers listen to requests on the well-known <a href="/wiki/Port_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Port number">port number</a> 4321.
</p><p>Rwhois was first specified in RFC 1714 in 1994 by <a href="/wiki/Network_Solutions" title="Network Solutions">Network Solutions</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-rfc1714_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rfc1714-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> but the specification was superseded in 1997 by <a class="external mw-magiclink-rfc" rel="nofollow" href="https://tools.ietf.org/html/rfc2167">RFC 2167</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rfc2167_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rfc2167-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The referral features of RWhois are different than the feature of a Whois server to refer responses to another server, which RWhois also implements.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One criticism of WHOIS is the lack of full access to the data.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Few parties have realtime access to the complete databases.
</p><p>Others cite the competing goal of <a href="/wiki/Domain_privacy" title="Domain privacy">domain privacy</a> as a criticism, although this problem is strongly mitigated by <a href="/wiki/Domain_privacy" title="Domain privacy">domain privacy</a> services. Currently, the <i>Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers</i> (<a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a>) broadly requires that the <a href="/wiki/Mail" title="Mail">mailing</a> <a href="/wiki/Address_(geography)" title="Address (geography)">address</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phone_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Phone number">phone number</a> and <a href="/wiki/E-mail_address" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail address">e-mail address</a> of those owning or administrating a domain name to be made publicly available through the "WHOIS" directories. The registrant's (domain owner's) contact details, such as address and telephone number, are easily accessible to anyone who queries a WHOIS server. However, that policy enables spammers, direct marketers, identity thieves or other attackers to loot the directory for personal information about these people. Although ICANN has been exploring changing WHOIS to enable greater privacy, there is a lack of consensus among major stakeholders as to what type of change should be made.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Some domain registrars offer private registrations (also known as <a href="/wiki/Domain_privacy" title="Domain privacy">domain privacy</a>), by which the contact information of the registrar is shown instead of the customer's. With the offer of private registration from many registrars, some of the risk has been mitigated.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies have shown that spammers can and do harvest plain-text email addresses from WHOIS servers.<sup id="cite_ref-icann_whois_spam_study_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-icann_whois_spam_study-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> For this reason, some WHOIS servers and websites offering WHOIS queries have implemented rate-limiting systems, such as web-based <a href="/wiki/CAPTCHA" title="CAPTCHA">CAPTCHA</a> and limited amounts of search queries per user <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP address</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The WHOIS requirements conflict with the <a href="/wiki/General_Data_Protection_Regulation" title="General Data Protection Regulation">General Data Protection Regulation</a> (GDPR), effective in the European Union 25 May 2018, which places strict regulations on the processing and publication of personally identifiable information. ICANN stated in November 2017 that it would not reprimand "noncompliance with contractual obligations related to the handling of registration data" if registrars provide alternative solutions for compliance with its rules, until the WHOIS requirements are updated to take GDPR into account.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The WHOIS protocol was not written with an international audience in mind. A WHOIS server and/or client cannot determine the text encoding in effect for the query or the database content. Many servers were originally using US-<a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">ASCII</a> and Internationalization concerns weren't taken into consideration until much later.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This might impact the usability or usefulness of the WHOIS protocol in countries outside the USA.<sup id="cite_ref-rfc3912_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rfc3912-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In the case of <a href="/wiki/Internationalized_domain_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Internationalized domain names">internationalized domain names</a> it is the responsibility of the client application to perform the translation of the domain name between its native language script and the DNS name in <a href="/wiki/Punycode" title="Punycode">punycode</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accuracy_of_information">Accuracy of information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Accuracy of information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In cases where the registrant's (Domain Owner) identity is public, anyone can easily confirm the status of a domain via WHOIS.
</p><p>In the case of private registrations, ascertaining registration information may be more difficult. If a registrant, who acquired a domain name, wants to verify the registrar has completed the registration process, three steps may be required: 
</p>
<ol><li>Perform a WHOIS and confirm that the resource is at least registered with ICANN,</li>
<li>Determine the name of the wholesale registrar, and</li>
<li>Contact the wholesaler and obtain the name of the retail registrar.</li></ol>
<p>This provides some confidence that the retailer actually registered the name. But if the registrar goes out of business, as with the failure of <a href="/wiki/RegisterFly" title="RegisterFly">RegisterFly</a> in 2007, the rightful domain holder with privacy-protected registrations may have difficulty regaining the administration of his/her domain name.<sup id="cite_ref-circleid.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-circleid.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Registrants using "private registration" can attempt to protect themselves by using a registrar that places customer data in escrow with a third party.
</p><p>ICANN requires that every registrant of a domain name be given the opportunity to correct any inaccurate contact data associated with his/her domain. For this reason, registrars are required to periodically send the holder the contact information on record for verification, but they do not provide any guarantee about the accuracy of information if the registrant provided inaccurate information.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_and_policy">Law and policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Law and policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-globalize" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Globe icon." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/48px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png" width="48" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/73px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/97px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="290" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">The examples and perspective in this section <b>deal primarily with the United States and do not represent a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias">worldwide view</a> of the subject</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You may <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a>, discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:WHOIS" title="Talk:WHOIS">talk page</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_wizard" title="Wikipedia:Article wizard">create a new article</a>, as appropriate.</span>  <small><i>(May 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>WHOIS has generated policy issues in the United States federal government. As noted above, WHOIS creates a <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a> issue which is also tied to <a href="/wiki/Free_speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Free speech">free speech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anonymity" title="Anonymity">anonymity</a>. However, WHOIS is an important tool for law enforcement officers investigating violations like <a href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam (electronic)">spam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phishing" title="Phishing">phishing</a> to track down the holders of domain names. As a result, law enforcement agencies have sought to make WHOIS records both open and verified:<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Trade_Commission" title="Federal Trade Commission">Federal Trade Commission</a> has testified about how inaccurate WHOIS records thwart their investigations.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Congressional hearings have been conducted about the importance of WHOIS in 2001, 2002 and 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <i>Fraudulent Online Identity Sanctions Act</i><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> "make it a violation of trademark and copyright law if a person knowingly provided, or caused to be provided, materially false contact information in making, maintaining, or renewing the registration of a domain name used in connection with the violation,"<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> where the latter "violation" refers to a prior violation of trademark or copyright law. The act does not make the submission of false WHOIS data illegal in itself, only if used to shield oneself from prosecution for crimes committed using that domain name.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="ICANN_proposal_to_abolish_WHOIS">ICANN proposal to abolish WHOIS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHOIS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: ICANN proposal to abolish WHOIS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Expert Working Group (EWG) of the Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (<a href="/wiki/ICANN" title="ICANN">ICANN</a>) recommended on 24 June 2013 that WHOIS should be scrapped. It recommends that WHOIS be replaced with a system that keeps information secret from most Internet users, and only discloses information for "permissible purposes".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> ICANN's list of permissible purposes includes domain-name research, domain-name sale and purchase, regulatory enforcement, personal data protection, legal actions, and abuse mitigation.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Although WHOIS has been a key tool of journalists in determining who was disseminating certain information on the Internet,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> the use of WHOIS by the free press is not included in ICANN’s proposed list of permissible purposes.
</p><p>The EWG collected public input on the initial report until 13 September 2013. Its final report was issued on 6 June 2014, without meaningful changes to the recommendations.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> ICANN is now in the "process of re-inventing WHOIS," working on "ICANN WHOIS Beta."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
