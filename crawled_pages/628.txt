<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Modern immigration to the United Kingdom</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom_since_1922&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigration to the United Kingdom since 1922">Immigration to the United Kingdom since 1922</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about immigration to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> since the independence of Ireland in 1922. For immigration before Irish Independence, see <a href="/wiki/Historical_immigration_to_Great_Britain" title="Historical immigration to Great Britain">Historical immigration to Great Britain</a>. For information and statistics on the resident foreign-born population of the UK, see <a href="/wiki/Foreign-born_population_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Foreign-born population of the United Kingdom">Foreign-born population of the United Kingdom</a>.</div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Welsh immigration" redirects here. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Welsh_emigration" class="mw-redirect" title="Welsh emigration">Welsh emigration</a>.</div>
<p>

Since 1945, <b>immigration to the United Kingdom</b> under <a href="/wiki/British_nationality_law" title="British nationality law">British nationality law</a> has been significant, in particular from the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and from the former <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> especially  <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Other immigrants have come as <a href="/wiki/Asylum_seekers" class="mw-redirect" title="Asylum seekers">asylum seekers</a>, seeking protection as <a href="/wiki/Refugees" class="mw-redirect" title="Refugees">refugees</a> under the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Convention_Relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees">1951 Refugee Convention</a>, or from <a href="/wiki/Member_state_of_the_European_Union" title="Member state of the European Union">member states of the European Union</a>, exercising one of the European Union's <a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_(European_Union)" class="mw-redirect" title="Four Freedoms (European Union)">Four Freedoms</a>
</p><p>About 70% of the population increase between the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2001">2001</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011</a> <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">censuses</a> was due to <a href="/wiki/Foreign-born_population_of_Great_Britain,_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign-born population of Great Britain, 2001">foreign-born</a> immigration. 7.5 million people (11.9% of the population at the time) were born overseas, although the census gives no indication of their immigration status or intended length of stay.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Provisional figures show that in 2013, 526,000 people arrived to live in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> whilst 314,000 left, meaning that net inward migration was 212,000. The number of people immigrating to the UK increased between 2012 and 2013 by 28,000, whereas the number emigrating fell by 7,000.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From April 2013 to April 2014, a total of 560,000 immigrants were estimated to have arrived in the UK, including 81,000 British citizens and 214,000 from other parts of the EU. An estimated 317,000 people left, including 131,000 British citizens and 83,000 other EU citizens. The top countries represented in terms of arrivals were: <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, approximately 125,800 foreign citizens were naturalised as British citizens. This figure fell from around 208,000 in 2013, which was the highest figure recorded since 1962, when records began. Between 2009-13, the average number of people granted British citizenship per year was 195,800. The main countries of previous nationality of those naturalised in 2014 were: India, Pakistan, the Philippines, Nigeria, Bangladesh, Nepal, China, South Africa, Poland and Somalia.<sup id="cite_ref-Blinder2015_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blinder2015-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The UK Government can also grant settlement to foreign nationals, which confers on them <a href="/wiki/Permanent_residency" title="Permanent residency">permanent residence</a> in the UK, without granting them British citizenship. Grants of settlement are made on the basis of various factors, including employment, family formation and reunification, and asylum (including to deal with backlogs of asylum cases).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The total number of grants of settlement was approximately 154,700 in 2013, compared to 241,200 in 2010 and 129,800 in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Blinder2015_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blinder2015-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In comparison, migration to and from <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> has increased since 2004 with the <a href="/wiki/Enlargement_of_the_European_Union" title="Enlargement of the European Union">accession</a> to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> of <a href="/wiki/A8_countries" title="A8 countries">eight Central and Eastern European states</a>, since there is <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_movement_for_workers" class="mw-redirect" title="Freedom of movement for workers">free movement of labour</a> within the EU.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2008, the UK Government began phasing in a new <a href="/wiki/Points-based_immigration_system_(United_Kingdom)" title="Points-based immigration system (United Kingdom)">points-based immigration system</a> for people from outside of the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_Library" title="House of Commons Library">House of Commons Library</a>, several definitions for a migrant exist in United Kingdom so that a migrant can be:
</p>
<ul><li>Someone whose country of birth is different to their country of residence.</li>
<li>Someone whose nationality is different to their country of residence.</li>
<li>Someone who changes their country of usual residence for a period of at least a year, so that the country of destination effectively becomes the country of usual residence.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Empire_and_the_Commonwealth">British Empire and the Commonwealth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: British Empire and the Commonwealth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the mid-eighteenth century until at least 1956, and longer in many areas, the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> covered a large proportion of the globe. Both during this time, and following the granting of independence to most colonies after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the vast majority of immigrants to the UK were from either current or former colonies, most notably those in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_East_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="South East Asia">South East Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Following the end of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Nationality_Act_1948" title="British Nationality Act 1948">British Nationality Act 1948</a> allowed the 800,000,000<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/British_subject" title="British subject">subjects</a> in the British Empire to live and work in the United Kingdom without needing a <a href="/wiki/Travel_visa" title="Travel visa">visa</a>, although this was not an anticipated consequence of the Act, which "was never intended to facilitate mass migration".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This migration was initially encouraged to help fill gaps in the UK <a href="/wiki/Labour_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour market">labour market</a> for both skilled and unskilled jobs, including in public services such as the newly-created <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> and <a href="/wiki/London_Transport_Executive" title="London Transport Executive">London Transport</a>. Many people were specifically brought to the UK on ships; notably the <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_Windrush" class="mw-redirect" title="Empire Windrush">Empire Windrush</a></i> in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Commonwealth" title="Commonwealth">Commonwealth</a> immigration, made up largely of <a href="/wiki/Economic_migrants" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic migrants">economic migrants</a>, rose from 3,000 per year in 1953 to 46,800 in 1956 and 136,400 in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The heavy numbers of migrants resulted in the establishment of a Cabinet committee in June 1950 to find "ways which might be adopted to check the immigration into this country of <a href="/wiki/Colored" title="Colored">coloured</a> people from <a href="/wiki/Crown_colony" title="Crown colony">British colonial</a> territories".<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the Committee recommended not to introduce restrictions, the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Immigrants_Act_1962" title="Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962">Commonwealth Immigrants Act</a> was passed in 1962 as a response to public sentiment that the new arrivals "should return to their own countries" and that "no more of them come to this country".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Introducing the legislation to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Home_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for the Home Department">Home Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Rab_Butler" title="Rab Butler">Rab Butler</a> stated that:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The justification for the control which is included in this Bill, which I shall describe in more detail in a few moments, is that a sizeable part of the entire population of the Earth is at present legally entitled to come and stay in this already densely populated country. It amounts altogether to one-quarter of the population of the globe and at present there are no factors visible which might lead us to expect a reversal or even a modification of the immigration trend.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite><a href="/wiki/Rab_Butler" title="Rab Butler">Rab Butler MP</a>, 16 November 1961</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The new Act required migrants to have a job before they arrived, to possess special skills or who would meet the "labour needs" of the national economy. In 1965, to combat the perceived injustice in the case where the wives of British subjects could not obtain British nationality, the British Nationality Act 1965 was adopted. Shortly afterwards, refugees from <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, fearing discrimination from their own national governments, began to arrive in Britain; as they had retained their British nationality granted by the 1948 Act, they were not subject to the later controls. The Conservative MP <a href="/wiki/Enoch_Powell" title="Enoch Powell">Enoch Powell</a> campaigned for tighter controls on immigration which resulted in the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Immigrants_Act_1968" title="Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1968">Commonwealth Immigrants Act</a> in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-NatArch_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatArch-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For the first time, the Act required migrants to have a "substantial connection with the United Kingdom", namely to be connected by birth or ancestry to a UK national. Those who did not could only obtain British nationality at the discretion of the national authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> One month after the adoption of the Act, Enoch Powell made his infamous <a href="/wiki/Rivers_of_Blood_speech" title="Rivers of Blood speech">Rivers of Blood speech</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>By 1972, with the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_1971" title="Immigration Act 1971">Immigration Act</a>, only holders of <a href="/wiki/Work_permit" title="Work permit">work permits</a>, or people with parents or grandparents born in the UK could gain entry – effectively stemming <a href="/wiki/Primary_immigration" title="Primary immigration">primary immigration</a> from Commonwealth countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Brah_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brah-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Act abolished the distinction between Commonwealth and non-Commonwealth entrants. The Conservative government nevertheless allowed, amid much controversy, the immigration of 27,000 individuals displaced from Uganda after the <a href="/wiki/1971_Ugandan_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1971 Ugandan coup d&#39;état">coup d'état</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Idi_Amin" title="Idi Amin">Idi Amin</a> in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-NatArch_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatArch-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s, an average of 72,000 immigrants were settling in the UK every year from the Commonwealth; this decreased in the 1980s and early-1990s to around 54,000 per year, only to rise again to around 97,000 by 1999. The total number of Commonwealth immigrants since 1962 is estimated at around 2,500,000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ireland_Act_1949" title="Ireland Act 1949">Ireland Act 1949</a> has the unusual status of recognising the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, but affirming that its <a href="/wiki/Citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizen">citizens</a> are not citizens of a foreign country for the purposes of any law in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This act was initiated at a time when Ireland withdrew from the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> after declaring itself a republic.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/German_migration_to_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="German migration to the United Kingdom">German migration to the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>In the lead-up to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, many people from <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, particularly those belonging to minorities which were persecuted under <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> rule, such as <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a>, sought to emigrate to the United Kingdom, and it is estimated that as many as 50,000 may have been successful. There were immigration caps on the number who could enter and, subsequently, some applicants were turned away. When the UK declared <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">war on Germany</a>, however, migration between the countries ceased.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Post-war_immigration_.281945.E2.80.931983.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_immigration_(1945–1983)">Post-war immigration (1945–1983)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-war immigration (1945–1983)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Non_UK_percentage.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Non_UK_percentage.svg/300px-Non_UK_percentage.svg.png" width="300" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Non_UK_percentage.svg/450px-Non_UK_percentage.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Non_UK_percentage.svg/600px-Non_UK_percentage.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="602" data-file-height="319" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Non_UK_percentage.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Percentage of non-UK born people in England and Wales from 1951-2011.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Following the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, substantial groups of people from <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a>-controlled territories settled in the UK, particularly <a href="/wiki/Polish_British" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish British">Poles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_British" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukrainian British">Ukrainians</a>. The UK recruited displaced people as so-called European Volunteer Workers in order to provide labour to industries that were required in order to aid economic recovery after the war.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/w/index.php?title=1951_UK_Census&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="1951 UK Census (page does not exist)">1951 UK Census</a>, the Polish-born population of the UK numbered some 162,339, up from 44,642 in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/British_Indians" class="mw-redirect" title="British Indians">Indians</a> began arriving in the UK in large numbers shortly after their country gained independence in 1947, although there were a number of people from India living the UK even in the earlier years. More than 60,000 arrived before 1955, many of whom drove buses, or worked in <a href="/wiki/Foundry" title="Foundry">foundries</a> or <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> factories. Later arrivals opened <a href="/wiki/Corner_shop" class="mw-redirect" title="Corner shop">corner shops</a> or ran <a href="/wiki/Post_office" title="Post office">post offices</a>. The flow of Indian immigrants peaked between 1965-1972, boosted in particular by Ugandan dictator <a href="/wiki/Idi_Amin" title="Idi Amin">Idi Amin</a>'s sudden decision to <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Asians_in_Uganda_in_1972" class="mw-redirect" title="Expulsion of Asians in Uganda in 1972">expel all 50,000</a> <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_people" title="Gujarati people">Gujarati</a> Indians from <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>. Around 30,000 Ugandan Asians emigrated to the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the independence of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, Pakistani immigration to the United Kingdom increased, especially during the 1950s and 1960s. Many Pakistanis came to Britain following the turmoil during the <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_India" title="Partition of India">partition of India</a> and the subsequent independence of Pakistan; among them were those who migrated to Pakistan upon displacement from India, and then emigrated to the UK, thus becoming secondary migrants.<sup id="cite_ref-Glou_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Glou-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Migration was made easier as Pakistan was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Independent2_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Independent2-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Pakistanis were invited by employers to fill labour shortages which arose after the Second World War. As Commonwealth citizens, they were eligible for most British civic rights. They found employment in the textile industries of <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, manufacturing in the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(region)" title="West Midlands (region)">West Midlands</a>, and car production and food processing industries of <a href="/wiki/Luton" title="Luton">Luton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slough" title="Slough">Slough</a>. It was common for Pakistani employees to work nightshifts and at other less-desirable hours.<sup id="cite_ref-insted.co.uk_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-insted.co.uk-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, there was a <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bangladeshis_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of Bangladeshis in the United Kingdom">stream of migrants</a> from <a href="/wiki/East_Pakistan" title="East Pakistan">East Pakistan</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Ember_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ember-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> During the 1970s, a large number of East African-Asians, most of whom already held <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passports</a> because they were brought to Africa by British colonialists, entered the UK from <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>.
</p><p>There was also an influx of refugees from <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, following the crushing of the <a href="/wiki/1956_Hungarian_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="1956 Hungarian revolution">1956 Hungarian revolution</a>, numbering 20,990 people.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Immigrants_Act_1962" title="Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962">Commonwealth Immigrants Act 1962</a>, all <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> citizens could enter and stay in the UK without any restriction. The Act made <a href="/wiki/Citizen_of_the_United_Kingdom_and_Colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizen of the United Kingdom and Colonies">Citizens of the United Kingdom and Colonies</a> (CUKCs), whose <a href="/wiki/Passport" title="Passport">passports</a> were not directly issued by the UK Government (i.e., passports issued by the Governor of a colony or by the Commander of a British <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorate</a>), subject to immigration control.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Enoch_Powell" title="Enoch Powell">Enoch Powell</a> gave the famous "<a href="/wiki/Rivers_of_Blood" class="mw-redirect" title="Rivers of Blood">Rivers of Blood</a>" speech on 20 April 1968 in which he warned his audience of what he believed would be the consequences of continued unchecked immigration from the Commonwealth to Britain. <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Leader of the Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party leader</a> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a> sacked Powell from his Shadow Cabinet the day after the speech, and he never held another senior political post. Powell received 110,000 letters &#160;&#8211;&#32; only 2,300 disapproving-<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> as a result of the speech and a Gallup poll at the end of April showed that 74% of those asked agreed with his speech.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Three days after the speech, on 23 April, as the Race Relations Bill was being debated in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a>, around 2,000 dockers walked off the job to march on Westminster protesting against Powell's dismissal,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and the next day 400 meat porters from Smithfield market handed in a 92-page petition in support of Powell.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Figure-1.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Figure-1.png/220px-Figure-1.png" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Figure-1.png/330px-Figure-1.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Figure-1.png/440px-Figure-1.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="387" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Figure-1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>UK foreign born population from 1851-2011 by percentage.</div></div></div>
<p>By 1972, only holders of <a href="/wiki/Work_permit" title="Work permit">work permits</a>, or people with parents or grandparents born in the UK could gain entry – significantly reducing <a href="/wiki/Primary_immigration" title="Primary immigration">primary immigration</a> from Commonwealth countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Brah_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brah-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span id="Contemporary_immigration_.281983_onwards.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_immigration_(1983_onwards)">Contemporary immigration (1983 onwards)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Contemporary immigration (1983 onwards)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Nationality_Act_1981" title="British Nationality Act 1981">British Nationality Act 1981</a>, which was enacted in 1983, distinguishes between British <a href="/wiki/Citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Citizen">citizens</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a> citizens. It also made a distinction between nationality <i>by descent</i> and nationality <i>other than by descent</i>. Citizens by descent cannot automatically pass on British nationality to a child born outside the United Kingdom or its Overseas Territories (though in some situations the child can be registered as a citizen).
</p><p>Immigration officers have to be satisfied about a person's nationality and identity and entry can be refused if they are not satisfied.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1980s and 1990s, the <a href="/wiki/Somali_Civil_War" title="Somali Civil War">civil war</a> in <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> led to a large number of Somali immigrants, comprising the majority of the current <a href="/wiki/Somalis_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Somalis in the United Kingdom">Somali population in the UK</a>. In the late-1980s, most of these early migrants were granted asylum, while those arriving later in the 1990s more often obtained temporary status. There has also been some secondary migration of Somalis to the UK from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>. The main driving forces behind this secondary migration included a desire to reunite with family and friends and for better employment opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-Identities_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Identities-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Non-European immigration rose significantly during the period from 1997, not least because of the <a href="/wiki/Blair_ministry" title="Blair ministry">government</a>'s abolition of the primary purpose rule in June 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> This change made it easier for UK residents to bring foreign spouses into the country.
The former government adviser Andrew Neather in the <i><a href="/wiki/London_Evening_Standard" title="London Evening Standard">Evening Standard</a></i> stated that the deliberate policy of ministers from late-2000 until early-2008 was to open up the UK to mass migration.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Immigration Rules, under the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Act_1971" title="Immigration Act 1971">Immigration Act 1971</a>, were updated in 2012 (Appendix FM) to create a strict minimum income threshold for non-EU spouses and children to be given <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_leave_to_remain" title="Indefinite leave to remain">leave to remain in the UK</a>. These rules were challenged in the courts, and in 2017 the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court</a> found that while "the minimum income threshold is accepted in principle" they decided that the rules and guidance were defective and unlawful until amended to give more weight to the interests of the children involved, and that sources of funding other than the British spouse's income should be considered.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian-20170222_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-20170222-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cco-201508_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cco-201508-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigrants_from_the_European_Union">Immigrants from the European Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Immigrants from the European Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_(European_Union)" class="mw-redirect" title="Four Freedoms (European Union)">Four Freedoms</a> of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, of which the United Kingdom is a member, is the right to the free movement of workers as codified in the <a href="/wiki/Directive_2004/38/EC_on_the_right_to_move_and_reside_freely" class="mw-redirect" title="Directive 2004/38/EC on the right to move and reside freely">Directive 2004/38/EC</a> and the <a href="/wiki/EEA_Regulations_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="EEA Regulations (UK)">EEA Regulations (UK)</a>.
</p><p>Since the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> on 1 May 2004, the UK has accepted immigrants from <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, although the substantial Maltese and <a href="/wiki/Greek_Cypriots" title="Greek Cypriots">Greek-Cypriots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Cypriots" title="Turkish Cypriots">Turkish-Cypriot</a> communities were established earlier through their Commonwealth connection. There are restrictions on the benefits that members of eight of these accession countries ('A8' nationals) can claim, which are covered by the <a href="/wiki/Worker_Registration_Scheme" class="mw-redirect" title="Worker Registration Scheme">Worker Registration Scheme</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Many other European Union member states exercised their right to temporary immigration control (which ended in 2011)<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> over entrants from these accession states,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> but some subsequently removed these restrictions ahead of the 2011 deadline.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Research conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Migration_Policy_Institute" title="Migration Policy Institute">Migration Policy Institute</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Equality_and_Human_Rights_Commission" title="Equality and Human Rights Commission">Equality and Human Rights Commission</a> suggests that, between May 2004 and September 2009, 1.5 million workers migrated from the new EU member states to the UK, but that many have returned home, with the result that the number of nationals of the new member states in the UK increased by some 700,000 over the same period.<sup id="cite_ref-MPI_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MPI-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Migration from Poland in particular has become temporary and circular in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, for the first time since the enlargement, more nationals of the eight Central and Eastern European states that joined the EU in 2004 left the UK than arrived.<sup id="cite_ref-A8_leaving_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A8_leaving-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Research commissioned by the Regeneration and Economic Development Analysis Expert Panel suggested migrant workers leaving the UK due to the recession are likely to return in the future and cited evidence of "strong links between initial temporary migration and intended permanent migration".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Government announced that the same rules would not apply to nationals of <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a> (A2 nationals) when those countries acceded to the EU in 2007. Instead, restrictions were put in place to limit migration to students, the self-employed, highly skilled migrants and food and agricultural workers.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Leader of the Labour Party (UK)">Leader of the Labour Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Miliband" title="Ed Miliband">Ed Miliband</a>, stated that he thought that the Labour government's decision to permit the unlimited immigration of eastern European migrants had been a mistake, arguing that they had underestimated the potential number of migrants and that the scale of migration had had a negative impact on wages.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A report by the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Communities and Local Government">Department for Communities and Local Government</a> (DCLG) entitled <i>International Migration and Rural Economies</i>, suggests that intra-EU migration since enlargement has resulted in migrants settling in rural locations without a prior history of immigration.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Research published by <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a> in July 2009 found that, on average, A8 migrants were younger and better educated than the native population, and that if they had the same demographic characteristics of natives, would be 13 per cent less likely to claim benefits and 28 per cent less likely to live in <a href="/wiki/Social_housing" class="mw-redirect" title="Social housing">social housing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Managed_migration">Managed migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Managed migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Migration_from_1970.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/UK_Migration_from_1970.svg/400px-UK_Migration_from_1970.svg.png" width="400" height="320" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/UK_Migration_from_1970.svg/600px-UK_Migration_from_1970.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/UK_Migration_from_1970.svg/800px-UK_Migration_from_1970.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Migration_from_1970.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shows immigration and emigration with net figures, including British and non-British citizens. Data from <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ons.gov.uk/ons/rel/migration1/migration-statistics-quarterly-report/august-2014/stb-msqr-august-2014.html">Office for National Statistics</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>"Managed migration" is the term for all legal labour and student migration from outside of the European Union and this accounts for a substantial percentage of overall immigration figures for the UK. Many of the immigrants who arrive under these schemes bring skills which are in short supply in the UK. This area of immigration is managed by <a href="/wiki/UK_Visas_and_Immigration" title="UK Visas and Immigration">UK Visas and Immigration</a>, a department within the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a>. Applications are made at UK embassies or consulates or directly to UK Visas and Immigration, depending upon the type of visa or permit required.
</p><p>In April 2006, changes to the managed migration system were proposed that would create one <a href="/wiki/Points-based_immigration_system_(United_Kingdom)" title="Points-based immigration system (United Kingdom)">points-based immigration system</a> for the UK in place of all other schemes. Tier 1 in the new system – which replaced the <a href="/wiki/Highly_Skilled_Migrant_Programme" title="Highly Skilled Migrant Programme">Highly Skilled Migrant Programme</a> – gives points for age, education, earning, previous UK experience but not for work experience. The points-based system was phased in over the course of 2008, replacing previous managed migration schemes such as the <a href="/wiki/Work_permit_(United_Kingdom)" title="Work permit (United Kingdom)">work permit system</a> and the Highly Skilled Migrant Programme.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A points-based system is composed of five tiers was first described by the <a href="/wiki/UK_Border_Agency" title="UK Border Agency">UK Border Agency</a> as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>Tier 1 – for highly skilled individuals, who can contribute to growth and productivity;</li>
<li>Tier 2 – for skilled workers with a job offer, to fill gaps in the United Kingdom workforce;</li>
<li>Tier 3 – for limited numbers of low-skilled workers needed to fill temporary labour shortages;</li>
<li>Tier 4 – for students;</li>
<li>Tier 5 – for temporary workers and young people covered by the Youth Mobility Scheme, who are allowed to work in the United Kingdom for a limited time to satisfy primarily non-economic objectives.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Migration_Advisory_Committee" title="Migration Advisory Committee">Migration Advisory Committee</a> was established in 2007 to give policy advice.
</p><p>In June 2010, The newly elected <a href="/wiki/Cameron%E2%80%93Clegg_coalition" title="Cameron–Clegg coalition">Coalition government</a> brought in a temporary cap on immigration of those entering the UK from outside the EU, with the limit set as 24,100, in order to stop an expected rush of applications before a permanent cap was imposed in April 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The cap caused tension within the coalition, and  then-Business Secretary <a href="/wiki/Vince_Cable" title="Vince Cable">Vince Cable</a> argued that it was harming British businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Others have argued that the cap would have a negative impact on Britain's status as a centre for scientific research.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For family relatives of <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Area" title="European Economic Area">European Economic Area</a> nationals living in the UK, there is the <a href="/wiki/EEA_family_permit" class="mw-redirect" title="EEA family permit">EEA family permit</a> which enables those family members to join their relatives already living and working in the UK.
</p><p>Though immigration is a matter that is reserved to the UK Government under the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">legislation</a> that established devolution for Scotland in 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> was able to get an agreement from the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a> for their <a href="/wiki/Fresh_Talent_Initiative" title="Fresh Talent Initiative">Fresh Talent Initiative</a> which was designed to encourage foreign graduates of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_universities" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish universities">Scottish universities</a> to stay in Scotland to look for employment.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The Fresh Talent Initiative ended in 2008, following the introduction of points-based system.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Refugees_and_asylum_seekers">Refugees and asylum seekers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Refugees and asylum seekers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Asylumapplicants.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Asylumapplicants.jpg/400px-Asylumapplicants.jpg" width="400" height="290" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Asylumapplicants.jpg/600px-Asylumapplicants.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Asylumapplicants.jpg/800px-Asylumapplicants.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2049" data-file-height="1486" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Asylumapplicants.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Asylum applications rose then fell during the 1990s and 2000s.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The UK is a signatory to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> <a href="/wiki/Convention_Relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees">1951 Refugee Convention</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Protocol_Relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Protocol Relating to the Status of Refugees">1967 Protocol</a> and has therefore a responsibility to offer protection to people who seek asylum it and fall into the legal definition of a "<a href="/wiki/Refugee#Legal_definition" title="Refugee">refugee</a>", and moreover not to return (or refoule) any displaced person to places where they would otherwise face persecution.  Cuts to legal aid prevent asylum seekers getting good advice or arguing their case effectively.  This can mean refugees being returned to a country where they face certain death.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The issue of immigration has been a controversial political issue since the late 1990s. Both the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> have suggested policies perceived as being "tough on asylum"<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> (although the Conservatives have dropped a previous pledge to limit the number of people who could claim asylum in the UK, which would likely have breached the UN Refugee Convention)<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_journalism" title="Tabloid journalism">tabloid</a> media frequently print headlines about an "immigration crisis".<sup id="cite_ref-ippr_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ippr-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is denounced, by those seeking to ensure that the UK upholds its international obligations, as disproportionate. Concern is also raised about the treatment of those held in detention and the practice of <a href="/wiki/Dawn_raid" class="mw-redirect" title="Dawn raid">dawn raiding</a> families, and holding young children in <a href="/wiki/Immigration_detention" title="Immigration detention">immigration detention</a> centres for long periods of time.<sup id="cite_ref-Nicol_Stephen_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol_Stephen-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Children_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Children-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> The policy of detaining asylum-seeking children was to be abandoned as part of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_%E2%80%93_Liberal_Democrat_Coalition_Agreement" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative – Liberal Democrat Coalition Agreement">coalition agreement</a> between the Conservatives and the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>, who formed a government in May 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> However, in July 2010 the government was accused of back-tracking on this promise after the Immigration Minister <a href="/wiki/Damian_Green" title="Damian Green">Damian Green</a> announced that the plan was to minimise, rather than end, child detention.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, critics of the UK's asylum policy often point out the "safe third country rule" – the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Regulation" title="Dublin Regulation">convention</a> that asylum seekers must apply in the first free nation they reach, not go "<a href="/wiki/Asylum_shopping" title="Asylum shopping">asylum shopping</a>" for the nation they prefer. EU courts have upheld this policy.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Research conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Refugee_Council" title="Refugee Council">Refugee Council</a> suggests that most asylum seekers in the UK had their destination chosen for them by external parties or agents, rather than choosing the UK themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2003, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> promised on television to reduce the number of asylum seekers by half within 7 months,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> apparently catching unawares the members of his own government with responsibility for immigration policy.  <a href="/wiki/David_Blunkett" title="David Blunkett">David Blunkett</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>, called the promise an <i>objective</i> rather than a <i>target</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was met according to official figures.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> There is also a Public Performance Target to remove more asylum seekers who have been judged not to be refugees under the international definition than new anticipated unfounded applications. This target was met early in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Official figures for numbers of people claiming asylum in the UK were at a 13-year low by March 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">Human rights</a> organisations such as <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a> have argued that the government's new policies, particularly those concerning <a href="/wiki/Immigration_detention" title="Immigration detention">detention</a> centres, have detrimental effects on asylum applicants<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> and their children,<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> and those facilities have seen a number of <a href="/wiki/Hunger_strikes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunger strikes">hunger strikes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suicides" class="mw-redirect" title="Suicides">suicides</a>. Others have argued that recent government policies aimed at reducing 'bogus' asylum claims have had detrimental impacts on those genuinely in need of protection.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to offering asylum, the UK operates two <a href="/wiki/Refugee_resettlement" class="mw-redirect" title="Refugee resettlement">refugee resettlement</a> schemes in co-operation with the <a href="/wiki/UNHCR" class="mw-redirect" title="UNHCR">UNHCR</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Gateway_Protection_Programme" title="Gateway Protection Programme">Gateway Resettlement Programme</a><sup id="cite_ref-Gateway_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gateway-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Syrian_Vulnerable_Persons_Resettlement_Programme" class="mw-redirect" title="Syrian Vulnerable Persons Resettlement Programme">Syrian Vulnerable Persons Resettlement Programme</a>.
</p><p>The UK hosts one of the largest populations of Iraqi refugees outside the Gulf region. About 65-70% of people originating from Iraq are Kurdish, and 70% of those from Turkey and 15% of those from Iran are Kurds.<sup id="cite_ref-Begikhani_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Begikhani-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Asylum seekers have been kept in detention after the courts ordered their release because the Home Office maintains detention is not dissimilar to emergency accommodation.  Immigrants with the right to stay in the UK are denied housing and cannot be released.  In other cases vulnerable asylum seekers are released onto the streets with nowhere to live.  In January 2018 the government repealed a law that previously allowed  homeless detainees to apply for housing while in detention if they had nowhere to live when released. Charities maintain around 2,000 detainees who before this applied for support each year can no longer do so.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illegal_immigration">Illegal immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Illegal immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" width="42" height="34" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="290" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span>  <small><i>(October 2015)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png/220px-UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png/330px-UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png/440px-UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png 2x" data-file-width="849" data-file-height="472" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UKBorderForceCalais03-ClandestineLeavingLorry.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>UK <a href="/wiki/Border_Force" title="Border Force">Border Force</a> detecting illegal Immigrants in <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Illegal_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom" title="Illegal immigration to the United Kingdom">Illegal immigration to the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Illegal immigrants in the UK include those who have:
</p>
<ul><li>entered the UK without authority</li>
<li>entered with false documents</li>
<li>overstayed their visas</li></ul>
<p>Although it is difficult to know how many people reside in the UK illegally, a Home Office study released in March 2005 estimated a population of between 310,000 and 570,000.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent study into irregular immigration states that "most irregular migrants have committed administrative offences rather than a serious crime".<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jack_Dromey" title="Jack Dromey">Jack Dromey</a>, Deputy General of the <a href="/wiki/Transport_and_General_Workers_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Transport and General Workers Union">Transport and General Workers Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> treasurer, suggested in May 2006 that there could be around 500,000 illegal workers. He called for a public debate on whether an amnesty should be considered.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Blunkett" title="David Blunkett">David Blunkett</a> has suggested that this might be done once the <a href="/wiki/British_national_identity_card" class="mw-redirect" title="British national identity card">identity card</a> scheme is rolled out.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/London_Citizens" class="mw-redirect" title="London Citizens">London Citizens</a>, a coalition of community organisations, is running a regularisation campaign called <i>Strangers into Citizens</i>, backed by figures including the former leader of the Catholic Church in England and Wales, the <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Cormac_Murphy-O%27Connor" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Cormac Murphy-O&#39;Connor">Cardinal Cormac Murphy-O'Connor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> Analysis by the <a href="/wiki/Institute_for_Public_Policy_Research" title="Institute for Public Policy Research">Institute for Public Policy Research</a> suggested that an amnesty could net the government up to  £1.038 billion per year in fiscal revenue, however the long term implications of such a measure are uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It has since been suggested that to deport all of the illegal immigrants from the UK might take 20 years and cost up to £12 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Former <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a> <a href="/wiki/Boris_Johnson" title="Boris Johnson">Boris Johnson</a>  commissioned a study into a possible amnesty for illegal immigrants, citing larger tax gains within the London area which is considered to be home to the majority of the country's population of such immigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2008, the government introduced new £10,000 fines for employers found to be employing illegal immigrants where there is negligence on the part of the employer, with unlimited fines or jail sentences for employers acting knowingly.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Women who are illegal immigrants and also domestic violence victims risk deportation and are deported if they complain about violence.  Women get brought illegally into the UK by men intending to abuse them.  Women are sometimes deterred from complaining about violence to them due to the risk of deportation, therefore perpetrators including rapists remain at large.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Martha_Spurrier" title="Martha Spurrier">Martha Spurrier</a> of <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(advocacy_group)" title="Liberty (advocacy group)">Liberty</a> said, "It will leave people afraid to report crime, robbing them of protection under the law and creating impunity for criminals who target vulnerable people with unsettled immigration status. This is criminalising victims and letting criminals off the hook."<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_of_European_Union_countries">Comparison of European Union countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Comparison of European Union countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Eurostat" title="Eurostat">Eurostat</a>, 47.3 million people lived in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> in 2010 who were born outside their resident country. This corresponds to 9.4% of the total EU population. Of these, 31.4 million (6.3%) were born outside the EU and 16.0 million (3.2%) were born in another EU member state. The largest absolute numbers of people born outside the EU were in Germany (6.4 million), France (5.1 million), the United Kingdom (4.7 million),  Spain (4.1 million), Italy (3.2 million), and the Netherlands (1.4 million).<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Citizenship_laws">Citizenship laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Modern_immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Citizenship laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Individuals wanting to apply for British citizenship have to demonstrate their commitment by learning English, Welsh or Gaelic and by having an understanding of British history, culture and traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> Any individual seeking to apply for naturalisation or indefinite leave to remain must pass the official <a href="/wiki/Life_in_the_United_Kingdom_test" title="Life in the United Kingdom test">Life in the UK test</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
