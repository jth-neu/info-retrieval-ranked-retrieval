<p><b>Vivien Leigh</b> (born <b>Vivian Mary Hartley</b>, and also known as <b>Lady Olivier</b> after 1947; 5&#160;November 1913&#160;&#8211;&#32;8&#160;July 1967) was an English stage and film actress.
</p><p>She won two <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award">Academy Awards</a> for <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Best Actress</a>, for her iconic performances as <a href="/wiki/Scarlett_O%27Hara" title="Scarlett O&#39;Hara">Scarlett O'Hara</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">Gone with the Wind</a></i> (1939) and <a href="/wiki/Blanche_DuBois" title="Blanche DuBois">Blanche DuBois</a> in the film version of <a href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(1951_film)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (1951 film)"><i>A Streetcar Named Desire</i></a> (1951), a role she had also played on stage in London's <a href="/wiki/West_End_theatre" title="West End theatre">West End</a> in 1949. She also won a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award_for_Best_Leading_Actress_in_a_Musical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tony Award for Best Leading Actress in a Musical">Tony Award</a> for her work in the <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> musical version of <i><a href="/wiki/Tovarich_(musical)" title="Tovarich (musical)">Tovarich</a></i> (1963).
</p><p>After completing her drama school education, Leigh appeared in small roles in four films in 1935 and progressed to the role of heroine in <i><a href="/wiki/Fire_Over_England" title="Fire Over England">Fire Over England</a></i> (1937). Lauded for her beauty, Leigh felt that her physical attributes sometimes prevented her from being taken seriously as an actress. Despite her fame as a screen actress, Leigh was primarily a stage performer. During her 30-year career, she played roles ranging from the heroines of <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="Noël Coward">Noël Coward</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a> comedies to classic <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespearean</a> characters such as <a href="/wiki/Ophelia" title="Ophelia">Ophelia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleopatra_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleopatra VII">Cleopatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juliet_Capulet" class="mw-redirect" title="Juliet Capulet">Juliet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Macbeth" title="Lady Macbeth">Lady Macbeth</a>. Later in life, she performed as a <a href="/wiki/Character_actress" class="mw-redirect" title="Character actress">character actress</a> in a few films.
</p><p>At the time, the public strongly identified Leigh with her second husband, <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a>, who was her spouse from 1940 to 1960. Leigh and Olivier starred together in many stage productions, with Olivier often directing, and in three films. She earned a reputation for being difficult to work with, and for much of her adult life, she suffered from <a href="/wiki/Bipolar_disorder" title="Bipolar disorder">bipolar disorder</a>, as well as recurrent bouts of chronic <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>, which was first diagnosed in the mid-1940s and ultimately claimed her life at the age of 53.<sup id="cite_ref-Laurence_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laurence-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Although her career had periods of inactivity, in 1999 the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a> ranked Leigh as <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years...100_Stars" title="AFI&#39;s 100 Years...100 Stars">the 16th greatest female movie star</a> of classic Hollywood cinema.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1913.E2.80.931934:_Early_life_and_acting_debut"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1913–1934:_Early_life_and_acting_debut">1913–1934: Early life and acting debut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1913–1934: Early life and acting debut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leigh was born Vivian Mary Hartley<sup id="cite_ref-Dict._2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dict.-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> on 5 November 1913 in <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a> on the campus of <a href="/wiki/St._Paul%27s_School,_Darjeeling" title="St. Paul&#39;s School, Darjeeling">St. Paul's School, Darjeeling</a>. She was the only child of Ernest Richard Hartley, a British broker, and his wife, Gertrude Mary Frances (née Yackjee; she also used her mother's maiden name of Robinson).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Her father was born in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 1882, while her mother, a devout Roman Catholic, was born in <a href="/wiki/Darjeeling" title="Darjeeling">Darjeeling</a> in 1888 and may have been of <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Armenians" title="Armenians">Armenian</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or Indian ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-Vickers6_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vickers6-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Gertrude's parents, who lived in India, were Michael John Yackjee (born 1840), a man of independent means, and Mary Teresa Robinson (born 1856), who was born to an <a href="/wiki/Irish_Indians" title="Irish Indians">Irish family</a> killed during the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Rebellion_of_1857" title="Indian Rebellion of 1857">Indian Rebellion of 1857</a> and grew up in an orphanage, where she met Yackjee; they married in 1872 and had five children, of whom Gertrude was the youngest.<sup id="cite_ref-Vickers6_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vickers6-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Ernest and Gertrude Hartley were married in 1912 in <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a>, London.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1917, Ernest Hartley was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Bangalore" title="Bangalore">Bangalore</a> as an officer in the <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">Indian Cavalry</a>, while Gertrude and Vivian stayed in <a href="/wiki/Ootacamund" class="mw-redirect" title="Ootacamund">Ootacamund</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At the age of three, young Vivian made her first stage appearance for her mother's amateur theatre group, reciting "<a href="/wiki/Little_Bo_Peep" class="mw-redirect" title="Little Bo Peep">Little Bo Peep</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Gertrude Hartley tried to instill an appreciation of literature in her daughter and introduced her to the works of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">Hans Christian Andersen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>, as well as stories of <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_folklore" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian folklore">Indian folklore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> At the age of six, Vivian was sent by her mother from <a href="/wiki/Loreto_Convent,_Darjeeling" title="Loreto Convent, Darjeeling">Loreto Convent, Darjeeling</a>, to the Convent of the Sacred Heart (now <a href="/wiki/Woldingham_School" title="Woldingham School">Woldingham School</a>) then situated in <a href="/wiki/Roehampton" title="Roehampton">Roehampton</a>, southwest London.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> One of her friends there was future actress <a href="/wiki/Maureen_O%27Sullivan" title="Maureen O&#39;Sullivan">Maureen O'Sullivan</a>, two years her senior, to whom Vivian expressed her desire to become "a great actress".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> She was removed from the school by her father, and travelling with her parents for four years, she attended schools in Europe, notably in <a href="/wiki/Dinard" title="Dinard">Dinard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biarritz" title="Biarritz">Biarritz</a>, Sacred Heart in San Remo on the Italian Riviera and Paris, becoming fluent in both French and Italian.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The family returned to Britain in 1931. She attended <i><a href="/wiki/A_Connecticut_Yankee_(film)" title="A Connecticut Yankee (film)">A Connecticut Yankee</a></i>, one of O'Sullivan's films playing in <a href="/wiki/West_End_of_London" title="West End of London">London's West End</a>, and told her parents of her ambitions to become an actress. Shortly after, her father enrolled Vivian at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Dramatic_Art" title="Royal Academy of Dramatic Art">Royal Academy of Dramatic Art</a> (RADA) in London.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vivian met Herbert Leigh Holman, known as Leigh Holman, a barrister 13 years her senior, in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Despite his disapproval of "theatrical people", they married on 20 December 1932 and she terminated her studies at RADA, her attendance and interest in acting having already waned after meeting Holman.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> On 12 October 1933 in London, she gave birth to a daughter, <a href="/wiki/Suzanne_Farrington" title="Suzanne Farrington">Suzanne</a>, later Mrs. Robin Farrington.<sup id="cite_ref-Leigh_Profile_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leigh_Profile-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;Note 1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1935.E2.80.931936:_Early_career"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1935–1936:_Early_career">1935–1936: Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1935–1936: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leigh's friends suggested she take a small role as a schoolgirl in the film <i>Things Are Looking Up</i>, which was her film debut, albeit uncredited as an extra.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> She engaged an agent, John Gliddon, who believed that "Vivian Holman" was not a suitable name for an actress. After rejecting his many suggestions, she took "Vivian Leigh" as her professional name.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_1984,_p._38_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_1984,_p._38-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;Note 2&#93;</a></sup> Gliddon recommended her to <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a> as a possible film actress, but Korda rejected her as lacking potential.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> She was cast in the play <i>The Mask of Virtue</i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Carroll" title="Sidney Carroll">Sidney Carroll</a> in 1935, and received excellent reviews, followed by interviews and newspaper articles. One such article was from the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">Daily Express</a></i>, in which the interviewer noted "a lightning change came over her face", which was the first public mention of the rapid changes in mood which had become characteristic of her.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Betjeman" title="John Betjeman">John Betjeman</a>, the future <a href="/wiki/Poet_laureate" title="Poet laureate">poet laureate</a>, described her as "the essence of English girlhood".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Korda attended her opening night performance, admitted his error, and signed her to a film contract.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_1984,_p._38_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_1984,_p._38-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> She continued with the play but, when Korda moved it to a larger theatre, Leigh was found to be unable to project her voice adequately or to hold the attention of so large an audience, and the play closed soon after.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In the playbill, Carroll had revised the spelling of her first name to "Vivien".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960 Leigh recalled her ambivalence towards her first experience of critical acclaim and sudden fame, commenting, "that some critics saw fit to be as foolish as to say that I was a great actress. And I thought, that was a foolish, wicked thing to say, because it put such an onus and such a responsibility onto me, which I simply wasn't able to carry. And it took me years to learn enough to live up to what they said for those first notices.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> I find it so stupid. I remember the critic very well and have never forgiven him."<sup id="cite_ref-ActorTalk_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ActorTalk-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1937.E2.80.931939:_Meeting_Laurence_Olivier"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1937–1939:_Meeting_Laurence_Olivier">1937–1939: Meeting Laurence Olivier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1937–1939: Meeting Laurence Olivier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Laurence Olivier saw Leigh in <i>The Mask of Virtue</i>, and a friendship developed after he congratulated her on her performance. Olivier and Leigh began an affair while acting as lovers in <i><a href="/wiki/Fire_Over_England" title="Fire Over England">Fire Over England</a></i> (1937), but Olivier was still married to actress <a href="/wiki/Jill_Esmond" title="Jill Esmond">Jill Esmond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> During this period, Leigh read the <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mitchell" title="Margaret Mitchell">Margaret Mitchell</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(novel)" title="Gone with the Wind (novel)">Gone with the Wind</a></i> and instructed her American agent to recommend her to <a href="/wiki/David_O._Selznick" title="David O. Selznick">David O. Selznick</a>, who was planning a film version.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._14_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._14-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> She remarked to a journalist, "I've cast myself as Scarlett O'Hara", and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> film critic <a href="/wiki/C._A._Lejeune" title="C. A. Lejeune">C. A. Lejeune</a> recalled a conversation of the same period in which Leigh "stunned us all" with the assertion that Olivier "won't play <a href="/wiki/Rhett_Butler" title="Rhett Butler">Rhett Butler</a>, but I shall play Scarlett O'Hara. Wait and see."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite her relative inexperience, Leigh was chosen to play Ophelia to Olivier's <a href="/wiki/Hamlet" title="Hamlet">Hamlet</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Old_Vic_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Vic Theatre">Old Vic Theatre</a> production staged at <a href="/wiki/Elsinore" class="mw-redirect" title="Elsinore">Elsinore</a>, Denmark.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Olivier later recalled an incident when her mood rapidly changed as she was preparing to go onstage. Without apparent provocation, she began screaming at him before suddenly becoming silent and staring into space. She was able to perform without mishap, and by the following day she had returned to normal with no recollection of the event. It was the first time Olivier witnessed such behaviour from her.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> They began living together, as their respective spouses had each refused to grant either of them a divorce.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Under the moral standards then enforced by the film industry, their relationship had to be kept from public view.
</p><p>Leigh appeared with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Taylor_(actor)" title="Robert Taylor (actor)">Robert Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Barrymore" title="Lionel Barrymore">Lionel Barrymore</a> and Maureen O'Sullivan in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Yank_at_Oxford" title="A Yank at Oxford">A Yank at Oxford</a></i> (1938), which was the first of her films to receive attention in the United States. During production, she developed a reputation for being difficult and unreasonable, partly because she disliked her secondary role but mainly because her petulant antics seemed to be paying dividends.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> After dealing with the threat of a lawsuit brought over a frivolous incident, Korda, however, instructed her agent to warn her that her option would not be renewed if her behaviour did not improve.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Her next role was in <i><a href="/wiki/Sidewalks_of_London" title="Sidewalks of London">Sidewalks of London</a></i>, also known as <i>St. Martin's Lane</i> (1938), with <a href="/wiki/Charles_Laughton" title="Charles Laughton">Charles Laughton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Olivier had been attempting to broaden his film career. He was not well known in the United States despite his success in Britain, and earlier attempts to introduce him to American audiences had failed. Offered the role of <a href="/wiki/Heathcliff_(Wuthering_Heights)" title="Heathcliff (Wuthering Heights)">Heathcliff</a> in <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Goldwyn" title="Samuel Goldwyn">Samuel Goldwyn</a>'s production of <i><a href="/wiki/Wuthering_Heights_(1939_film)" title="Wuthering Heights (1939 film)">Wuthering Heights</a></i> (1939), he travelled to Hollywood, leaving Leigh in London. Goldwyn and the film's director, <a href="/wiki/William_Wyler" title="William Wyler">William Wyler</a>, offered Leigh the secondary role of Isabella, but she refused, preferring the role of Cathy, which went to <a href="/wiki/Merle_Oberon" title="Merle Oberon">Merle Oberon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Gone_with_the_Wind"><i>Gone with the Wind</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gone with the Wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg/220px-Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg" width="220" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg/330px-Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg/440px-Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="709" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gable-Leigh_GWTW-b.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Clark_Gable" title="Clark Gable">Clark Gable</a> and Leigh strike an amorous pose in <i>Gone with the Wind</i> (1939)</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">Hollywood</a> was in the midst of a widely publicized search to find an actress to portray <a href="/wiki/Scarlett_O%27Hara" title="Scarlett O&#39;Hara">Scarlett O'Hara</a> in David O. Selznick's production of <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_Wind_(film)" title="Gone with the Wind (film)">Gone with the Wind</a></i> (1939).<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._14_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._14-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> At the time, <a href="/wiki/Myron_Selznick" title="Myron Selznick">Myron Selznick</a>—David's brother and Leigh's American theatrical <a href="/wiki/Agent_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Agent (law)">agent</a>—was the London representative of the Myron Selznick Agency. In February 1938, Leigh made a request to Myron Selznick that she be considered to play the part of Scarlett O'Hara.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>David O. Selznick watched her performances that month in <i>Fire Over England</i> and <i>A Yank at Oxford</i> and thought that she was excellent but in no way a possible Scarlett because she was "too British". Leigh travelled to Los Angeles, however, to be with Olivier and to try to convince David Selznick that she was the right person for the part. Myron Selznick also represented Olivier and when he met Leigh, he felt that she possessed the qualities that his brother was searching for.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> According to legend, Myron Selznick took Leigh and Olivier to the set where the burning of the <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a> Depot scene was being filmed and stage-managed an encounter, where he introduced Leigh, derisively addressing his younger brother, "Hey, genius, meet your Scarlett O'Hara."<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The following day, Leigh read a scene for Selznick, who organized a <a href="/wiki/Screen_test" title="Screen test">screen test</a> with director <a href="/wiki/George_Cukor" title="George Cukor">George Cukor</a> and wrote to his wife, "She's the Scarlett dark horse and looks damn good. Not for anyone's ear but your own: it's narrowed down to <a href="/wiki/Paulette_Goddard" title="Paulette Goddard">Paulette Goddard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Arthur" title="Jean Arthur">Jean Arthur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joan_Bennett" title="Joan Bennett">Joan Bennett</a> and Vivien Leigh".<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The director, George Cukor, concurred and praised Leigh's "incredible wildness". She secured the role of Scarlett soon after.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg/220px-Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg/330px-Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg/440px-Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg 2x" data-file-width="996" data-file-height="731" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_Gone_Wind_Restored.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leigh's portrayal of <a href="/wiki/Scarlett_O%27Hara" title="Scarlett O&#39;Hara">Scarlett O'Hara</a></div></div></div><p>Filming proved difficult for Leigh. Cukor was dismissed and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Victor_Fleming" title="Victor Fleming">Victor Fleming</a>, with whom Leigh frequently quarrelled. She and <a href="/wiki/Olivia_de_Havilland" title="Olivia de Havilland">Olivia de Havilland</a> secretly met with Cukor at night and on weekends for his advice about how they should play their parts.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._22_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._22-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Leigh befriended Clark Gable, his wife <a href="/wiki/Carole_Lombard" title="Carole Lombard">Carole Lombard</a> and Olivia de Havilland; but she clashed with <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Howard_(actor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Leslie Howard (actor)">Leslie Howard</a>, with whom she was required to play several emotional scenes.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_p._22_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_p._22-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Leigh was sometimes required to work seven days a week, often late into the night, which added to her distress; and she missed Olivier, who was working in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_1984,_pp._22–23_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_1984,_pp._22–23-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> On a long-distance telephone call to Olivier, she declared: "Puss, my puss, how I hate film acting! Hate, hate, and never want to do another film again!"<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_1984,_pp._22–23_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_1984,_pp._22–23-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Quoted in a 2006 biography of Olivier, Olivia de Havilland defended Leigh against claims of her <a href="/wiki/Mania" title="Mania">manic behaviour</a> during the filming of <i>Gone with the Wind</i>: "Vivien was impeccably professional, impeccably disciplined on <i>Gone with the Wind</i>. She had two great concerns: doing her best work in an extremely difficult role and being separated from Larry [Olivier], who was in New York."<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Gone with the Wind</i> brought Leigh immediate attention and fame; but she was quoted as saying, "I'm not a film star&#160;– I'm an actress. Being a film star&#160;– just a film star&#160;– is such a false life, lived for fake values and for publicity. Actresses go on for a long time and there are always marvellous parts to play."<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor_1984,_pp._22–23_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor_1984,_pp._22–23-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The film won 10 <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a> including a <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="Academy Award for Best Actress">Best Actress</a> award for Leigh,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> who also won a <a href="/wiki/New_York_Film_Critics_Circle_Award_for_Best_Actress" title="New York Film Critics Circle Award for Best Actress">New York Film Critics Circle Award for Best Actress</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NYFCC1939_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYFCC1939-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1940.E2.80.931949:_Marriage_and_early_joint_projects_with_Olivier"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1940–1949:_Marriage_and_early_joint_projects_with_Olivier">1940–1949: Marriage and early joint projects with Olivier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1940–1949: Marriage and early joint projects with Olivier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 1940, Jill Esmond agreed to divorce Laurence Olivier, and Leigh Holman agreed to divorce Vivien, although they maintained a strong friendship for the rest of Leigh's life. Esmond was granted custody of Tarquin, her son with Olivier. Holman was granted custody of Suzanne, his daughter with Leigh. On 31 August 1940, Olivier and Leigh were married at the <a href="/wiki/San_Ysidro_Ranch" title="San Ysidro Ranch">San Ysidro Ranch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara</a>, California, in a ceremony attended only by their hosts, <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Colman" title="Ronald Colman">Ronald</a> and Benita Colman and witnesses, <a href="/wiki/Katharine_Hepburn" title="Katharine Hepburn">Katharine Hepburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Garson_Kanin" title="Garson Kanin">Garson Kanin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Leigh had made a screen test and hoped to co-star with Olivier in <i><a href="/wiki/Rebecca_(1940_film)" title="Rebecca (1940 film)">Rebecca</a></i>, which was to be directed by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a> with Olivier in the leading role. After viewing Leigh's screen test, David Selznick noted that "she doesn't seem right as to sincerity or age or innocence", a view shared by Hitchcock and Leigh's mentor, George Cukor.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Selznick observed that she had shown no enthusiasm for the part until Olivier had been confirmed as the lead actor, so he cast <a href="/wiki/Joan_Fontaine" title="Joan Fontaine">Joan Fontaine</a>. He refused to allow her to join Olivier in <i><a href="/wiki/Pride_and_Prejudice_(1940_film)" title="Pride and Prejudice (1940 film)">Pride and Prejudice</a></i> (1940), and <a href="/wiki/Greer_Garson" title="Greer Garson">Greer Garson</a> played the role Leigh had wanted for herself.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Waterloo_Bridge_(1940_film)" title="Waterloo Bridge (1940 film)">Waterloo Bridge</a></i> (1940) was to have starred Olivier and Leigh; however, Selznick replaced Olivier with Robert Taylor, then at the peak of his success as one of <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer</a>'s most popular male stars.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Her top billing reflected her status in Hollywood, and the film was popular with audiences and critics.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg/220px-Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg" width="220" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg/330px-Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg/440px-Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg 2x" data-file-width="734" data-file-height="652" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivian_Leigh_Laurence_Olivier_That_Hamilton_Woman.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leigh and <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/That_Hamilton_Woman" title="That Hamilton Woman">That Hamilton Woman</a></i> (1941)</div></div></div>
<p>The Oliviers mounted a stage production of <i><a href="/wiki/Romeo_and_Juliet" title="Romeo and Juliet">Romeo and Juliet</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a>. The New York press publicised the adulterous nature of the beginning of Olivier and Leigh's relationship and questioned their ethics in not returning to the UK to help with the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">war</a> effort.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;Note 3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Critics were hostile in their assessment of <i>Romeo and Juliet</i>. <a href="/wiki/Brooks_Atkinson" title="Brooks Atkinson">Brooks Atkinson</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> wrote: "Although Miss Leigh and Mr. Olivier are handsome young people, they hardly act their parts at all."<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> While most of the blame was attributed to Olivier's acting and direction, Leigh was also criticised, with <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Grebanier" title="Bernard Grebanier">Bernard Grebanier</a> commenting on the "thin, shopgirl quality of Miss Leigh's voice".<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The couple had invested almost all of their combined savings of $40,000 in the project, and the failure was a financial disaster for them.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Oliviers filmed <i><a href="/wiki/That_Hamilton_Woman" title="That Hamilton Woman">That Hamilton Woman</a></i> (1941) with Olivier as <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson" class="mw-redirect" title="Horatio Nelson">Horatio Nelson</a> and Leigh as <a href="/wiki/Emma_Hamilton" class="mw-redirect" title="Emma Hamilton">Emma Hamilton</a>. With the United States not yet having entered the war, it was one of several Hollywood films made with the aim of arousing a pro-British sentiment among American audiences.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> The film was popular in the United States and an outstanding success in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> arranged a screening for a party that included <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> and, on its conclusion, addressed the group, saying, "Gentlemen, I thought this film would interest you, showing great events similar to those in which you have just been taking part." The Oliviers remained favourites of Churchill, attending dinners and occasions at his request for the rest of his life; and, of Leigh, he was quoted as saying, "By Jove, she's a clinker."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Oliviers returned to Britain in March 1943,<sup id="cite_ref-Click.NPM_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Click.NPM-69">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> and Leigh toured through North Africa that same year as part of a revue for the armed forces stationed in the region. She reportedly turned down a studio contract worth $5,000 a week to volunteer as part of the war effort.<sup id="cite_ref-Click.NPM_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Click.NPM-69">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Leigh performed for troops before falling ill with a persistent cough and fevers.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> In 1944 she was diagnosed as having tuberculosis in her left <a href="/wiki/Human_lung" class="mw-redirect" title="Human lung">lung</a> and spent several weeks in hospital before appearing to have recovered. Leigh was filming <i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_and_Cleopatra_(film)" title="Caesar and Cleopatra (film)">Caesar and Cleopatra</a></i> (1945) when she discovered she was pregnant, then had a <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">miscarriage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Leigh temporarily fell into a deep depression that hit its low point, with her falling to the floor, sobbing in an hysterical fit.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> This was the first of many major bipolar disorder breakdowns. Olivier later came to recognise the symptoms of an impending episode&#160;– several days of hyperactivity followed by a period of depression and an explosive breakdown, after which Leigh would have no memory of the event, but would be acutely embarrassed and remorseful.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier,_Broadbeach,_Australia,_1948.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier%2C_Broadbeach%2C_Australia%2C_1948.jpg/220px-Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier%2C_Broadbeach%2C_Australia%2C_1948.jpg" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier%2C_Broadbeach%2C_Australia%2C_1948.jpg/330px-Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier%2C_Broadbeach%2C_Australia%2C_1948.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier%2C_Broadbeach%2C_Australia%2C_1948.jpg/440px-Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier%2C_Broadbeach%2C_Australia%2C_1948.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="391" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_and_Laurence_Olivier,_Broadbeach,_Australia,_1948.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Leigh and Olivier in Australia, June 1948</div></div></div>
<p>With her doctor's approval, Leigh was well enough to resume acting in 1946, starring in a successful London production of <a href="/wiki/Thornton_Wilder" title="Thornton Wilder">Thornton Wilder</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Skin_of_Our_Teeth" title="The Skin of Our Teeth">The Skin of Our Teeth</a></i>; but her films of this period, <i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_and_Cleopatra_(film)" title="Caesar and Cleopatra (film)">Caesar and Cleopatra</a></i> (1945) and <i><a href="/wiki/Anna_Karenina_(1948_film)" title="Anna Karenina (1948 film)">Anna Karenina</a></i> (1948), were not great commercial successes. All British films in this period were adversely affected by a Hollywood boycott of British films.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> In 1947 Olivier was <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighted</a> and Leigh accompanied him to <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Palace" title="Buckingham Palace">Buckingham Palace</a> for the investiture. She became Lady Olivier.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> After their divorce, according to the style granted to the divorced wife of a knight, she became known socially as Vivien, Lady Olivier.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1948 Olivier was on the board of directors for the Old Vic Theatre, and he and Leigh embarked on a six-month tour of Australia and New Zealand to raise funds for the theatre. Olivier played the lead in <i><a href="/wiki/Richard_III_(play)" title="Richard III (play)">Richard III</a></i> and also performed with Leigh in <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_for_Scandal" title="The School for Scandal">The School for Scandal</a></i> and <i>The Skin of Our Teeth</i>. The tour was an outstanding success and, although Leigh was plagued with <a href="/wiki/Insomnia" title="Insomnia">insomnia</a> and allowed her understudy to replace her for a week while she was ill, she generally withstood the demands placed upon her, with Olivier noting her ability to "charm the press". Members of the company later recalled several quarrels between the couple as Olivier was increasingly resentful of the demands placed on him during the tour.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The most dramatic altercation occurred in <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a>, New Zealand, when her shoes were not found and Leigh refused to go onstage without them. An exhausted and exasperated Olivier screamed an obscenity at her and slapped her face, and a devastated Leigh slapped him in return, dismayed that he would hit her publicly. Subsequently, she made her way to the stage in borrowed pumps, and in seconds, had "dried her tears and smiled brightly onstage".<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> By the end of the tour, both were exhausted and ill. Olivier told a journalist, "You may not know it, but you are talking to a couple of walking corpses." Later, he would observe that he "lost Vivien" in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The success of the tour encouraged the Oliviers to make their first West End appearance together, performing the same works with one addition, <i><a href="/wiki/Antigone_(Sophocles)" class="mw-redirect" title="Antigone (Sophocles)">Antigone</a>,</i> included at Leigh's insistence because she wished to play a role in a <a href="/wiki/Tragedy" title="Tragedy">tragedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1949.E2.80.931951:_Play_and_film_roles_in_A_Streetcar_Named_Desire"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1949–1951:_Play_and_film_roles_in_A_Streetcar_Named_Desire">1949–1951: Play and film roles in <i>A Streetcar Named Desire</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1949–1951: Play and film roles in A Streetcar Named Desire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg/220px-Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg/330px-Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg/440px-Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="706" data-file-height="540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_in_Streetcar_Named_Desire_trailer_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>As Blanche DuBois, from the trailer for the film version of <a href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(1951_film)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (1951 film)"><i>A Streetcar Named Desire</i> (1951)</a></div></div></div>
<p>Leigh next sought the role of <a href="/wiki/Blanche_DuBois" title="Blanche DuBois">Blanche DuBois</a> in the West End stage production of <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Williams" title="Tennessee Williams">Tennessee Williams</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (play)">A Streetcar Named Desire</a></i> and was cast after Williams and the play's producer <a href="/wiki/Irene_Mayer_Selznick" title="Irene Mayer Selznick">Irene Mayer Selznick</a> saw her in <i><a href="/wiki/The_School_for_Scandal" title="The School for Scandal">The School for Scandal</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Antigone" title="Antigone">Antigone</a></i>; Olivier was contracted to direct.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> The play contained a <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a> scene and references to <a href="/wiki/Promiscuity" title="Promiscuity">promiscuity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a>, and was destined to be controversial; the media discussion about its suitability added to Leigh's anxiety. Nevertheless, she believed strongly in the importance of the work.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When the West End production of <i>Streetcar</i> opened in October 1949, <a href="/wiki/J._B._Priestley" title="J. B. Priestley">J. B. Priestley</a> denounced the play and Leigh's performance; and the critic <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Tynan" title="Kenneth Tynan">Kenneth Tynan</a>, who was to make a habit of dismissing her stage performances,<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> commented that Leigh was badly miscast because British actors were "too well-bred to emote effectively on stage". Olivier and Leigh were chagrined that part of the commercial success of the play lay in audience members attending to see what they believed would be a salacious story, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Greek_tragedy" title="Greek tragedy">Greek tragedy</a> that they envisioned. The play also had strong supporters,<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> among them Noël Coward, who described Leigh as "magnificent".<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 326 performances, Leigh finished her run, and she was soon assigned to reprise her role as Blanche DuBois in the <a href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(1951_film)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (1951 film)">film version</a> of the play.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;Note 4&#93;</a></sup> Her irreverent and often bawdy sense of humour allowed her to establish a rapport with Brando, but she had an initial difficulty in working with director <a href="/wiki/Elia_Kazan" title="Elia Kazan">Elia Kazan</a>, who was displeased with the direction that Olivier had taken in shaping the character of Blanche.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Kazan had favoured <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Tandy" title="Jessica Tandy">Jessica Tandy</a> and later, Olivia de Havilland over Leigh, but knew she had been a success on the London stage as Blanche.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_p._167_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_p._167-86">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> He later commented that he did not hold her in high regard as an actress, believing that "she had a small talent." As work progressed, however, he became "full of admiration" for "the greatest determination to excel of any actress I've known. She'd have crawled over broken glass if she thought it would help her performance." Leigh found the role gruelling and commented to the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i>, "I had nine months in the theatre of Blanche DuBois. Now she's in command of me."<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Olivier accompanied her to Hollywood where he was to co-star with <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Jones" title="Jennifer Jones">Jennifer Jones</a> in <a href="/wiki/William_Wyler" title="William Wyler">William Wyler</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Carrie_(1952_film)" title="Carrie (1952 film)">Carrie</a></i> (1952).
</p><p>Leigh's performance in <i>A Streetcar Named Desire</i> won glowing reviews, as well as a second Academy Award for Best Actress,<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">British Academy of Film and Television Arts</a> (BAFTA) Award for Best British Actress, and a New York Film Critics Circle Award for Best Actress.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Tennessee Williams commented that Leigh brought to the role "everything that I intended, and much that I had never dreamed of". Leigh herself had mixed feelings about her association with the character; in later years, she said that playing Blanche DuBois "tipped me over into madness".<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1951.E2.80.931960:_Struggle_with_mental_illness"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1951–1960:_Struggle_with_mental_illness">1951–1960: Struggle with mental illness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1951–1960: Struggle with mental illness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1951, Leigh and Laurence Olivier performed two plays about <a href="/wiki/Cleopatra_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleopatra VII">Cleopatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Antony_and_Cleopatra" title="Antony and Cleopatra">Antony and Cleopatra</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Caesar_and_Cleopatra_(play)" title="Caesar and Cleopatra (play)">Caesar and Cleopatra</a></i>, alternating the play each night and winning good reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> They took the productions to New York, where they performed a season at the <a href="/wiki/Ziegfeld_Theatre_(1927)" title="Ziegfeld Theatre (1927)">Ziegfeld Theatre</a> into 1952.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The reviews there were also mostly positive, but film critic Kenneth Tynan angered them when he suggested that Leigh's was a mediocre talent that forced Olivier to compromise his own.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Tynan's diatribe almost precipitated another collapse; Leigh, terrified of failure and intent on achieving greatness, dwelt on his comments and ignored the positive reviews of other critics.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 1953, Leigh travelled to <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Ceylon</a> to film <i><a href="/wiki/Elephant_Walk" title="Elephant Walk">Elephant Walk</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Finch" title="Peter Finch">Peter Finch</a>. Shortly after filming commenced, she had a nervous breakdown and <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> replaced her with <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Taylor" title="Elizabeth Taylor">Elizabeth Taylor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> Olivier returned her to their home in Britain, where, between periods of incoherence, Leigh told him she was in love with Finch and had been having an affair with him.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> Over a period of several months, she gradually recovered. As a result of this episode, many of the Oliviers' friends learned of her problems. <a href="/wiki/David_Niven" title="David Niven">David Niven</a> said she had been "quite, quite mad". Noël Coward expressed surprise in his diary that "things had been bad and getting worse since 1948 or thereabouts".<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Leigh's romantic relationship with Finch began in 1948, and waxed and waned for several years, ultimately flickering out as her mental condition deteriorated.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks-100">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in 1953, Leigh recovered sufficiently to play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sleeping_Prince_(play)" title="The Sleeping Prince (play)">The Sleeping Prince</a></i> with Olivier, and in 1955 they performed a season at <a href="/wiki/Stratford-upon-Avon" title="Stratford-upon-Avon">Stratford-upon-Avon</a> in Shakespeare's <i><a href="/wiki/Twelfth_Night,_or_What_You_Will" class="mw-redirect" title="Twelfth Night, or What You Will">Twelfth Night</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Macbeth" title="Macbeth">Macbeth</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Titus_Andronicus" title="Titus Andronicus">Titus Andronicus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> They played to capacity houses and attracted generally good reviews, Leigh's health seemingly stable. <a href="/wiki/John_Gielgud" title="John Gielgud">John Gielgud</a> directed <i>Twelfth Night</i> and wrote, "...&#160;perhaps I will still make a good thing of that divine play, especially if he will let me pull her little ladyship (who is brainier than he but <i>not</i> a born actress) out of her timidity and safeness. He dares too confidently&#160;... but she hardly dares at all and is terrified of overreaching her technique and doing anything that she has not killed the spontaneity of by overpractice."<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> In 1955 Leigh starred in <a href="/wiki/Anatole_Litvak" title="Anatole Litvak">Anatole Litvak</a>'s film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Deep_Blue_Sea_(1955_film)" title="The Deep Blue Sea (1955 film)">The Deep Blue Sea</a></i>; co-star <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_More" title="Kenneth More">Kenneth More</a> felt he had poor chemistry with Leigh during the filming.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg/220px-Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg/330px-Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg/440px-Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg 2x" data-file-width="477" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_1958.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photograph by <a href="/wiki/Roloff_Beny" title="Roloff Beny">Roloff Beny</a>, 1958</div></div></div>
<p>In 1956, Leigh took the lead role in the Noël Coward play <i><a href="/wiki/South_Sea_Bubble_(play)" title="South Sea Bubble (play)">South Sea Bubble</a></i>, but withdrew from the production when she became pregnant. Several weeks later, she miscarried and entered a period of <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">depression</a> that lasted for months.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> She joined Olivier for a European tour of <i>Titus Andronicus</i>, but the tour was marred by Leigh's frequent outbursts against Olivier and other members of the company. After their return to London, her former husband, Leigh Holman, who could still exert a strong influence on her, stayed with the Oliviers and helped calm her.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1958, considering her marriage to be over, Leigh began a relationship with actor <a href="/wiki/John_Merivale" title="John Merivale">Jack Merivale</a>, who knew of Leigh's medical condition and assured Olivier that he would care for her. In 1959, when she achieved a success with the Noël Coward comedy <i><a href="/wiki/Look_After_Lulu!" title="Look After Lulu!">Look After Lulu!</a></i>, a critic working for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> described her as "beautiful, delectably cool and matter of fact, she is mistress of every situation".<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960 she and Olivier divorced and Olivier soon married actress <a href="/wiki/Joan_Plowright" title="Joan Plowright">Joan Plowright</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> In his autobiography, Olivier discussed the years of strain they had experienced because of Leigh's illness: "Throughout her possession by that uncannily evil monster, manic depression, with its deadly ever-tightening spirals, she retained her own individual canniness&#160;– an ability to disguise her true mental condition from almost all except me, for whom she could hardly be expected to take the trouble."<sup id="cite_ref-Laurence_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laurence-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1961.E2.80.931967:_Final_years_and_death"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1961–1967:_Final_years_and_death">1961–1967: Final years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1961–1967: Final years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Merivale proved to be a stabilising influence for Leigh, but despite her apparent contentment, she was quoted by <a href="/wiki/Radie_Harris" title="Radie Harris">Radie Harris</a> as confiding that she "would rather have lived a short life with Larry [Olivier] than face a long one without him".<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> Her first husband Leigh Holman also spent considerable time with her. Merivale joined her for a tour of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">Latin America</a> that lasted from July 1961 until May 1962, and Leigh enjoyed positive reviews without sharing the spotlight with Olivier.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> Though she was still beset by bouts of depression, she continued to work in the theatre and, in 1963, won a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a> for Best Actress in a Musical for her role in <i><a href="/wiki/Tovarich_(musical)" title="Tovarich (musical)">Tovarich</a></i>. She also appeared in the films <i><a href="/wiki/The_Roman_Spring_of_Mrs._Stone" title="The Roman Spring of Mrs. Stone">The Roman Spring of Mrs. Stone</a></i> (1961) and <i><a href="/wiki/Ship_of_Fools_(film)" title="Ship of Fools (film)">Ship of Fools</a></i> (1965).<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Leigh's last screen appearance in <i>Ship of Fools</i> was both a triumph and emblematic of her illnesses that were taking root. Producer and director Stanley Kramer, who ended up with the film, planned to star Leigh but was initially unaware of her fragile mental and physical state.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;Note 5&#93;</a></sup> Later recounting her work, Kramer remembered her courage in taking on the difficult role, "She was ill, and the courage to go ahead, the courage to make the film&#160;– was almost unbelievable."<sup id="cite_ref-Articles_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Articles-113">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> Leigh's performance was tinged by paranoia and resulted in outbursts that marred her relationship with other actors, although both <a href="/wiki/Simone_Signoret" title="Simone Signoret">Simone Signoret</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Marvin" title="Lee Marvin">Lee Marvin</a> were sympathetic and understanding.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> In one unusual instance during the attempted rape scene, Leigh became distraught and hit Marvin so hard with a spiked shoe that it marked his face.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> Leigh won the <i>L'Étoile de Cristal</i> for her performance in a leading role in <i>Ship of Fools</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bean_p._279._116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bean_p._279.-116">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">&#91;Note 6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 1967 Leigh was rehearsing to appear with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Redgrave" title="Michael Redgrave">Michael Redgrave</a> in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Albee" title="Edward Albee">Edward Albee</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/A_Delicate_Balance_(play)" title="A Delicate Balance (play)">A Delicate Balance</a></i> when her tuberculosis recurred.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> Following several weeks of rest, she seemed to recover. On the night of 7 July 1967, Merivale left her as usual at their Eaton Square flat to perform in a play, and he returned home just before midnight to find her asleep. About 30 minutes later (by now 8 July), he entered the bedroom and discovered her body on the floor. She had been attempting to walk to the bathroom and, as her lungs filled with liquid, she collapsed and suffocated.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup> Merivale first contacted her family and later was able to reach Olivier, who was receiving treatment for <a href="/wiki/Prostate_cancer" title="Prostate cancer">prostate cancer</a> in a nearby hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-Edwards_p._284_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards_p._284-120">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> In his autobiography, Olivier described his "grievous anguish" as he immediately travelled to Leigh's residence, to find that Merivale had moved her body onto the bed. Olivier paid his respects, and "stood and prayed for forgiveness for all the evils that had sprung up between us",<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup> before helping Merivale make funeral arrangements; Olivier stayed until her body was removed from the flat.<sup id="cite_ref-Edwards_p._284_120-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Edwards_p._284-120">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">&#91;Note 7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Her death was publicly announced on 8 July, and the lights of every theatre in central London were extinguished for an hour.<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Catholic</a> service for Leigh was held at <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s,_Cadogan_Street" title="St Mary&#39;s, Cadogan Street">St. Mary's Church, Cadogan Street</a>, London. Her funeral was attended by the luminaries of British stage and screen.<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup> According to the provisions of her will, Leigh was <a href="/wiki/Cremation" title="Cremation">cremated</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Golders_Green_Crematorium" title="Golders Green Crematorium">Golders Green Crematorium</a> and her ashes were scattered on the lake at her summer home, Tickerage Mill, near <a href="/wiki/Blackboys" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackboys">Blackboys</a>, East Sussex, England.<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> A memorial service was held at <a href="/wiki/St_Martin-in-the-Fields" title="St Martin-in-the-Fields">St Martin-in-the-Fields</a>, with a final tribute read by John Gielgud.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> In 1968 Leigh became the first actress honoured in the United States, by "The Friends of the Libraries at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Southern_California" title="University of Southern California">University of Southern California</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> The ceremony was conducted as a memorial service, with selections from her films shown and tributes provided by such associates as George Cukor, who screened the tests that Leigh had made for <i>Gone with the Wind</i>, the first time the screen tests had been seen in 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg/220px-Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg/330px-Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg/440px-Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vivien_Leigh_blue_plaque.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/English_heritage" class="mw-redirect" title="English heritage">English heritage</a> <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> at Leigh's final home at 54 Eaton Square in <a href="/wiki/Belgravia" title="Belgravia">Belgravia</a></div></div></div>
<p>Leigh was considered to be one of the most beautiful actresses of her day, and her directors emphasised this in most of her films. When asked if she believed her beauty had been an impediment to being taken seriously as an actress, she said, "People think that if you look fairly reasonable, you can't possibly act, and as I only care about acting, I think beauty can be a great handicap, if you really want to look like the part you're playing, which isn't necessarily like you."<sup id="cite_ref-ActorTalk_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ActorTalk-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Director George Cukor described Leigh as a "consummate actress, hampered by beauty",<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup> and Laurence Olivier said that critics should "give her credit for being an actress and not go on forever letting their judgments be distorted by her great beauty."<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> Garson Kanin shared their viewpoint and described Leigh as "a stunner whose ravishing beauty often tended to obscure her staggering achievements as an actress. Great beauties are infrequently great actresses&#160;– simply because they don't need to be. Vivien was different; ambitious, persevering, serious, often inspired."<sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Leigh explained that she played "as many different parts as possible" in an attempt to learn her craft and to dispel prejudice about her abilities. She believed that comedy was more difficult to play than drama because it required more precise timing and said that more emphasis should be placed upon comedy as part of an actor's training. Nearing the end of her career, which ranged from Noël Coward comedies to Shakespearean tragedies, she observed, "It's much easier to make people cry than to make them laugh."<sup id="cite_ref-ActorTalk_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ActorTalk-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Her early performances brought her immediate success in Britain, but she remained largely unknown in other parts of the world until the release of <i>Gone with the Wind</i>. In December 1939, film critic <a href="/wiki/Frank_Nugent" title="Frank Nugent">Frank Nugent</a> wrote in <i>The New York Times</i>, "Miss Leigh's Scarlett has vindicated the absurd talent quest that indirectly turned her up. She is so perfectly designed for the part by art and nature that any other actress in the role would be inconceivable",<sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup> and as her fame escalated, she was featured on the cover of <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine as Scarlett. In 1969 critic <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Sarris" title="Andrew Sarris">Andrew Sarris</a> commented that the success of the film had been largely due to "the inspired casting" of Leigh,<sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup> and in 1998, wrote that "she lives in our minds and memories as a dynamic force rather than as a static presence".<sup id="cite_ref-135" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-135">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> Film historian and critic <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Maltin" title="Leonard Maltin">Leonard Maltin</a> described the film as one of the all-time greats, writing in 1998 that Leigh "brilliantly played" her role.<sup id="cite_ref-136" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-136">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Her performance in the West End production of <i>A Streetcar Named Desire</i>, described by the theatre writer <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Hartnoll" title="Phyllis Hartnoll">Phyllis Hartnoll</a> as "proof of greater powers as an actress than she had hitherto shown", led to a lengthy period during which she was considered one of the finest actresses in British theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-137" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-137">&#91;130&#93;</a></sup> Discussing the subsequent film version, <a href="/wiki/Pauline_Kael" title="Pauline Kael">Pauline Kael</a> wrote that Leigh and Marlon Brando gave "two of the greatest performances ever put on film" and that Leigh's was "one of those rare performances that can truly be said to evoke both fear and pity."<sup id="cite_ref-138" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-138">&#91;131&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Her greatest critic was Kenneth Tynan who ridiculed Leigh's performance opposite Olivier in the 1955 production of <i>Titus Andronicus</i>, commenting that she "receives the news that she is about to be ravished on her husband's corpse with little more than the mild annoyance of one who would have preferred foam rubber."<sup id="cite_ref-139" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-139">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup> He was also critical of her reinterpretation of <a href="/wiki/Lady_Macbeth_(Shakespeare)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lady Macbeth (Shakespeare)">Lady Macbeth</a> in 1955, saying that her performance was insubstantial and lacked the necessary fury demanded of the role.<sup id="cite_ref-Bean_p._181_140-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bean_p._181-140">&#91;133&#93;</a></sup> After her death, however, Tynan revised his opinion, describing his earlier criticism as "one of the worst errors of judgment" he had ever made. He came to believe that Leigh's interpretation, in which Lady Macbeth uses her sexual allure to keep Macbeth enthralled, "made more sense&#160;... than the usual battle-axe" portrayal of the character.<sup id="cite_ref-141" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-141">&#91;Note 8&#93;</a></sup> In a survey of theatre critics conducted shortly after Leigh's death, several named her performance as Lady Macbeth as one of her greatest achievements in theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-142" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-142">&#91;134&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1969 a plaque to Leigh was placed in the Actors' Church, <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s,_Covent_Garden" title="St Paul&#39;s, Covent Garden">St Paul's, Covent Garden</a>, London. In 1985 a portrait of her was included in a series of United Kingdom postage stamps, along with Sir Alfred Hitchcock, Sir <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sellers" title="Peter Sellers">Peter Sellers</a> and David Niven to commemorate "British Film Year".<sup id="cite_ref-143" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-143">&#91;135&#93;</a></sup> In April 2013 she was again included in another series, this time celebrating the 100th anniversary of her birth, achieving the rare accolade for a non-royal of appearing on British stamps on more than one occasion.
The <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> in London purchased the papers of Laurence Olivier from his estate in 1999. Known as <i>The Laurence Olivier Archive</i>, the collection includes many of Vivien Leigh's personal papers, including numerous letters she wrote to Olivier. The papers of Vivien Leigh, including letters, photographs, contracts and diaries, are owned by her daughter, Mrs. Suzanne Farrington. In 1994 the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Australia" title="National Library of Australia">National Library of Australia</a> purchased a photograph album, monogrammed "L &amp; V O" and believed to have belonged to the Oliviers, containing 573 photographs of the couple during their 1948 tour of Australia. It is now held as part of the record of the history of the performing arts in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-144" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-144">&#91;136&#93;</a></sup> In 2013, an archive of Vivien Leigh's letters, diaries, photographs, annotated film and theatre scripts and her numerous awards was acquired by the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-145" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-145">&#91;137&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vivien_Leigh&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<li id="cite_note-87"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-87">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Leigh's fee of $100,000 for <i><a href="/wiki/A_Streetcar_Named_Desire_(1951_film)" title="A Streetcar Named Desire (1951 film)">A Streetcar Named Desire</a></i> made her the highest paid British actress in 1951; her costar, <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a>, was paid $75,000 for his role as <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kowalski" title="Stanley Kowalski">Stanley Kowalski</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_p._167_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker_p._167-86">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup></span>
