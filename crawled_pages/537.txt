<p><b>William Wordsworth</b> (7 April 1770&#160;– 23 April 1850) was a major English <a href="/wiki/Romantic_poetry" title="Romantic poetry">Romantic</a> poet who, with <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a>, helped to launch the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic Age</a> in <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a> with their joint publication <i><a href="/wiki/Lyrical_Ballads" title="Lyrical Ballads">Lyrical Ballads</a></i> (1798).
</p><p>Wordsworth's <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">magnum opus</a> is generally considered to be <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prelude" title="The Prelude">The Prelude</a></i>, a semi-autobiographical poem of his early years that he revised and expanded a number of times. It was posthumously titled and published, before which it was generally known as "the poem to Coleridge".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Wordsworth was Britain's <a href="/wiki/Poet_laureate" title="Poet laureate">poet laureate</a> from 1843 until his death from <a href="/wiki/Pleurisy" title="Pleurisy">pleurisy</a> on 23 April 1850.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Early_life_of_William_Wordsworth" title="Early life of William Wordsworth">Early life of William Wordsworth</a></div>
<p>The second of five children born to John Wordsworth and Ann Cookson, William Wordsworth was born on 7 April 1770 in what is now named <a href="/wiki/Wordsworth_House" title="Wordsworth House">Wordsworth House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cockermouth" title="Cockermouth">Cockermouth</a>, Cumberland,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> part of the scenic region in northwestern England known as the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a>. His sister, the poet and diarist <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Wordsworth" title="Dorothy Wordsworth">Dorothy Wordsworth</a>, to whom he was close all his life, was born the following year, and the two were baptised together. They had three other siblings: Richard, the eldest, who became a lawyer; John, born after Dorothy, who went to sea and died in 1805 when the ship of which he was captain, the <i><a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Abergavenny_(1796_EIC_ship)" title="Earl of Abergavenny (1796 EIC ship)">Earl of Abergavenny</a></i>, was wrecked off the south coast of England; and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wordsworth_(Trinity)" title="Christopher Wordsworth (Trinity)">Christopher</a>, the youngest, who entered the Church and rose to be Master of <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wordsworth's father was a legal representative of <a href="/wiki/James_Lowther,_1st_Earl_of_Lonsdale" title="James Lowther, 1st Earl of Lonsdale">James Lowther, 1st Earl of Lonsdale</a> and, through his connections, lived in a large mansion in the small town. He was frequently away from home on business, so the young William and his siblings had little involvement with him and remained distant from him until his death in 1783.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, he did encourage William in his reading, and in particular set him to commit large portions of verse to memory, including works by <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">Milton</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Spenser" title="Edmund Spenser">Spenser</a>. William was also allowed to use his father's library.
William also spent time at his mother's parents' house in <a href="/wiki/Penrith,_Cumbria" title="Penrith, Cumbria">Penrith</a>, Cumberland, where he was exposed to the moors, but did not get along with his grandparents or his uncle, who also lived there. His hostile interactions with them distressed him to the point of contemplating suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wordsworth was taught to read by his mother and attended, first, a tiny school of low quality in Cockermouth, then a school in Penrith for the children of upper-class families, where he was taught by Ann Birkett, who insisted on instilling in her students traditions that included pursuing both scholarly and local activities, especially the festivals around Easter, May Day and <a href="/wiki/Shrove_Tuesday" title="Shrove Tuesday">Shrove Tuesday</a>. Wordsworth was taught both the Bible and the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Spectator_(1711)" title="The Spectator (1711)">Spectator</a></i>, but little else. It was at the school in Penrith that he met the Hutchinsons, including Mary, who later became his wife.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the death of Wordsworth's mother, in 1778, his father sent him to <a href="/wiki/Hawkshead_Grammar_School" title="Hawkshead Grammar School">Hawkshead Grammar School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> (now in <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>) and sent Dorothy to live with relatives in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>. She and William did not meet again for another nine years.
</p><p>Wordsworth made his debut as a writer in 1787 when he published a sonnet in <i><a href="/wiki/The_European_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="The European Magazine">The European Magazine</a></i>. That same year he began attending <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="St John&#39;s College, Cambridge">St John's College, Cambridge</a>. He received his BA degree in 1791.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He returned to Hawkshead for the first two summers of his time at Cambridge, and often spent later holidays on <a href="/wiki/Hiking#Walking_tours_today" title="Hiking">walking tours</a>, visiting places famous for the beauty of their <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">landscape</a>. In 1790 he went on a walking tour of Europe, during which he toured the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> extensively, and visited nearby areas of France, Switzerland, and Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett2015_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett2015-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_Annette_Vallon">Relationship with Annette Vallon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relationship with Annette Vallon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 1791, Wordsworth visited <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolutionary France">Revolutionary France</a> and became enchanted with the Republican movement. He fell in love with a French woman, Annette Vallon, who in 1792 gave birth to their daughter Caroline. Financial problems and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>'s tense relations with France forced him to return to England alone the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  The circumstances of his return and his subsequent behaviour raised doubts as to his declared wish to marry Annette, but he supported her and his daughter as best he could in later life. The <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">Reign of Terror</a> left Wordsworth thoroughly disillusioned with the French Revolution and the outbreak of armed hostilities between Britain and France prevented him from seeing Annette and his daughter for some years.
</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Amiens" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace of Amiens">Peace of Amiens</a> again allowing travel to France, in 1802 Wordsworth and his sister Dorothy visited Annette and Caroline in <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a>. The purpose of the visit was to prepare Annette for the fact of his forthcoming marriage to Mary Hutchinson.<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Afterwards he wrote the sonnet "<a href="/wiki/It_is_a_beauteous_evening,_calm_and_free" title="It is a beauteous evening, calm and free">It is a beauteous evening, calm and free</a>," recalling a seaside walk with the 9-year-old Caroline, whom he had never seen before that visit. Mary was anxious that Wordsworth should do more for Caroline. Upon Caroline's marriage, in 1816, Wordsworth settled £30 a year on her (equivalent to £1360 as of the year 2000), payments which continued until 1835, when they were replaced by a capital settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_publication_and_Lyrical_Ballads">First publication and <i>Lyrical Ballads</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First publication and Lyrical Ballads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg/220px-William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg" width="220" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg/330px-William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg/440px-William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1896" data-file-height="2244" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Wordsworth_at_28_by_William_Shuter2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wordsworth in 1798, about the time he began <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prelude" title="The Prelude">The Prelude</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div><p> The year 1793 saw the first publication of poems by Wordsworth, in the collections <i>An Evening Walk</i> and <i>Descriptive Sketches</i>. In 1795 he received a legacy of 900 pounds from Raisley Calvert and became able to pursue a career as a poet.
</p><p>It was also in 1795 that he met <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a> in Somerset. The two poets quickly developed a close friendship. In 1797, Wordsworth and his sister Dorothy moved to <a href="/wiki/Alfoxton_House" title="Alfoxton House">Alfoxton House</a>, Somerset, just a few miles away from Coleridge's home in <a href="/wiki/Nether_Stowey" title="Nether Stowey">Nether Stowey</a>. Together Wordsworth and Coleridge (with insights from Dorothy) produced <i><a href="/wiki/Lyrical_Ballads" title="Lyrical Ballads">Lyrical Ballads</a></i> (1798), an important work in the English <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romantic movement</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The volume gave neither Wordsworth's nor Coleridge's name as author. One of Wordsworth's most famous poems, "<a href="/wiki/Tintern_Abbey_(poem)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tintern Abbey (poem)">Tintern Abbey</a>", was published in this collection, along with Coleridge's "<a href="/wiki/The_Rime_of_the_Ancient_Mariner" title="The Rime of the Ancient Mariner">The Rime of the Ancient Mariner</a>". The second edition, published in 1800, had only Wordsworth listed as the author, and included a preface to the poems.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It was augmented significantly in the next edition, published in 1802.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In this preface, which some scholars consider a central work of Romantic literary theory, Wordsworth discusses what he sees as the elements of a new type of verse, one that is based on the "real language of men" and avoids the poetic diction of much 18th-century verse. Wordsworth also gives his famous definition of poetry as "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings: it takes its origin from emotion recollected in tranquility", and calls his own poems in the book "experimental". A fourth and final edition of <i>Lyrical Ballads</i> was published in 1805.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Borderers"><i>The Borderers</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Borderers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1795-97, Wordsworth wrote his only play, <i>The Borderers</i>, a verse tragedy set during the reign of <a href="/wiki/King_Henry_III_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King Henry III of England">King Henry III of England</a>, when Englishmen in the North Country came into conflict with Scottish rovers. He attempted to get the play staged in November 1797, but it was rejected by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Harris_(theatre_manager)" title="Thomas Harris (theatre manager)">Thomas Harris</a>, the manager of the <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Covent Garden Theatre">Covent Garden Theatre</a>, who proclaimed it "impossible that the play should succeed in the representation". The rebuff was not received lightly by Wordsworth and the play was not published until 1842, after substantial revision.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany_and_move_to_the_Lake_District">Germany and move to the Lake District</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Germany and move to the Lake District">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wordsworth, Dorothy and Coleridge travelled to Germany in the autumn of 1798. While Coleridge was intellectually stimulated by the journey, its main effect on Wordsworth was to produce homesickness.<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> During the harsh winter of 1798–99 Wordsworth lived with Dorothy in <a href="/wiki/Goslar" title="Goslar">Goslar</a>, and, despite extreme stress and loneliness, began work on the autobiographical piece that was later titled <i>The Prelude</i>. He wrote a number of other famous poems in Goslar, including "<a href="/wiki/The_Lucy_poems" title="The Lucy poems">The Lucy poems</a>". In the Autumn of 1799, Wordsworth and his sister returned to England and visited the Hutchinson family at Sockburn.  When Coleridge arrived back in England he travelled to the North with their publisher Joseph Cottle to meet Wordsworth and undertake a proposed tour of the Lake District.  This was the immediate cause of the siblings settling at <a href="/wiki/Dove_Cottage" title="Dove Cottage">Dove Cottage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Grasmere" title="Grasmere">Grasmere</a> in the Lake District, this time with another poet, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Southey" title="Robert Southey">Robert Southey</a> nearby. Wordsworth, Coleridge and Southey came to be known as the "<a href="/wiki/Lake_Poets" title="Lake Poets">Lake Poets</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Throughout this period many of Wordsworth's poems revolved around themes of death, endurance, separation and grief.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg/220px-Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg/330px-Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg/440px-Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dove_Cottage_-_geograph.org.uk_-_70618.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dove Cottage (Town End, Grasmere) – home of William and Dorothy Wordsworth, 1799–1808; home of Thomas De Quincey, 1809–1820</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marriage and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1802, Lowther's heir, <a href="/wiki/William_Lowther,_1st_Earl_of_Lonsdale" title="William Lowther, 1st Earl of Lonsdale">William Lowther, 1st Earl of Lonsdale</a>, paid the 4,000 pounds owed to Wordsworth's father through Lowther's failure to pay his aide.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It was this repayment that afforded Wordsworth the financial means to marry. On 4 October, following his visit with Dorothy to France to arrange matters with Annette, Wordsworth married his childhood friend Mary Hutchinson.<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Dorothy continued to live with the couple and grew close to Mary. The following year Mary gave birth to the first of five children, three of whom predeceased her and William:
</p>
<ul><li>John Wordsworth (18 June 1803 – 1875). Married four times:</li></ul>
<ol><li>Isabella Curwen (d. 1848) had six children: Jane, Henry, William, John, Charles and Edward.</li>
<li>Helen Ross (d. 1854). No children.</li>
<li>Mary Ann Dolan (d. after 1858) had one daughter Dora (b. 1858).</li>
<li>Mary Gamble. No children.</li></ol>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dora_Wordsworth" title="Dora Wordsworth">Dora Wordsworth</a> (16 August 1804 – 9 July 1847). Married <a href="/wiki/Edward_Quillinan" title="Edward Quillinan">Edward Quillinan</a> in 1841.</li>
<li>Thomas Wordsworth (15 June 1806 – 1 December 1812).</li>
<li>Catherine Wordsworth (6 September 1808 – 4 June 1812).</li>
<li>William "Willy" Wordsworth (12 May 1810 – 1883). Married Fanny Graham and had four children: Mary Louisa, William, Reginald, Gordon</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autobiographical_work_and_Poems_in_Two_Volumes">Autobiographical work and <i>Poems in Two Volumes</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Autobiographical work and Poems in Two Volumes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(April 2017)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Wordsworth had for years been making plans to write a long philosophical poem in three parts, which he intended to call <i>The Recluse</i>. In 1798–99 he started an autobiographical poem, which he referred to as the "<a href="/wiki/The_Prelude" title="The Prelude">poem to Coleridge</a>" and which he planned would serve as an appendix to a larger work called <i>The Recluse</i>. In 1804 he began expanding this autobiographical work, having decided to make it a prologue rather than an appendix. He completed this work, now generally referred to as the first version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prelude" title="The Prelude">The Prelude</a></i>, in 1805, but refused to publish such a personal work until he had completed the whole of <i>The Recluse</i>. The death of his brother John, also in 1805, affected him strongly and may have influenced his decisions about these works.
</p><p>Wordsworth's philosophical allegiances as articulated in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prelude" title="The Prelude">The Prelude</a></i> and in such shorter works as "<a href="/wiki/Lines_written_a_few_miles_above_Tintern_Abbey" title="Lines written a few miles above Tintern Abbey">Lines written a few miles above Tintern Abbey</a>" have been a source of critical debate. It was long supposed that Wordsworth relied chiefly on Coleridge for philosophical guidance, but more recently scholars have suggested that Wordsworth's ideas may have been formed years before he and Coleridge became friends in the mid-1790s. In particular, while he was in revolutionary Paris in 1792, the 22-year-old Wordsworth made the acquaintance of the mysterious traveler <a href="/wiki/John_%22Walking%22_Stewart" class="mw-redirect" title="John &quot;Walking&quot; Stewart">John "Walking" Stewart</a> (1747–1822),<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> who was nearing the end of his thirty years of wandering, on foot, from <a href="/wiki/Madras" class="mw-redirect" title="Madras">Madras</a>, India, through <a href="/wiki/Persia" class="mw-redirect" title="Persia">Persia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>, across Africa and Europe, and up through the fledgling United States. By the time of their association, Stewart had published an ambitious work of original materialist philosophy entitled <i>The Apocalypse of Nature</i> (London, 1791), to which many of Wordsworth's philosophical sentiments may well be indebted.
</p><p>
In 1807 Wordsworth published <i><a href="/wiki/Poems_in_Two_Volumes" class="mw-redirect" title="Poems in Two Volumes">Poems in Two Volumes</a></i>, including "<a href="/wiki/Ode:_Intimations_of_Immortality_from_Recollections_of_Early_Childhood" class="mw-redirect" title="Ode: Intimations of Immortality from Recollections of Early Childhood">Ode: Intimations of Immortality from Recollections of Early Childhood</a>". Up to this point Wordsworth was known only for <i>Lyrical Ballads</i>, and he hoped that this new collection would cement his reputation. Its reception was lukewarm, however.</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg/220px-Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg/330px-Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg/440px-Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rydal_Mount_-_geograph.org.uk_-_959824.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rydal Mount – home to Wordsworth 1813–1850. Hundreds of visitors came here to see him over the years</div></div></div>
<p>In 1810, Wordsworth and Coleridge were estranged over the latter's opium addiction,<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and in 1812, his son Thomas died at the age of 6, six months after the death of 3-year-old Catherine. The following year he received an appointment as Distributor of Stamps for Westmorland, and the stipend of £400 a year made him financially secure. In 1813, he and his family, including Dorothy, moved to <a href="/wiki/Rydal_Mount" title="Rydal Mount">Rydal Mount</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambleside" title="Ambleside">Ambleside</a> (between Grasmere and Rydal Water), where he spent the rest of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Prospectus">The Prospectus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The Prospectus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1814 Wordsworth published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Excursion" title="The Excursion">The Excursion</a></i> as the second part of the three-part work <i>The Recluse</i>, even though he had not completed the first part or the third part, and never did. He did, however, write a poetic Prospectus to "The Recluse" in which he laid out the structure and intention of the whole work. The Prospectus contains some of Wordsworth's most famous lines on the relation between the human mind and nature:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;... my voice proclaims<br />
How exquisitely the individual Mind<br />
(And the progressive powers perhaps no less<br />
Of the whole species) to the external World<br />
Is fitted:—and how exquisitely, too—<br />
Theme this but little heard of among Men,<br />
The external World is fitted to the Mind;<br />
And the creation (by no lower name<br />
Can it be called) which they with blended might<br />
Accomplish ...<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>Some modern critics<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> suggest that there was a decline in his work beginning around the mid-1810s, perhaps because most of the concerns that characterised his early poems (loss, death, endurance, separation and abandonment) had been resolved in his writings and his life.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  By 1820, he was enjoying considerable success accompanying a reversal in the contemporary critical opinion of his earlier works.
</p><p>Following the death of his friend the painter <a href="/wiki/William_Green_(painter)" title="William Green (painter)">William Green</a> in 1823, Wordsworth also mended his relations with Coleridge.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The two were fully reconciled by 1828, when they toured the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a> together.<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Dorothy suffered from a severe illness in 1829 that rendered her an invalid for the remainder of her life. Coleridge and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lamb" title="Charles Lamb">Charles Lamb</a> both died in 1834, their loss being a difficult blow to Wordsworth. The following year saw the passing of <a href="/wiki/James_Hogg" title="James Hogg">James Hogg</a>. Despite the death of many contemporaries, the popularity of his poetry ensured a steady stream of young friends and admirers to replace those he lost.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_beliefs">Religious beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Religious beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wordsworth's youthful political radicalism, unlike Coleridge's, never led him to rebel against his religious upbringing. He remarked in 1812 that he was willing to shed his blood for the established <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, reflected in the Ecclesiastical Sketches of 1822. This religious conservatism also colours <a href="/wiki/The_Excursion" title="The Excursion">The Excursion</a> (1814), a long poem that became extremely popular during the nineteenth century; it features three central characters, the Wanderer; the Solitary, who has experienced the hopes and miseries of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>; and the Pastor, who dominates the last third of the poem.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Laureateship_and_other_honours">Laureateship and other honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Laureateship and other honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wordsworth remained a formidable presence in his later years. In 1837, the Scottish poet and playwright <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Baillie" title="Joanna Baillie">Joanna Baillie</a> reflected on her long acquaintance with Wordsworth. "He looks like a man that one must not speak to unless one has some sensible thing to say. However he does occasionally converse cheerfully &amp; well; and when one knows how benevolent &amp; excellent he is, it disposes one to be very much pleased with him."<sup id="cite_ref-Baillie2010_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baillie2010-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1838, Wordsworth received an honorary doctorate in Civil Law from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Durham" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Durham">University of Durham</a> and the following year he was awarded the same honorary degree by the University of Oxford, when <a href="/wiki/John_Keble" title="John Keble">John Keble</a> praised him as the "poet of humanity", praise greatly appreciated by Wordsworth.<sup id="cite_ref-webbio_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webbio-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>   (It has been argued that Wordsworth was a great influence on Keble's immensely popular book of devotional poetry, <i>The Christian Year</i> (1827).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>)  In 1842, the government awarded him a Civil List pension of £300 a year.
</p><p>Following the death of Robert Southey in 1843 Wordsworth became <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom">Poet Laureate</a>. He initially refused the honour, saying that he was too old, but accepted when the Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Peel" title="Robert Peel">Robert Peel</a>, assured him that "you shall have nothing required of you". Wordsworth thus became the only poet laureate to write no official verses. The sudden death of his daughter Dora in 1847 at the age of only 42 was difficult for the aging poet to take and in his depression, he completely gave up writing new material.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wordsworth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG/170px-WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG/255px-WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG/340px-WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WilliamWordsworth_Grave.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gravestone of William Wordsworth, <a href="/wiki/Grasmere" title="Grasmere">Grasmere</a>, Cumbria</div></div></div>
<p>William Wordsworth died at home at Rydal Mount from an aggravated case of <a href="/wiki/Pleurisy" title="Pleurisy">pleurisy</a> on 23 April 1850,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and was buried at <a href="/wiki/St_Oswald%27s_Church,_Grasmere" title="St Oswald&#39;s Church, Grasmere">St Oswald's Church, Grasmere</a>. His widow Mary published his lengthy autobiographical "poem to Coleridge" as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prelude" title="The Prelude">The Prelude</a></i> several months after his death. Though it failed to arouse much interest at that time, it has since come to be widely recognised as his masterpiece.
</p>
