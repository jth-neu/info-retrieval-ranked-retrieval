<p><b>AltaVista</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">Web search engine</a> established in 1995. It became one of the most-used early search engines, but lost ground to <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google</a> and was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> in 2003, which retained the brand, but based all AltaVista searches on its own search engine. On July 8, 2013, the service was shut down by Yahoo! and since then, the domain has <a href="/wiki/URL_redirection" title="URL redirection">redirected</a> to <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search" title="Yahoo! Search">Yahoo!'s own search site</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-defunct_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defunct-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AltaVista&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "AltaVista" is formed from the words for "high view" in Spanish (alta + vista).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AltaVista&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AltaVista was created by researchers at <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">Digital Equipment Corporation</a>'s Network Systems Laboratory and Western Research Laboratory who were trying to provide services to make finding files on the public network easier.<sup id="cite_ref-ray-ray-selzer_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ray-ray-selzer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Flaherty" title="Paul Flaherty">Paul Flaherty</a> came up with the original idea,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Daniel_B._Banks,_Jr._2011_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daniel_B._Banks,_Jr._2011-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Louis_Monier" title="Louis Monier">Louis Monier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Burrows" title="Michael Burrows">Michael Burrows</a>, who wrote the <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">Web crawler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Search_engine_indexing" title="Search engine indexing">indexer</a>, respectively. The name "AltaVista" was chosen in relation to the surroundings of their company at <a href="/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California" title="Palo Alto, California">Palo Alto, California</a>. AltaVista publicly launched as an Internet search engine on December 15, 1995 at altavista.digital.com.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At launch, the service had two innovations that put it ahead of other search engines available at the time: it used a fast, multi-threaded crawler (Scooter) that could cover many more webpages than were believed to exist at the time,  and it had an efficient back-end search, running on advanced hardware.
</p><p>As of 1998, it used 20 <a href="/wiki/Multiprocessing" title="Multiprocessing">multi-processor</a> machines using <a href="/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation" title="Digital Equipment Corporation">DEC</a>'s 64-bit <a href="/wiki/DEC_Alpha" title="DEC Alpha">Alpha</a> processor. Together, the <a href="/wiki/Front_and_back_ends" title="Front and back ends">back-end</a> machines had 130 GB of <a href="/wiki/Random-access_memory" title="Random-access memory">RAM</a> and 500 GB of <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disk drive</a> space, and received 13 million queries every day.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This made AltaVista the first searchable, full-text <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">database</a> of a large part of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Another distinguishing feature of AltaVista was its <a href="/wiki/Minimalism_(computing)" title="Minimalism (computing)">minimalistic</a> interface, which was lost when it became a <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">Web portal</a>, but regained when it refocused its efforts on its search function.  It also allowed the user to limit search results from a domain, reducing the likelihood of multiple results from the same source.
</p><p>AltaVista's site was an immediate success. Traffic increased steadily from 300,000 hits on the first day to more than 80 million hits per day two years later. The ability to search the web, and AltaVista's service in particular, became the subject of numerous articles and even some books.<sup id="cite_ref-ray-ray-selzer_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ray-ray-selzer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  AltaVista itself became one of the top destinations on the web, and in 1997 it earned US$50 million in <a href="/wiki/Sponsor_(commercial)" title="Sponsor (commercial)">sponsorship</a> revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-John_Battelle_2005_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John_Battelle_2005-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By using the data collected by the crawler, employees from AltaVista, together with others from <a href="/wiki/IBM" title="IBM">IBM</a> and <a href="/wiki/Compaq" title="Compaq">Compaq</a>, were the first to analyze the strength of connections within the budding World Wide Web in a seminal study in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_transactions">Business transactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AltaVista&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Business transactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, AltaVista became the exclusive provider of search results for <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a>.  In 1998, Digital was sold to Compaq and in 1999, Compaq redesigned AltaVista as a Web portal, hoping to compete with Yahoo!. Under CEO Rod Schrock, AltaVista abandoned its streamlined search page, and focused on adding features such as shopping and free e-mail.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  In June 1998, Compaq paid AltaVista Technology Incorporated ("ATI") $3.3 million for the <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain name</a> <i>altavista.com</i>&#160;– Jack Marshall, cofounder of ATI, had registered the name in 1994.
</p><p>In June 1999, Compaq sold a majority stake in AltaVista to <a href="/wiki/ModusLink_Global_Solutions" title="ModusLink Global Solutions">CMGI</a>, an Internet investment company.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> CMGI filed for an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a> (IPO) for AltaVista to take place in April 2000, but when the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">Internet bubble</a> collapsed, the IPO was cancelled.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, it became clear that AltaVista's <a href="/wiki/Web_portal" title="Web portal">Web portal</a> strategy was unsuccessful, and the search service began losing market share, especially to <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>.  After a series of layoffs and several management changes, AltaVista gradually shed its portal features and refocused on search.  By 2002, AltaVista had improved the quality and freshness of its results and redesigned its user interface.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2003, AltaVista was bought by <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Search_Marketing#Origins_of_GoTo.com" title="Yahoo! Search Marketing">Overture Services, Inc.</a> for $140 million.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In July 2003, Overture was taken over by Yahoo!.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> After Yahoo! purchased Overture, AltaVista used the same search index as Yahoo! Search.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2010, a Yahoo! employee leaked <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_PowerPoint" title="Microsoft PowerPoint">PowerPoint</a> slides indicating that the search engine would shut down as part of a consolidation at Yahoo!.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  In May 2011, the shutdown commenced, and all results began to be returned on a Yahoo! page.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Free_services">Free services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AltaVista&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Free services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>AltaVista provided <a href="/wiki/Babel_Fish_(website)" title="Babel Fish (website)">Babel Fish</a>, a <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">Web</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Machine_translation" title="Machine translation">machine translation</a> application that translated text or web pages from one of several languages into another.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> It was later superseded by Yahoo! Babel Fish and now redirects to <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a>'s translation service.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> AltaVista also provided a free email service, which shut down on March 31, 2002 at 12:00 PM PST and had 400,000 (200,000 active) registered emails before shutting down.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shutdown">Shutdown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AltaVista&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Shutdown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 28, 2013, Yahoo! announced on its <a href="/wiki/Tumblr" title="Tumblr">Tumblr</a> page that AltaVista would shut down on July 8, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Since the day AltaVista shut down, visits to AltaVista's home page are redirected to Yahoo!'s main page.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
