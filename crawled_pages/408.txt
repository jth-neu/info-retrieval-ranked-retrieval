<p><i><b>The Lancet</b></i> is a weekly <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">peer-reviewed</a> <a href="/wiki/General_medical_journal" title="General medical journal">general medical journal</a>. It is among the world's oldest, most prestigious, and best known general medical journals.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The Lancet</i> was founded in 1823 by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wakley" title="Thomas Wakley">Thomas Wakley</a>, an English <a href="/wiki/Surgeon" title="Surgeon">surgeon</a> who named it after the surgical instrument called a <a href="/wiki/Lancet_(surgery)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lancet (surgery)">lancet</a>, as well as after the architectural term "<a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture#Lancet_arch" title="Gothic architecture">lancet arch</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> a window with a sharp pointed arch, to indicate the "light of wisdom" or "to let in light".
</p><p><i>The Lancet</i> publishes original research articles, review articles ("seminars" and "reviews"), editorials, book reviews, correspondence, as well as news features and case reports. <i>The Lancet</i> has been owned by <a href="/wiki/Elsevier" title="Elsevier">Elsevier</a> since 1991. As of  2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the editor-in-chief is <a href="/wiki/Richard_Horton_(editor)" title="Richard Horton (editor)">Richard Horton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The journal has editorial offices in London, New York, and <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2017 Journal Citation Reports, <i>The Lancet</i> was ranked second (with an <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a> of 53.254), after <i>The New England Journal of Medicine</i> (impact factor 79.258).<sup id="cite_ref-2014-journal_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2014-journal-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specialty_journals">Specialty journals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Specialty journals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>The Lancet</i> also publishes several <a href="/wiki/Specialty_(medicine)" title="Specialty (medicine)">specialty</a> journals: <i><b>The Lancet Neurology</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">neurology</a>), <i><b>The Lancet Oncology</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Oncology" title="Oncology">oncology</a>), <i><b>The Lancet Infectious Diseases</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Infectious_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Infectious disease">infectious diseases</a>), <i><b>The Lancet Respiratory Medicine</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Respiratory_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Respiratory medicine">respiratory medicine</a>), <i><b>The Lancet Psychiatry</b></i>  (<a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>), <i><b>The Lancet Diabetes and Endocrinology</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Endocrinology" title="Endocrinology">endocrinology</a>), and <i><b>The Lancet Gastroenterology &amp; Hepatology</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Gastroenterology" title="Gastroenterology">Gastroenterology</a>) all of which publish original research and reviews. In 2013, <i><b>The Lancet Global Health</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Global_health" title="Global health">global health</a>) became the group's first fully <a href="/wiki/Open_access_journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access journal">open access journal</a>. In 2014, <i><b>The Lancet Haematology</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Haematology" class="mw-redirect" title="Haematology">haematology</a>) and <i><b>The Lancet HIV</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" class="mw-redirect" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a>) were launched, both as online only research titles. In 2017, <i><b>The Lancet Child &amp; Adolescent Health</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Pediatrics" title="Pediatrics">paediatrics</a>) will launch. The three established specialty journals (<i>The Lancet Neurology</i>, <i>The Lancet Oncology</i>, and <i>The Lancet Infectious Diseases</i>) have built up strong reputations in their medical specialty. According to the 2013 Journal Citation Reports by Thomson Reuters, <i>The Lancet Neurology</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s impact factor is 22, <i>The Lancet Oncology</i> 25, and <i>The Lancet Infectious Diseases</i> 20.<sup id="cite_ref-2013-journal_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013-journal-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There is also an online website for students entitled <i><b>The Lancet Student</b></i> in blog format, launched in 2007.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Volume_renumbering">Volume renumbering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Volume renumbering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to 1990, <i>The Lancet</i> had volume numbering that reset every year. Issues in January to June were in volume <i>i</i>, with the rest in volume <i>ii</i>. In 1990, the journal moved to a sequential volume numbering scheme, with two volumes per year. Volumes were retro-actively assigned to the years prior to 1990, with the first issue of 1990 being assigned volume 335, and the last issue of 1989 assigned volume 334. The table of contents listing on <a href="/wiki/ScienceDirect" title="ScienceDirect">ScienceDirect</a> uses this new numbering scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Richard_Horton_(editor)#Controversies" title="Richard Horton (editor)">Richard Horton#Controversies</a></div>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Recentism" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>appears to be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Recentism" title="Wikipedia:Recentism">slanted towards recent events</a>.</b><span class="hide-when-compact"> Please try to keep recent events in historical perspective and add more content related to non-recent events.</span>  <small><i>(January 2016)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><i>The Lancet</i> has taken a political stand on several important medical and non-medical issues. Recent examples include criticism of the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO), rejection of the WHO's claims of the efficacy of <a href="/wiki/Homeopathy" title="Homeopathy">homoeopathy</a> as a therapeutic option,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> disapproval during the time <a href="/wiki/Reed_Exhibitions" title="Reed Exhibitions">Reed Exhibitions</a> (a division of <a href="/wiki/Reed_Elsevier" class="mw-redirect" title="Reed Elsevier">Reed Elsevier</a>) hosted <a href="/wiki/Arms_industry" title="Arms industry">arms industry</a> fairs, a call in 2003 for <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> to be made illegal,<sup id="cite_ref-Ferriman2003_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferriman2003-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and a call for an independent investigation into the <a href="/wiki/Kunduz_hospital_airstrike" title="Kunduz hospital airstrike">American bombing of a hospital</a> in Afghanistan in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Autism_and_vaccine_controversy_.281998.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Autism_and_vaccine_controversy_(1998)">Autism and vaccine controversy (1998)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Autism and vaccine controversy (1998)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MMR_vaccine_controversy#1998_The_Lancet_paper" title="MMR vaccine controversy">MMR vaccine controversy §&#160;1998 The Lancet paper</a></div>
<p><i>The Lancet</i> was criticized after it published a paper in 1998 in which the authors suggested a <a href="/wiki/MMR_vaccine_controversy" title="MMR vaccine controversy">link</a> between the <a href="/wiki/MMR_vaccine" title="MMR vaccine">MMR vaccine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lyall2004_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lyall2004-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In February 2004, <i>The Lancet</i> published a statement by 10 of the paper's 13 coauthors repudiating the possibility that MMR could cause autism.<sup id="cite_ref-retraction_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-retraction-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The editor-in-chief, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Horton_(editor)" title="Richard Horton (editor)">Richard Horton</a>, went on the record to say the paper had "fatal conflicts of interest" because the study's lead author, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Wakefield" title="Andrew Wakefield">Andrew Wakefield</a>, had a serious conflict of interest that he had not declared to <i>The Lancet</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The journal completely retracted the paper on 2 February 2010, after Wakefield was found to have acted unethically in conducting the research.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The Lancet</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s six editors, including the editor-in-chief, were also criticized in 2011 because they had "covered up" the "Wakefield concocted fear of MMR" with an "avalanche of denials" in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-Deer_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deer-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Tobacco_control_.282003.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tobacco_control_(2003)">Tobacco control (2003)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tobacco control (2003)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A December 2003 editorial by the journal, titled "How do you sleep at night, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Mr Blair?</a>", called for <a href="/wiki/Smoking" title="Smoking">tobacco use</a> to be <a href="/wiki/Smoking_ban" title="Smoking ban">completely banned</a> in the UK. The <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Physicians" title="Royal College of Physicians">Royal College of Physicians</a> rejected their argument. John Britton, chairman of the college's tobacco advisory group, praised the journal for discussing the health problem, but he concluded that a "ban on tobacco would be a nightmare." Amanda Sandford, spokesperson for the anti-tobacco group <a href="/wiki/Action_on_Smoking_and_Health" title="Action on Smoking and Health">Action on Smoking and Health</a>, stated that criminalizing a behaviour 26% of the population commit "is ludicrous." She also said: "We can't turn the clock back. If tobacco were banned we would have 13 million people desperately craving a drug that they would not be able to get." The deputy editor of <i>The Lancet</i> responded to the criticism by arguing that no other measures besides a total ban would likely be able to reduce tobacco use.<sup id="cite_ref-Laurance2003_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laurance2003-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The smokers rights group <a href="/wiki/FOREST" title="FOREST">FOREST</a> stated that the editorial gave them "amusement and disbelief". Director Simon Clark called the journal "<a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">fascist</a>" and argued that it is hypocritical to ban tobacco while allowing unhealthy <a href="/wiki/Junk_food" title="Junk food">junk foods</a>, alcohol consumption, and participation in <a href="/wiki/Extreme_sports" class="mw-redirect" title="Extreme sports">extreme sports</a>. Health Secretary <a href="/wiki/John_Reid,_Baron_Reid_of_Cardowan" title="John Reid, Baron Reid of Cardowan">John Reid</a> reiterated that his government is committed to helping people give up smoking. He added: "Despite the fact that this is a serious problem, it is a little bit extreme for us in Britain to start locking people up because they have an ounce of tobacco somewhere."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Iraq_War_death_toll_controversy_.282004.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq_War_death_toll_controversy_(2004)">Iraq War death toll controversy (2004)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Iraq War death toll controversy (2004)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Lancet</i> also published a controversial estimate of the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>'s Iraqi death toll—around 100,000—in 2004. In 2006, a follow-up study by the same team suggested that the violent death rate in Iraq was not only consistent with the earlier estimate, but had increased considerably in the intervening period (see <a href="/wiki/Lancet_surveys_of_Iraq_War_casualties" title="Lancet surveys of Iraq War casualties"><i>Lancet</i> surveys of casualties of the Iraq War</a>). The second survey estimated that there had been 654,965 excess Iraqi deaths as a consequence of the war. The 95% <a href="/wiki/Confidence_interval" title="Confidence interval">confidence interval</a> was 392,979 to 942,636. 1,849 households that contained 12,801 people were surveyed.<sup id="cite_ref-Coghlan2006_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coghlan2006-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The estimates provided in the second article are much higher than those published in other surveys from the same time. Most notably, the "Iraq Family Health Survey" published in the <i>New England Journal of Medicine</i> surveyed 9,345 households across Iraq and estimated 151,000 deaths due to violence (95% uncertainty range, 104,000 to 223,000) over the same period covered in the second <i>Lancet</i> survey by Burnham et al. The <i>NEJM</i> article stated that the second <i>Lancet</i> survey "considerably overestimated the number of violent deaths" and said the <i>Lancet</i> results were "highly improbable, given the internal and external consistency of the data and the much larger sample size and quality-control measures taken in the implementation of the IFHS."
</p>
<h3><span id="Fabricated_articles_withdrawn_.282006.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabricated_articles_withdrawn_(2006)">Fabricated articles withdrawn (2006)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fabricated articles withdrawn (2006)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 2006, it was revealed that data had been fabricated in an article<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> by the <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a> cancer researcher <a href="/wiki/Jon_Sudb%C3%B8" title="Jon Sudbø">Jon Sudbø</a> and 13 co-authors published in <i>The Lancet</i> in October 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hafstad2006_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hafstad2006-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Several articles in other scientific journals were withdrawn following the withdrawal in <i>The Lancet</i>. Within a week, the <i>New England Journal of Medicine</i> published an expression of editorial concern regarding its published research papers by the same author, and in November 2006 the journal withdrew two oral cancer studies led by the Norwegian researcher.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Dispute_with_the_Vatican_about_condoms_and_AIDS_prevention_.282009.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dispute_with_the_Vatican_about_condoms_and_AIDS_prevention_(2009)">Dispute with the Vatican about condoms and AIDS prevention (2009)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dispute with the Vatican about condoms and AIDS prevention (2009)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a 2009 editorial, the journal accused <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> of publicly distorting scientific evidence on condoms to promote Catholic doctrine on chastity in AIDS prevention.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The Vatican defended itself by pointing to an earlier <i>Lancet</i> article published in 2000 which asserted that condoms could not possibly be sufficient in solving the AIDS crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="India_and_superbugs_.282010.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="India_and_superbugs_(2010)">India and superbugs (2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India and superbugs (2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2010, <i>The Lancet Infectious Diseases</i> published an article about an enzyme conferring multi-drug-resistance properties in bacteria,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> which had previously been named New Delhi metallo-beta-lactamase or NDM-1 based on the assumed origin of the mechanism.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The article reported 44 clinical isolates of bacteria positive for NDM-1 from Chennai, 26 from Haryana, 37 (from 29 patients) from the UK, and 73 from other sites in India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh. Of the 29 UK patients, 17 had a history of travel to India or Pakistan within 1 year, and 14 had been admitted to hospital in these countries. The authors of the article cited <a href="/wiki/Medical_tourism" title="Medical tourism">medical tourism</a> to India for the spread of bacteria carrying NDM-1, which the Indian government denied.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SIFY_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SIFY-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Health_impact_of_alcohol_.282010.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_impact_of_alcohol_(2010)">Health impact of alcohol (2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Health impact of alcohol (2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A December 2010 article determined that alcohol had the worst medical and social effects compared to other recreational substances such as heroin and crack cocaine. The drugs marijuana, ecstasy, and LSD scored far lower in terms of related harms. The authors did not advocate alcohol prohibition, but they suggested that the government raise the price of alcohol until it was no longer widely available.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Gavin Partington, spokesman of the Wine and Spirit Trade Association, responded to the report by saying that alcohol abuse affects "a minority" who needs "education, treatment and enforcement". He also remarked that millions of British citizens enjoy alcohol as "a regular and enjoyable social drink".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="PACE_study_.282011.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="PACE_study_(2011)">PACE study (2011)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: PACE study (2011)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, The Lancet published a study by the UK-based "PACE trial management group", which reported success with graded exercise therapy and cognitive behavioural therapy for <a href="/wiki/Chronic_fatigue_syndrome" title="Chronic fatigue syndrome">chronic fatigue syndrome</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> a follow-up study was published in <i>Lancet Psychiatry</i> in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>   The studies attracted criticism from some patients and researchers, especially with regard to data analysis that was different from that described in the original protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-SlateCFS_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SlateCFS-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In a 2015 <i>Slate</i> article biostatistician Bruce Levin of Columbia University was quoted saying "The Lancet needs to stop <a href="/wiki/Wagon_fort#Overview" title="Wagon fort">circling the wagons</a> and be open", and that "one of the tenets of good science is transparency"; while Ronald Davis of Stanford University said that "the Lancet should step up to the plate and pull that paper".<sup id="cite_ref-SlateCFS_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SlateCFS-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  Horton defended the Lancet's publication of the trial and called the critics "a fairly small, but highly organized, very vocal and very damaging group of individuals who have, I would say, actually hijacked this agenda and distorted the debate so that it actually harms the overwhelming majority of patients."<sup id="cite_ref-SlateCFS_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SlateCFS-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Starting in 2011, critics of the studies filed Freedom of Information Act requests to get access to the authors' primary data, in order to learn what the trial’s results would have been under the original protocol.  In 2016 some of the data was released, which allowed calculation of results based on the original protocol and found that additional treatment led to no significant improvement in recovery rates over the control condition.<sup id="cite_ref-WilshireCFS_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WilshireCFS-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Open_Letter_for_the_People_of_Gaza_.282014.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Open_Letter_for_the_People_of_Gaza_(2014)">Open Letter for the People of Gaza (2014)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lancet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Open Letter for the People of Gaza (2014)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2014, <i>The Lancet</i> published an "Open letter for the people of <a href="/wiki/Gaza_Strip" title="Gaza Strip">Gaza</a>" in their correspondence section.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> As reported in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i>, the letter "condemned <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> in the strongest possible terms, but strikingly made no mention of <a href="/wiki/Hamas" title="Hamas">Hamas</a>' atrocities."<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The authors of the letter include doctors who "are apparently sympathetic to the views of <a href="/wiki/David_Duke" title="David Duke">David Duke</a>, a white supremacist and former <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a> <a href="/wiki/Grand_Wizard" title="Grand Wizard">Grand Wizard</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz220914_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz220914-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> One of the doctors responded by saying: "I legitimately use my right of freedom of opinion and do not agree or value the politics of the government of Israel, nor of many others, including <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> in and out of Israel." A second one responded with: "I didn't know who David Duke was, or that he was connected to the Ku Klux Klan. I am concerned that if there is any truth in the video, that <a href="/wiki/Antisemitic_canard#Accusations_of_controlling_the_media" title="Antisemitic canard">Jews control the media, politics and banking</a>, what on earth is going on? I was worried."<sup id="cite_ref-Telegraph_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The editor of <i>The Lancet</i>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Horton_(editor)" title="Richard Horton (editor)">Richard Horton</a>, said: "I have no plans to retract the letter, and I would not retract the letter even if it was found to be substantiated."<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz220914_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz220914-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> However, Horton subsequently came to Israel's <a href="/wiki/Rambam_Hospital" class="mw-redirect" title="Rambam Hospital">Rambam Hospital</a> for a visit and said that he "deeply, deeply regrets"<sup id="cite_ref-Lazareva_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lazareva-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz031014_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz031014-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> publishing the letter.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Pepys" title="Mark Pepys">Mark Pepys</a> wrote: “The failure of the Menduca et al. authors to disclose their extraordinary conflicts of interest... are the most serious, unprofessional and unethical errors. The transparent effort to conceal this vicious and substantially mendacious partisan political diatribe as an innocent humanitarian appeal has no place in any serious publication, let alone a professional medical journal, and would disgrace even the lowest of the gutter press." In addition, Pepys accuses Horton personally, saying that "Horton's behavior in this case is consistent with his longstanding and wholly inappropriate use of <i>The Lancet</i> as a vehicle for his own extreme political views. It has greatly detracted from the former high standing of the journal." In response, Horton said: "How can you separate politics and health? The two go hand-in-hand."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
