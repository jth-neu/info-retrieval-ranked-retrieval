<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Crime in the United Kingdom</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

<b>Crime in the United Kingdom</b> describes acts of <a href="/wiki/Violent_crime" title="Violent crime">violent crime</a> and non-violent <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> that take place within the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. Courts and police systems are separated into three sections, based on the different <a href="/wiki/Judicial_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Judicial system">judicial systems</a> of <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.
</p><p>Responsibility for crime in England and Wales is split between the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a>, the government department responsible for reducing and preventing crime,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> along with <a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Law enforcement in the United Kingdom">law enforcement in the United Kingdom</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Justice (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Justice</a>, which runs the Justice system, including its <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">courts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prisons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland, this responsibility falls on the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Office_and_Procurator_Fiscal_Service" title="Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service">Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service</a>, which acts as the sole public prosecutor in Scotland, and is therefore responsible for the prosecution of crime in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_England_and_Wales#Criminal_cases" title="Courts of England and Wales">Courts of England and Wales §&#160;Criminal cases</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CrimeinUK.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/CrimeinUK.png/300px-CrimeinUK.png" width="300" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/CrimeinUK.png/450px-CrimeinUK.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/CrimeinUK.png/600px-CrimeinUK.png 2x" data-file-width="899" data-file-height="511" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CrimeinUK.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crime in England and Wales from the <a href="/wiki/Crime_Survey_for_England_and_Wales" title="Crime Survey for England and Wales">Crime Survey</a> (in 000s of crimes).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Crime_Survey_for_England_and_Wales" title="Crime Survey for England and Wales">Crime Survey for England and Wales</a> 2015 figures show that crime in England and Wales is currently at its lowest level since the CSEW began in 1981, having decreased dramatically from its peak in 1995 and by 31% in the past five years alone.<sup id="cite_ref-CSEWstats_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSEWstats-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> For example, 1.32 million violent crimes were counted in 2014/2015 compared to 4.2 million in 1994/95. By contrast, official police statistics are unreliable, due to widespread and inconsistent practices of "<a href="/wiki/No_criming" class="mw-redirect" title="No criming">no criming</a>", with 19% of reported overall crimes not recorded by police, one quarter of sexual crimes and one third of violent crimes.<sup id="cite_ref-hmic-victims_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hmic-victims-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-patrick_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patrick-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are two kinds of criminal trial in England and Wales: 'summary' and 'on indictment'. For an adult, summary trials take place in a <a href="/wiki/Magistrates%27_Court_(England_and_Wales)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magistrates&#39; Court (England and Wales)">magistrates' court</a>, while trials on <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictment</a> take place in the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a>. Despite the possibility of two venues for trial, almost all criminal cases, however serious, commence in the magistrates' courts.
Offences may also be deemed 'either way', depending on the seriousness of the individual offence. This means they may be tried in either the Magistrates or Crown Court depending on the circumstances. A person may even be convicted by the Magistrates court and sent to the Crown for sentence (where the magistrates feel they do not have adequate sentencing powers). Further more, even if the Magistrates retain the jurisdiction of an offence, the defendant has the right to elect a Crown Court trial by jury.
</p><p>Rural crime has increased in England and Wales, criminals have become more organized and more sophisticated while budget cuts have redured the effectiveness of police response.  Expensive agricultural equipment is stolen and taken out of the country or used in ram raids, also livestock is stolen.  It is suspected stolen livestock is slaughtered illegally outside licensed slaughterhouses and then illegally enters the food chain.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Crime_in_London" title="Crime in London">Crime in London</a> was the highest in England and Wales in 2009 (111 per 1000 of the population), followed by <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> (101 per 1000).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  Crime by women in London is usually caused by poverty, drug or alcohol addiction or psychiatric illness.  Other types of intervention are frequently more effective than prison at preventing women reoffending.  Specialist centres can help women avoid offending or reoffending. Prison has disastrous effects on the women's children and after their release makes it harder for the women to find work and lead honest lives.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Scotland#Criminal_courts" title="Courts of Scotland">Courts of Scotland §&#160;Criminal courts</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheriff Court">Sheriff Court</a> is the main criminal court; this sits locally. The procedure followed may either be solemn, where the Sheriff sits with a jury of 15; or summary, where the Sheriff sits alone. From 10 December 2007, the maximum penalty that may be imposed in summary cases is 12 months' imprisonment or a £10,000 fine, in solemn cases 5 years' imprisonment or an unlimited fine.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The highest criminal court in Scotland is the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Northern_Ireland#Subordinate_courts" title="Courts of Northern Ireland">Courts of Northern Ireland §&#160;Subordinate courts</a></div>
<p>In Northern Ireland, <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Northern_Ireland#Subordinate_courts" title="Courts of Northern Ireland">magistrates' courts</a> hear less-serious criminal cases and conduct preliminary hearings in more serious criminal cases. The Crown Court in Northern Ireland hears more serious criminal cases. These are indictable offences and "either way" offences which are committed for trial in the Crown Court rather than the magistrates' courts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent_of_crime">Extent of crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extent of crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crime_statistics_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Crime statistics in the United Kingdom">Crime statistics in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Crime_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Crime in Northern Ireland">Crime in Northern Ireland</a></div>
<p>In England and Wales, there were 618,000 recorded "violence against the person" crimes which caused an injury in 2015. Other areas of crime included <a href="/wiki/Robbery" title="Robbery">robbery</a> (124,000), burglary (713,000) and vehicle theft (874,000).<sup id="cite_ref-CSEWstats_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSEWstats-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> England and Wales has a prison population of over 80,000 (2007 estimate), equivalent to 149 people per 100,000. This is considerably less than the USA (762) but more than the Republic of Ireland (76). and a little more than the EU average (123).<sup id="cite_ref-abstract_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abstract-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007&#8211;8, there were 114 homicide victims in Scotland,<sup id="cite_ref-scotland_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotland-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> a slight decrease on the previous year. In the third quarter of 2009, there were a little over 17,000 <a href="/wiki/Full_time_equivalent" class="mw-redirect" title="Full time equivalent">full time equivalent</a> serving police officers. There were around 375,000 crimes in 2008&#8211;9, a fall of 2% on the previous year. These included around 12,500 non-sexual violent acts, 168,000 crimes of dishonesty (housebreaking, theft and shoplifting are included in this category) and 110,000 acts of <a href="/wiki/Wilful_fire_raising" title="Wilful fire raising">fire-raising</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a>. In the 2008&#8211;9 period, there was a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_prison_population" title="United Kingdom prison population">prison population</a> in Scotland of about 7,300,<sup id="cite_ref-scotland_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotland-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> equating to 142 people per 100,000 population, very similar to England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-abstract_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abstract-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between April 2008 and 2009, there were just over 110,000 crimes recorded by the <a href="/wiki/Police_Service_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Police Service of Northern Ireland">Police Service of Northern Ireland</a>, an increase of 1.5% on the previous year.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Northern Ireland has around 7,500 serving full-time equivalent police positions, and a prison population of 1,500, 83 per 100,000 of the population, lower than the rest of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-abstract_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abstract-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Recorded crime rose 11% in the year to March 2018 while sexual offenses rose 24%, 5% of sex offenses and 3% of rape cases lead to a charge or summonse.  Crime detection rates are falling from 15% in 2015 to 9% recently.  75% of theft cases and 57% of robberies including muggings were closed without a suspect identified.  Homicides rose 12% during the year to March 2018.  Reduction in the numbers of police officers could be a factor.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  Police officer numbers fell to 122,404 in England and Wales, the lowest since comparable records began in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_crime_stories">Real crime stories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crime_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Real crime stories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern Britain">Early Modern Britain</a>, real crime stories were a popular form of entertainment. These stories were written about in <a href="/wiki/Pamphlets" class="mw-redirect" title="Pamphlets">pamphlets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broadside_(printing)" title="Broadside (printing)">broadsides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chapbooks" class="mw-redirect" title="Chapbooks">chapbooks</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Newgate_Calendar" title="The Newgate Calendar">The Newgate Calendar</a></i>.  These real crime stories were the subject of popular gossip and discussion of the day.  While only a few people may have been able to attend a trial or an execution, these stories allowed for the entertainment of such events to be extended to a much greater population.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> These crime stories depicted the gruesome details of criminal acts, trials and executions with the intent to  "articulate a particular set of values, inculcate a certain behavioral model and bolster a social order perceived as threatened".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The publication of these stories was done in order for the larger population to learn from the mistakes of their fellow Englishmen. They stressed the idea of learning from others wrongdoings to the extent that they would place warnings within the <a href="/wiki/Epitaphs" class="mw-redirect" title="Epitaphs">epitaphs</a> of executed criminals.  For example, in the epitaph of John Smith, a highway thief and murderer, said, "thereto remain, a Terrour to affright All wicked Men that do in Sins delight...this is the Reason, and the Cause that they May Warning take."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The epitaph ends with the Latin phrase "Faelix quem faciunt aliena pericula cantum” which means “fortunate the man who learns caution from the perils of others."
</p>
