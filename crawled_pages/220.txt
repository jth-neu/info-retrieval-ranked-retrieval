<p><i><b>Halal</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">حلال</span>&#8206; <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="American Library Association – Library of Congress transliteration">ḥalāl</i></i>, "permissible"), also spelled <i><b>hallal</b></i> or <i><b>halaal</b></i>, refers to what is permissible or lawful in traditional <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic law</a>. It is frequently applied to permissible food and drinks.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>, the word <i>halal</i> is contrasted with <i><a href="/wiki/Haram" title="Haram">haram</a></i> (forbidden).<sup id="cite_ref-campo_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-campo-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Fiqh" title="Fiqh">Islamic jurisprudence</a>, this binary opposition was elaborated into a more complex classification known as "<a href="/wiki/Ahkam" title="Ahkam">the five decisions</a>": <a href="/wiki/Fard" title="Fard">mandatory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mustahabb" title="Mustahabb">recommended</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mubah" title="Mubah">neutral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makruh" title="Makruh">reprehensible</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Haram" title="Haram">forbidden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vikor_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vikor-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Faq%C4%ABh" class="mw-redirect" title="Faqīh">Islamic jurists</a> disagree on whether the term <i>halal</i> covers the first three or the first four of these categories.<sup id="cite_ref-vikor_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vikor-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In recent times, Islamic movements seeking to mobilize the masses and authors writing for a popular audience have emphasized the simpler distinction of <i>halal</i> and <i>haram</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-campo_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-campo-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>halal</i> is particularly associated with <a href="/wiki/Islamic_dietary_laws" title="Islamic dietary laws">Islamic dietary laws</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Quran">In the Quran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In the Quran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
The words <i>halal</i> and <i>haram</i> are the usual terms used in the Quran to designate the categories of lawful or allowed and unlawful or forbidden.<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis,_Minnesota.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis%2C_Minnesota.jpg/220px-Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis%2C_Minnesota.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis%2C_Minnesota.jpg/330px-Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis%2C_Minnesota.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis%2C_Minnesota.jpg/440px-Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis%2C_Minnesota.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3036" data-file-height="3036" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Halal_Market_in_Minneapolis,_Minnesota.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Halal Market in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis, Minnesota</a>.</div></div></div><p>In the Quran, the <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">root</a> h-l-l denotes lawfulness and may also indicate exiting the ritual state of a pilgrim and entering a profane state.<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In both these senses, it has an opposite meaning to that conveyed by the root h-r-m (cf. <i><a href="/wiki/Haram" title="Haram">haram</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ihram" title="Ihram">ihram</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In a literal sense, the root h-l-l may refer to dissolution (e.g., breaking of an oath) or alighting (e.g., of God's wrath).<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Lawfulness is usually indicated in the Quran by means of the verb <i>ahalla</i> (to make lawful), with God as the stated or implied subject.<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The terms <i>halal</i> and <i>haram</i> parallel the Hebrew terms <i>mutar</i> (permitted, loosened) and <i>asur</i> (forbidden), and — particularly with respect to dietary rules — the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> categories of clean and unclean.<sup id="cite_ref-EoQ_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EoQ-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foods">Foods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Foods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_dietary_laws" title="Islamic dietary laws">Islamic dietary laws</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG/220px-%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG/330px-%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG/440px-%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1984" data-file-height="1488" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%E6%B8%85%E7%9C%9F%E4%B8%AD%E5%9C%8B%E7%89%9B%E8%82%89%E9%A4%A8.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <i>halal</i> sign in Chinese (清真) at a restaurant in <a href="/wiki/Taipei" title="Taipei">Taipei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Several food companies offer <i>halal</i> processed foods and products, including <i>halal</i> <a href="/wiki/Foie_gras" title="Foie gras">foie gras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spring_roll" title="Spring roll">spring rolls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chicken_nugget" title="Chicken nugget">chicken nuggets</a>, ravioli, lasagna, pizza, and <a href="/wiki/Baby_food" title="Baby food">baby food</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
<i>Halal</i> <a href="/wiki/TV_dinner" title="TV dinner">ready meals</a> are a growing consumer market for Muslims in Britain and America and are offered by an increasing number of retailers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian_cuisine" title="Vegetarian cuisine">Vegetarian cuisine</a> is <i>halal</i> if it does not contain alcohol.
</p><p>The most common example of <i>haram</i> (non-halal) food is pork (pig meat products). While pork is the only meat that categorically may not be consumed by Muslims (the Quran forbids it,<sup id="cite_ref-corpus.quran.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-corpus.quran.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Sura 2:173 and 16:115 <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-forbidden_food_1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbidden_food_1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>) other foods not in a state of purity are also considered <i>haram</i>. The criteria for non-pork items include their source, the cause of the animal's death, and how it was processed. It also depends on the Muslim's <i><a href="/wiki/Madhab" class="mw-redirect" title="Madhab">madhab</a></i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Halal_Market_Woodbury,_Minnesota_(US).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Halal_Market_Woodbury%2C_Minnesota_%28US%29.jpg/220px-Halal_Market_Woodbury%2C_Minnesota_%28US%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Halal_Market_Woodbury%2C_Minnesota_%28US%29.jpg/330px-Halal_Market_Woodbury%2C_Minnesota_%28US%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Halal_Market_Woodbury%2C_Minnesota_%28US%29.jpg/440px-Halal_Market_Woodbury%2C_Minnesota_%28US%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4048" data-file-height="3036" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Halal_Market_Woodbury,_Minnesota_(US).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <i>halal</i> market store for groceries in <a href="/wiki/Woodbury,_Minnesota" title="Woodbury, Minnesota">Woodbury, Minnesota</a> in the United States.</div></div></div>
<p>Muslims must also ensure that all foods (particularly processed foods), as well as non-food items like cosmetics and pharmaceuticals, are <i>halal</i>. Frequently, these products contain animal by-products or other ingredients that are not permissible for Muslims to eat or use on their bodies. Foods which are not considered <i>halal</i> for Muslims to consume include blood<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and intoxicants such as <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A Muslim who would otherwise starve to death is allowed to eat non-<i>halal</i> food if there is no <i>halal</i> food available.<sup id="cite_ref-forbidden_food_1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbidden_food_1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Genetically_modified_organisms_.28GMO.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetically_modified_organisms_(GMO)">Genetically modified organisms (GMO)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genetically modified organisms (GMO)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At a conference called "Agri-biotechnology: Shariah Compliance" held in Malaysia in December 2010 by the Malaysian Biotechnology Information Centre (MABIC) and International Halal Integrity Alliance (IHIA), participants "adopted a resolution that accepts GM crops and products as halal should all ingredients used to develop them are from halal sources....The only Haram [forbidden] cases are limited to products derived from <i>Haram</i> origin retaining their original characteristics that are not substantially changed."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>An article from 2000 stated: "Should a product be brought to market with a gene from a <i>haram</i> source [such as pig DNA in a soy product], today it would at least be considered Mashbooh — questionable — if not outright <i>haram</i>. However, all biotechnology-derived foods on the market today are from approved sources."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Certification">Certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Globally, <i>halal</i> food certification has been criticized by anti-halal lobby groups and individuals using social media.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Critics have argued that the practice results in added costs; a requirement to officially certify intrinsically-halal foods leads to consumers subsidising a particular religious belief.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Australian_Federation_of_Islamic_Councils" title="Australian Federation of Islamic Councils">Australian Federation of Islamic Councils</a> spokesman <a href="/wiki/Keysar_Trad" title="Keysar Trad">Keysar Trad</a> told a journalist in July 2014 that this was an attempt to exploit <a href="/wiki/Islamophobia_in_Australia" title="Islamophobia in Australia">anti-Muslim sentiments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Halal_food_outrage_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halal_food_outrage-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business">Business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dubai_Chamber_of_Commerce_and_Industry" title="Dubai Chamber of Commerce and Industry">Dubai Chamber of Commerce</a> estimated the global industry value of <i>halal</i> food consumer purchases to be $1.1 trillion in 2013, accounting for 16.6 percent of the global food and beverage market, with an annual growth of 6.9 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Growth regions include <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> ($197 million market value in 2012) and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> ($100 million).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> market for <i>halal</i> food has an estimated annual growth of around 15 percent and is worth an estimated $30 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Method_of_slaughter">Method of slaughter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Method of slaughter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dhabihah" title="Dhabihah">Dhabihah</a></div>
<p>The food must come from a supplier that uses <i>halal</i> practices. <i><a href="/wiki/Dhabihah" title="Dhabihah"><i lang="ar-Latn" title="American Library Association – Library of Congress transliteration">Dhabīḥah</i></a></i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">ذَبِيْحَة</span>) is the prescribed method of slaughter for all meat sources, excluding fish and other sea-life, per Islamic law. This method of slaughtering animals consists of using a well-sharpened knife to make a swift, deep incision that cuts the front of the throat, the carotid artery, trachea, and jugular veins.<sup id="cite_ref-halalcertification.ie_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halalcertification.ie-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The head of an animal that is slaughtered using <i>halal</i> methods is aligned with the <a href="/wiki/Qiblah" class="mw-redirect" title="Qiblah">qiblah</a>. In addition to the direction, permitted animals should be slaughtered upon utterance of the Islamic prayer Bismillah "<a href="/wiki/Basmala" title="Basmala">in the name of God</a>".
</p><p>The slaughter can be performed by a Muslim or an adherent of religions traditionally known as <a href="/wiki/People_of_the_Book" title="People of the Book">People of the Book</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Blood must be drained from the veins. <a href="/wiki/Carrion" title="Carrion">Carrion</a> (carcasses of dead animals, such as animals who died in the wild) cannot be eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-forbidden_food_1_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbidden_food_1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Additionally, an animal that has been strangled, beaten (to death), killed by a fall, gored (to death), savaged by a beast of prey (unless finished off by a human), or sacrificed on a stone altar cannot be eaten.<sup id="cite_ref-forbidden_food_2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbidden_food_2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The animal may be stunned prior to having its throat cut. The UK Food Standards Agency figures from 2011 suggest that 84% of cattle, 81% of sheep and 88% of chickens slaughtered for <i>halal</i> meat were stunned before they died.
Supermarkets selling <i>halal</i> products also report that all animals are stunned before they are slaughtered. Tesco, for example, says "the only difference between the <i>halal</i> meat it sells and other meat is that it was blessed as it was killed."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/British_Veterinary_Association" title="British Veterinary Association">British Veterinary Association</a>, along with citizens who have assembled a petition with 100,000<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> signatures, have raised concerns regarding a proposed <i>halal</i> <a href="/wiki/Abattoir" class="mw-redirect" title="Abattoir">abattoir</a> in Wales, in which animals are not to be <a href="/wiki/Stunning" title="Stunning">stunned</a> prior to killing.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Concerns about animal suffering from slaughter without prior stunning has resulted in the ban of slaughter of unstunned animals in Denmark, Luxembourg, The Netherlands, Norway, Sweden and Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Generally, killing animals in Islam is only permissible for two main reasons, to be eaten<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and to eliminate a danger, e.g. a rabid dog.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meat_slaughtered_or_prepared_by_non-Muslims">Meat slaughtered or prepared by non-Muslims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Meat slaughtered or prepared by non-Muslims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_and_Jewish_dietary_laws_compared" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic and Jewish dietary laws compared">Islamic and Jewish dietary laws compared</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_dietary_laws" title="Christian dietary laws">Christian dietary laws</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a>, animals slaughtered by Christians or Jews is <i>halal</i> only if the slaughter is carried out by jugular slice and mentioned before slaughter that the purpose is of permissible consumption and the slaughter is carried out following the name of the God (indicating that you are grateful for God's blessings), unless explicitly prohibited, like <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">pork</a>. The requirement to invoke Allah's name is a must. In other words, the word <i>ṭaʻām</i> refers to <i lang="ar-Latn" title="American Library Association – Library of Congress transliteration">dhabīḥah</i> meat; i.e., the meat prepared after the slaughter of an animal by cutting the throat (i.e., the jugular vein, the carotid arteries, and the trachea) and during slaughter Allâh's name is invoked (Ibn ʻAbbās, Mujāhid, ʻIkrimah&#8205;&#8212;&#8204;all quoted by Ṭabarī, Ibn Kathīr).<sup id="cite_ref-halalcertification.ie_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halalcertification.ie-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kosher" class="mw-redirect" title="Kosher">Kosher</a> meats are permitted to be eaten by Muslims.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> This is due to the similarity between both methods of slaughter and the similar principles of kosher meat which are observed by Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
