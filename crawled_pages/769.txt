in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is the political, economic, or military predominance or control of one <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> over others.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> (8th century BC – 6th century AD), hegemony denoted the politico-military dominance of a <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a> over other city-states.<sup id="cite_ref-TheColumbia_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheColumbia-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The dominant state is known as the <i>hegemon</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 19th century, hegemony came to denote the "Social or cultural predominance or ascendancy; predominance by one group within a society or milieu". Later, it could be used to mean "a group or regime which exerts undue influence within a society".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Also, it could be used for the geopolitical and the cultural predominance of one country over others, from which was derived <i>hegemonism</i>, as in the idea that the <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">Great Powers</a> meant to establish <a href="/wiki/European_colonialism" title="European colonialism">European hegemony</a> over <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fontana_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fontana-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/International_relations_theory" title="International relations theory">international relations theory</a>, hegemony denotes a situation of (i) great material asymmetry in favour of one state, who has (ii) enough military power to systematically defeat any potential contester in the system, (iii) controls the access to raw materials, natural resources, capital and markets, (iv) has competitive advantages in the production of value added goods, (v) generates an accepted ideology reflecting this status quo; and (vi) is functionally differentiated from other states in the system, being expected to provide certain public goods such as security, or commercial and financial stability.
</p><p>
The <a href="/wiki/Marxist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist">Marxist</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">cultural hegemony</a>, associated particularly with <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">Antonio Gramsci</a>, is the idea that the <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling class</a> can manipulate the <a href="/wiki/Value_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Value system">value system</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mores" title="Mores">mores</a> of a society, so that their view becomes the world view (<i><a href="/wiki/Weltanschauung" class="mw-redirect" title="Weltanschauung">Weltanschauung</a></i>): in Terry Eagleton's words, "Gramsci normally uses the word hegemony to mean the ways in which a governing power wins consent to its rule from those it subjugates".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Authoritarianism" title="Authoritarianism">authoritarian</a> rule, cultural hegemony "is hegemonic only if those affected by it also consent to and struggle over its common sense".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:362BCThebanHegemony.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/362BCThebanHegemony.png/300px-362BCThebanHegemony.png" width="300" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/362BCThebanHegemony.png/450px-362BCThebanHegemony.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/362BCThebanHegemony.png/600px-362BCThebanHegemony.png 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1882" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:362BCThebanHegemony.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> under the hegemony of <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Greece" title="Thebes, Greece">Thebes</a>, 371–362 BC</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cultural_imperialism" title="Cultural imperialism">cultural imperialism</a>, the leader state dictates the internal politics and the <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">societal</a> character of the subordinate states that constitute the hegemonic <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence" title="Sphere of influence">sphere of influence</a>, either by an internal, sponsored government or by an external, installed government.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg/250px-Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg.png" width="250" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg/375px-Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg/500px-Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="992" data-file-height="794" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_Macedonia_336_BC-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The League of Corinth hegemony: the Kingdom of Macedonia (362 BC) (red) and the Corinthian League (yellow)</div></div></div>
<p>From the post-classical Latin word <i>hegemonia</i> (from 1513 or earlier) from the Greek word ἡγεμονία <i>hēgemonía</i>, meaning "authority, rule, political supremacy", related to the word ἡγεμών <i>hēgemōn</i> "leader".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_examples"><span id="Hegemonies_in_history"></span>Historical examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="8th.E2.80.931st_centuries_BC"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="8th–1st_centuries_BC">8th–1st centuries BC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 8th–1st centuries BC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_world" title="Greco-Roman world">Greco–Roman</a> world of 5th century BC European <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">classical antiquity</a>, the <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sparta" title="Sparta">Sparta</a> was the <i>hegemon</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesian_League" title="Peloponnesian League">Peloponnesian League</a> (6th to 4th centuries BC) and King <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Macedon" title="Philip II of Macedon">Philip II of Macedon</a> was the hegemon of the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Corinth" title="League of Corinth">League of Corinth</a> in 337 BC (a kingship he willed to his son, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>). Likewise, the role of <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> within the short-lived <a href="/wiki/Delian_League" title="Delian League">Delian League</a> (478–404 BC) was that of a "hegemon".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Ancient historians such as <a href="/wiki/Xenophon" title="Xenophon">Xenophon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ephorus" title="Ephorus">Ephorus</a> were the first who used the term in its modern sense.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Ancient <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, Chinese hegemony existed during the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> (c. 770–480 BC), when the weakened rule of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Zhou_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Zhou Dynasty">Eastern Zhou Dynasty</a> led to the relative autonomy of the <a href="/wiki/Five_Hegemons_(Spring_and_Autumn_period)" class="mw-redirect" title="Five Hegemons (Spring and Autumn period)">Five Hegemons</a> (<i>Ba</i> in Chinese [霸]). They were appointed by <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> lord conferences, and thus were nominally obliged to uphold the <a href="/wiki/Imperium" title="Imperium">imperium</a> of the Zhou Dynasty over the subordinate states.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1st.E2.80.9314th_centuries_AD"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1st–14th_centuries_AD">1st–14th centuries AD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1st–14th centuries AD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1st and 2nd century Europe was dominated by the hegemonic peace of the <i><a href="/wiki/Pax_Romana" title="Pax Romana">Pax Romana</a></i>. It was instituted by the emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, and was accompanied by a series of brutal military campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the 7th century to the 12th century, the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid Caliphate</a> dominated the vast territories they governed, with other states like the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> paying tribute.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 7th century India, <a href="/wiki/Harsha" title="Harsha">Harsha</a>, ruler of a large empire in northern India from AD&#160;606 to 647, brought most of the north under his hegemony. He preferred not to rule as a central government, but left "conquered kings on their thrones and contenting himself with tribute and homage."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the late 9th to the early 11th century, the empire developed by <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> achieved hegemony in Europe, with dominance over France, Italy and <a href="/wiki/Burgundy" title="Burgundy">Burgundy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 14th century, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a> became the hegemon in the Mediterranean Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="15th.E2.80.9319th_centuries"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="15th–19th_centuries">15th–19th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 15th–19th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
In <i>The Politics of International Political Economy</i>, Jayantha Jayman writes "If we consider the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> dominated global system from as early as the 15th century, there have been several hegemonic powers and contenders that have attempted to create the world order in their own images." He lists several contenders for historical hegemony.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></p><blockquote>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/History_of_Portugal_(1415%E2%80%931578)" title="History of Portugal (1415–1578)">Portugal 1494 to 1580</a> (end of <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> to <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Union" title="Iberian Union">Spanish/Hapsburg assimilation of Portugal</a>). Based on <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries" title="Portuguese discoveries">Portugal's dominance in navigation</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dutch_Golden_Age" title="Dutch Golden Age">The Netherlands 1580 to 1688</a> (<a href="/wiki/Union_of_Utrecht" title="Union of Utrecht">1579 Treaty of Utrecht</a> marks the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a>, William of Orange's arrival in England). Based on <a href="/wiki/Economic_history_of_the_Netherlands_(1500%E2%80%931815)" title="Economic history of the Netherlands (1500–1815)">Dutch control of credit and money</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Early_modern_Britain" title="Early modern Britain">Britain 1688 to 1792</a> (Glorious Revolution to <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>). Based on British textiles and command of the high seas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/History_of_the_British_Isles#19th_century" title="History of the British Isles">Britain 1815 to 1914</a> (<a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> to <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>). Based on British industrial supremacy and railroads.</li></ul></blockquote>
<p>To this list could be added the hegemony of <a href="/wiki/Habsburg_Spain" title="Habsburg Spain">Habsburg Spain</a> in 16th century Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> However, after an attempt by <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Phillip IV</a> to restore it,  by the middle of the 17th century "Spain's pretensions to hegemony had definitely and irremediably failed."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 16th and 17th-century Holland, the Dutch Republic's <a href="/wiki/Mercantilism" title="Mercantilism">mercantilist</a> dominion was an early instance of commercial hegemony, made feasible with the development of wind power for the efficient production and delivery of goods and services. This, in turn, made possible the Amsterdam <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">stock market</a> and concomitant dominance of world trade.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In France, King <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a> (1638–1715) and (<a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_the_French" title="Emperor of the French">Emperor</a>) <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> I (1799–1815) attempted French hegemony via economic, cultural and military domination of most of <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental Europe</a>. However, Jeremy Black writes that, because of Britain, France "was unable to enjoy the benefits" of this hegemony.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the defeat and exile of Napoleon, hegemony largely passed to the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, which became the largest empire in history, with <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> (1837–1901) ruling over one-quarter of the world's land and population at its zenith.  Like the Dutch, the British Empire was primarily seaborne; many British possessions were located around the rim of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, as well as numerous islands in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. Britain also controlled the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and large portions of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
In Europe, Germany, rather than Britain, may have been the strongest power after 1871, but Samuel Newland writes:</p><blockquote><p>Bismarck defined the road ahead as … no expansion, no push for hegemony in Europe. Germany was to be the strongest power in Europe but without being a hegemon. … His basic axioms were first, no conflict among major powers in Central Europe; and second, German security without German hegemony."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The early 20th century, like the late 19th century was characterized by multiple <a href="/wiki/Great_Powers" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Powers">Great Powers</a> but no global hegemon. World War I weakened the strongest of the <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">Imperial Powers</a>, Great Britain, but also strengthened the United States and, to a lesser extent, Japan. Both of these states' governments pursued policies to expand their regional <a href="/wiki/Spheres_of_influence" class="mw-redirect" title="Spheres of influence">spheres of influence</a>, the US in <a href="/wiki/Banana_Wars" title="Banana Wars">Latin America</a> and Japan in <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">East Asia</a>. France, the UK, Italy, the Soviet Union and later <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> (1933–1945) all either maintained imperialist policies based on spheres of influence or attempted to conquer territory but none achieved the status of a global hegemonic power.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> was established and the five strongest global powers (China, France, the UK, the US, and the USSR) were given permanent seats on the <a href="/wiki/U.N._Security_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="U.N. Security Council">U.N. Security Council</a>, the organization's most powerful decision making body. Following the war, the US and the USSR were the two strongest global powers and this created a bi-polar power dynamic in international affairs, commonly referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. The hegemonic conflict was <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideological</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">communism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a>, as well as geopolitical, between the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> countries (1955–1991) and <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>/<a href="/wiki/SEATO" class="mw-redirect" title="SEATO">SEATO</a>/<a href="/wiki/CENTO" class="mw-redirect" title="CENTO">CENTO</a> countries (1949–present). During the Cold War both hegemons competed against each other directly (during the <a href="/wiki/Arms_race" title="Arms race">arms race</a>) and indirectly (via <a href="/wiki/Proxy_war" title="Proxy war">proxy wars</a>). The result was that many countries, no matter how remote, were drawn into the conflict when it was suspected that their governments' policies might destabilize the <a href="/wiki/Balance_of_power_(international_relations)" title="Balance of power (international relations)">balance of power</a>. Reinhard Hildebrandt calls this a period of "dual-hegemony", where "two dominant states have been stabilizing their European spheres of influence <i>against and alongside each other</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> 
Proxy wars became battle grounds between forces supported either directly or indirectly by the hegemonic powers and included the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Laotian_Civil_War" title="Laotian Civil War">Laotian Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arab%E2%80%93Israeli_conflict" title="Arab–Israeli conflict">Arab–Israeli conflict</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(1978%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (1978–present)">Afghan War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Angolan_Civil_War" title="Angolan Civil War">Angolan Civil War</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Central_American_crisis" title="Central American crisis">Central American Civil Wars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">dissolution of the Soviet Union</a> in 1991 the United States was the world's sole hegemonic power.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various perspectives on whether the US was or continues to be a hegemon have been presented since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. The American political scientists <a href="/wiki/John_Mearsheimer" title="John Mearsheimer">John Mearsheimer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Nye" title="Joseph Nye">Joseph Nye</a> have argued that the US is not a true hegemon because it has neither the financial nor the military resources to impose a proper, formal, global hegemony.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, Anna Cornelia Beyer, in her book about counter-terrorism, argues that <a href="/wiki/Global_governance" title="Global governance">global governance</a> is a product of American leadership and describes it as hegemonic governance.<sup id="cite_ref-Beyer_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beyer-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Socialist Party (France)">French Socialist</a> politician <a href="/wiki/Hubert_V%C3%A9drine" title="Hubert Védrine">Hubert Védrine</a> in 1999 described the US as a hegemonic <a href="/wiki/Hyperpower" title="Hyperpower">hyperpower</a>, because of its unilateral military actions worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Pentagon</a> strategist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Luttwak" title="Edward Luttwak">Edward Luttwak</a>, in <i>The Grand Strategy of the Roman Empire</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> outlined three stages, with hegemonic being the first, followed by imperial. In his view the transformation proved to be fatal and eventually led to the fall of the Roman Empire. His book <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">gives implicit advice</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> to continue the present hegemonic strategy and refrain from establishing an empire.
</p><p>In 2006, author <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zhu_Zhiqun&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Zhu Zhiqun (page does not exist)">Zhu Zhiqun</a> claimed that China is already on the way to becoming the world hegemon and that the focus should be on how a peaceful transfer of power can be achieved between the US and China.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Though others have disagreed.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_science">Political science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Political science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">Cultural hegemony</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_(orthographic_projection).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg/220px-North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg.png" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg/330px-North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg/440px-North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_%28orthographic_projection%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="550" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization_(orthographic_projection).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> countries account for over 70% of <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_military_expenditures" title="List of countries by military expenditures">global military expenditure</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-sipri1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sipri1-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> alone accounting for 43% of global military expenditure in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gramsci.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Gramsci.png/200px-Gramsci.png" width="200" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Gramsci.png 1.5x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="408" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gramsci.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">Antonio Gramsci</a> (1891–1937), the <a href="/wiki/Intellectual" title="Intellectual">theoretician</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">cultural hegemony</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the historical writing of the 19th century, the denotation of <i>hegemony</i> extended to describe the predominance of one country upon other countries; and, by extension, <i>hegemonism</i> denoted the Great Power politics (c. 1880s – 1914) for establishing hegemony (indirect imperial rule), that then leads to a definition of <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a> (direct foreign rule). In the early 20th century, in the field of <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>, the Italian <a href="/wiki/Marxist_philosophy" title="Marxist philosophy">Marxist philosopher</a> <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Gramsci" title="Antonio Gramsci">Antonio Gramsci</a> developed the theory of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">cultural domination</a> (an analysis of <a href="/wiki/Economic_class" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic class">economic class</a>) to include <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a>; hence, the philosophic and sociologic theory of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_hegemony" title="Cultural hegemony">cultural hegemony</a> analysed the <a href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Norm (sociology)">social norms</a> that established the <a href="/wiki/Social_structure" title="Social structure">social structures</a> (social and economic classes) with which the <a href="/wiki/Ruling_class" title="Ruling class">ruling class</a> establish and exert cultural <a href="/wiki/Dominance_hierarchy" title="Dominance hierarchy">dominance</a> to impose their <i><a href="/wiki/Weltanschauung" class="mw-redirect" title="Weltanschauung">Weltanschauung</a></i> (world view)—justifying the social, political, and economic <i><a href="/wiki/Status_quo" title="Status quo">status quo</a></i>—as natural, inevitable, and beneficial to every social class, rather than as artificial <a href="/wiki/Social_construct" class="mw-redirect" title="Social construct">social constructs</a> beneficial solely to the ruling class.<sup id="cite_ref-TheColumbia_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheColumbia-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fontana_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fontana-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the Gramsci analysis derived the <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> denotation of hegemony as <i>leadership</i>; thus, the historical example of <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> as the militarily and culturally predominant province of the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> (Second Reich 1871–1918); and the personal and intellectual predominance of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> upon the <a href="/wiki/French_Consulate" title="French Consulate">French Consulate</a> (1799–1804).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Contemporarily, in <i><a href="/wiki/Hegemony_and_Socialist_Strategy" title="Hegemony and Socialist Strategy">Hegemony and Socialist Strategy</a></i> (1985), <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Laclau" title="Ernesto Laclau">Ernesto Laclau</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chantal_Mouffe" title="Chantal Mouffe">Chantal Mouffe</a> defined hegemony as a political relationship of <a href="/wiki/Power_(philosophy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Power (philosophy)">power</a> wherein a sub-ordinate society (collectivity) perform social tasks that are culturally unnatural and not beneficial to them, but that are in exclusive benefit to the <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperial</a> interests of the hegemon, the superior, ordinate power; hegemony is a military, political, and economic relationship that occurs as an <a href="/wiki/Articulation_(sociology)" title="Articulation (sociology)">articulation</a> within political <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Beyer analysed the contemporary hegemony of the United States at the example of the Global War on Terrorism and presented the mechanisms and processes of American exercise of power in 'hegemonic governance'.<sup id="cite_ref-Beyer_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beyer-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hegemony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Academics have argued that in the <a href="/wiki/Praxis_(process)" title="Praxis (process)">praxis</a> of hegemony, imperial dominance is established by means of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_imperialism" title="Cultural imperialism">cultural imperialism</a>, whereby the leader state (hegemon) dictates the internal politics and the <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">societal</a> character of the subordinate states that constitute the hegemonic <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence" title="Sphere of influence">sphere of influence</a>, either by an internal, sponsored government or by an external, installed government. The imposition of the hegemon's way of life—an imperial <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracies</a> (social, economic, educational, governing)—transforms the concrete imperialism of direct military domination into the abstract <a href="/wiki/Political_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Political power">power</a> of the <i>status quo</i>, indirect imperial domination.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Critics have said that this view is "deeply condescending" and "treats people&#160;... as blank slates on which global capitalism's moving finger writes its message, leaving behind another cultural automaton as it moves on."<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Suggested examples of cultural imperialism include the latter-stage <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empires</a>, the 19th- and 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Reich" title="Reich">Reichs</a> of <a href="/wiki/German_Unification" class="mw-redirect" title="German Unification">unified Germany</a> (1871–1945),<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and by the end of the 20th century, the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Culturally, hegemony also is established by means of <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>, specifically the imposed <i>lingua franca</i> of the hegemon (leader state), which then is the official source of <a href="/wiki/Information" title="Information">information</a> for the people of the society of the sub-ordinate state. Writing on language and power, Andrea Mayr says, "As a practice of power, hegemony operates largely through language."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In contemporary society, an example of the use of language in this way is in the way Western countries set up educational systems in African countries mediated by Western languages.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another example of this is found in the way language helped "diminish the traditions" of African Americans in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
