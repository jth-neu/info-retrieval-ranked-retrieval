<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Conquest of Wales by Edward I of England</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Wales">Conquest of Wales</a>)</span></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg/170px-Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg" width="170" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg/255px-Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg/340px-Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg 2x" data-file-width="366" data-file-height="582" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gal_nations_edward_i.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Edward I <i>Longshanks</i>, King of England (1272–1307)</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Conquest of Wales by Edward I</b>, sometimes referred to as the <b>Edwardian Conquest of Wales</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> took place between 1277 and 1283. It resulted in the defeat and annexation of the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Wales" title="Principality of Wales">Principality of Wales</a>, and the other last remaining independent <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a> principalities, by <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I, King of England</a>.
</p><p>By the 13th century <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> was divided between native Welsh principalities and the territories of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Norman</a> <a href="/wiki/Marcher_lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcher lords">Marcher lords</a>. The leading principality was <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a> whose princes had gained control of the greater part of the country, making the other remaining Welsh princes their vassals, and had taken the title <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>. Although English monarchs had made several attempts to seize control of the native Welsh territories, it was not until Edward's war of conquest against <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd" title="Llywelyn ap Gruffudd">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd</a> ("Llywelyn the Last") of 1277 to 1283 that this was achieved on a lasting basis.
</p><p>In two campaigns, in 1277 and 1282/1283 respectively, Edward first significantly reduced the territory of the Principality of Wales and then completely overran it, as well as the other remaining Welsh principalities. Most of the conquered territory was retained as a royal fief, and these lands subsequently became, by custom, the territorial endowment of the heir to the English throne with the title <i>Prince of Wales</i>. The remainder would be granted to Edward's supporters as new Marcher lordships. Although the territories would not be effectively incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542</a>, Edward's conquest marked the end of Welsh independence.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background:_Wales_in_the_High_Middle_Ages">Background: Wales in the High Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background: Wales in the High Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Wales" title="History of Wales">History of Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd_in_the_High_Middle_Ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Gwynedd in the High Middle Ages">Gwynedd in the High Middle Ages</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Wales" title="Norman invasion of Wales">Norman invasion of Wales</a></div>

<p>Following a series of invasions beginning shortly after their <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">conquest of England</a> in 1066, the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> seized much of Wales and established quasi-independent <a href="/wiki/Marcher_lord" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcher lord">Marcher lordships</a>, owing allegiance to the English crown.<sup id="cite_ref-c110_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c110-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, Welsh principalities such as <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Gwynedd" title="Kingdom of Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Powys" title="Kingdom of Powys">Powys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deheubarth" title="Deheubarth">Deheubarth</a> survived and from the end of the 11th century, the Welsh began pushing back the Norman advance.<sup id="cite_ref-c110_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-c110-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Over the following century the Welsh recovery fluctuated and the English kings, notably <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a>, several times sought to conquer or establish <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">suzerainty</a> over the native Welsh principalities.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, by the end of the 12th century the Marcher lordships were reduced to the south and south east of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-Walker_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The principality of Gwynedd was the dominant power in Wales in the first half of the 13th century, with Powys and Deheubarth becoming <a href="/wiki/Tributary_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Tributary states">tributary states</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Gwynedd's princes now assumed the title "<a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales" title="Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> But war with England in 1241 and 1245, followed by a dynastic dispute in the succession to the throne, weakened Gwynedd and allowed <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_England" title="Henry III of England">Henry III</a> to seize <a href="/wiki/Perfeddwlad" title="Perfeddwlad">Perfeddwlad</a> (also known as the "Four Cantrefs",<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> the eastern part of the principality).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Robison_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robison-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, from 1256 a resurgent Gwynedd under <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ap_Gruffudd" title="Llywelyn ap Gruffudd">Llywelyn ap Gruffudd</a> (who became known as "Llywelyn the Last") resumed the war with Henry and took back Perfeddwlad.<sup id="cite_ref-Robison_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robison-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Montgomery" title="Treaty of Montgomery">Treaty of Montgomery</a> of 1267, peace was restored and, in return for doing <a href="/wiki/Homage_(feudal)" title="Homage (feudal)">homage</a> to the English king, Llywelyn was recognised as Prince of Wales and his re-conquest of <a href="/wiki/Perfeddwlad" title="Perfeddwlad">Perfeddwlad</a> was accepted by Henry.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, sporadic warfare between Llywelyn and some of the <a href="/wiki/Marcher_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcher Lords">Marcher Lords</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_de_Clare,_7th_Earl_of_Gloucester" title="Gilbert de Clare, 7th Earl of Gloucester">Gilbert de Clare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Mortimer,_1st_Baron_Mortimer" title="Roger Mortimer, 1st Baron Mortimer">Roger Mortimer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_de_Bohun,_3rd_Earl_of_Hereford" title="Humphrey de Bohun, 3rd Earl of Hereford">Humphrey de Bohun</a> continued.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conquest">Conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immediate_causes_of_war">Immediate causes of war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Immediate causes of war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Henry III died in 1272 and was succeeded by his son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>. Whereas Henry's ineffectiveness had led to the collapse of royal authority in England during his reign,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Edward was a vigorous and forceful ruler and an able military leader.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1274, tension between Llywelyn and Edward increased when <a href="/wiki/Gruffydd_ap_Gwenwynwyn" title="Gruffydd ap Gwenwynwyn">Gruffydd ap Gwenwynwyn</a> of Powys and Llywelyn's younger brother <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_ap_Gruffydd" title="Dafydd ap Gruffydd">Dafydd ap Gruffydd</a> defected to the English and sought Edward's protection.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Because of the continuing conflict with the Marcher Lords and Edward's harbouring of defectors, when Edward demanded that Llywelyn come to <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> in 1275 to do homage to him, Llywelyn refused.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> For Edward, a further provocation came from Llywelyn's planned marriage to <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_de_Montfort" class="mw-redirect" title="Eleanor de Montfort">Eleanor</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_6th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester">Simon de Montfort</a>, the leader of a rebellion against the crown during the reign of Edward's father.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In November 1276, Edward declared war on Llywelyn.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, his objective was to put down a recalcitrant vassal rather than to begin a war of conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich150151_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich150151-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Invasion_of_1277">Invasion of 1277</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Invasion of 1277">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early in 1277, before the main royal army had been mustered, Edward deployed, in south and mid-Wales, a mixture of forces comprising paid troops, some of the marcher lords' retainers and knights of the royal household. They met with considerable success as many of the native Welsh rulers, resentful of Llywelyn's overlordship, surrendered and joined the English.<sup id="cite_ref-Prestwich150151_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prestwich150151-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In July 1277, Edward launched a <a href="/wiki/Punitive_expedition" title="Punitive expedition">punitive expedition</a> into North Wales with his own army of 15,500 — of whom 9,000 were Welshmen from the south — raised through a traditional feudal summons.<sup id="cite_ref-P151_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P151-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> From Chester the army marched into Gwynedd, camping first at <a href="/wiki/Flint,_Flintshire" title="Flint, Flintshire">Flint</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Rhuddlan" title="Rhuddlan">Rhuddlan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deganwy" title="Deganwy">Deganwy</a>, most likely causing significant damage to the areas it advanced through. A fleet from the <a href="/wiki/Cinque_ports" class="mw-redirect" title="Cinque ports">Cinque ports</a> provided naval support.<sup id="cite_ref-P151_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P151-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Llywelyn soon realised his position was hopeless and quickly surrendered. The campaign never came to a major battle. However, Edward decided to negotiate a settlement rather than attempt total conquest. It may be that he was running short of
men and supplies by November 1277 and, in any case, complete conquest of Llywelyn's territories had not been his objective.<sup id="cite_ref-P151_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P151-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaty_of_Aberconwy">Treaty of Aberconwy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Treaty of Aberconwy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Aberconwy" title="Treaty of Aberconwy">Treaty of Aberconwy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg/250px-Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg.png" width="250" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg/375px-Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg/500px-Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1054" data-file-height="901" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gwynedd_after_the_Treaty_of_Aberconwy_1277.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gwynedd after the Treaty of Aberconwy 1277 <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #87de87; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Gwynedd, Llywelyn ap Gruffudd's principality</div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #aaeeff; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Territories of Dafydd ap Gruffudd</div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ff8080; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Territories ceded to the English Crown</div></div></div></div>
<p>By the Treaty of Aberconwy in November 1277, Llywelyn was left only with the western part of Gwynedd, though he was allowed to retain the title of Prince of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-P413_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P413-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Eastern Gwynedd was split between Edward and Llywelyn's brother Dafydd, with the remainder of the lands that had been tributary to him becoming effectively Edward's.<sup id="cite_ref-P413_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P413-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a result of both territorial expropriation and the submission of the ruling families, Deheubarth, Powys and mid-Wales became a mixture of directly controlled royal land and pliant English protectorates.<sup id="cite_ref-D337_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D337-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Edward's victory was comprehensive and it represented a major redistribution of power and territory in Wales in Edward's favour.<sup id="cite_ref-D338_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D338-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Edward now enjoyed a degree of direct control in the native Welsh areas which no previous English king had achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-D337_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D337-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Campaign_of_1282.E2.80.9383"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign_of_1282–83">Campaign of 1282–83</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Campaign of 1282–83">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>War broke out again in 1282, as a result of a rebellion by Llywelyn's brother Dafydd, who was discontented with the reward he had received from Edward in 1277.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Dafydd launched a series of attacks co-ordinated with the Welsh rulers in <a href="/wiki/Deheubarth" title="Deheubarth">Deheubarth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Powys_Fadog" title="Powys Fadog">North Powys</a>, who had been Llywelyn's vassals until 1277 and were now Edward's vassals.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Llywelyn and the other Welsh leaders, including those in the south, joined in and it soon assumed a very different character from the 1277 campaign. It became a national struggle enjoying wide support among the Welsh, who were provoked particularly by Edward's attempts to impose <a href="/wiki/English_law" title="English law">English law</a> on the Welsh.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Edward, however, soon began to see it as a war of conquest rather than just a punitive expedition to put down a rebellion.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Llywelyn5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Llywelyn5.jpg/170px-Llywelyn5.jpg" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Llywelyn5.jpg/255px-Llywelyn5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Llywelyn5.jpg/340px-Llywelyn5.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2336" data-file-height="3504" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Llywelyn5.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Llywelyn Monument at <a href="/wiki/Cilmeri" title="Cilmeri">Cilmeri</a> which marks the site of the Battle of Orewin Bridge</div></div></div>
<p>The English launched a three-pronged attack, with Edward leading his army into North Wales along much the same route as in 1277, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Mortimer,_1st_Baron_Mortimer" title="Roger Mortimer, 1st Baron Mortimer">Roger Mortimer</a> operating in mid-Wales and the <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_de_Clare,_7th_Earl_of_Gloucester" title="Gilbert de Clare, 7th Earl of Gloucester">Earl of Gloucester</a> advancing with a substantial army in the south.<sup id="cite_ref-P154_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P154-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Initially the Welsh were successful. In June 1282, Gloucester was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Llandeilo_Fawr" title="Battle of Llandeilo Fawr">Battle of Llandeilo Fawr</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Edward replaced him with <a href="/wiki/William_de_Valence,_1st_Earl_of_Pembroke" title="William de Valence, 1st Earl of Pembroke">William de Valence, Earl of Pembroke</a> who raided in the south as far as <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a> but failed to engage with a Welsh army.<sup id="cite_ref-P154_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P154-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Edward then suffered a set-back in mid-Wales when his commander there, Roger Mortimer, died in October.<sup id="cite_ref-P154_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P154-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> On 6 November, while <a href="/wiki/John_Peckham" title="John Peckham">John Peckham</a>, Archbishop of Canterbury, was conducting peace negotiations, <a href="/wiki/Luke_de_Tany" title="Luke de Tany">Luke de Tany</a>, Edward's commander in <a href="/wiki/Anglesey" title="Anglesey">Anglesey</a>, decided to carry out a surprise attack. Shortly after Tany and his men had crossed over a <a href="/wiki/Pontoon_bridge" title="Pontoon bridge">pontoon bridge</a> they had built to the mainland, they were ambushed by the Welsh and suffered heavy losses at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Moel-y-don" title="Battle of Moel-y-don">Battle of Moel-y-don</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, the war turned in Edward's favour when Llywelyn unexpectedy marched out of North Wales towards <a href="/wiki/Builth" class="mw-redirect" title="Builth">Builth</a> in mid-Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-P155_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P155-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> He was lured into a trap and killed at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Orewin_Bridge" title="Battle of Orewin Bridge">Battle of Orewin Bridge</a> on 11 December 1282.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Taking advantage of this fortuitous event, Edward raised a new army and boldly marched into <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia" title="Snowdonia">Snowdonia</a> in January 1283 and captured <a href="/wiki/Dolwyddelan_Castle" title="Dolwyddelan Castle">Dolwyddelan Castle</a> in the heartland of the Welsh resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-P155_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P155-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> At the same time de Valence in the south advanced from <a href="/wiki/Cardigan,_Ceredigion" title="Cardigan, Ceredigion">Cardigan</a> into <a href="/wiki/Meirionnydd" title="Meirionnydd">Meirionnydd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-P155_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P155-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The combination of de Valence's pressure from the south and the king's advance into the north was too much for the Welsh forces.<sup id="cite_ref-P155_30-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P155-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The conquest of Gwynedd was completed with the capture in June 1283 of Dafydd, who had succeeded his brother as prince the previous December.<sup id="cite_ref-C510_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C510-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Dafydd was taken to <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury" title="Shrewsbury">Shrewsbury</a> and executed as a traitor the following autumn.<sup id="cite_ref-C510_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C510-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg/200px-Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg.png" width="200" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg/300px-Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg/400px-Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="744" data-file-height="1052" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wales_after_the_Statute_of_Rhuddlan_1284.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Royal lands after the conquest</b> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #ffb380; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;Marcher lordships</div></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Territorial_settlement">Territorial settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Territorial settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dd><i>See also</i>: <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Wales#1284_to_1542:_annexed_to_the_English_crown" title="Principality of Wales">Principality of Wales: 1284 to 1542</a></dd></dl>
<p>Edward divided the territory of the Welsh principalities between himself (that is, retained under direct royal control) and his supporters through feudal grants, which in practice became new Marcher lordships.<sup id="cite_ref-P204_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P204-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The lordships created were mainly grants to <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Normans</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Henry_de_Lacy,_3rd_Earl_of_Lincoln" title="Henry de Lacy, 3rd Earl of Lincoln">Earl of Lincoln</a> who received the <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Denbigh" title="Lordship of Denbigh">lordship of Denbigh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-P204_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P204-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> But additionally, Edward's Welsh allies received back their own lands, but on a feudal basis; for instance, Owain ap Gruffydd ap Gwenwynwyn, of the princely house of <a href="/wiki/Powys_Wenwynwyn" title="Powys Wenwynwyn">Powys Wenwynwyn</a>, received his ancestral lands as the <a href="/wiki/Powys_Wenwynwyn#The_Lordship_of_Powys" title="Powys Wenwynwyn">lordship of Powys</a> and became known as <a href="/wiki/Owen_de_la_Pole" title="Owen de la Pole">Owen de la Pole</a> (or "Poole").<sup id="cite_ref-P204_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P204-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lands retained under direct royal control were organised under the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Rhuddlan" title="Statute of Rhuddlan">Statute of Rhuddlan</a> of 1284, which declared that they were  "annexed and united" to the English crown,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> although they did not become part of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>.  They were the King's personal fief and in 1301, they were bestowed on Edward's son, <a href="/wiki/Edward_II_of_England" title="Edward II of England">Edward of Caernarfon</a> (the future Edward II), with the title "Prince of Wales" and thereafter the lands and title became the customary endowment of the heir to the throne.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Statute of Rhuddlan divided the territory under royal control into six <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shire counties</a> on the English model, administered by royal officials.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The Statute also enforced the adoption of <a href="/wiki/English_common_law" class="mw-redirect" title="English common law">English common law</a> in Wales, albeit with some local variation.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnett2004_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett2004-37">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Welsh_law" title="Welsh law">Welsh law</a> continued to be used in some civil cases such as land inheritance, though with changes, for example illegitimate sons could no longer claim part of the inheritance, which Welsh law had allowed them to do.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rest of Wales continued to be constituted as the <a href="/wiki/March_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="March of Wales">March of Wales</a> under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Marcher_lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcher lords">Marcher lords</a> as before, from the 1290s Edward began intervening in the affairs of the March to a much greater extent.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonisation_and_castle_building">Colonisation and castle building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Colonisation and castle building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg/220px-North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg/330px-North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg/440px-North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="838" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Wales_Caernarfon_Castle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Caernarfon Castle, the "capital" of English rule in North Wales for two centuries after the conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>From 1277, and particularly after 1283, Edward embarked on a policy of English colonisation and settlement of Wales, creating new towns like <a href="/wiki/Flint,_Flintshire" title="Flint, Flintshire">Flint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhuddlan" title="Rhuddlan">Rhuddlan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Outside of the towns, Welsh peasants were evicted from key areas and their land resettled by English peasants: for example, in the Lordship of Denbigh 10,000 acres were occupied by English settlers by 1334.<sup id="cite_ref-Haskins_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haskins-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Edward's main concern following his victory was to ensure the military security of his new territories and the stone castle was to be the primary means for achieving this.<sup id="cite_ref-D338_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D338-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/James_of_Saint_George" title="James of Saint George">James of Saint George</a>, Edward's master-builder, <a href="/wiki/Castles_and_Town_Walls_of_King_Edward_in_Gwynedd" title="Castles and Town Walls of King Edward in Gwynedd">a series of imposing castles</a> was built, using a distinctive design and the most advanced defensive features of the day, to form a "ring of stone" around north Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Among the major buildings were the castles of <a href="/wiki/Beaumaris_Castle" title="Beaumaris Castle">Beaumaris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caernarfon_Castle" title="Caernarfon Castle">Caernarfon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conwy_Castle" title="Conwy Castle">Conwy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harlech_Castle" title="Harlech Castle">Harlech</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_rebellions">Further rebellions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Further rebellions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rebellions continued to occur in Wales sporadically. These included revolts in 1287–8, and more seriously, in 1294 under <a href="/wiki/Madog_ap_Llywelyn" title="Madog ap Llywelyn">Madog ap Llywelyn</a>, a distant relative of Llywelyn ap Gruffudd<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and in 1316–1318 by <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_Bren" title="Llywelyn Bren">Llywelyn Bren</a>, Lord of <a href="/wiki/Senghenydd" title="Senghenydd">Senghenydd</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In the 1370s, <a href="/wiki/Owain_Lawgoch" title="Owain Lawgoch">Owain Lawgoch</a> the last representative in the male line of the ruling house of Gwynedd planned two invasions of Wales with French support.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In 1400, a Welsh nobleman, <a href="/wiki/Owain_Glynd%C5%B5r" title="Owain Glyndŵr">Owain Glyndŵr</a> (or <i>Owen Glendower</i>), led the most serious revolt against English rule.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> None of these rebellions succeeded and by the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542">Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542</a> Wales was effectively incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences_for_England">Consequences for England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Conquest_of_Wales_by_Edward_I_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Consequences for England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There was an unforeseen constitutional impact for England.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The financial cost of the conquest was heavy. Including the construction of the new castles, Edward spent around £173,000 to achieve it.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, the exchequer had to bear the cost of the ongoing military presence in Wales, including maintenance of the castles. The king's financial need contributed to the extension of the role and membership of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">English Parliament</a> as taxes were needed to be raised in consequence.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
