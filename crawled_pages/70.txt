<p><b>LookSmart</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Search_advertising" title="Search advertising">search advertising</a> company. It provides search, powered by <a href="/wiki/Google_Search" title="Google Search">Google Search</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pay-per-click" title="Pay-per-click">pay-per-click</a> and contextual advertising services. LookSmart also licenses and manages search ad networks as <a href="/wiki/White-label_product" title="White-label product">white-label products</a>. It abides by the click measurement guidelines of the <a href="/wiki/Interactive_Advertising_Bureau" title="Interactive Advertising Bureau">Interactive Advertising Bureau</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LookSmart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The company was founded as Homebase in late 1995 in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, Australia by husband and wife <a href="/wiki/Evan_Thornley" title="Evan Thornley">Evan Thornley</a> and Tracy Ellery, executives of <a href="/wiki/McKinsey_%26_Company" title="McKinsey &amp; Company">McKinsey &amp; Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Reader%27s_Digest" title="Reader&#39;s Digest">Reader's Digest</a> invested $5 million in the company for an 80% stake.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1996, the company launched its Looksmart service.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After leadership changes at Reader's Digest, the company was sold back to the founders as well as Martin Hosking, with Reader's Digest providing a $1.5 million loan and retaining a 10% equity stake.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 1998, the company raised $2.3 million from Amwin and $6.0 from <a href="/wiki/Cox_Media_Group" title="Cox Media Group">Cox Media Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macquarie_Group" title="Macquarie Group">Macquarie Group</a> and was valued at $23.2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The company also relocated to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>.
</p><p>In 1998, the company reached an agreement to provide directory and listing services for <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>. The deal provided the company with $30 million upfront and guaranteed payments of $5 million per year for 5 years.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1999, the company had 500 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 1999, the company raised $59.6 million based on a post-money valuation of $430 million.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 20, 1999, during the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>, the company became a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Initial_public_offering" title="Initial public offering">initial public offering</a>, raising $92 million based on a $1 billion valuation for the company.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By October 1999, the stock price reached $30 per share, giving the company a <a href="/wiki/Market_capitalization" title="Market capitalization">market capitalization</a> of $2.5 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The founders' 15% stake was worth $375 million.<sup id="cite_ref-smart_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2000, the stock price peaked at over $70 per share.<sup id="cite_ref-wise_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wise-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2000, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Zeal_(web)" title="Zeal (web)">Zeal</a> for $20 million.<sup id="cite_ref-wise_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wise-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The founders only sold around 5% of their stock before the dot-com bubble collapsed.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2001, the company fired 172 employees or 31% of its staff to cut costs.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2002, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/WiseNut" title="WiseNut">WiseNut</a> for $9.25 million in stock.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2002, Thornley resigned as CEO but stayed on as chairman. Three of the seven members of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> resigned. By then the stock was trading at 16 cents per share.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2003, the company announced that Microsoft, which accounted for 70% of the company's revenues, was testing its own search tools and the stock dropped 20% in a day.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 7, 2003, Microsoft announced that it would not renew its agreement with Looksmart and the company's stock price plunged 52% in a day.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2003, the company settled a lawsuit filed in 2002 by customers after the company converted thousands of websites that originally had paid a onetime submission fee into a cost-per-click payment model.<sup id="cite_ref-enterprise_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enterprise-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2004, Thornley resigned as chairman and was replaced by Teresa Dial. 
</p><p>In September 2004, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Furl" title="Furl">Furl</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-furl_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-furl-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, the company sold its Australian operations.<sup id="cite_ref-smart_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smart-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2004, David Hills was appointed CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-change_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2007, John Simonelli, the <a href="/wiki/CFO" class="mw-redirect" title="CFO">CFO</a> of the company resigned.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2007, the company sold <a href="/wiki/Grub_(search_engine)" title="Grub (search engine)">Grub</a> to <a href="/wiki/Wikia" title="Wikia">Wikia</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> for $50,000.<sup id="cite_ref-200710K_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-200710K-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2007, Hills resigned as CEO.<sup id="cite_ref-change_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2007, the company sold Zeal for $50,000.<sup id="cite_ref-200710K_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-200710K-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2009, the company sold Furl to <a href="/wiki/Diigo" title="Diigo">Diigo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-furl_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-furl-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2013, Michael Onghai was named CEO.
</p><p>In August 2013, the company acquired Syncapse.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2015, the company transferred all of its assets to a new entity and the former entity was merged with Pyxis Tankers so that Pyxis could become a <a href="/wiki/Public_company" title="Public company">public company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2017, the company deregistered its common stock and suspended its public reporting obligations.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
