<p>The <b>Bretons</b> (<a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a>: <i lang="br">Bretoned</i>, <small>Breton pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Breton" title="Help:IPA/Breton">[breˈtɔ̃nɛt]</a></span>) are a <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> located in the region of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. They trace much of their heritage to groups of <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brittonic</a> speakers who emigrated from <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">southwestern Great Britain</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, mostly during the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_invasion_of_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon invasion of Britain">Anglo-Saxon invasion of Britain</a>. They migrated in waves from the 3rd to 9th century (most heavily from 450 to 600) into <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a>, which was subsequently named Brittany after them.<sup id="cite_ref-bretonmigrations_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bretonmigrations-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main traditional language of Brittany is <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> (<i>Brezhoneg</i>), spoken in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Brittany" title="Lower Brittany">Lower Brittany</a> (i.e. the western part of the peninsula). Breton is spoken by around 206,000 people as of 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The other principal minority language of Brittany is <a href="/wiki/Gallo_language" title="Gallo language">Gallo</a>; Gallo is spoken only in Upper Brittany, where Breton is less dominant. As one of the <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">Brittonic languages</a>, Breton is related closely to <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> and more distantly to <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Gallo_language" title="Gallo language">Gallo language</a> is one of the <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d&#39;oïl">langues d'oïl</a></i>. Currently, most Bretons' native language is standard <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>.
</p><p>Brittany and its people are counted as one of the six <a href="/wiki/Celtic_nations" title="Celtic nations">Celtic nations</a>. Ethnically, along with the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Welsh_people" title="Welsh people">Welsh</a>, the Bretons are <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Britons" title="Celtic Britons">Celtic Britons</a>. The actual number of ethnic Bretons in Brittany and France as a whole is difficult to assess as the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_France" title="Government of France">government of France</a> does not collect statistics on ethnicity. The population of Brittany, based on a January 2007 estimate, was 4,365,500.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It is said that, in 1914, over 1 million people spoke Breton west of the boundary between Breton and Gallo-speaking regions – roughly 90% of the population of the western half of Brittany. In 1945, it was about 75%, and today, in all of Brittany, the most optimistic estimate would be that 20% of Bretons can speak Breton. Brittany has a population of roughly four million, including the department of <a href="/wiki/Loire-Atlantique" title="Loire-Atlantique">Loire-Atlantique</a>, which the <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy government</a> separated from historical Brittany in 1941. 75% of the estimated 200,000 to 250,000 Breton speakers using Breton as an everyday language today are over the age of 65.
</p><p>A strong historical emigration has created a Breton diaspora within the French borders and in the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_departments_and_territories_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas departments and territories of France">overseas departments and territories of France</a>; it is mainly established in the <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> area, where more than one million people claim Breton heritage. Many Breton families have also emigrated to the Americas, predominantly to <a href="/wiki/Breton_Canadians" title="Breton Canadians">Canada</a> (mostly <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Breton_Americans" title="Breton Americans">United States</a>. People from the region of Brittany were among the first white settlers to permanently settle the French West Indies, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>, where remnants of their culture can still be seen to this day.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The only places outside Brittany that still retain significant Breton customs are in <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France" title="Île-de-France">Île-de-France</a> (mainly Le Quartier du <a href="/wiki/Montparnasse" title="Montparnasse">Montparnasse</a> in Paris), <a href="/wiki/Le_Havre" title="Le Havre">Le Havre</a> and in <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Eles_des_Saintes" title="Îles des Saintes">Îles des Saintes</a>, where a group of Breton families settled in the mid-17th century.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Brittany" title="History of Brittany">History of Brittany</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_origins_of_the_Bretons">Historical origins of the Bretons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical origins of the Bretons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Britonia6hcentury.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Britonia6hcentury.png/220px-Britonia6hcentury.png" width="220" height="386" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Britonia6hcentury.png/330px-Britonia6hcentury.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Britonia6hcentury.png/440px-Britonia6hcentury.png 2x" data-file-width="1670" data-file-height="2928" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Britonia6hcentury.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Brittonic-speaking community around the sixth century. The sea was a communication medium rather than a barrier.</div></div></div>
<p>In the late 4th century, large numbers of British auxiliary troops in the Roman army may have been stationed in <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">Armorica</a>. The 9th-century <i><a href="/wiki/Historia_Brittonum" title="Historia Brittonum">Historia Brittonum</a></i> states that the emperor <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Maximus" title="Magnus Maximus">Magnus Maximus</a>, who withdrew Roman forces from Britain, settled his troops in the province. <a href="/wiki/Nennius" title="Nennius">Nennius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a> mention a second wave of Britons settling in Armorica in the following century to escape the invading <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scoti" title="Scoti">Scoti</a>. Modern archaeology also supports a two-wave migration.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is generally accepted that the Brittonic speakers who arrived gave the region its current name as well as the <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton language</a>, <i>Brezhoneg</i>, a sister language to Welsh and Cornish.
</p><p>There are numerous records of Celtic Christian missionaries migrating from Britain during the second wave of Breton colonisation, especially the legendary <a href="/wiki/Brittany#Religion" title="Brittany">seven founder-saints of Brittany</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Gildas" title="Gildas">Gildas</a>. As in Cornwall, many Breton towns are named after these early saints. The Irish saint <a href="/wiki/Columbanus" title="Columbanus">Columbanus</a> was also active in Brittany and is commemorated accordingly at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saint-Columban&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Saint-Columban (page does not exist)">Saint-Columban</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carnac" title="Carnac">Carnac</a>.
</p><p>In the Early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, Brittany was divided into three kingdoms — <a href="/wiki/Domnon%C3%A9e" title="Domnonée">Domnonée</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornouaille" title="Cornouaille">Cornouaille</a> (Kernev), and Bro Waroc'h (<i>Broërec</i>) — which eventually were incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brittany" title="Duchy of Brittany">Duchy of Brittany</a>. The first two kingdoms seem to derive their names from the homelands of the migrating tribes in Britain, Cornwall (Kernow) and <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a>). Bro Waroc'h ("land of <a href="/wiki/Waroch_II" title="Waroch II">Waroch</a>", now <a href="/wiki/Bro_Gwened" title="Bro Gwened">Bro Gwened</a>) derives from the name of one of the first known Breton rulers, who dominated the region of <a href="/wiki/Vannes" title="Vannes">Vannes</a> (Gwened). The rulers of Domnonée, such as <a href="/wiki/Conomor" title="Conomor">Conomor</a>, sought to expand their territory, claiming overlordship over all Bretons, though there was constant tension between local lords.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Bretons were the most prominent of the non-Norman forces in the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a>. A number of Breton families were of the highest rank in the new society and were tied to the Normans by marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Scottish <a href="/wiki/Clan_Stewart" title="Clan Stewart">Clan Stewart</a> and the royal <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">House of Stuart</a> have Breton origins. <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rufus" title="Alan Rufus">Alan Rufus</a>, also known as Alan the Red, was both a cousin and knight in the retinue of William the Conqueror. Following his service at Hastings, he was rewarded with large estates in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>. At the time of his death, he was by far the richest noble in England. His manorial holding at <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_North_Yorkshire" title="Richmond, North Yorkshire">Richmond</a> ensured a Breton presence in northern England. The <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Richmond" title="Earl of Richmond">Earldom of Richmond</a> later became an <a href="/wiki/Appanage" title="Appanage">appanage</a> of the Dukes of Brittany.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Breton_identity">Modern Breton identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern Breton identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Brittany_(Gwenn_ha_du).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Flag_of_Brittany_%28Gwenn_ha_du%29.svg/150px-Flag_of_Brittany_%28Gwenn_ha_du%29.svg.png" width="150" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Flag_of_Brittany_%28Gwenn_ha_du%29.svg/225px-Flag_of_Brittany_%28Gwenn_ha_du%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Flag_of_Brittany_%28Gwenn_ha_du%29.svg/300px-Flag_of_Brittany_%28Gwenn_ha_du%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1350" data-file-height="900" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flag_of_Brittany_(Gwenn_ha_du).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The modern flag of <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>: <i>Gwenn-ha-du</i> (White-and-black)</div></div></div>
<p>Many people throughout France claim Breton ethnicity, including a few French celebrities such as <a href="/wiki/Marion_Cotillard" title="Marion Cotillard">Marion Cotillard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malik_Zidi" title="Malik Zidi">Malik Zidi</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Poivre_d%27Arvor" title="Patrick Poivre d&#39;Arvor">Patrick Poivre d'Arvor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yoann_Gourcuff" title="Yoann Gourcuff">Yoann Gourcuff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nolwenn_Leroy" title="Nolwenn Leroy">Nolwenn Leroy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yann_Tiersen" title="Yann Tiersen">Yann Tiersen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 15 years of disputes in the French courts, the European Court of Justice recognized Breton Nationality for the six children of Jean-Jacques and Mireille Manrot-Le Goarnig; they are "European Citizens of Breton Nationality".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, Jonathan Le Bris started a legal battle against the French administration to claim this status.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breton_diaspora">Breton diaspora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breton diaspora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Breton diaspora includes Breton immigrants in some cities of France like <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Le_Havre" title="Le Havre">Le Havre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">Toulon</a>, Breton Canadians and Breton Americans, along with other French immigrants in other parts of the Americas. Some of the more notable examples include <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kerouac" title="Jack Kerouac">Jack Kerouac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Celine_Dion" title="Celine Dion">Celine Dion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augusto_Pinochet" title="Augusto Pinochet">Augusto Pinochet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Carrey" title="Jim Carrey">Jim Carrey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_Stallone" title="Sylvester Stallone">Sylvester Stallone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Breton people are predominantly members of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, with minorities in the <a href="/wiki/Reformed_Church_of_France" title="Reformed Church of France">Reformed Church of France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irreligion" title="Irreligion">non-religious people</a>. Brittany was one of the most staunchly Catholic regions in all of France. Attendance at Sunday <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">mass</a> dropped during the 1970s and the 1980s, but other religious practices such as <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimages</a> have experienced a revival. This includes the <i><a href="/wiki/Tro_Breizh" title="Tro Breizh">Tro Breizh</a></i>, which takes place in the shrines of the seven founding saints of Breton Christianity. The Christian tradition is widely respected by both believers and nonbelievers, who see it as a symbol of Breton heritage and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg/220px-Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg" width="220" height="290" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg/330px-Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg/440px-Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1237" data-file-height="1630" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bretagne_Finistere_StJeanTrolimon_11032.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sculpted <a href="/wiki/Calvary" title="Calvary">calvaries</a> can be found in many villages.</div></div></div>
<p>Breton religious tradition places great emphasis on the "<a href="/wiki/Seven_founder_saints_of_Brittany" class="mw-redirect" title="Seven founder saints of Brittany">Seven Founder Saints</a>":
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Paul_Aurelian" title="Paul Aurelian">Paul Aurelian</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Pol-de-L%C3%A9on" title="Saint-Pol-de-Léon">Saint-Pol-de-Léon</a> (Breton: <i>Kastell-Paol</i>),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tudwal" title="Tudwal">Tudwal</a> (<i>Sant Tudwal</i>), at <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A9guier" title="Tréguier">Tréguier</a> (Breton: <i>Landreger</i>),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Saint_Brioc" title="Saint Brioc">Brioc</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Brieuc" title="Saint-Brieuc">Saint-Brieuc</a> (Breton: <i>Sant-Brieg</i>, Gallo: <i>Saent-Berioec</i>),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malo_(saint)" title="Malo (saint)">Malo</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Malo" title="Saint-Malo">Saint-Malo</a> (Breton: <i>Sant-Maloù</i>, Gallo: <i>Saent-Malô</i>),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Samson_of_Dol" title="Samson of Dol">Samson of Dol</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Dol-de-Bretagne" title="Dol-de-Bretagne">Dol-de-Bretagne</a> (Breton: <i>Dol</i>, Gallo: <i>Dóu</i>),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Padarn" title="Padarn">Padarn</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Vannes" title="Vannes">Vannes</a> (Breton: <i>Gwened</i>),</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Corentin_of_Quimper" title="Corentin of Quimper">Corentin</a> (<i>Sant Kaourintin</i>), at <a href="/wiki/Quimper" title="Quimper">Quimper</a> (Breton: <i>Kemper</i>).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pardons">Pardons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pardons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Pardon_(ceremony)" title="Pardon (ceremony)">Pardon</a></i> is the patron saint's <a href="/wiki/Feast_day" class="mw-redirect" title="Feast day">feast day</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a>. It often begins with a procession followed by mass in honour of the saint. <i>Pardons</i> are often accompanied by small village fairs.
The three most famous <i>pardons</i> are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Saint_Anne" title="Saint Anne">Sainte-Anne</a> d'<a href="/wiki/Auray" title="Auray">Auray</a>/Santez-Anna-Wened</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A9guier" title="Tréguier">Tréguier</a>/Landreger, in honour of St Yves</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Locronan" title="Locronan">Locronan</a>/Lokorn, in honour of St <a href="/wiki/Ronan_of_Locronan" title="Ronan of Locronan">Ronan</a>, with a <i><a href="/wiki/Trom%C3%A9nie" class="mw-redirect" title="Troménie">troménie</a></i> (a procession, 12&#160;km long) and numerous people in traditional costumes</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tro_Breizh">Tro Breizh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tro Breizh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is an ancient <a href="/wiki/Pilgrimage" title="Pilgrimage">pilgrimage</a> called the <i><a href="/wiki/Tro_Breizh" title="Tro Breizh">Tro Breizh</a></i> (tour of Brittany) which involves pilgrims walking around Brittany from the grave of one of the Seven Founder Saints to another. Nowadays pilgrims complete the circuit over the course of several years. In 2002, the Tro Breizh included a special pilgrimage to Wales, symbolically making the reverse journey of the Welshmen Paul Aurelian, Brioc, and Samson. According to Breton religious tradition, whoever does not make the pilgrimage at least once in his lifetime will be condemned to make it after his death, advancing only by the length of his coffin each seven years.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore_and_traditional_belief">Folklore and traditional belief</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Folklore and traditional belief">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some pagan customs from the old pre-Christian tradition remain the <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> of Brittany. The most powerful folk figure is the <a href="/wiki/Ankou" title="Ankou">Ankou</a> or the "Reaper of Death".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png/220px-Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png/330px-Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png/440px-Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png 2x" data-file-width="617" data-file-height="408" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Percentage_of_breton_speakers_in_the_breton_countries_in_2004.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Regional statistics of Breton speakers, in 2004</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton language</a></div>
<p>The Breton language is a very important part of Breton identity. Breton itself is one of the <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">Brittonic languages</a> and is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a> and more distantly to <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Breton is thus an <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">Insular Celtic language</a> and is more distantly related to the long-extinct <a href="/wiki/Continental_Celtic_languages" title="Continental Celtic languages">Continental Celtic languages</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Gaulish_language" title="Gaulish language">Gaulish</a> that were formerly spoken on the European mainland, including the areas colonised by the ancestors of the Bretons.
</p><p>In eastern Brittany, a regional <i><a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d&#39;oïl">langue d'oïl</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Gallo_language" title="Gallo language">Gallo</a>, developed; it shares certain <a href="/wiki/Areal_feature" title="Areal feature">areal features</a> such as points of vocabulary, idiom, and pronunciation with Breton but is a <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance language</a>). Neither language has official status under French law; however, some still use Breton as an everyday language (particularly the older generation) and bilingual road signs are common in the west of Brittany.
</p><p>From 1880 to the mid-20th century, Breton was banned from the French school system and children were punished for speaking it in a similar way to the application of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Not" title="Welsh Not">Welsh Not</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> during the 19th and 20th centuries. The situation changed in 1951 with the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Deixonne_Law&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deixonne Law (page does not exist)">Deixonne Law</a> allowing Breton language and culture to be taught 1–3 hours a week in the public school system on the proviso that a teacher was both able and prepared to do so. In modern times, a number of schools and colleges have emerged with the aim of providing Breton-medium education or bilingual Breton/French education.<sup id="cite_ref-omniglot.com_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-omniglot.com-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are four main Breton dialects: Gwenedeg (<a href="/wiki/Vannes" title="Vannes">Vannes</a>), Kerneveg (<a href="/wiki/Cornouaille" title="Cornouaille">Cornouaille</a>), Leoneg (<a href="/wiki/Viscounty_of_L%C3%A9on" title="Viscounty of Léon">Leon</a>) and Tregerieg (<a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A9gor" title="Trégor">Trégor</a>), which have varying degrees of mutual intelligibility. In 1908, a standard orthography was devised. The fourth dialect, Gwenedeg, was not included in this reform, but was included in the later orthographic reform of 1941.<sup id="cite_ref-omniglot.com_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-omniglot.com-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Breton-language_media">Breton-language media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Breton-language media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Newspapers, magazines and online journals available in Breton include <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Al_Lanv&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Al Lanv (page does not exist)">Al Lanv</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> based in Quimper, <i><a href="/wiki/Al_Liamm" title="Al Liamm">Al Liamm</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Louarnig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Louarnig (page does not exist)">Louarnig</a>-<a href="/w/index.php?title=Rouzig&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rouzig (page does not exist)">Rouzig</a>, and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brema%C3%B1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bremañ (page does not exist)">Bremañ</a></i>.
</p><p>There are a number of radio stations with broadcasts in the Breton language, namely <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arvorig_FM&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arvorig FM (page does not exist)">Arvorig FM</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=France_Bleu_Armorique&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="France Bleu Armorique (page does not exist)">France Bleu Armorique</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=France_Bleu_Breizh-Izel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="France Bleu Breizh-Izel (page does not exist)">France Bleu Breizh-Izel</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Bro_Gwened&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Radio Bro Gwened (page does not exist)">Radio Bro Gwened</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_Kerne" title="Radio Kerne">Radio Kerne</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radio_Kreiz_Breizh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Radio Kreiz Breizh (page does not exist)">Radio Kreiz Breizh</a>.
</p><p>Television programmes in Breton are also available on <a href="/w/index.php?title=France_3_Breizh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="France 3 Breizh (page does not exist)">France 3 Breizh</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=France_3_Iroise&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="France 3 Iroise (page does not exist)">France 3 Iroise</a>, <a href="/wiki/TV_Breizh" title="TV Breizh">TV Breizh</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=TV_Rennes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="TV Rennes (page does not exist)">TV Rennes</a>. There are also a number of Breton language weekly and monthly magazines.<sup id="cite_ref-omniglot.com_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-omniglot.com-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fest_noz_3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Fest_noz_3.jpg/220px-Fest_noz_3.jpg" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Fest_noz_3.jpg/330px-Fest_noz_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Fest_noz_3.jpg/440px-Fest_noz_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1427" data-file-height="1043" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fest_noz_3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Fest-noz" class="mw-redirect" title="Fest-noz">fest-noz</a> in the Pays Gallo in September 2007 as part of the <i>Mill Góll</i> festival</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fest-noz">Fest-noz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Fest-noz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Fest_Noz" title="Fest Noz">fest-noz</a></i> is a traditional festival (essentially a dance) in <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a>. Many <i>festoù-noz</i> are held outside Brittany, taking regional Breton culture outside Brittany. Although the traditional dances of the <i>fest-noz</i> are old, some dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the <i>fest-noz</i> tradition is itself more recent, dating back to the 1950s.
Fest-Noz was officially registered on Wednesday, December 5, 2012 by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> on the "Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_dance">Traditional dance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Traditional dance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many traditional Breton dances, the most well-known being <i><a href="/wiki/Gavotte" title="Gavotte">gavottes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/An_Dro" title="An Dro">an dro</a></i>, the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanter_dro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hanter dro (page does not exist)">hanter dro</a></i>, and the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plinn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Plinn (page does not exist)">plinn</a></i>. During the <i>fest-noz</i>, most dances are practised in a chain or in a circle (holding a finger), however there are also dances in pairs and choreographed dances with sequences and figures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_Breton_music">Traditional Breton music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Traditional Breton music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two main types of Breton music are a choral <a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a> tradition called <i><a href="/wiki/Kan_ha_diskan" title="Kan ha diskan">kan ha diskan</a></i>, and music involving instruments, including purely instrumental music. Traditional instruments include the <a href="/wiki/Bombard_(music)" title="Bombard (music)">bombard</a> (similar to an oboe) and two types of <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Brittany#Bagpipes" title="Music of Brittany">bagpipes</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Veuze" title="Veuze">veuze</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Biniou" class="mw-redirect" title="Biniou">binioù kozh</a></i>). Other instruments often found are the diatonic <a href="/wiki/Accordion" title="Accordion">accordion</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a>, and occasionally <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Hurdy-gurdy" title="Hurdy-gurdy">hurdy-gurdy</a>. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Highland_Bagpipe" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Highland Bagpipe">Great Highland Bagpipe</a> (and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binio%C3%B9_bras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Binioù bras (page does not exist)">binioù bras</a></i>) became commonplace in Brittany through the <i><a href="/wiki/Bagad" title="Bagad">bagado</a>ù</i> (Breton pipe bands) and thus often replaced the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Binio%C3%B9-kozh&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Binioù-kozh (page does not exist)">binioù-kozh</a></i>. The basic <a href="/wiki/Clarinet" title="Clarinet">clarinet</a> (<i>treujenn-gaol</i>) had all but disappeared but has regained popularity over the past few years.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Breton_music">Modern Breton music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Modern Breton music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nowadays groups with many different styles of music may be found, ranging from <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> to <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Red_Cardell" title="Red Cardell">Red Cardell</a>, ethno-rock, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diwall&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Diwall (page does not exist)">Diwall</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Skeduz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Skeduz (page does not exist)">Skeduz</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a>. Some modern <i>fest-noz</i> groups also use electronic keyboards and synthesisers, for example <a href="/w/index.php?title=Strobinell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Strobinell (page does not exist)">Strobinell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sonerien_Du" title="Sonerien Du">Sonerien Du</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Les_Baragouineurs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Les Baragouineurs (page does not exist)">Les Baragouineurs</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantec&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Plantec (page does not exist)">Plantec</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breton_cuisine">Breton cuisine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Breton cuisine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg/180px-Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg" width="180" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg/270px-Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg/360px-Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg 2x" data-file-width="480" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chouchenn_mead_of_Brittany.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Chouchenn" class="mw-redirect" title="Chouchenn">Chouchenn</a></i></div></div></div>
<p>Breton cuisine contains many elements from the wider French culinary tradition. Local specialities include:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%AApe" title="Crêpe">Crêpe</a></i> – froment (sweet)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Galette" title="Galette">Galette</a></i> – buckwheat (salty)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Chouchenn" class="mw-redirect" title="Chouchenn">Chouchenn</a></i> – a type of Breton <a href="/wiki/Mead" title="Mead">mead</a></li>
<li><i>Fars forn</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Far_breton" class="mw-redirect" title="Far breton">far breton</a></i>) – a kind of sweet <a href="/wiki/Suet_pudding" title="Suet pudding">suet pudding</a> with prunes</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Kouign-amann" title="Kouign-amann">Kouign-amann</a></i> – butter pastry</li>
<li><i>Krampouezh</i> (<a href="/wiki/Cr%C3%AApes" class="mw-redirect" title="Crêpes">crêpes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Galette" title="Galette">galettes</a>) – thin pancakes made either from <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a> flour; usually eaten as a main course</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lambig" title="Lambig">Lambig</a></i> – apple <i><a href="/wiki/Eau_de_vie" title="Eau de vie">eau de vie</a></i></li>
<li><i>Sistr</i> – cider</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols_of_Brittany">Symbols of Brittany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bretons&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Symbols of Brittany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditional Breton symbols and/or symbols of Brittany include the national anthem <i><a href="/wiki/Bro_Gozh_ma_Zado%C3%B9" title="Bro Gozh ma Zadoù">Bro Gozh ma Zadoù</a></i> based on the Welsh <i><a href="/wiki/Land_of_My_Fathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Land of My Fathers">Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau</a></i>. The traditional motto of the former Dukes of Brittany is <i>Kentoc'h mervel eget bezañ saotret</i> in Breton, or <i>Potius mori quam fœdari</i> in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>. The "national day" is observed on 1 August,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">Feast</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ivo_of_Kermartin" title="Ivo of Kermartin">Saint Erwann</a> (Saint Yves). The ermine is an important symbol of Brittany reflected in the ancient blazons of the Duchy of Brittany and also in the <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Chivalric order">chivalric order</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Ermine_(France)" title="Order of the Ermine (France)">L’Ordre de l’Hermine</a></i> (The Order of the Ermine).
</p>
