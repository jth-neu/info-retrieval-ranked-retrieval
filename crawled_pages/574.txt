<p>The <b>North African Campaign</b> of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> took place in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> from 10 June 1940 to 13 May 1943. It included campaigns fought in the <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libyan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egyptian</a> deserts (<a href="/wiki/Western_Desert_Campaign" title="Western Desert Campaign">Western Desert Campaign</a>, also known as the Desert War) and in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a>), as well as <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tunisia_Campaign" class="mw-redirect" title="Tunisia Campaign">Tunisia Campaign</a>).
</p><p>The campaign was fought between the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a>, many of whom had <a href="/wiki/Colonialism" title="Colonialism">colonial</a> interests in Africa dating from the late 19th century, and the <a href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" class="mw-redirect" title="Axis Powers">Axis Powers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Allied war effort was dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a> and exiles from <a href="/wiki/German-occupied_Europe" title="German-occupied Europe">German-occupied Europe</a>. The United States officially entered the war in December 1941 and began direct military assistance in North Africa on 11 May 1942.
</p><p>Fighting in North Africa started with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Italian</a> declaration of war on 10 June 1940. On 14 June, the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>'s <a href="/wiki/11th_Hussars" title="11th Hussars">11th Hussars</a> (assisted by elements of the <a href="/wiki/1st_Royal_Tank_Regiment" title="1st Royal Tank Regiment">1st Royal Tank Regiment</a>, 1st RTR) crossed the border from Egypt into Libya and captured the Italian <a href="/wiki/Fort_Capuzzo" title="Fort Capuzzo">Fort Capuzzo</a>. This was followed by an Italian <a href="/wiki/Counter-offensive" title="Counter-offensive">counter-offensive</a> into Egypt and the capture of <a href="/wiki/Sidi_Barrani" title="Sidi Barrani">Sidi Barrani</a> in September 1940 and again in December 1940 following a British Commonwealth counteroffensive, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Compass" title="Operation Compass">Operation Compass</a>. During Operation Compass, the <a href="/wiki/Tenth_Army_(Italy)" title="Tenth Army (Italy)">Italian 10th Army</a> was destroyed and the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">German</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Afrika_Korps" title="Afrika Korps">Afrika Korps</a></i>—commanded by <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Rommel" title="Erwin Rommel">Erwin Rommel</a>, who later became known as "The Desert Fox"—was dispatched to North Africa in February 1941 during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Sonnenblume" title="Operation Sonnenblume">Operation <i>Sonnenblume</i></a> to reinforce Italian forces in order to prevent a complete Axis defeat.
</p><p>A fluctuating series of battles for control of Libya and regions of Egypt followed, reaching a climax in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_El_Alamein" title="Second Battle of El Alamein">Second Battle of El Alamein</a> in October 1942 when British Commonwealth forces under the command of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Lieutenant-general (United Kingdom)">Lieutenant-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Montgomery" title="Bernard Montgomery">Bernard Montgomery</a> inflicted a decisive defeat on Rommel's Afrika Korps and forced its remnants into Tunisia. After the Anglo-American landings (<a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a>) in North-West Africa in November 1942, and subsequent battles against <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy France</a> forces (who then changed sides), the Allies encircled several hundred thousand German and Italian personnel in northern Tunisia and finally forced their surrender in May 1943.
</p><p>Operation Torch in November 1942 was a compromise operation that met the British objective of securing victory in North Africa while allowing <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">American armed forces</a> the opportunity to engage in the fight against Nazi Germany on a limited scale.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In addition, as <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>, the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, had long been pleading for a second front to be opened to engage the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> and relieve pressure on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>, it provided some degree of relief for the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Front_(World_War_II)" title="Eastern Front (World War II)">Eastern Front</a> by diverting Axis forces to the North African theatre.  Over half the German <a href="/wiki/Ju_52" class="mw-redirect" title="Ju 52">Ju 52</a> transport planes that were needed to supply the encircled German and Romanian forces at Stalingrad were tied up supplying Axis forces in North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Information gleaned via British <a href="/wiki/Ultra_(cryptography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultra (cryptography)">Ultra</a> code-breaking intelligence proved critical to Allied success in North Africa. Victory for the Allies in this campaign immediately led to the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italian Campaign</a>, which culminated in the downfall of the fascist government in Italy and the elimination of Germany's main European ally.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Desert_Campaign">Western Desert Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_African_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Western Desert Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Western_Desert_Campaign" title="Western Desert Campaign">Western Desert Campaign</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Italian_empire_1942.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/02/Italian_empire_1942.PNG/220px-Italian_empire_1942.PNG" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/02/Italian_empire_1942.PNG/330px-Italian_empire_1942.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/02/Italian_empire_1942.PNG 2x" data-file-width="344" data-file-height="241" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Italian_empire_1942.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Italian Empire in November 1942.</div></div></div>
<p>On 10 May 1940, the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> had started the Battle of France (or <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France"><i>Westfeldzug</i></a>). One month later, it was plain to see that France would have to surrender within two weeks (the <a href="/wiki/Second_Armistice_at_Compi%C3%A8gne" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Armistice at Compiègne">Armistice at Compiègne</a> took place on 22 June 1940).
</p><p>On 10 June 1940, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861%E2%80%931946)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861–1946)">Kingdom of Italy</a> aligned itself with <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> and declared war upon <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> British forces (along with Indian and Rhodesian troop said under the empire) based in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> were ordered to undertake defensive measures, but to act as non-provocatively as possible.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> However, on 11 June they began a series of raids against Italian positions in Libya.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">defeat of France</a> on 25 June, Italian forces in <a href="/wiki/Italian_Tripolitania" title="Italian Tripolitania">Tripolitania</a>—facing French troops based in <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>—redeployed to <a href="/wiki/Italian_Cyrenaica" title="Italian Cyrenaica">Cyrenaica</a> to reinforce the <a href="/wiki/Tenth_Army_(Italy)" title="Tenth Army (Italy)">Italian Tenth Army</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Macksey25_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macksey25-24">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This, coupled with the steadily degrading equipment of the British forces led General <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Wavell" class="mw-redirect" title="Archibald Wavell">Archibald Wavell</a> to order an end to raiding and placed the defence of the Egyptian border on a small screening force.<sup id="cite_ref-Macksey38_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macksey38-25">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Italian dictator <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> ordered the Tenth Army to <a href="/wiki/Italian_invasion_of_Egypt" title="Italian invasion of Egypt">invade Egypt</a> by 8 August. Two days later, no invasion having been launched, Mussolini ordered Marshal <a href="/wiki/Rodolfo_Graziani" title="Rodolfo Graziani">Graziani</a> that, the moment German forces launched <a href="/wiki/Operation_Sea_Lion" title="Operation Sea Lion">Operation Sea Lion</a>, he was to attack.<sup id="cite_ref-Macksey35_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macksey35-26">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> On 8 September, the Italians—hampered by the lack of transport and enfeebled by the low level of training among officers and weakened by the state of its supporting arms<sup id="cite_ref-Macksey25_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macksey25-24">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> – were ordered to invade Egypt the following day. The battle plan was to advance along the coastal road, while limited armoured forces operated on the desert flank.<sup id="cite_ref-Macksey38_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macksey38-25">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> To counter the Italian advance, Wavell ordered his screening forces to harass the advancing Italians, falling back towards <a href="/wiki/Mersa_Matruh" title="Mersa Matruh">Mersa Matruh</a>, where the main British infantry force was based. Positioned on the desert flank was the <a href="/wiki/7th_Armoured_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="7th Armoured Division (United Kingdom)">7th Armoured Division</a>, which would strike the flank of the Italian force.<sup id="cite_ref-Macksey40_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macksey40-27">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 16 September, the Italian force had advanced to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maktila&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Maktila (page does not exist)">Maktila</a>, around 80&#160;mi (130&#160;km) west of Mersa Matruh, where they halted due to supply problems.<sup id="cite_ref-Macksey47_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macksey47-29">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Despite Mussolini urging that the advance carry on, Graziani ordered his force to dig in around <a href="/wiki/Sidi_Barrani" title="Sidi Barrani">Sidi Barrani</a>, and fortified camps were established in forward locations; additional troops were also positioned behind the main force.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In response to the dispersed Italian camps, the British planned a limited five-day attack, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Compass" title="Operation Compass">Operation Compass</a>, to strike at these fortified camps one by one.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The British Commonwealth force, totalling 36,000 men,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> attacked the forward elements of the 10-division-strong Italian army on 9 December.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Following their initial success, the forces of Operation Compass<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> pursued the retreating Italian forces.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In January, the small port at <a href="/wiki/Bardia" title="Bardia">Bardia</a> was taken,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> soon followed by the seizure of the fortified port of <a href="/wiki/Tobruk" title="Tobruk">Tobruk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
Some 40,000 Italians were captured in and around the two ports, with the remainder of the Tenth Army retreating along the coast road back to <a href="/wiki/El_Agheila" title="El Agheila">El Agheila</a>. <a href="/wiki/Richard_O%27Connor" title="Richard O&#39;Connor">Richard O'Connor</a> sent the 7th Armoured Division across the desert, with a small reconnaissance group reaching <a href="/wiki/Beda_Fomm" title="Beda Fomm">Beda Fomm</a> some ninety minutes before the Italians, cutting off their retreat. Although desperate attempts were made to overcome the British force at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Beda_Fomm" title="Battle of Beda Fomm">Battle of Beda Fomm</a>, the Italians were unable to  break through, and the remnants of the retreating army surrendered. Thus, over the course of 10 weeks Allied forces had destroyed the Italian Tenth Army and reached El Agheila, taking 130,000 prisoners of war in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wav3268_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wav3268-41">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MatildaII.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/MatildaII.jpg/220px-MatildaII.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/MatildaII.jpg/330px-MatildaII.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/MatildaII.jpg/440px-MatildaII.jpg 2x" data-file-width="748" data-file-height="561" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MatildaII.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A British <a href="/wiki/Matilda_II" title="Matilda II">Matilda Mk II</a> during Operation Compass displaying a captured Italian flag, 24 January 1941</div></div></div>
<p>Mussolini requested help from his German ally while the Italian Commando Supremo speedily sent several large motorized and armoured forces to protect their colonies in North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> This greatly expanded reinforcement included the soon to be renowned <i>Ariete</i> Armoured division under General Ettore Baldassarre.<sup id="cite_ref-Walkerp67_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walkerp67-43">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, the Germans hastily assembled a motorized force, whose lead elements arrived in Tripoli in February. This relatively small expeditionary force, termed the <i><a href="/wiki/Afrika_Korps" title="Afrika Korps">Afrika Korps</a></i> by Hitler, was placed under the command of Erwin Rommel. His orders were to reinforce the Italians and block Allied attempts to drive them out of the region.<sup id="cite_ref-Jentz82_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz82-44">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rommel,_p._109_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rommel,_p._109-45">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> However, the initial commitment of only one panzer division and subsequently, no more than two panzer and one motorized divisions, indicated the limited extent of German involvement and commitment in this theater of operations.<sup id="cite_ref-Walkerp67_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walkerp67-43">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The bulk of the reinforcements were Italian and therefore it was up to the Italians to do the bulk of the fighting. The forward Allied force—now named <a href="/wiki/XIII_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="XIII Corps (United Kingdom)">XIII Corps</a>—adopted a defensive posture and over the coming months was built up, before having most of its veteran forces <a href="/wiki/Operation_Lustre" title="Operation Lustre">redeployed to Greece</a>. In addition, the 7th Armoured Division was withdrawn to the Nile delta.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Play2_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Play2-47">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jentz85_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz85-48">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The veteran forces were replaced by inexperienced forces, ill-equipped to face German armour.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg/220px-Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg/330px-Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg/440px-Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2036" data-file-height="1382" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Il_capo_di_stato_maggiore_Ugo_Cavallero_con_i_generali_Ettore_Bastico_e_Curio_Barbasetti_di_Prun_in_un_campo_di_aviazione_nell%27estate_1942.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Italian generals Ugo Cavallero and Ettore Bastico discussing the war at an Italian air base in Libya 1942</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg/170px-IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg" width="170" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg/255px-IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg/340px-IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg 2x" data-file-width="786" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:IWM-E-6724-Crusader-19411126.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>British <a href="/wiki/Crusader_tank" title="Crusader tank">Crusader tanks</a> moving to forward positions during Operation Crusader, 26 November 1941</div></div></div>
<p>Although Rommel had been ordered to simply hold the line, an armoured reconnaissance soon became a full-fledged offensive from El Agheila in March 1941.<sup id="cite_ref-Jentz82_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz82-44">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rommel,_p._109_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rommel,_p._109-45">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In March–April, the Allied forces were forced back <sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and leading general officers captured. The <a href="/wiki/9th_Division_(Australia)" title="9th Division (Australia)">Australian 9th Infantry Division</a> fell back to the fortress port of Tobruk,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and the remaining British and Commonwealth forces withdrew a further 100&#160;mi (160&#160;km) east to the Libyan–Egyptian border.<sup id="cite_ref-playfair3335_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-playfair3335-52">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> With <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Tobruk" title="Siege of Tobruk">Tobruk under siege</a> from the main Italian-German force, a small battlegroup continued to press eastwards. Capturing Fort Capuzzo and Bardia in passing, it then advanced into Egypt, and by the end of April had taken Sollum and the tactically important <a href="/wiki/Halfaya_Pass" title="Halfaya Pass">Halfaya Pass</a>. Rommel garrisoned these positions, reinforcing the battle-group and ordering it onto the defensive.<sup id="cite_ref-P160_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P160-53">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though isolated by land, Tobruk's garrison continued to receive supplies and replacements, delivered by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> at night. Rommel's forces did not have the strength or training to take the fortress. This created a supply problem for his forward units. His front-line positions at Sollum were at the end of an extended supply chain that stretched back to Tripoli and had to bypass the coast road at Tobruk. Further, he was constantly threatened by a breakout of the British forces at Tobruk.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Without Tobruk in Axis hands, further advances into Egypt were impractical.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jentz128_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jentz128-57">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Allies soon launched a small-scale counter-attack called <a href="/wiki/Operation_Brevity" title="Operation Brevity">Operation Brevity</a>. This was an attempt to push the Axis forces off the key passes at the border, which gained some initial success, but the advanced position could not be held. <i>Brevity</i> was then followed up by a much larger-scale offensive, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Battleaxe" title="Operation Battleaxe">Operation Battleaxe</a>. Intended to relieve the siege at Tobruk, this operation also failed.
</p><p>Following the failure of Operation <i>Battleaxe</i>, Archibald Wavell was relieved of command and replaced by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Auchinleck" title="Claude Auchinleck">Claude Auchinleck</a>. The Western Desert Force was reinforced with a second corps, XXX Corps, with the two corps forming the <a href="/wiki/British_Eighth_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="British Eighth Army">Eighth Army</a>. Eighth Army was made up of army forces from the Commonwealth nations, including the British Army, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Army" title="Australian Army">Australian Army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Army" title="British Indian Army">British Indian Army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Army" title="New Zealand Army">New Zealand Army</a>, the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Army" title="South African Army">South African Army</a>, and  the <a href="/wiki/Sudan_Defence_Force" title="Sudan Defence Force">Sudan Defence Force</a>. There was also a brigade of <a href="/wiki/Free_French" class="mw-redirect" title="Free French">Free French</a> under <a href="/wiki/Marie-Pierre_Koenig" class="mw-redirect" title="Marie-Pierre Koenig">Marie-Pierre Koenig</a>. The new formation launched a new offensive, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crusader" title="Operation Crusader">Operation Crusader</a>, in November 1941. After a see-saw battle, the <a href="/wiki/70th_Infantry_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="70th Infantry Division (United Kingdom)">70th Division</a> garrisoning Tobruk was relieved and the Axis forces were forced to fall back. By January 1942, the <a href="/wiki/Front_line" title="Front line">front line</a> was again at El Agheila.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg/170px-The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg" width="170" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg/255px-The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg/340px-The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="795" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Campaign_in_North_Africa_1940-1943_E18485.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>German prisoners captured during the Second Battle of El Alamein, November 1942</div></div></div>
<p>After receiving supplies and reinforcements from <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>, the Axis attacked again, defeating the Allies at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gazala" title="Battle of Gazala">Gazala</a> in June and capturing Tobruk. The Axis forces drove the Eighth Army back over the Egyptian border, but their advance was stopped in July only 90&#160;mi (140&#160;km) from <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">Alexandria</a> in the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_El_Alamein" title="First Battle of El Alamein">First Battle of El Alamein</a>.
</p><p>Of great significance, on 29 June US reports from Egypt of British military operations stopped using the compromised <a href="/wiki/Black_(code)" title="Black (code)">"Black Code"</a> which the Axis were reading, so learning of British "strengths, positions, losses, reinforcements, supply, situation, plans, morale etc".
</p><p>General Auchinleck, although he had checked Rommel's advance at the First Battle of El Alamein, was replaced by General <a href="/wiki/Harold_Alexander" class="mw-redirect" title="Harold Alexander">Harold Alexander</a>. Lieutenant-General <a href="/wiki/William_Gott" title="William Gott">William Gott</a> was promoted from <a href="/wiki/XIII_Corps_(United_Kingdom)" title="XIII Corps (United Kingdom)">XIII Corps</a> commander to take command of the entire Eighth Army, but he was killed when his aircraft was intercepted and shot down over Egypt. He was replaced by Lieutenant-General Bernard Montgomery.
</p><p>At the end of June, the Axis forces made a second attempt to break through the Allied defences at El Alamein at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Alam_Halfa" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Alam Halfa">Alam Halfa</a>, but were unsuccessful. After a lengthy period of build-up and training, the Eighth Army launched a major offensive, decisively defeating the Italian-German army during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_El_Alamein" title="Second Battle of El Alamein">Second Battle of El Alamein</a> in late October 1942, driving the Axis forces westward and capturing Tripoli in mid-January 1943. By February, the Eighth Army was facing the Italian-German <i>Panzer</i> Army near the <a href="/wiki/Mareth_Line" title="Mareth Line">Mareth Line</a> and came under command of General <a href="/wiki/Harold_Alexander" class="mw-redirect" title="Harold Alexander">Harold Alexander</a>'s <a href="/wiki/18th_Army_Group" title="18th Army Group">18th Army Group</a> for the concluding phase of the war in North Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Tunisia_Campaign" class="mw-redirect" title="Tunisia Campaign">Tunisia Campaign</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Torch">Operation Torch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_African_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operation Torch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27,_November_1942._A12661.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27%2C_November_1942._A12661.jpg/170px-American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27%2C_November_1942._A12661.jpg" width="170" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27%2C_November_1942._A12661.jpg/255px-American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27%2C_November_1942._A12661.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27%2C_November_1942._A12661.jpg/340px-American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27%2C_November_1942._A12661.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2480" data-file-height="3298" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:American_troops_on_board_a_landing_craft_heading_for_the_beaches_at_Oran_in_Algeria_during_Operation_%27Torch%27,_November_1942._A12661.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>American troops on board a <a href="/wiki/Landing_Craft_Assault" title="Landing Craft Assault">Landing Craft Assault</a> heading into Oran, November 1942</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a></div>
<p><b>Operation Torch</b> started on 8 November 1942, and finished on 11 November. In an attempt to pincer German and Italian forces, <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> forces (American and British Commonwealth), landed in <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy</a>-held <a href="/wiki/French_North_Africa" title="French North Africa">French North Africa</a> under the assumption that there would be little to no resistance. Nevertheless, Vichy French forces put up a strong and bloody resistance to the Allies in <a href="/wiki/Oran" title="Oran">Oran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, but not in <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>, where a <i><a href="/wiki/Coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="Coup d&#39;état">coup d'état</a></i> by the French resistance on 8 November succeeded in neutralizing the French XIX Corps before the landing and arresting the Vichy commanders. Consequently, the landings met no practical opposition in Algiers, and the city was captured on the first day along with the entire Vichy African command. After three days of talks and threats, Generals <a href="/wiki/Mark_W._Clark" title="Mark W. Clark">Mark Clark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a> compelled the Vichy Admiral <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Darlan" title="François Darlan">François Darlan</a> (and General <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Juin" title="Alphonse Juin">Alphonse Juin</a>) to order the cessation of armed resistance in Oran and Morocco by French forces on 10–11 November with the provision that Darlan would be head of a Free French administration. During Operation <i>Torch</i>, American, Vichy French and German navy vessels fought the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Battle_of_Casablanca" title="Naval Battle of Casablanca">Naval Battle of Casablanca</a>, ending in an American victory.
</p><p>The Allied landings prompted the Axis occupation of Vichy France (<a href="/wiki/Case_Anton" title="Case Anton">Case Anton</a>). In addition, the French fleet was captured at Toulon by the Italians, something which did them little good as the <a href="/wiki/Scuttling_of_the_French_fleet_in_Toulon" title="Scuttling of the French fleet in Toulon">main portion of the fleet had been scuttled</a> to prevent their use by the Axis. The Vichy army in North Africa joined the Allies (see <a href="/wiki/Free_French_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Free French Forces">Free French Forces</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tunisian_Campaign">Tunisian Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_African_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tunisian Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tunisia_Campaign" class="mw-redirect" title="Tunisia Campaign">Tunisia Campaign</a></div>
<p>Following the Operation <i>Torch</i> landings, (from early November 1942), the Germans and Italians initiated a buildup of troops in Tunisia to fill the vacuum left by Vichy troops which had withdrawn. During this period of weakness, the Allies decided against a rapid advance into Tunisia while they wrestled with the Vichy authorities. Many of the Allied soldiers were tied up in garrison duties because of the uncertain status and intentions of the Vichy forces.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TigerITankTunis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/TigerITankTunis.jpg/220px-TigerITankTunis.jpg" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/TigerITankTunis.jpg/330px-TigerITankTunis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/TigerITankTunis.jpg/440px-TigerITankTunis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TigerITankTunis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tiger 712 of the <a href="/wiki/501st_heavy_tank_battalion_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="501st heavy tank battalion (Germany)">501st heavy tank battalion</a> was surrendered to the US and subsequently transferred to the United States Army Armor &amp; Cavalry Museum<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>By mid-November, the Allies were able to advance into Tunisia but only in single division strength. By early December, the <b>Eastern Task Force</b>—which had been redesignated as the <a href="/wiki/First_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="First Army (United Kingdom)">British First Army</a> under <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Lieutenant-general (United Kingdom)">Lieutenant-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Arthur_Noel_Anderson" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenneth Arthur Noel Anderson">Kenneth Anderson</a>—was composed of the <a href="/wiki/78th_Infantry_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="78th Infantry Division (United Kingdom)">British 78th Infantry Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/6th_Armoured_Division_(United_Kingdom)" title="6th Armoured Division (United Kingdom)">British 6th Armoured Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/1st_Parachute_Brigade_(United_Kingdom)" title="1st Parachute Brigade (United Kingdom)">1st Parachute Brigade</a>, <a href="/wiki/No._6_Commando" title="No. 6 Commando">No. 6 Commando</a> and elements of <a href="/wiki/1st_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Armored Division (United States)">US 1st Armored Division</a>. But by this time, one German and five Italian divisions had been shipped from Europe and the remoteness of Allied airfields from the front line gave the Axis clear air superiority over the battlefield. The Allies were halted and pushed back having advanced eastwards to within 30 kilometres (19&#160;mi) of Tunis.
</p><p>During the winter, there followed a period of stalemate during which time both sides continued to build up their forces. By the new year, the British First Army had one British, one US and one French Corps (a second British Corps headquarters was activated in April). In the second half of February, in eastern Tunisia, Rommel and von Arnim had some successes against the mainly inexperienced French and US troops, most notably in routing the <a href="/wiki/II_Corps_(United_States)" title="II Corps (United States)">US II Corps</a> commanded by <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Fredendall" title="Lloyd Fredendall">Lloyd Fredendall</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kasserine_Pass" title="Battle of Kasserine Pass">Battle of Kasserine Pass</a>.
</p><p>By the beginning of March, the <a href="/wiki/Eighth_Army_(United_Kingdom)" title="Eighth Army (United Kingdom)">British Eighth Army</a>—advancing westward along the North African coast—had reached the Tunisian border. Rommel and von Arnim found themselves in an Allied "two army" pincer. They were outflanked, outmanned and outgunned. The British Eighth Army bypassed the Axis defence on the <a href="/wiki/Mareth_Line" title="Mareth Line">Mareth Line</a> in late March and First Army in central Tunisia launched their main offensive in mid-April to squeeze the Axis forces until their resistance in Africa collapsed. The Axis forces surrendered on 13 May 1943 yielding over 275,000 <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a>. The last Axis force to surrender in North Africa was the 1st Italian Army.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> This huge loss of experienced troops greatly reduced the military capacity of the Axis powers, although the largest percentage of Axis troops escaped Tunisia. This defeat in Africa led to all Italian colonies in Africa being captured.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligence">Intelligence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_African_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Intelligence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Axis">Axis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_African_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Axis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A,_Nordafrika,_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A%2C_Nordafrika%2C_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A%2C_Nordafrika%2C_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A%2C_Nordafrika%2C_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A%2C_Nordafrika%2C_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A%2C_Nordafrika%2C_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A%2C_Nordafrika%2C_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="790" data-file-height="463" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_101I-443-1575-19A,_Nordafrika,_Sch%C3%BCtzenpanzer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A German Signals reception unit in the desert</div></div></div>
<p>The Axis had considerable success in intelligence gathering through radio communication intercepts and monitoring unit radio traffic. The most important success came through intercepting the reports of Colonel <a href="/wiki/Bonner_Fellers" title="Bonner Fellers">Bonner Fellers</a>, the US military attaché in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>. He had been tasked by General <a href="/wiki/George_Marshall" title="George Marshall">George Marshall</a> with providing detailed reports on the military situation in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-Deac_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deac-61">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Fellers talked with British military and civilian headquarters personnel, read documents and visited the battlefront. Known to the Germans as "die gute Quelle" (the good source) or more jokingly as 'the little fellow', he transmitted his reports back to Washington using the "<a href="/wiki/Black_(code)" title="Black (code)">Black Code</a>" of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">US State Department</a>. However, in September 1941, the Italians had stolen a code book containing the Black Code, photographed it and returned it to the US embassy in Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> The Italians shared parts of their intercepts with their German allies. In addition the "<a href="/wiki/Cipher_Department_of_the_High_Command_of_the_Wehrmacht" title="Cipher Department of the High Command of the Wehrmacht">Chiffrierabteilung</a>" (German military cipher branch) were soon able to break the code. Fellers' reports were very detailed and played a significant role in informing the Germans of allied strength and intentions between January and June 1942.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg/220px-Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg" width="220" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg/330px-Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg/440px-Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1454" data-file-height="1426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Il_maggiore_Alessandro_Menchiori_chiede_notizie_ad_un_carrista_dell_Ariete_nell_utunno_inverno_1941.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An Italian M13/40 tank belonging to the Ariete Armoured Division</div></div></div>
<p>In addition, the Italian <i>Servizio Informazioni Segrete</i> or SIS code-breakers were able to successfully intercept much radio encrypted signals intelligence (SIGINT) from British aircraft traffic as well as first-class ciphers from British vessels and land bases, providing <a href="/wiki/Supermarina" title="Supermarina">Supermarina</a> (Regia Marina) with timely warnings of Allied intentions in the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Indeed, so successful was the Italian SIS in handling the bulk of Axis naval intelligence in the Mediterranean, that "Britain's offensive use of SIGINT was largely negated by Italy's defensive SIGINT."<sup id="cite_ref-O’Hara_and_Cernuschi_2013_135_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O’Hara_and_Cernuschi_2013_135-64">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Afrika Korps had the intelligence services of the 621st Signals Battalion mobile monitoring element which arrived in North Africa in late April 1941,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEForty199897_and_148_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEForty199897_and_148-65">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> commanded by Hauptmann Alfred Seeböhm. The 621st Signals Battalion monitored radio communications among British units.<sup id="cite_ref-Deac_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deac-61">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Unfortunately for the Allies, the British not only failed to change their codes with any frequency, they were also prone to poor radio discipline in combat. Their officers made frequent open, uncoded transmissions to their commands, allowing the Germans to more easily identify British units and deployments.<sup id="cite_ref-Deac_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deac-61">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The situation changed after a counterattack during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gazala" title="Battle of Gazala">Battle of Gazala</a> resulted in the 621st Signals Battalion being overrun and destroyed, and a number of their documents captured, alerting British intelligence to the problem.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewin_p._252_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewin_p._252-66">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The British responded by instituting an improved call signal procedure, introducing radiotelephonic codes, imposing rigid wireless silence on reserve formations, padding out real messages with dummy traffic, tightening up on their radio discipline in combat and creating an entire fake signals network in the southern sector.<sup id="cite_ref-Lewin_p._252_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lewin_p._252-66">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Allies">Allies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_African_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Allies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Colossus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Colossus.jpg/220px-Colossus.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Colossus.jpg/330px-Colossus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Colossus.jpg/440px-Colossus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3504" data-file-height="2337" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Colossus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Colossus Mark II computer at Bletchley Park</div></div></div>
<p>Allied codebreakers read much enciphered German message traffic, especially that encrypted with the <a href="/wiki/Enigma_machine" title="Enigma machine">Enigma machine</a>. The Allies' <a href="/wiki/Ultra_(cryptography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultra (cryptography)">Ultra</a> programme was initially of limited value, as it took too long to get the information to the commanders in the field, and at times provided information that was less than helpful.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In terms of anticipating the next move the Germans would make, reliance on Ultra sometimes backfired. Part of the reason the initial German attacks in March 1941 were so successful was that Ultra intercepts had informed Wavell that OKW had clearly directed Rommel not to take any offensive action, but to wait until he was further reinforced with the 15th Panzer Division in May.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Rommel received this information, but placed more value on his own assessment of the situation. Trusting that the Germans had no intention of taking major action, the British command did not respond until it was too late.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, Rommel did not generally provide OKW or the Italian Comando Supremo details of his planned operations, for he thought the Italians too prone to leak the information. Thus on 21 January 1942, when Rommel struck out on his second offensive from El Agheila, Commando Supremo was just as surprised to learn of it as the British were.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Ultra intercepts provided the British with such information as the name of the new German commander, his time of arrival, and the numbers and condition of the Axis forces, but they might not correctly reveal Rommel's intentions.
</p><p>The primary benefit of Ultra intercepts to the effort in North Africa was to aid in cutting the Axis supply line to Tunisia. Ultra intercepts provided valuable information about the times and routes of Axis supply shipments across the Mediterranean. This was critical in providing the British with the opportunity to intercept and destroy them. During the time when Malta was under heavy air attack, the ability to act on this information was limited, but as Allied air and naval strength improved, the information became instrumental to Allied success. It is estimated that 40% to 60% of Axis supply shipping was located and destroyed due to decrypted information.<sup id="cite_ref-cl.cam.ac.uk_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cl.cam.ac.uk-71">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> However, this claim is strongly disputed by the authors Vincent P. O'Hara and Enrico Cernuschi (2013) who claim that authors like F.H. Hinsley have greatly exaggerated the effects of ULTRA. For example, they claim that intelligence provided by ULTRA had little impact in stopping Italian convoys reaching North Africa. Of the 2.67 million tons of materiel, fuel, and munitions shipped to Africa — nearly all in Italian vessels and under Italian escort — 2.24 million tons managed to arrive despite the best efforts of ULTRA and the British Navy to prevent it.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> In effect, "Ultra did not deny the Axis armies the supplies they needed to reach the Nile." <sup id="cite_ref-O’Hara_and_Cernuschi_2013_135_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O’Hara_and_Cernuschi_2013_135-64">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Heavy losses of German paratroopers in Crete, made possible by Ultra warnings of the drop times and locations, meant that Hitler hesitated in attacking Malta,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> which aided the British in gaining control of the Mediterranean, as did the losses of the Italian Navy at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_Matapan" title="Battle of Cape Matapan">Battle of Cape Matapan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> To conceal the fact that German coded messages were being read, a fact critical to the overall Allied war effort, British command required a flyover mission be carried out before a convoy could be attacked in order to give the appearance that a reconnaissance flight had discovered the target.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_African_Campaign&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3d/Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg/170px-Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3d/Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg/255px-Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3d/Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg/340px-Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2250" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wehrmacht_fuel_barrel_in_Tunesia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wehrmacht fuel barrel in Tunisia, 2010</div></div></div>
<p>After victory by the Allies in the North African Campaign, the stage was set for the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Campaign_(World_War_II)" title="Italian Campaign (World War II)">Italian Campaign</a> to begin. The <a href="/wiki/Allied_invasion_of_Sicily" title="Allied invasion of Sicily">invasion of Sicily</a> followed two months later. Nearly 400,000 Axis and Allied troops were either lost, injured, or died of disease by the end of the North African Campaign.
</p>
