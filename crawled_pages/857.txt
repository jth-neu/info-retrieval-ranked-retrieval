<p>The <b>Norman conquest of England</b> (in Britain, often called the <b>Norman Conquest</b> or <b>the Conquest</b>) was the 11th-century invasion and occupation of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> by an army of <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bretons" title="Bretons">Breton</a>, Flemish and French soldiers led by Duke William II of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Normandy</a>, later styled <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>.
</p><p>William's claim to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">English throne</a> derived from his familial relationship with the childless <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> King <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a>, who may have encouraged William's hopes for the throne. Edward died in January 1066 and was succeeded by his brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Harold_Godwinson" title="Harold Godwinson">Harold Godwinson</a>. The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Norwegian_monarchs" title="List of Norwegian monarchs">Norwegian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Harald_Hardrada" title="Harald Hardrada">Harald Hardrada</a> invaded northern England in September 1066 and was victorious at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fulford" title="Battle of Fulford">Battle of Fulford</a>, but Harold defeated and killed him at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a> on 25 September. Within days, William landed in southern England. Harold marched south to oppose him, leaving a significant portion of his army in the north. Harold's army confronted William's invaders on 14 October at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a>; William's force defeated Harold, who was killed in the engagement.
</p><p>Although William's main rivals were gone, he still faced rebellions over the following years and was not secure on his throne until after 1072. The lands of the resisting <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_British_peerage#Feudal_origins" title="History of the British peerage">English elite</a> were confiscated; some of the elite fled into exile. To control his new kingdom, William granted lands to his followers and built <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castles</a> commanding military strongpoints throughout the land. Other effects of the conquest included the <a href="/wiki/Noble_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble court">court</a> and government, the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman language</a> as the language of the elites, and changes in the composition of the upper classes, as William <a href="/wiki/Feoffment" title="Feoffment">enfeoffed</a> lands to be held directly from the king. More gradual changes affected the agricultural classes and village life: the main change appears to have been the formal elimination of <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_British_Isles#Before_1066" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavery in the British Isles">slavery</a>, which may or may not have been linked to the invasion. There was little alteration in the structure of government, as the new Norman administrators took over many of the forms of Anglo-Saxon government.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rollo_spol.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Rollo_spol.jpg/170px-Rollo_spol.jpg" width="170" height="442" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Rollo_spol.jpg/255px-Rollo_spol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/Rollo_spol.jpg 2x" data-file-width="287" data-file-height="747" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rollo_spol.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>13th-century depiction of <a href="/wiki/Rollo" title="Rollo">Rollo</a> (top) and his descendants <a href="/wiki/William_I_Longsword" class="mw-redirect" title="William I Longsword">William I Longsword</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_Normandy" title="Richard I of Normandy">Richard I of Normandy</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 911 the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_dynasty" title="Carolingian dynasty">Carolingian French</a> ruler <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Simple" title="Charles the Simple">Charles the Simple</a> allowed a group of <a href="/wiki/Vikings" title="Vikings">Vikings</a> under their leader <a href="/wiki/Rollo" title="Rollo">Rollo</a> to settle in <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Normandy</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint-Clair-sur-Epte" title="Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte">Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte</a>. In exchange for the land, the Norsemen under Rollo were expected to provide protection along the coast against further Viking invaders.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates8_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates8-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Their settlement proved successful, and the Vikings in the region became known as the "Northmen" from which "Normandy" and "Normans" are derived.<sup id="cite_ref-Normans15_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Normans15-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Normans quickly adopted the indigenous culture as they became assimilated by the French, renouncing <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">paganism</a> and converting to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates12_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates12-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They adopted the <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d&#39;oïl">langue d'oïl</a> of their new home and added features from their own <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Norse language</a>, transforming it into the <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman language</a>. They intermarried with the local population<sup id="cite_ref-Bates20_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates20-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and used the territory granted to them as a base to extend the frontiers of the duchy westward, annexing territory including the <a href="/wiki/Bessin" title="Bessin">Bessin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">Cotentin Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avranches" title="Avranches">Avranches</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Capet53_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Capet53-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1002 English king <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelred_the_Unready" title="Æthelred the Unready">Æthelred the Unready</a> married <a href="/wiki/Emma_of_Normandy" title="Emma of Normandy">Emma of Normandy</a>, the sister of <a href="/wiki/Richard_II,_Duke_of_Normandy" title="Richard II, Duke of Normandy">Richard II, Duke of Normandy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Unready54_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unready54-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Their son <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a>, who spent many years in exile in Normandy, succeeded to the English throne in 1042.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruling3_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruling3-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This led to the establishment of a powerful Norman interest in English politics, as Edward drew heavily on his former hosts for support, bringing in Norman courtiers, soldiers, and clerics and appointing them to positions of power, particularly in the Church. Childless and embroiled in conflict with the formidable <a href="/wiki/Godwin,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Godwin, Earl of Wessex">Godwin, Earl of Wessex</a> and his sons, Edward may also have encouraged Duke William of Normandy's ambitions for the English throne.<sup id="cite_ref-Unification86_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Unification86-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When King Edward died at the beginning of 1066, the lack of a clear heir led to a disputed succession in which several contenders laid claim to the throne of England.<sup id="cite_ref-Death167_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Death167-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Edward's immediate successor was the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Wessex" title="Earl of Wessex">Earl of Wessex</a>, Harold Godwinson, the richest and most powerful of the English aristocrats. Harold was elected king by the <a href="/wiki/Witenagemot" title="Witenagemot">Witenagemot</a> of England and crowned by the <a href="/wiki/Ealdred_(archbishop_of_York)" title="Ealdred (archbishop of York)">Archbishop of York, Ealdred</a>, although Norman propaganda claimed the ceremony was performed by <a href="/wiki/Stigand" title="Stigand">Stigand</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">uncanonically</a> elected <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Death167_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Death167-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Walker136_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walker136-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Harold was immediately challenged by two powerful neighbouring rulers. Duke William claimed that he had been promised the throne by King Edward and that Harold had sworn agreement to this;<sup id="cite_ref-BatesWilliam73_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BatesWilliam73-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> King Harald III of Norway, commonly known as Harald Hardrada, also contested the succession. His claim to the throne was based on an agreement between his predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Magnus_the_Good" title="Magnus the Good">Magnus the Good</a>, and the earlier English king, <a href="/wiki/Harthacnut" title="Harthacnut">Harthacnut</a>, whereby if either died without heir, the other would inherit both England and Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-Death188_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Death188-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> William and Harald at once set about assembling troops and ships to invade England.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruling12_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruling12-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Tostig.27s_raids_and_the_Norwegian_invasion"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tostig's_raids_and_the_Norwegian_invasion">Tostig's raids and the Norwegian invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tostig&#039;s raids and the Norwegian invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a></div>
<p>In early 1066, Harold's exiled brother, <a href="/wiki/Tostig_Godwinson" title="Tostig Godwinson">Tostig Godwinson</a>, raided southeastern England with a fleet he had recruited in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, later joined by other ships from <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> Threatened by Harold's fleet, Tostig moved north and raided in <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, but he was driven back to his ships by the brothers <a href="/wiki/Edwin,_Earl_of_Mercia" title="Edwin, Earl of Mercia">Edwin, Earl of Mercia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Morcar_of_Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Morcar of Northumbria">Morcar, Earl of Northumbria</a>. Deserted by most of his followers, Tostig withdrew to Scotland, where he spent the summer recruiting fresh forces.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold144_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold144-26">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup> King Harold spent the summer on the south coast with a large army and fleet waiting for William to invade, but the bulk of his forces were militia who needed to harvest their crops, so on 8 September Harold dismissed them.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold145_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold145-28">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>King Harald Hardrada invaded northern England in early September, leading a fleet of more than 300 ships carrying perhaps 15,000 men. Harald's army was further augmented by the forces of Tostig, who threw his support behind the Norwegian king's bid for the throne. Advancing on York, the Norwegians defeated a northern English army under Edwin and Morcar on 20 September at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fulford" title="Battle of Fulford">Battle of Fulford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold154_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold154-29">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The two earls had rushed to engage the Norwegian forces before King Harold could arrive from the south. Although Harold Godwinson had married Edwin and Morcar's sister <a href="/wiki/Ealdgyth,_daughter_of_Earl_%C3%86lfgar" title="Ealdgyth, daughter of Earl Ælfgar">Ealdgyth</a>, the two earls may have distrusted Harold and feared that the king would replace Morcar with Tostig. The end result was that their forces were devastated and unable to participate in the rest of the campaigns of 1066, although the two earls survived the battle.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren65_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren65-30">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hardrada moved on to York, which surrendered to him. After taking hostages from the leading men of the city, on 24 September the Norwegians moved east to the tiny village of <a href="/wiki/Stamford_Bridge,_East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="Stamford Bridge, East Riding of Yorkshire">Stamford Bridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren73_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren73-31">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> King Harold probably learned of the Norwegian invasion in mid-September and rushed north, gathering forces as he went.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold158_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold158-32">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The royal forces probably took nine days to cover the distance from London to York, averaging almost 25 miles (40 kilometres) per day. At dawn on 25 September Harold's forces reached York, where he learned the location of the Norwegians.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren74_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren74-33">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The English then marched on the invaders and took them by surprise, defeating them in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Stamford_Bridge" title="Battle of Stamford Bridge">Battle of Stamford Bridge</a>. Harald of Norway and Tostig were killed, and the Norwegians suffered such horrific losses that only 24 of the original 300 ships were required to carry away the survivors. The English victory was costly, however, as Harold's army was left in a battered and weakened state, and far from the English Channel.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold158_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold158-32">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_invasion">Norman invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Norman invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_preparations_and_forces">Norman preparations and forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Norman preparations and forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>William assembled a large invasion fleet and an army gathered from Normandy and all over France, including large contingents from <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and Flanders.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates79_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates79-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> He mustered his forces at <a href="/wiki/Saint-Valery-sur-Somme" title="Saint-Valery-sur-Somme">Saint-Valery-sur-Somme</a> and was ready to cross the Channel by about 12 August.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas192_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas192-35">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The exact numbers and composition of William's force are unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-36">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A contemporary document claims that William had 726 ships, but this may be an inflated figure.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett25_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett25-37">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Figures given by contemporary writers are highly exaggerated, varying from 14,000 to 150,000 men.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson163_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson163-38">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Modern historians have offered a range of estimates for the size of William's forces: 7000–8000 men, 1000–2000 of them cavalry;<sup id="cite_ref-Bennet26_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennet26-39">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> 10,000–12,000 men;<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson163_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson163-38">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> 10,000 men, 3000 of them cavalry;<sup id="cite_ref-Marren89_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren89-40">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> or 7500 men.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-36">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The army would have consisted of a mix of cavalry, infantry, and archers or crossbowmen, with about equal numbers of cavalry and archers and the foot soldiers equal in number to the other two types combined.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett27_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett27-41">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Although later lists of <a href="/wiki/Companions_of_William_the_Conqueror" title="Companions of William the Conqueror">companions of William the Conqueror</a> are extant, most are padded with extra names; only about 35 individuals can be reliably claimed to have been with William at Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-36">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marren108_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren108-42">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;e&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_of_Poitiers" title="William of Poitiers">William of Poitiers</a> states that William obtained Pope <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_II" title="Pope Alexander II">Alexander II</a>'s consent for the invasion, signified by a papal banner, along with diplomatic support from other European rulers. Although Alexander did give papal approval to the conquest after it succeeded, no other source claims papal support before the invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;f&#93;</a></sup> William's army assembled during the summer while an invasion fleet in Normandy was constructed. Although the army and fleet were ready by early August, adverse winds kept the ships in Normandy until late September. There were probably other reasons for William's delay, including intelligence reports from England revealing that Harold's forces were deployed along the coast. William would have preferred to delay the invasion until he could make an unopposed landing.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft120_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft120-45">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Landing_and_Harold.27s_march_south"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Landing_and_Harold's_march_south">Landing and Harold's march south</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Landing and Harold&#039;s march south">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg/220px-BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg" width="220" height="93" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg/330px-BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg/440px-BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg 2x" data-file-width="836" data-file-height="354" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BayeuxTapestryScene39.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Landing in England scene from the <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry" title="Bayeux Tapestry">Bayeux Tapestry</a>, depicting ships coming in and horses landing</div></div></div>
<p>The Normans crossed to England a few days after Harold's victory over the Norwegians at Stamford Bridge on 25 September, following the dispersal of Harold's naval force. They landed at <a href="/wiki/Pevensey" title="Pevensey">Pevensey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> on 28 September and erected a wooden castle at <a href="/wiki/Hastings" title="Hastings">Hastings</a>, from which they raided the surrounding area.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates79_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates79-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> This ensured supplies for the army, and as Harold and his family held many of the lands in the area, it weakened William's opponent and made him more likely to attack to put an end to the raiding.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren98_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren98-47">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Harold, after defeating his brother Tostig and Harald Hardrada in the north, left much of his force there, including Morcar and Edwin, and marched the rest of his army south to deal with the threatened Norman invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter72_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter72-48">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> It is unclear when Harold learned of William's landing, but it was probably while he was travelling south. Harold stopped in London for about a week before reaching Hastings, so it is likely that he took a second week to march south, averaging about 27 miles (43 kilometres) per day,<sup id="cite_ref-Marren93_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren93-49">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> for the nearly 200 miles (320 kilometres) to London.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft124_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft124-50">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Although Harold attempted to surprise the Normans, William's scouts reported the English arrival to the duke. The exact events preceding the battle remain obscure, with contradictory accounts in the sources, but all agree that William led his army from his castle and advanced towards the enemy.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson180_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson180-51">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Harold had taken up a defensive position at the top of Senlac Hill (present-day <a href="/wiki/Battle,_East_Sussex" title="Battle, East Sussex">Battle, East Sussex</a>), about 6 miles (10 kilometres) from William's castle at Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren99_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren99-52">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Contemporary sources do not give reliable data on the size and composition of Harold's army, although two Norman sources give figures of 1.2&#160;million or 400,000 men.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson128_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson128-53">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Recent historians have suggested figures of between 5000 and 13,000 for Harold's army at Hastings,<sup id="cite_ref-Lawson130_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawson130-54">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> but most agree on a range of between 7000 and 8000 English troops.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett28_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett28-55">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Marren105_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren105-56">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> These men would have comprised a mix of the <i><a href="/wiki/Fyrd" title="Fyrd">fyrd</a></i> (militia mainly composed of foot soldiers) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Housecarls" class="mw-redirect" title="Housecarls">housecarls</a></i>, or nobleman's personal troops, who usually also fought on foot. The main difference between the two types was in their armour; the <i>housecarls</i> used better protecting armour than that of the <i>fyrd</i>. The English army does not appear to have had many archers, although some were present.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett28_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett28-55">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The identities of few of the Englishmen at Hastings are known; the most important were Harold's brothers <a href="/wiki/Gyrth" class="mw-redirect" title="Gyrth">Gyrth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leofwine_Godwinson" title="Leofwine Godwinson">Leofwine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett20_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett20-36">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> About 18 other named individuals can reasonably be assumed to have fought with Harold at Hastings, including two other relatives.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren107_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren107-43">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;g&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hastings">Hastings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hastings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hastings" title="Battle of Hastings">Battle of Hastings</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg/220px-Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg" width="220" height="236" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg/330px-Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg/440px-Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1606" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bayeuxtapestrydeathofharold.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Likely depiction of Harold's death from the Bayeux Tapestry</div></div></div>
<p>The battle began at about 9&#160;am on 14 October 1066 and lasted all day, but while a broad outline is known, the exact events are obscured by contradictory accounts in the sources.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft126_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft126-58">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Although the numbers on each side were probably about equal, William had both cavalry and infantry, including many archers, while Harold had only foot soldiers and few archers.<sup id="cite_ref-Carpenter73_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carpenter73-59">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The English soldiers formed up as a <a href="/wiki/Shield_wall" title="Shield wall">shield wall</a> along the ridge, and were at first so effective that William's army was thrown back with heavy casualties. Some of William's Breton troops panicked and fled, and some of the English troops appear to have pursued the fleeing Bretons. Norman cavalry then attacked and killed the pursuing troops. While the Bretons were fleeing, rumours swept the Norman forces that the duke had been killed, but William rallied his troops. Twice more the Normans made <a href="/wiki/Withdrawal_(military)#Feigned_retreat" title="Withdrawal (military)">feigned withdrawals</a>, tempting the English into pursuit, and allowing the Norman cavalry to attack them repeatedly.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft127_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft127-60">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The available sources are more confused about events in the afternoon, but it appears that the decisive event was the death of Harold, about which different stories are told. William of Jumieges claimed that Harold was killed by the duke. The <a href="/wiki/Bayeux_Tapestry" title="Bayeux Tapestry">Bayeux Tapestry</a> has been claimed to show Harold's death by an arrow to the eye, but this may be a later reworking of the tapestry to conform to 12th-century stories that Harold had died from an arrow wound to the head.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft129_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft129-61">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Other sources stated that no one knew how Harold died because the press of battle was so tight around the king that the soldiers could not see who struck the fatal blow.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren137_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren137-62">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> William of Poitiers gives no details at all about Harold's death.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett77_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett77-63">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_of_Hastings">Aftermath of Hastings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Aftermath of Hastings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The day after the battle, Harold's body was identified, either by his armour or marks on his body.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;h&#93;</a></sup> The bodies of the English dead, who included some of Harold's brothers and his <i>housecarls</i>, were left on the battlefield,<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft131_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft131-66">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> although some were removed by relatives later.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett81_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett81-67">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gytha_Thorkelsd%C3%B3ttir" title="Gytha Thorkelsdóttir">Gytha</a>, Harold's mother, offered the victorious duke the weight of her son's body in gold for its custody, but her offer was refused. William ordered that Harold's body be thrown into the sea, but whether that took place is unclear.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft131_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft131-66">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Another story relates that Harold was buried at the top of a cliff.<sup id="cite_ref-Marren146_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marren146-68">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Waltham_Abbey_(abbey)" class="mw-redirect" title="Waltham Abbey (abbey)">Waltham Abbey</a>, which had been founded by Harold, later claimed that his body had been buried there secretly.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft131_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft131-66">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Later legends claimed that Harold did not die at Hastings, but escaped and became a hermit at Chester.<sup id="cite_ref-Gravett81_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gravett81-67">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After his victory at Hastings, William expected to receive the submission of the surviving English leaders, but instead <a href="/wiki/Edgar_the_%C3%86theling" class="mw-redirect" title="Edgar the Ætheling">Edgar the Ætheling</a><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;i&#93;</a></sup> was proclaimed king by the Witenagemot, with the support of Earls Edwin and Morcar, Stigand, the Archbishop of Canterbury, and Ealdred, the Archbishop of York.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas204_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas204-71">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> William therefore advanced, marching around the coast of <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> to London. He defeated an English force that attacked him at <a href="/wiki/Southwark" title="Southwark">Southwark</a>, but being unable to storm <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a> he sought to reach the capital by a more circuitous route.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas205_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas205-72">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>William moved up the <a href="/wiki/Thames" class="mw-redirect" title="Thames">Thames</a> valley to cross the river at <a href="/wiki/Wallingford,_Oxfordshire" title="Wallingford, Oxfordshire">Wallingford</a>, Berkshire; while there he received the submission of Stigand. He then travelled north-east along the <a href="/wiki/Chilterns" class="mw-redirect" title="Chilterns">Chilterns</a>, before advancing towards London from the north-west, fighting further engagements against forces from the city. Having failed to muster an effective military response, Edgar's leading supporters lost their nerve, and the English leaders surrendered to William at <a href="/wiki/Berkhamsted" title="Berkhamsted">Berkhamsted</a>, Hertfordshire. William was acclaimed King of England and crowned by Ealdred on 25 December 1066, in <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas205_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas205-72">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;j&#93;</a></sup> The new king attempted to conciliate the remaining English nobility by confirming Morcar, Edwin and <a href="/wiki/Waltheof,_Earl_of_Northumbria" title="Waltheof, Earl of Northumbria">Waltheof, the Earl of Northumbria</a>, in their lands as well as giving some land to Edgar the Ætheling. William remained in England until March 1067, when he returned to Normandy with English prisoners, including Stigand, Morcar, Edwin, Edgar the Ætheling, and Waltheof.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft138_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft138-75">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English_resistance">English resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: English resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_rebellions">First rebellions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: First rebellions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the submission of the English nobles, resistance continued for several years.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas212_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas212-76">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> William left control of England in the hands of his half-brother <a href="/wiki/Bishop_Odo" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop Odo">Odo</a> and one of his closest supporters, <a href="/wiki/William_fitzOsbern" class="mw-redirect" title="William fitzOsbern">William fitzOsbern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft138_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft138-75">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> In 1067 rebels in Kent launched an unsuccessful attack on Dover Castle in combination with <a href="/wiki/Eustace_II_of_Boulogne" class="mw-redirect" title="Eustace II of Boulogne">Eustace II of Boulogne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas212_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas212-76">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a> landowner <a href="/wiki/Eadric_the_Wild" title="Eadric the Wild">Eadric the Wild</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;k&#93;</a></sup> in alliance with the Welsh rulers of <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a> and <a href="/wiki/Powys" title="Powys">Powys</a>, raised a revolt in western <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a>, fighting Norman forces based in <a href="/wiki/Hereford" title="Hereford">Hereford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas212_76-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas212-76">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> These events forced William to return to England at the end of 1067.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft138_75-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft138-75">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> In 1068 William <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Exeter_(1068)" title="Siege of Exeter (1068)">besieged rebels in Exeter</a>, including Harold's mother Gytha, and after suffering heavy losses managed to negotiate the town's surrender.<sup id="cite_ref-Harold186_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harold186-79">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> In May, William's wife <a href="/wiki/Matilda_of_Flanders" title="Matilda of Flanders">Matilda</a> was crowned queen at Westminster, an important symbol of William's growing international stature.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft140_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft140-80">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Later in the year Edwin and Morcar raised a revolt in Mercia with Welsh assistance, while <a href="/wiki/Gospatric,_Earl_of_Northumbria" title="Gospatric, Earl of Northumbria">Gospatric</a>, the newly appointed Earl of Northumbria,<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;l&#93;</a></sup> led a rising in Northumbria, which had not yet been occupied by the Normans. These rebellions rapidly collapsed as William moved against them, building castles and installing garrisons as he had already done in the south.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas214_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas214-83">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Edwin and Morcar again submitted, while Gospatric fled to Scotland, as did Edgar the Ætheling and his family, who may have been involved in these revolts.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams24_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams24-84">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, Harold's sons, who had taken refuge in Ireland, raided <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>, Devon and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> from the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams20_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams20-85">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revolts_of_1069">Revolts of 1069</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Revolts of 1069">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Harrying_of_the_North" title="Harrying of the North">Harrying of the North</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Baile_Hill,_York.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Baile_Hill%2C_York.JPG/220px-Baile_Hill%2C_York.JPG" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Baile_Hill%2C_York.JPG/330px-Baile_Hill%2C_York.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Baile_Hill%2C_York.JPG/440px-Baile_Hill%2C_York.JPG 2x" data-file-width="698" data-file-height="352" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Baile_Hill,_York.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The remains of <a href="/wiki/Baile_Hill" title="Baile Hill">Baile Hill</a>, the second <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey_castle" title="Motte-and-bailey castle">motte-and-bailey castle</a> built by William in York</div></div></div>
<p>Early in 1069 the newly installed Norman Earl of Northumbria, <a href="/wiki/Robert_de_Comines" title="Robert de Comines">Robert de Comines</a>, and several hundred soldiers accompanying him were massacred at Durham; the Northumbrian rebellion was joined by Edgar, Gospatric, <a href="/wiki/Siward_Barn" title="Siward Barn">Siward Barn</a> and other rebels who had taken refuge in Scotland. The castellan of York, Robert fitzRichard, was defeated and killed, and the rebels besieged the Norman castle at York. William hurried north with an army, defeated the rebels outside York and pursued them into the city, massacring the inhabitants and bringing the revolt to an end.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams27_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams27-86">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> He built a second castle at York, strengthened Norman forces in Northumbria and then returned south. A subsequent local uprising was crushed by the garrison of York.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams27_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams27-86">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Harold's sons launched a second raid from Ireland and were defeated in Devon by Norman forces under Count <a href="/wiki/Brian_of_Brittany" title="Brian of Brittany">Brian</a>, a son of <a href="/wiki/Eudes,_Count_of_Penthi%C3%A8vre" class="mw-redirect" title="Eudes, Count of Penthièvre">Eudes, Count of Penthièvre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams35_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams35-87">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> In August or September 1069 a large fleet sent by <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_II_of_Denmark" title="Sweyn II of Denmark">Sweyn II of Denmark</a> arrived off the coast of England, sparking a new wave of rebellions across the country. After abortive raids in the south, the Danes joined forces with a new Northumbrian uprising, which was also joined by Edgar, Gospatric and the other exiles from Scotland as well as Waltheof. The combined Danish and English forces defeated the Norman garrison at York, seized the castles and took control of Northumbria, although a raid into Lincolnshire led by Edgar was defeated by the Norman garrison of <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_England" title="Lincoln, England">Lincoln</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams35a_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams35a-88">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the same time resistance flared up again in western Mercia, where the forces of Eadric the Wild, together with his Welsh allies and further rebel forces from <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> and Shropshire, attacked the castle at <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury" title="Shrewsbury">Shrewsbury</a>. In the south-west, rebels from Devon and Cornwall attacked the Norman garrison at Exeter but were repulsed by the defenders and scattered by a Norman relief force under Count Brian. Other rebels from <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a>, Somerset and neighbouring areas besieged <a href="/wiki/Montacute_Castle" title="Montacute Castle">Montacute Castle</a> but were defeated by a Norman army gathered from London, <a href="/wiki/Winchester" title="Winchester">Winchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salisbury" title="Salisbury">Salisbury</a> under <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_de_Montbray" title="Geoffrey de Montbray">Geoffrey of Coutances</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams35a_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams35a-88">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, William attacked the Danes, who had moored for the winter south of the Humber in Lincolnshire, and drove them back to the north bank. Leaving <a href="/wiki/Robert_of_Mortain" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert of Mortain">Robert of Mortain</a> in charge of Lincolnshire, he turned west and defeated the Mercian rebels in battle at <a href="/wiki/Stafford" title="Stafford">Stafford</a>. When the Danes attempted to return to Lincolnshire, the Norman forces there again drove them back across the Humber. William advanced into Northumbria, defeating an attempt to block his crossing of the swollen <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a> at <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a>. The Danes fled at his approach, and he occupied York. He bought off the Danes, who agreed to leave England in the spring, and during the winter of 1069–70 his forces systematically devastated Northumbria in the <a href="/wiki/Harrying_of_the_North" title="Harrying of the North">Harrying of the North</a>, subduing all resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams35a_88-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams35a-88">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> As a symbol of his renewed authority over the north, William ceremonially wore his crown at York on Christmas Day 1069.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft142_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft142-81">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In early 1070, having secured the submission of Waltheof and Gospatric, and driven Edgar and his remaining supporters back to Scotland, William returned to Mercia, where he based himself at Chester and crushed all remaining resistance in the area before returning to the south.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams35a_88-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams35a-88">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">Papal legates</a> arrived and at Easter re-crowned William, which would have symbolically reasserted his right to the kingdom. William also oversaw a purge of prelates from the Church, most notably Stigand, who was deposed from Canterbury. The papal legates also imposed <a href="/wiki/Penance" title="Penance">penances</a> on William and those of his supporters who had taken part in Hastings and the subsequent campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft145_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft145-89">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> As well as Canterbury, the <a href="/wiki/See_of_York" class="mw-redirect" title="See of York">see of York</a> had become vacant following the death of Ealdred in September 1069. Both sees were filled by men loyal to William: <a href="/wiki/Lanfranc" title="Lanfranc">Lanfranc</a>, abbot of William's foundation at <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Saint-%C3%89tienne,_Caen" title="Abbey of Saint-Étienne, Caen">Caen</a>, received Canterbury while <a href="/wiki/Thomas_of_Bayeux" title="Thomas of Bayeux">Thomas of Bayeux</a>, one of William's chaplains, was installed at York. Some other bishoprics and abbeys also received new bishops and abbots and William confiscated some of the wealth of the English monasteries, which had served as repositories for the assets of the native nobles.<sup id="cite_ref-Bennett56_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett56-90">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Danish_troubles">Danish troubles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Danish troubles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg/220px-Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg" width="220" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg/330px-Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg/440px-Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1539" data-file-height="1503" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coin_king_of_denmark_sven_estridsen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coin of Sweyn II of Denmark</div></div></div>
<p>In 1070 Sweyn II of Denmark arrived to take personal command of his fleet and renounced the earlier agreement to withdraw, sending troops into <a href="/wiki/The_Fens" title="The Fens">the Fens</a> to join forces with English rebels led by <a href="/wiki/Hereward_the_Wake" title="Hereward the Wake">Hereward the Wake</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;m&#93;</a></sup> at that time based on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Isle of Ely</a>. Sweyn soon accepted a further payment of <a href="/wiki/Danegeld" title="Danegeld">Danegeld</a> from William, and returned home.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas221_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas221-93">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> After the departure of the Danes the Fenland rebels remained at large, protected by the marshes, and early in 1071 there was a final outbreak of rebel activity in the area. Edwin and Morcar again turned against William, and although Edwin was quickly betrayed and killed, Morcar reached <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a>, where he and Hereward were joined by exiled rebels who had sailed from Scotland. William arrived with an army and a fleet to finish off this last pocket of resistance. After some costly failures the Normans managed to construct a pontoon to reach the Isle of Ely, defeated the rebels at the bridgehead and stormed the island, marking the effective end of English resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-Williams49_94-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Williams49-94">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Morcar was imprisoned for the rest of his life; Hereward was pardoned and had his lands returned to him.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft146_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft146-95">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_resistance">Last resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Last resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Earls" title="Revolt of the Earls">Revolt of the Earls</a></div>
<p>William faced difficulties in his continental possessions in 1071,<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas225_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas225-96">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> but in 1072 he returned to England and marched north to confront King <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_III_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm III of Scotland">Malcolm III of Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;n&#93;</a></sup> This campaign, which included a land army supported by a fleet, resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Abernethy" title="Treaty of Abernethy">Treaty of Abernethy</a> in which Malcolm expelled Edgar the Ætheling from Scotland and agreed to some degree of subordination to William.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft146_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft146-95">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> The exact status of this subordination was unclear&#160;– the treaty merely stated that Malcolm became William's man. Whether this meant only for Cumbria and Lothian or for the whole Scottish kingdom was left ambiguous.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas227_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas227-98">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1075, during William's absence, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_de_Gael" title="Ralph de Gael">Ralph de Gael</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Norfolk" title="Earl of Norfolk">Earl of Norfolk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roger_de_Breteuil,_2nd_Earl_of_Hereford" title="Roger de Breteuil, 2nd Earl of Hereford">Roger de Breteuil</a> the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Hereford" title="Earl of Hereford">Earl of Hereford</a>, conspired to overthrow him in the <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_the_Earls" title="Revolt of the Earls">Revolt of the Earls</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas231_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas231-99">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> The exact reason for the rebellion is unclear, but it was launched at the wedding of Ralph to a relative of Roger's, held at <a href="/wiki/Exning" title="Exning">Exning</a>. Another earl, Waltheof, despite being one of William's favourites, was also involved, and some Breton lords were ready to offer support. Ralph also requested Danish aid. William remained in Normandy while his men in England subdued the revolt. Roger was unable to leave his stronghold in Herefordshire because of efforts by <a href="/wiki/Wulfstan_(died_1095)" title="Wulfstan (died 1095)">Wulfstan</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Worcester" title="Bishop of Worcester">Bishop of Worcester</a>, and <a href="/wiki/%C3%86thelwig" title="Æthelwig">Æthelwig</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Abbot_of_Evesham" title="Abbot of Evesham">Abbot of Evesham</a>. Ralph was bottled up in <a href="/wiki/Norwich_Castle" title="Norwich Castle">Norwich Castle</a> by the combined efforts of Odo of Bayeux, Geoffrey of Coutances, <a href="/wiki/Richard_fitz_Gilbert" title="Richard fitz Gilbert">Richard fitzGilbert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_de_Warenne,_1st_Earl_of_Surrey" title="William de Warenne, 1st Earl of Surrey">William de Warenne</a>. Norwich was besieged and surrendered, and Ralph went into exile. Meanwhile, the Danish king's brother, <a href="/wiki/Canute_IV_of_Denmark" title="Canute IV of Denmark">Cnut</a>, had finally arrived in England with a fleet of 200 ships, but he was too late as Norwich had already surrendered. The Danes then raided along the coast before returning home.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas231_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas231-99">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> William did not return to England until later in 1075, to deal with the Danish threat and the aftermath of the rebellion, celebrating Christmas at Winchester.<sup id="cite_ref-Bates181_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bates181-100">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Roger and Waltheof were kept in prison, where Waltheof was executed in May 1076. By that time William had returned to the continent, where Ralph was continuing the rebellion from Brittany.<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas231_99-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas231-99">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Control_of_England">Control of England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Control of England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Londres_297..jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Londres_297..jpg/170px-Londres_297..jpg" width="170" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Londres_297..jpg/255px-Londres_297..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Londres_297..jpg/340px-Londres_297..jpg 2x" data-file-width="644" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Londres_297..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a>, originally begun by William the Conqueror to control London<sup id="cite_ref-Douglas216_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Douglas216-101">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Once England had been conquered, the Normans faced many challenges in maintaining control.<sup id="cite_ref-Stafford102_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stafford102-102">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> They were few in number compared to the native English population; including those from other parts of France, historians estimate the number of Norman landholders at around 8000.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery82_103-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery82-103">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> William's followers expected and received lands and titles in return for their service in the invasion,<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery79_104-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery79-104">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> but William claimed ultimate possession of the land in England over which his armies had given him <i>de facto</i> control, and asserted the right to dispose of it as he saw fit.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery84_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery84-105">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Henceforth, all land was "held" directly from the king in <a href="/wiki/Feudal_land_tenure" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal land tenure">feudal tenure</a> in return for military service.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery84_105-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery84-105">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> A Norman lord typically had properties located in a piecemeal fashion throughout England and Normandy, and not in a single geographic block.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery83_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery83-106">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To find the lands to compensate his Norman followers, William initially confiscated the estates of all the English lords who had fought and died with Harold and redistributed part of their lands.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery75_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery75-107">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> These confiscations led to revolts, which resulted in more confiscations, a cycle that continued for five years after the Battle of Hastings.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery79_104-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery79-104">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> To put down and prevent further rebellions the Normans constructed castles and fortifications in unprecedented numbers,<sup id="cite_ref-Chibnall11_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chibnall11-108">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> initially mostly on the <a href="/wiki/Motte-and-bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="Motte-and-bailey">motte-and-bailey</a> pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-Forts110_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forts110-109">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> Historian Robert Liddiard remarks that "to glance at the urban landscape of Norwich, Durham or Lincoln is to be forcibly reminded of the impact of the Norman invasion".<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> William and his barons also exercised tighter control over inheritance of property by widows and daughters, often forcing marriages to Normans.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery89_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery89-111">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A measure of William's success in taking control is that, from 1072 until the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bouvines" title="Battle of Bouvines">Capetian conquest</a> of Normandy in 1204, William and his successors were largely absentee rulers. For example, after 1072, William spent more than 75 per cent of his time in France rather than England. While he needed to be personally present in Normandy to defend the realm from foreign invasion and put down internal revolts, he set up royal administrative structures that enabled him to rule England from a distance.<sup id="cite_ref-Mastery91_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mastery91-112">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elite_replacement">Elite replacement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Elite replacement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A direct consequence of the invasion was the almost total elimination of the old English aristocracy and the loss of English control over the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> in England. William systematically dispossessed English landowners and conferred their property on his continental followers. The <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> meticulously documents the impact of this colossal programme of expropriation, revealing that by 1086 only about 5 per cent of land in England south of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">Tees</a> was left in English hands. Even this tiny residue was further diminished in the decades that followed, the elimination of native landholding being most complete in southern parts of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Natives were also removed from high governmental and ecclesiastical office. After 1075 all earldoms were held by Normans, and Englishmen were only occasionally appointed as sheriffs. Likewise in the Church, senior English office-holders were either expelled from their positions or kept in place for their lifetimes and replaced by foreigners when they died. By 1096 no bishopric was held by any Englishman, and English abbots became uncommon, especially in the larger monasteries.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English_emigration">English emigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: English emigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/New_England_(medieval)" title="New England (medieval)">New England (medieval)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg/220px-The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg" width="220" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg/330px-The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg/440px-The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2245" data-file-height="1230" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_body_of_Leo_V_is_dragged_to_the_Hippodrome_through_the_Skyla_Gate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of the <a href="/wiki/Varangian_Guard" title="Varangian Guard">Varangian Guard</a> from the 12th-century <a href="/wiki/Madrid_Skylitzes" title="Madrid Skylitzes">Madrid Skylitzes</a></div></div></div>
<p>Following the conquest, many Anglo-Saxons, including groups of nobles, fled the country<sup id="cite_ref-Byzantium_116-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byzantium-116">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> for Scotland, Ireland, or Scandinavia.<sup id="cite_ref-Daniell_117-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daniell-117">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> Members of King Harold Godwinson's family sought refuge in Ireland and used their bases in that country for unsuccessful invasions of England.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft140_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft140-80">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> The largest single exodus occurred in the 1070s, when a group of Anglo-Saxons in a fleet of 235 ships sailed for the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Daniell_117-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daniell-117">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> The empire became a popular destination for many English nobles and soldiers, as the Byzantines were in need of mercenaries.<sup id="cite_ref-Byzantium_116-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byzantium-116">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> The English became the predominant element in the elite <a href="/wiki/Varangian_Guard" title="Varangian Guard">Varangian Guard</a>, until then a largely Scandinavian unit, from which the emperor's bodyguard was drawn.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> Some of the English migrants were settled in Byzantine frontier regions on the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> coast, and established towns with names such as New London and New York.<sup id="cite_ref-Byzantium_116-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Byzantium-116">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governmental_systems">Governmental systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Governmental systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Domesday_book--w.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Domesday_book--w.jpg/170px-Domesday_book--w.jpg" width="170" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Domesday_book--w.jpg/255px-Domesday_book--w.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Domesday_book--w.jpg/340px-Domesday_book--w.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="807" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Domesday_book--w.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Page from the Warwickshire Domesday survey</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_England_1086.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Map_of_England_1086.png/170px-Map_of_England_1086.png" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Map_of_England_1086.png/255px-Map_of_England_1086.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Map_of_England_1086.png/340px-Map_of_England_1086.png 2x" data-file-width="2398" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_England_1086.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>English counties in 1086</div></div></div>
<p>Before the Normans arrived, Anglo-Saxon governmental systems were more sophisticated than their counterparts in Normandy.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas59_119-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas59-119">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Conquest187_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conquest187-120">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> All of England was divided into administrative units called <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shires</a>, with subdivisions; the royal court was the centre of government, and a justice system based on local and regional tribunals existed to secure the rights of free men.<sup id="cite_ref-Govern176_121-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Govern176-121">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> Shires were run by officials known as shire <a href="/wiki/Reeve_(England)" title="Reeve (England)">reeves</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriffs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas60_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas60-122">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> Most medieval governments were always on the move, holding court wherever the weather and food or other matters were best at the moment;<sup id="cite_ref-Conquest31_123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conquest31-123">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> England had a permanent treasury at <a href="/wiki/Winchester,_Hampshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Winchester, Hampshire">Winchester</a> before William's conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-Conquest194_124-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conquest194-124">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> One major reason for the strength of the English monarchy was the wealth of the kingdom, built on the English system of taxation that included a land tax, or the geld. English coinage was also superior to most of the other currency in use in northwestern Europe, and the ability to mint coins was a royal monopoly.<sup id="cite_ref-Conquest36_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conquest36-125">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> The English kings had also developed the system of issuing <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">writs</a> to their officials, in addition to the normal medieval practice of issuing <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Conquest198_126-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conquest198-126">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> Writs were either instructions to an official or group of officials, or notifications of royal actions such as appointments to office or a grant of some sort.<sup id="cite_ref-Writ_127-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Writ-127">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This sophisticated medieval form of government was handed over to the Normans and was the foundation of further developments.<sup id="cite_ref-Govern176_121-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Govern176-121">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup> They kept the framework of government but made changes in the personnel, although at first the new king attempted to keep some natives in office. By the end of William's reign most of the officials of government and the royal household were Normans. The language of official documents also changed, from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> to Latin. The forest laws were introduced, leading to the setting aside of large sections of England as <a href="/wiki/Royal_forest" title="Royal forest">royal forest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas60_122-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas60-122">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> The Domesday survey was an administrative catalogue of the landholdings of the kingdom, and was unique to medieval Europe. It was divided into sections based on the shires, and listed all the landholdings of each <a href="/wiki/Tenant-in-chief" title="Tenant-in-chief">tenant-in-chief</a> of the king as well as who had held the land before the conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-Conquest200_128-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conquest200-128">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most obvious effects of the conquest was the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">Anglo-Norman</a>, a northern <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>-influenced dialect of <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a>, as the language of the ruling classes in England, displacing Old English. <a href="/wiki/Norman_French" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman French">Norman French</a> words entered the English language, and a further sign of the shift was the usage of names common in France instead of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_name" title="Germanic name">Anglo-Saxon names</a>. Male names such as <a href="/wiki/William_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="William (name)">William</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert" title="Robert">Robert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard" title="Richard">Richard</a> soon became common; female names changed more slowly. The Norman invasion had little impact on <a href="/wiki/Placenames" class="mw-redirect" title="Placenames">placenames</a>, which had changed significantly after earlier <a href="/wiki/Norse_activity_in_the_British_Isles" title="Norse activity in the British Isles">Scandinavian invasions</a>. It is not known precisely how much English the Norman invaders learned, nor how much the knowledge of Norman French spread among the lower classes, but the demands of trade and basic communication probably meant that at least some of the Normans and native English were bilingual.<sup id="cite_ref-Conquest323_129-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conquest323-129">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> never developed a working knowledge of English and for centuries afterwards English was not well understood by the nobility.<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration_and_intermarriage">Immigration and intermarriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Immigration and intermarriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An estimated 8000 Normans and other continentals settled in England as a result of the conquest, although exact figures cannot be established. Some of these new residents intermarried with the native English, but the extent of this practice in the years immediately after Hastings is unclear. Several marriages are attested between Norman men and English women during the years before 1100, but such marriages were uncommon. Most Normans continued to contract marriages with other Normans or other continental families rather than with the English.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft321_131-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft321-131">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> Within a century of the invasion, intermarriage between the native English and the Norman immigrants had become common. By the early 1160s, <a href="/wiki/Ailred_of_Rievaulx" class="mw-redirect" title="Ailred of Rievaulx">Ailred of Rievaulx</a> was writing that intermarriage was common in all levels of society.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas107_132-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas107-132">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Society">Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg/220px-Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg/330px-Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg/440px-Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anglo-Saxon_village_at_West_Stow_6337_Keith_Evans.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern-day reconstruction of an Anglo-Saxon village at <a href="/wiki/West_Stow_Anglo-Saxon_village" class="mw-redirect" title="West Stow Anglo-Saxon village">West Stow</a></div></div></div>
<p>The impact of the conquest on the lower levels of English society is difficult to assess. The major change was the elimination of <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_British_Isles" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavery in the British Isles">slavery in England</a>, which had disappeared by the middle of the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft327_133-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft327-133">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> There were about 28,000 slaves listed in <i>Domesday Book</i> in 1086, fewer than had been enumerated for 1066. In some places, such as Essex, the decline in slaves was 20 per cent for the 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Clanchy93_134-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clanchy93-134">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> The main reasons for the decline in slaveholding appear to have been the disapproval of the Church and the cost of supporting slaves, who unlike <a href="/wiki/Serf" class="mw-redirect" title="Serf">serfs</a>, had to be maintained entirely by their owners.<sup id="cite_ref-Ruling94_135-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ruling94-135">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> The practice of slavery was not outlawed, and the <i><a href="/wiki/Leges_Henrici_Primi" title="Leges Henrici Primi">Leges Henrici Primi</a></i> from the reign of King Henry I continue to mention slaveholding as legal.<sup id="cite_ref-Clanchy93_134-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clanchy93-134">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of the free peasants of Anglo-Saxon society appear to have lost status and become indistinguishable from the non-free serfs. Whether this change was due entirely to the conquest is unclear, but the invasion and its after-effects probably accelerated a process already under way. The spread of towns and increase in nucleated settlements in the countryside, rather than scattered farms, was probably accelerated by the coming of the Normans to England.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft327_133-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft327-133">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup> The lifestyle of the peasantry probably did not greatly change in the decades after 1066.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft329_136-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft329-136">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> Although earlier historians argued that women became less free and lost rights with the conquest, current scholarship has mostly rejected this view. Little is known about women other than those in the landholding class, so no conclusions can be drawn about peasant women's status after 1066. Noblewomen appear to have continued to influence political life mainly through their kinship relationships. Both before and after 1066 aristocratic women could own land, and some women continued to have the ability to dispose of their property as they wished.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft281_137-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft281-137">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_conquest_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Debate over the conquest started almost immediately. The <i><a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_Chronicle" title="Anglo-Saxon Chronicle">Anglo-Saxon Chronicle</a></i>, when discussing the death of William the Conqueror, denounced him and the conquest in verse, but the king's obituary notice from William of Poitiers, a Frenchman, was full of praise. Historians since then have argued over the facts of the matter and how to interpret them, with little agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-Clanchy31_138-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clanchy31-138">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> The theory or myth of the "<a href="/wiki/Norman_yoke" title="Norman yoke">Norman yoke</a>" arose in the 17th century,<sup id="cite_ref-Debate6_139-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debate6-139">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> the idea that Anglo-Saxon society had been freer and more equal than the society that emerged after the conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-Debate38_140-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debate38-140">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup> This theory owes more to the period in which it was developed than to historical facts, but it continues to be used to the present day in both political and popular thought.<sup id="cite_ref-Huscroft318_141-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huscroft318-141">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 20th and 21st centuries historians have focused less on the rightness or wrongness of the conquest itself, instead concentrating on the effects of the invasion. Some, such as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Southern" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Southern">Richard Southern</a>, have seen the conquest as a critical turning point in history.<sup id="cite_ref-Clanchy31_138-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clanchy31-138">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> Southern stated that "no country in Europe, between the rise of the barbarian kingdoms and the 20th century, has undergone so radical a change in so short a time as England experienced after 1066".<sup id="cite_ref-QClanchy32_142-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QClanchy32-142">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> Other historians, such as H. G. Richardson and G. O. Sayles, believe that the transformation was less radical.<sup id="cite_ref-Clanchy31_138-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clanchy31-138">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup> In more general terms, Singman has called the conquest "the last echo of the national migrations that characterized the early Middle Ages".<sup id="cite_ref-Singmanxv_143-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singmanxv-143">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup> The debate over the impact of the conquest depends on how change after 1066 is measured. If <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a> was already evolving before the invasion, with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a>, castles or other changes in society, then the conquest, while important, did not represent radical reform. But the change was dramatic if measured by the elimination of the English nobility or the loss of Old English as a literary language. Nationalistic arguments have been made on both sides of the debate, with the Normans cast as either the persecutors of the English or the rescuers of the country from a decadent Anglo-Saxon nobility.<sup id="cite_ref-Clanchy31_138-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clanchy31-138">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
