<p><b>Snooker</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a>: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>, <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ʊ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;push&#39;">ʊ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-Macmillan_British_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macmillan_British-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Macmillan_American_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macmillan_American-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Cue_sports" title="Cue sports">cue sport</a> which originated among <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> officers stationed in India in the latter half of the 19th century. It is played on a rectangular <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Snooker_tables" title="Billiard table">table</a> covered with a green cloth, or <a href="/wiki/Baize" title="Baize">baize</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Pockets_2" title="Billiard table">pockets</a> at each of the four corners and in the middle of each long side. Using a <a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">cue</a> and 22 <a href="/wiki/Billiard_ball#Snooker" title="Billiard ball">coloured balls</a>, players must strike the white ball (or "cue ball") to <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#pot" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § pot" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">pot</span></a></dfn> the remaining balls in the correct sequence, accumulating points for each pot. An individual game, or frame, is won by the player who scores the most points. A match is won when a player wins a predetermined number of frames.
</p><p>Snooker gained its own identity in 1884 when army officer <a href="/wiki/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain" title="Neville Francis Fitzgerald Chamberlain">Sir Neville Chamberlain</a> (not the <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Prime Minister</a> of that name), while stationed in <a href="/wiki/Ooty" title="Ooty">Ooty</a>, devised a set of rules that combined <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_pool" title="Pyramid pool">pyramid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life_pool" title="Life pool">life pool</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Neville_Chamberlain_disambiguation_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neville_Chamberlain_disambiguation-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The word "snooker" was a long-used military term used to describe inexperienced or first-year personnel. The game grew in popularity in England, and the Billiards Association and Control Club was formed in 1919. It is now governed by the <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Billiards_and_Snooker_Association" title="World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association">World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association</a> (WPBSA).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">World Snooker Championship</a> has taken place since <a href="/wiki/1927_World_Snooker_Championship" title="1927 World Snooker Championship">1927</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Joe_Davis" title="Joe Davis">Joe Davis</a> becoming a key figure in the early growth of the sport with 15 championship wins from 1927 to 1946. The modern era began in 1969 after the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> commissioned the snooker television show <i><a href="/wiki/Pot_Black" title="Pot Black">Pot Black</a></i> and began to air the World Championship in <a href="/wiki/1978_World_Snooker_Championship" title="1978 World Snooker Championship">1978</a>, leading to the sport's new peak in popularity. <a href="/wiki/Ray_Reardon" title="Ray Reardon">Ray Reardon</a> dominated the game in the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Davis" title="Steve Davis">Steve Davis</a> in the 1980s, and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hendry" title="Stephen Hendry">Stephen Hendry</a> in the 1990s; <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_O%27Sullivan" title="Ronnie O&#39;Sullivan">Ronnie O'Sullivan</a> has won the most world titles since 2000.
</p><p>Top professional players now compete regularly around the world and earn millions of pounds.<sup id="cite_ref-earnings_and_era_dominance_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-earnings_and_era_dominance-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The sport has become increasingly popular in China.<sup id="cite_ref-BeebDing2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeebDing2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_snooker" title="History of snooker">History of snooker</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png/170px-Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png" width="170" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png/255px-Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png/340px-Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png 2x" data-file-width="579" data-file-height="796" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain" title="Neville Francis Fitzgerald Chamberlain">Sir Neville Chamberlain</a>, a British Army officer who devised the game and its rules in the late 19th century.</div></div></div>
<p>The origin of snooker dates back to the latter half of the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-origins_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-origins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the 1870s, <a href="/wiki/English_billiards" title="English billiards">billiards</a> was a popular activity amongst <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> officers stationed in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and several variations of the game were devised during this time. One such variation originated at the officers' <a href="/wiki/Mess" title="Mess">mess</a> of the 11th <a href="/wiki/Devonshire_Regiment" title="Devonshire Regiment">Devonshire Regiment</a> in 1875,<sup id="cite_ref-heritage2008_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heritage2008-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which combined the rules of two <a href="/wiki/Pocket_billiards" class="mw-redirect" title="Pocket billiards">pocket billiards</a> games, <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_pool" title="Pyramid pool">pyramid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Life_pool" title="Life pool">life pool</a>. The former was played with fifteen red balls and one black positioned in a triangle, while the latter involved the potting of designated coloured balls.<sup id="cite_ref-jothiarun&#39;s_balls_book_of_billiards_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jothiarun&#39;s_balls_book_of_billiards-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The game developed its own identity in 1884 when its first set of rules was finalised by <a href="/wiki/Neville_Francis_Fitzgerald_Chamberlain" title="Neville Francis Fitzgerald Chamberlain">Sir Neville Chamberlain</a>, an English officer who helped develop and popularise the game at <a href="/wiki/Stone_House,_Ooty" title="Stone House, Ooty">Stone House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ooty" title="Ooty">Ooty</a> on a table built by Burroughes &amp; Watts that was brought over by boat.<sup id="cite_ref-telegraph2014_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph2014-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The word "snooker" was a slang term for first-year cadets and inexperienced military personnel, but Chamberlain would often use it to describe the inept performance of one of his fellow officers at the table. The name instantly stuck with the players.<sup id="cite_ref-origins_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-origins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jothiarun&#39;s_balls_book_of_billiards_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jothiarun&#39;s_balls_book_of_billiards-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-titan_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-titan-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag relies on an unreliable source. Just some unknown-author rambling on vendor website. There are real sources for this out there. (November 2017)">unreliable source</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1887, snooker was given its first definite reference in England in a copy of <i><a href="/wiki/Sporting_Life_(British_newspaper)" title="Sporting Life (British newspaper)">Sporting Life</a></i> which caused a growth in popularity.<sup id="cite_ref-heritage2008_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heritage2008-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Chamberlain came out as the game's inventor in a letter to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Field_(magazine)" title="The Field (magazine)">The Field</a></i> published on 19 March 1938, 63 years after the fact.<sup id="cite_ref-heritage2008_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heritage2008-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Snooker grew in popularity across the Indian colonies and the United Kingdom, but it remained a game mainly for the <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a>, and many gentlemen's clubs that had a billiards table would not allow non-members inside to play. To accommodate the growing interest, smaller and more open snooker-specific clubs were formed. In 1919, the Billiards Association and the Billiards Control Board merged to form the Billiards Association and Control Club (BA&amp;CC) and a new, standard set of rules for snooker first became official.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGadsbyWilliams20128_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGadsbyWilliams20128-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The game of snooker grew in the latter half of the 19th century and the early 20th century, and by <a href="/wiki/1927_World_Snooker_Championship" title="1927 World Snooker Championship">1927</a> the first <a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">World Snooker Championship</a><sup id="cite_ref-origins_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-origins-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> had been organised by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Davis" title="Joe Davis">Joe Davis</a> who, as a professional <a href="/wiki/English_billiards" title="English billiards">English billiards</a> and snooker player, moved the game from a <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">pastime</a> activity into a more professional sphere.<sup id="cite_ref-CnVTimeline_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CnVTimeline-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Davis won every world championship until 1946 when he retired. The game went into a decline through the 1950s and 1960s with little interest generated outside of those who played. In 1959, Davis introduced a variation of the game known as "snooker plus" (see the <i>Variations</i> section below) to try to improve the game's popularity by adding two extra colours, but it never caught on.
</p><p>A major advance occurred in 1969, when <a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">David Attenborough</a> commissioned the snooker television series <i><a href="/wiki/Pot_Black" title="Pot Black">Pot Black</a></i> to demonstrate the potential of <a href="/wiki/Color_television#Europe" title="Color television">colour television</a> with the green table and multi-coloured balls being ideal for showing off the advantages of colour broadcasting.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cue_China_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cue_China-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The series became a ratings success and was for a time the second-most popular show on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FCS-PB_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FCS-PB-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Interest in the game increased and the <a href="/wiki/1978_World_Snooker_Championship" title="1978 World Snooker Championship">1978 World Snooker Championship</a> was the first to be fully televised.<sup id="cite_ref-commonwealth_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-commonwealth-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OTT-40_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OTT-40-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The game quickly became a mainstream game<sup id="cite_ref-GuardianThatch_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianThatch-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> in the UK, Ireland and much of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> and has enjoyed much success since the late 1970s, with most of the ranking tournaments being televised. In 1985 a total of 18.5 million viewers watched the <a href="/wiki/1985_World_Snooker_Championship_final" title="1985 World Snooker Championship final">concluding frame</a> of the world championship final between <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Taylor" title="Dennis Taylor">Dennis Taylor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Davis" title="Steve Davis">Steve Davis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The loss of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_advertising" class="mw-redirect" title="Tobacco advertising">tobacco sponsorship</a> during the 2000s led to a decrease in the number of professional tournaments, although some new sponsors were sourced;<sup id="cite_ref-GuardianSponsor_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianSponsor-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and the popularity of the game in Asia with emerging talents such as <a href="/wiki/Liang_Wenbo" title="Liang Wenbo">Liang Wenbo</a> and more established players such as <a href="/wiki/Ding_Junhui" title="Ding Junhui">Ding Junhui</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marco_Fu" title="Marco Fu">Marco Fu</a>, boosted the sport in the Far East.<sup id="cite_ref-BeebDing2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeebDing2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BeebDing1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BeebDing1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> By 2007, the BBC dedicated 400 hours to snooker coverage compared to just 14 minutes forty years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, promoter <a href="/wiki/Barry_Hearn" title="Barry Hearn">Barry Hearn</a> gained a controlling interest in World Snooker Ltd., the professional sport's commercial arm, pledging to revitalise the "moribund" professional game. Under his direction, the number of professional tournaments has increased, certain tournament formats have been changed in an attempt to increase their appeal, and, by 2013, total prize money had more than doubled from £3 million to more than £7 million.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules">Rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rules_of_snooker" title="Rules of snooker">Rules of snooker</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg/220px-Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg.png" width="220" height="418" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg/330px-Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg/440px-Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="758" data-file-height="1440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Snooker_table_drawing_2.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Snooker_tables" title="Billiard table">Snooker table</a> with balls placed in their starting positions. At the start of the game, the cue ball (white) may be placed anywhere in the semicircle, known as the "D".</div></div></div>

<p>The objective of the game is to score more <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#point" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Point" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">points</span></a></dfn> than one's opponent by potting <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#object_ball" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Object ball" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">object balls</span></a></dfn> in the correct order. At the start of a frame, the balls are positioned as shown, and the players then take turns to hit shots by striking the cue ball with the <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#cue_tip" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Cue tip" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">tip</span></a></dfn> of the <a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">cue</a>, their aim being to pot one of the red balls into a pocket and thereby score a point, or, if this is not possible, to at least hit a red ball so as to avoid making a foul shot. If the striker pots a red ball, he or she must then pot one of the six "colours" (in snooker, the term <i>colour</i> is understood to exclude the red balls). If the player successfully pots a colour, the value of that ball is added to the player's score, and the ball is returned to its starting position on the table. After that, the player must pot another red ball, then another colour, and so on. This process continues until the striker fails to pot the desired ball, at which point the opponent comes to the table to play the next shot.
</p><p>The game continues in this manner until all the reds are potted and only the six colours are left on the table. At this point the colours must be potted in the order from least to most valuable ball&#160;– that is, yellow first (two points), then green (three points), brown (four points), blue (five points), pink (six points) and finally black (seven points), the balls not being returned to play. When the final ball is potted, the player with more points wins.<sup id="cite_ref-wsrulessnooker_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsrulessnooker-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> If the scores are equal when all the balls have been potted, the black is placed back on its spot as a <a href="/wiki/Tiebreaker" title="Tiebreaker">tiebreaker</a>. A player may also concede a frame while on strike if he or she thinks there are not enough points available on the table to beat the opponent's score. In professional snooker this is a common occurrence.

<p>Points may also be scored in a game when a player's opponent <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#foul" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Foul" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">fouls</span></a></dfn>. A foul can occur for various reasons, most commonly for failing to hit the correct ball (e.g. hitting a colour first when the player was attempting to hit a red), or for sending the cue ball into a pocket. The former may occur when the player fails to escape from "<dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#snooker" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Snooker" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">a snooker</span></a></dfn>"&#160;– a situation in which the previous player leaves the cue ball positioned such that no legal ball can be struck directly without obstruction by an illegal ball. Points gained from a foul vary from a minimum of four, to a maximum of seven if the black ball is involved.<sup id="cite_ref-wsrulessnooker_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsrulessnooker-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The total number of consecutive points (excluding fouls) that a player amasses during one <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#visit" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Visit" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">visit</span></a></dfn> to the table is known as a "<dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#break" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Break" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">break</span></a></dfn>". A player attaining a break of 15, for example, could have reached it by potting a red then a black, then a red then a pink, before failing to pot the next red. The traditional <a href="/wiki/Maximum_break" title="Maximum break">maximum break</a> in snooker is achieved by potting all reds with blacks then all colours, yielding 147 points; this is often known as a "147" or a "maximum".<sup id="cite_ref-BBC147_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC147-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The highest possible break is a <a href="/wiki/Maximum_break#Breaks_exceeding_147" title="Maximum break">155 break</a>, also known as a "super maximum". This is achieved via the opponent leaving a <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#free_ball" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § free ball" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">free ball</span></a></dfn>, with the black being potted as the additional colour, and then potting 15 reds and blacks with the colours. <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Cope" title="Jamie Cope">Jamie Cope</a> has the distinction of being the first player in snooker history to post a verified 155 break, achieved in a practice frame in 2005.
</p><p>One <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#game" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Game" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">game</span></a></dfn>, from the balls in their starting position until the last ball is potted, is called a "<dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#frame" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Frame" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">frame</span></a></dfn>". A <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#match" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Match" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">match</span></a></dfn> generally consists of a predetermined number of frames and the player who wins the most frames wins the match. Most professional matches require a player to win five frames, and are called "best of nine" as that is the maximum possible number of frames. Tournament finals are usually best of 17 or best of 19, while the world championship uses longer matches&#160;– ranging from best of 19 in the qualifiers and the first round proper, up to 35 frames in length (first to 18), and is played over two days, extended if necessary until a winner is determined.<sup id="cite_ref-BBCWSN1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBCWSN1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Professional and competitive amateur matches are officiated by a referee who is the sole judge of fair play. The referee also replaces the colours on the table when necessary and calls out how many points the player has scored during a break. Professional players usually play the game in a sporting manner, declaring fouls the referee has missed, acknowledging good shots from their opponent, or holding up a hand to apologise for fortunate shots, also known as "flukes".
</p>

<p><a href="#Equipment">Accessories</a> used for snooker include <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#chalk" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Chalk" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">chalk</span></a></dfn> for the tip of the cue, <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#rest" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Rest" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">rests</span></a></dfn> of various sorts (needed often, due to the length of a full-size table), a <a href="/wiki/Rack_(billiards)" title="Rack (billiards)">triangle</a> to <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#rack" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Rack" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">rack</span></a></dfn> the reds, and a scoreboard. One drawback of snooker on a full-size table is the size of the room (22 by 16 feet (6.7&#160;m ×&#160;4.9&#160;m)), which is the minimum required for comfortable cueing room on all sides.<sup id="cite_ref-roomsize_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roomsize-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> This limits the number of locations in which the game can easily be played. While <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Pool_(pocket_billiards)_tables" title="Billiard table">pool tables</a> are common to many <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">pubs</a>, snooker tends to be played either in private surroundings or in public <a href="/wiki/Billiard_hall" title="Billiard hall">snooker halls</a>. The game can also be played on smaller tables using fewer red balls. The variants in table size are: 10&#160;ft × 5&#160;ft, 9&#160;ft × 4.5&#160;ft, 8&#160;ft × 4&#160;ft, 6&#160;ft × 3&#160;ft (the smallest for realistic play) and 4&#160;ft × 2&#160;ft. Smaller tables can come in a variety of styles, such as fold-away or dining-table convertible.
</p><p>A traditional snooker scoreboard resembles an abacus, and records units, tens and hundreds via horizontal sliding pointers.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> They are typically attached to a wall by the snooker table. A simple scoring bead is also sometimes used, called a "scoring string", or "scoring wire". Each bead (segment of the string) represents a single point. Snooker players typically move one or several beads with their cue.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance_and_tournaments">Governance and tournaments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Governance and tournaments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_snooker_tournaments" title="List of snooker tournaments">List of snooker tournaments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Snooker_organisations" title="Category:Snooker organisations">Snooker organisations</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Billiards_and_Snooker_Association" title="World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association">World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association</a> (WPBSA, also known as World Snooker), founded in 1968 as the Professional Billiard Players' Association,<sup id="cite_ref-wpbsafounding_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wpbsafounding-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> is the governing body for the professional game.<sup id="cite_ref-biggestbreak_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-biggestbreak-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-infighting2_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infighting2-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-infighting3_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-infighting3-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The amateur game (including youth competition) is governed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Billiards_and_Snooker_Federation" title="International Billiards and Snooker Federation">International Billiards and Snooker Federation</a> (IBSF).<sup id="cite_ref-ibsf_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ibsf-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tournaments">Tournaments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tournaments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Professional snooker players can play on the <a href="/wiki/World_Professional_Billiards_and_Snooker_Association" title="World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association">World Snooker</a> main tour ranking circuit. <a href="/wiki/Snooker_world_rankings" title="Snooker world rankings">Ranking points</a>, earned by players through their performances over the previous two seasons, determine the current world rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-WSrankingchart_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSrankingchart-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> A player's ranking determines what level of qualification he or she requires for ranking tournaments. The elite of professional snooker are generally regarded as the "top 16" ranking players,<sup id="cite_ref-top16-1_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top16-1-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> who are not required to pre-qualify for three of the tournaments, namely the <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Masters_(snooker)" title="Shanghai Masters (snooker)">Shanghai Masters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australian_Goldfields_Open" title="Australian Goldfields Open">Australian Open</a> and the World Snooker Championship.<sup id="cite_ref-top16-2_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top16-2-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The tour contains 96 players&#160;– the top 64 from the previous two seasons, the 8 highest-ranked professional players on the <a href="/wiki/Players_Tour_Championship" title="Players Tour Championship">Players Tour Championship</a> Order of Merit who are not in the top 64, 12 players from the Q School, and various regional, junior and amateur champions.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg/180px-World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg" width="180" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg/270px-World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg/360px-World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1226" data-file-height="2097" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:World_Snooker_Championship_Trophy_edited.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Championship" title="World Snooker Championship">World Snooker Championship</a> trophy</div></div></div>
<p>The most important event in professional snooker is the world championship,<sup id="cite_ref-wsc1_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wsc1-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> held annually since 1927 (except during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and between 1958 and 1963). The tournament has been held at the <a href="/wiki/Crucible_Theatre" title="Crucible Theatre">Crucible Theatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, England, since 1977, and was sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Tobacco" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Tobacco">Embassy</a> from 1976 to 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-GuardianSponsor_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuardianSponsor-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Since 2005, tobacco companies have not been allowed to sponsor sporting events in the United Kingdom, and the world championship has had to find a new sponsor. It was announced in January 2006 that the 2006–2010 world championships would be sponsored by online casino <a href="/wiki/888_Holdings" title="888 Holdings">888.com</a>. The championship is currently sponsored by <a href="/wiki/Betfred" title="Betfred">Betfred</a>.com after 888.com pulled out of their five-year sponsorship deal after three years.<sup id="cite_ref-sponsor_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sponsor-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
On 15 April 2009 the World Snooker Championship website announced that Betfred.com would be the new sponsor of the world championship for the next four years.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The world championship is the most highly valued prize in professional snooker,<sup id="cite_ref-wcvalue_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wcvalue-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> both in terms of financial reward (£300,000 for the winner, formerly £250,000)<sup id="cite_ref-wcprizemoney_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wcprizemoney-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> as well as ranking points and prestige. The world championship is televised extensively in the UK by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a><sup id="cite_ref-bbc_deal_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_deal-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and gains significant coverage in Europe on <a href="/wiki/Eurosport" title="Eurosport">Eurosport</a><sup id="cite_ref-eurosport_deal_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eurosport_deal-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and in the Far East.
</p><p>The group of tournaments that come next in importance are the other ranking tournaments. Players in these tournaments score <a href="/wiki/Snooker_world_rankings" title="Snooker world rankings">world ranking points</a>. A high ranking ensures qualification for the next year's tournaments, opportunities to play in  invitational tournaments and an advantageous draw in tournaments.<sup id="cite_ref-top16-1_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top16-1-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The most prestigious of these, after the world championship, is the <a href="/wiki/UK_Championship_(snooker)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Championship (snooker)">UK Championship</a>. Third in line are the invitational tournaments, to which most of the highest ranked players are invited. The most important tournament in this category is <a href="/wiki/Masters_(snooker)" title="Masters (snooker)">the Masters</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-masters1_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-masters1-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> which to most players is the second or third most sought-after prize.<sup id="cite_ref-dottmasters_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dottmasters-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an attempt to answer criticisms that televised matches can be slow or get bogged down in lengthy safety exchanges and that long matches cause problems for advertisers,<sup id="cite_ref-yawn_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yawn-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> an alternative series of timed tournaments has been organised by <a href="/wiki/Matchroom_Sport" title="Matchroom Sport">Matchroom Sport</a> chairman <a href="/wiki/Barry_Hearn" title="Barry Hearn">Barry Hearn</a>. The shot-timed <a href="/wiki/Premier_League_Snooker" title="Premier League Snooker">Premier League Snooker</a> was established, with seven players invited to compete at regular United Kingdom venues, televised on <a href="/wiki/Sky_Sports" title="Sky Sports">Sky Sports</a>. Players had twenty-five seconds to take each shot, with five time-outs per player per match. While some success was achieved with this format, it generally did not receive the same amount of press attention or status as the regular ranking tournaments. However, this event has been taken out of the tour since 2013, when the <a href="/wiki/Champion_of_Champions_(snooker)" title="Champion of Champions (snooker)">Champion of Champions</a> was established.
</p><p>There are also other tournaments that have less importance, earn no world ranking points and are not televised. These can change on a year-to-year basis depending on calendars and sponsors.
</p><p>In 2015, the World Professional Billiards and Snooker Association submitted an unsuccessful bid for snooker to be played at the <a href="/wiki/2020_Summer_Olympics" title="2020 Summer Olympics">2020 Summer Olympics</a> in Tokyo.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Another bid has been put forward for <a href="/wiki/2024_Summer_Olympics" title="2024 Summer Olympics">Paris 2024</a> through the newly formed <b><a href="/wiki/World_Snooker_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Snooker Federation">World Snooker Federation</a></b>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several players, such as Ronnie O'Sullivan, Mark Allen and Steve Davis, have warned that there are so many tournaments that players risk burning out. In 2012, O'Sullivan played fewer tournaments in order to spend more time with his children, and ended the 2012–13 season ranked No. 19 in the world. Furthermore, he did not play any tournament in 2013 except the world championship, which he won.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>

</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg/220px-London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg/330px-London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg/440px-London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4368" data-file-height="2912" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:London_-_Royal_Automobile_Club_-_3024.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cue-tip chalk, cue, white chalk-board chalk, and a sliding score-keeper</div></div></div>
<dl><dt>Table</dt>
<dd>The playing surface, 11 feet 8.5 inches by 5 feet 10 inches for a standard full-size table, with six pocket holes, one at each corner and one at the centre of each of the longer side cushions. For further information see <i><a href="/wiki/Billiard_table" title="Billiard table">Billiard table</a></i>, specifically the section <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Snooker_and_English_billiards_tables" title="Billiard table">Snooker and English billiards tables</a>.</dd>
<dt>Cloth</dt>
<dd>The fully wool cloth is usually green, with a <a href="/wiki/Billiard_table#Cloth" title="Billiard table">directional nap</a> running from the baulk end of the table towards the end with the black ball spot. The cloth is often called "baize"; however baize is a much inferior type of cloth sometimes used on pool tables. The nap will affect the direction of the cue ball depending on which direction the cue ball is shot and also on whether left or right side (spin) is placed on the ball. Even if the cue ball is hit in exactly the same way, the nap will cause a different effect depending on whether the ball is hit down table (towards the black ball spot) or up table towards the baulk line. The cloth on a snooker table is not vacuumed, as this can destroy the nap. The cloth is brushed in a straight line from the baulk end to the far end with multiple brush strokes that are straight in direction (i.e. not across the table). Some table men will also then drag a dampened cloth wrapped around a short piece of board (like a <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_lumber#Dimensional_lumber" class="mw-redirect" title="Dimensional lumber">two by four</a>), or straight back of a brush to collect any remaining fine dust and help lay the nap down. The table is then ironed. Strachan cloth as used in official snooker tournaments is made up of 100% wool. Some other cloths include a small percentage of nylon.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cues_Cues_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cues_Cues-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></dd>
<dt>Balls</dt>
<dd>22 balls (15 red, 6 colour balls and a white cue ball), 52.5 mm or 2&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>16</sub></span> inches in diameter. For further information see <i><a href="/wiki/Billiard_ball" title="Billiard ball">Billiard ball</a></i>, particularly the section <a href="/wiki/Billiard_ball#Snooker" title="Billiard ball">Snooker</a>.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Cue_stick" title="Cue stick">Cue</a></dt>
<dd>A stick, made of wood or fibreglass, tapering to a tip, usually ending in leather, which is used to strike the cue-ball.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Cue_sports#Chalk" title="Cue sports">Cue-tip chalk</a></dt>
<dd>The tip of the cue is "chalked" to ensure good contact between the cue and the cue-ball. This "chalk" is generally a silica-based compound rather than actual chalk of the type used on blackboards.</dd>
<dt>Extension</dt>
<dd>A shorter baton that fits over, or screws into, the back end of the cue, effectively lengthening it, used for shots where the cue ball is a long distance from the player.</dd>
<dt>Rest</dt>
<dd>A stick with an X-shaped head that is used to support the cue when the cue ball is out of reach at normal extension.</dd>
<dt>Rest head adaptor</dt>
<dd>An attachment that slips onto a conventional rest head to make a spider or to give a slightly different bridge.</dd>
<dt>Hook rest</dt>
<dd>Identical to the normal rest, yet with a hooked metal end. It is used to set the rest around another ball. The hook rest is the most recent invention in snooker.</dd>
<dt>Spider</dt>
<dd>Similar to the rest but with an arch-shaped head; it is used to elevate and support the tip of the cue above the height of the cue-ball.</dd>
<dt>Swan (or swan-neck spider or giraffe)</dt>
<dd>This equipment, consisting of a rest with a single extended neck and a fork-like prong at the end, is used to give extra cueing distance over a group of balls. If not available, a regular X rest can be placed on a spider so it in turn hangs the required distance beyond to provide similar support.</dd>
<dt>Triangle/rack</dt>
<dd>The piece of equipment is used for gathering the red balls into the formation required for the break to start a frame.</dd>
<dt>Extended rest</dt>
<dd>Similar to the regular rest, but with a mechanism at the butt end which makes it possible to extend the rest by up to three feet.</dd>
<dt>Extended spider</dt>
<dd>A hybrid of the swan and the spider. Its purpose is to bridge over large packs of reds. Is less common these days in professional snooker but can be used in situations where the position of one or more balls prevents the spider being placed where the striker desires.</dd>
<dt>Half butt</dt>
<dd>Usually housed underneath the side of the table, the half butt is a combination of a table length rest and cue which is rarely used unless the cue ball needs to be struck in such a way that the entire length of the table is the actual obstacle.</dd>
<dt>Ball marker</dt>
<dd>A multi-purpose instrument with a "D" shaped notch, which a referee can place next to a ball, in order to mark the position of it. They can then remove the ball to clean it; also used to judge if a ball is preventing a colour from being placed on its spot and to judge if the cue ball can hit the extreme edge of a "ball on" when awarding a free ball (by placing it alongside the potentially intervening ball).</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_players">Notable players</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable players">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stephen_Maguire,_Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan,_and_Michaela_Tabb_at_German_Masters_Snooker_Final_(DerHexer)_2012-02-05_05_cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stephen_Maguire%2C_Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan%2C_and_Michaela_Tabb_at_German_Masters_Snooker_Final_%28DerHexer%29_2012-02-05_05_cropped.jpg/220px-Stephen_Maguire%2C_Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan%2C_and_Michaela_Tabb_at_German_Masters_Snooker_Final_%28DerHexer%29_2012-02-05_05_cropped.jpg" width="220" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Stephen_Maguire%2C_Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan%2C_and_Michaela_Tabb_at_German_Masters_Snooker_Final_%28DerHexer%29_2012-02-05_05_cropped.jpg/330px-Stephen_Maguire%2C_Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan%2C_and_Michaela_Tabb_at_German_Masters_Snooker_Final_%28DerHexer%29_2012-02-05_05_cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cb/Stephen_Maguire%2C_Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan%2C_and_Michaela_Tabb_at_German_Masters_Snooker_Final_%28DerHexer%29_2012-02-05_05_cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="422" data-file-height="536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stephen_Maguire,_Ronnie_O%E2%80%99Sullivan,_and_Michaela_Tabb_at_German_Masters_Snooker_Final_(DerHexer)_2012-02-05_05_cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ronnie_O%27Sullivan" title="Ronnie O&#39;Sullivan">Ronnie O'Sullivan</a> has won the most world titles in the 21st century (in 2001, 2004, 2008, 2012 and 2013).</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_snooker_players_by_number_of_ranking_titles" title="List of snooker players by number of ranking titles">List of snooker players by number of ranking titles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Century_break#List_of_players_with_over_100_century_breaks" title="Century break">List of snooker players with over 100 century breaks</a></div>
<p>In the professional era that began with <a href="/wiki/Joe_Davis" title="Joe Davis">Joe Davis</a> in the 1930s and continues until the present day, a relatively small number of players have succeeded at the top level.<sup id="cite_ref-number1_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-number1-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hardwork_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hardwork-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Through the decades, certain players have tended to dominate the game, but none more than its original star player, Joe Davis. Davis was world champion for twenty years, retiring unbeaten after claiming his fifteenth world title in 1946 when the tournament was reinstated after the Second World War. Davis was unbeaten in world championship play, and was only ever beaten four times in his entire life, with all four defeats coming after his world championship retirement and inflicted by his own brother Fred. He did lose matches in handicapped tournaments, but on level terms these four defeats were the only losses of his entire career.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> He was also world billiards champion. It is regarded as highly unlikely that anyone will ever dominate the game to his level again.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
After Davis retired from world championship play, the next dominant force was his younger brother <a href="/wiki/Fred_Davis_(snooker_player)" title="Fred Davis (snooker player)">Fred Davis</a> who had lost the 1940 final by a single frame. By 1947 he was deemed ready by his brother to take over the mantle but lost the world final to the Scotsman <a href="/wiki/Walter_Donaldson" title="Walter Donaldson">Walter Donaldson</a>. After this setback, Davis and Donaldson contested the next four finals, Davis proving the stronger player. After the abandonment of the world championship in 1953, with the 1952 final boycotted by British professionals, the Professional Match Play Championship became the unofficial world championship in all but name.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Fred Davis won the event every year until its penultimate one, when in 1957 he did not enter.
After winning three official and five unofficial world titles, his absence from the 1957 tournament was to prove vital, as its winner, John Pulman, was automatically awarded the official world title on resumption of the tournament in 1964. Davis would try, but never regain the world title again.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Pulman" title="John Pulman">John Pulman</a> was the king of the 1960s, when the world championship was played on a challenge basis. However, when the tournament reverted to a knockout formula in 1969, he did not prosper.  <a href="/wiki/Ray_Reardon" title="Ray Reardon">Ray Reardon</a> became the dominant force in the 1970s, winning six titles, with <a href="/wiki/John_Spencer_(snooker_player)" title="John Spencer (snooker player)">John Spencer</a> winning three.
<a href="/wiki/Steve_Davis" title="Steve Davis">Steve Davis</a>' first world title in 1981 made him only the 11th world champion since 1927, including the winner of the boycotted 1952 title, Horace Lindrum. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hendry" title="Stephen Hendry">Stephen Hendry</a> became the 14th in 1990 and dominated through the 1990s. Reardon won six (1970, 1973–1976 and 1978), Davis also six (1981, 1983, 1984 and 1987–1989) and Hendry seven (1990, 1992–1996 and 1999). <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_O%27Sullivan" title="Ronnie O&#39;Sullivan">Ronnie O'Sullivan</a> is the closest to dominance in the modern era, having won the title on five occasions in the 21st century (2001, 2004, 2008, 2012 and 2013). <a href="/wiki/Mark_Williams_(snooker_player)" title="Mark Williams (snooker player)">Mark Williams</a> has won three times (2000, 2003, and 2018) and <a href="/wiki/John_Higgins_(snooker_player)" title="John Higgins (snooker player)">John Higgins</a> four times (1998, 2007, 2009, 2011) but since the beginning of the century, there has not been a dominant force like in previous decades, and the modern era has seen many players playing to a similar standard, instead of one player raising the bar. Davis, for example, won more ranking tournaments than the rest of the top 64 players put together by 1985. By retaining his title in 2013, O'Sullivan became the first player to successfully defend the world championship since 1996 when Hendry won the sixth of his seven titles, his fifth in a row, and then later by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Selby" title="Mark Selby">Mark Selby</a> in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-World_Champions_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-World_Champions-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Snooker_variants" title="Category:Snooker variants">Snooker variants</a></div>
<ul><li><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="American snooker (page does not exist)">American snooker</a>, a variant dating to 1925, usually played on a 10&#160;ft ×&#160;5&#160;ft (3.0&#160;m ×&#160;1.5&#160;m) table with <span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span>&#160;in (54&#160;mm) balls, and a simpler rule set influenced by <a href="/wiki/Pool_(cue_sports)" title="Pool (cue sports)">pool</a>. (Despite its name, the strictly amateur American snooker is not governed or recognised by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Snooker_Association" title="United States Snooker Association">United States Snooker Association</a>, but by the <a href="/wiki/Billiard_Congress_of_America" title="Billiard Congress of America">Billiard Congress of America</a>.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Power_Snooker" title="Power Snooker">Power Snooker</a>, a variant with only nine reds, in a diamond-shaped pack, instead of 15 in a triangle, and matches limited to 30 minutes.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sinuca_brasileira" title="Sinuca brasileira">Sinuca brasileira</a></i>, a Brazilian version with only one red ball, and divergent rules.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Six-red_snooker" title="Six-red snooker">Six-red snooker</a>, a variant played with only six reds in a triangular pack.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Snookerpool&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Snookerpool (page does not exist)">Snookerpool</a>, a variant played on an American pool table with ten reds in a triangular pack.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snooker_plus" class="mw-redirect" title="Snooker plus">Snooker plus</a>, a variant with two additional <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_cue_sports_terms#colour_ball" title="Glossary of cue sports terms"><span title="See entry at: Glossary of cue sports terms § Colour ball" style="color:inherit;" class="glossary-link">colour balls</span></a></dfn> (8pt orange and 10pt purple), allowing a <a href="/wiki/Maximum_break" title="Maximum break">maximum break</a> of 210.<sup id="cite_ref-SGame_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SGame-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The variation was created by Joe Davis in 1959 and used at the <a href="/wiki/1959_News_of_the_World_Snooker_Plus_Tournament" title="1959 News of the World Snooker Plus Tournament">1959 News of the World Snooker Plus Tournament</a>. It failed to gain popularity.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ten-red_snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ten-red snooker (page does not exist)">Ten-red snooker</a>, a variant played with only ten reds in a triangle.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Volunteer_snooker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Volunteer snooker (page does not exist)">Volunteer snooker</a>, a variant from the early 1900s.</li></ul>
