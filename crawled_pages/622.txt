<p>The <b>Government of the United Kingdom</b>, formally referred to as <b>Her Majesty's Government</b>, is the central government of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland">United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland</a>. It is also commonly referred to as simply the <b>UK Government</b> or the <b>British Government</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-direct1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-direct1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The government is led by the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>, who selects all the remaining <a href="/wiki/British_Government_frontbench" title="British Government frontbench">ministers</a>. The prime minister and the other most senior ministers belong to the supreme decision-making committee, known as the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cabinet of the United Kingdom">Cabinet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-direct1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-direct1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The government ministers all sit in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>, and are <a href="/wiki/Accountability#Political_accountability" title="Accountability">accountable</a> to it. The government is <a href="/wiki/Fusion_of_powers" title="Fusion of powers">dependent on Parliament</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and since the <a href="/wiki/Fixed-term_Parliaments_Act_2011" title="Fixed-term Parliaments Act 2011">Fixed-terms Parliaments Act 2011</a>, <a href="/wiki/General_elections_in_the_uk" class="mw-redirect" title="General elections in the uk">general elections</a> are held every five years to elect a new <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>, unless there is a successful <a href="/wiki/Vote_of_no_confidence" class="mw-redirect" title="Vote of no confidence">vote of no confidence</a> in the government or a <a href="/wiki/Supermajority#United_Kingdom" title="Supermajority">two-thirds vote</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Snap_election" title="Snap election">snap election</a> (as was the case in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017">2017</a>) in the House of Commons, in which case an election may be held sooner. After an election, the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">monarch</a> (currently <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a>) selects as prime minister the leader of the party most likely to command the confidence of the House of Commons, usually by possessing a majority of MPs.<sup id="cite_ref-parliament1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parliament1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/Uncodified_constitution" title="Uncodified constitution">uncodified</a> <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">British constitution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive authority</a> lies with the monarch, although this authority is exercised only by, or on the advice of, the prime minister and the cabinet.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Cabinet members advise the monarch as members of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Most_Honourable_Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Most Honourable Privy Council">Privy Council</a>. They also exercise power directly as leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_the_United_Kingdom_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Departments of the United Kingdom Government">Government Departments</a>.
</p><p>The current prime minister is <a href="/wiki/Theresa_May" title="Theresa May">Theresa May</a>, who took office on 13 July 2016. She is the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>, which won a majority of seats in the House of Commons in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">general election</a> on 7 May 2015, when <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> was the party leader; although at the last general election she failed to secure a majority government. Prior to this, Cameron and the Conservatives led a coalition from 2010 to 2015 with the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>, in which Cameron was prime minister.
</p><p>The Government is occasionally referred to with the <a href="/wiki/Metonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Metonym">metonym</a> Westminster, due to that being where many of the offices of the government are situated, especially by members in the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Scotland">Government of Scotland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> in order to differentiate it from their own.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_in_Parliament">Government in Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Government in Parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A key principle of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Constitution of the United Kingdom">British Constitution</a> is that the government is responsible to Parliament. This is called <a href="/wiki/Responsible_government" title="Responsible government">responsible government</a>.
</p><p>The United Kingdom is a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> in which the reigning monarch (that is, the King or Queen who is the Head of State at any given time) does not make any open political decisions. All political decisions are taken by the government and Parliament. This constitutional state of affairs is the result of a long history of constraining and reducing the political power of the monarch, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a> in 1215.
</p><p>Parliament is split into two houses: the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>. The House of Commons is the lower house and is the more powerful. The House of Lords is the upper house and although it can vote to amend proposed laws, the House of Commons can usually vote to overrule its amendments. Although the House of Lords can introduce bills, most important laws are introduced in the House of Commons – and most of those are introduced by the government, which schedules the vast majority of parliamentary time in the Commons. Parliamentary time is essential for bills to be passed into law, because they must pass through a number of readings before becoming law. Prior to introducing a bill, the government may run a public consultation to solicit feedback from the public and businesses, and often may have already introduced and discussed the policy in the <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen&#39;s Speech">Queen's Speech</a>, or in an <a href="/wiki/Election_manifesto" class="mw-redirect" title="Election manifesto">election manifesto</a> or <a href="/wiki/Party_platform" title="Party platform">party platform</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Crown" title="Minister of the Crown">Ministers of the Crown</a> are responsible to the House in which they sit; they make statements in that House and take questions from members of that House. For most senior ministers this is usually the elected House of Commons rather than the House of Lords. There have been some recent exceptions to this: for example, cabinet ministers <a href="/wiki/Peter_Mandelson" title="Peter Mandelson">Lord Mandelson</a> (First Secretary of State) and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Adonis" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Adonis">Lord Adonis</a> (Secretary of State for Transport) sat in the Lords and were responsible to that House during the government of <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Crowned_Portcullis.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Crowned_Portcullis.svg/150px-Crowned_Portcullis.svg.png" width="150" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Crowned_Portcullis.svg/225px-Crowned_Portcullis.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Crowned_Portcullis.svg/300px-Crowned_Portcullis.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="460" data-file-height="550" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Crowned_Portcullis.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">British Parliament</a></div></div></div>
<p>Since the start of <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward VII of the United Kingdom">Edward VII</a>'s reign in 1901, the prime minister has always been an elected member of Parliament (MP) and therefore directly accountable to the House of Commons. A similar convention applies to the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_the_Exchequer" title="Chancellor of the Exchequer">Chancellor of the Exchequer</a>. It would likely be politically unacceptable for the budget speech to be given in the Lords, with MPs unable to directly question the Chancellor, especially now that the Lords have very limited powers in relation to money bills. The last Chancellor of the Exchequer to be a member of the House of Lords was <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Denman,_1st_Baron_Denman" title="Thomas Denman, 1st Baron Denman">Lord Denman</a>, who served as interim Chancellor of the Exchequer for one month in 1834.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under the British system, the government is required by convention and for practical reasons to maintain the confidence of the House of Commons. It requires the support of the House of Commons for the maintenance of <a href="/wiki/Loss_of_supply" title="Loss of supply">supply</a> (by voting through the government's budgets) and to pass <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a>. By convention, if a government <a href="/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence" title="Motion of no confidence">loses the confidence</a> of the House of Commons it must either resign or a General Election is held. The support of the Lords, while useful to the government in getting its legislation passed without delay, is not vital. A government is not required to resign even if it loses the confidence of the Lords and is defeated in key votes in that House. The House of Commons is thus the <a href="/wiki/Responsible_house" title="Responsible house">Responsible house</a>.
</p><p>The prime minister is held to account during <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister%27s_Questions" title="Prime Minister&#39;s Questions">Prime Minister's Questions</a> (PMQs) which provides an opportunity for MPs from all parties to question the PM on any subject. There are also departmental questions when ministers answer questions relating to their specific departmental brief. Unlike PMQs both the cabinet ministers for the department and junior ministers within the department may answer on behalf of the government, depending on the topic of the question.
</p><p>During debates on legislation proposed by the government, ministers—usually with departmental responsibility for the <a href="/wiki/Bill_(proposed_law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill (proposed law)">bill</a>—will lead the debate for the government and respond to points made by MPs or Lords.
</p><p>Committees<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> of both the House of Commons and House of Lords hold the government to account, scrutinise its work and examine in detail proposals for legislation. Ministers appear before committees to give evidence and answer questions.
</p><p>Government ministers are also required by convention and the <a href="/wiki/Ministerial_Code_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministerial Code (United Kingdom)">Ministerial Code</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> when Parliament is sitting, to make major statements regarding government policy or issues of national importance to Parliament. This allows MPs or Lords to question the government on the statement. When the government instead chooses to make announcements first outside Parliament, it is often the subject of significant criticism from MPs and the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" title="Speaker of the House of Commons (United Kingdom)">Speaker of the House of Commons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Her_Majesty.27s_Government_and_the_Crown"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Her_Majesty's_Government_and_the_Crown">Her Majesty's Government and the Crown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Her Majesty&#039;s Government and the Crown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prerogative_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Prerogative in the United Kingdom">Royal Prerogative in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg/170px-Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg.png" width="170" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg/255px-Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg/340px-Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1550" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Coat_of_Arms_of_the_United_Kingdom.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">British monarchy</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">British monarch</a>, currently <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a>, is the head of state and the sovereign, but not the head of government.
</p><p>The monarch takes little direct part in governing the country, and remains neutral in political affairs. However, the legal authority of the state that is vested in the sovereign, known as <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">The Crown</a>, remains the source of the executive power exercised by the government.
</p><p>In addition to <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">explicit statutory authority</a>, in many areas the Crown also possesses a body of powers known as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prerogative" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Prerogative">Royal Prerogative</a>, which can be used for many purposes, from the issue or withdrawal of passports to declaration of war. By long-standing custom, most of these powers are delegated from the sovereign to various ministers or other officers of the Crown, who may use them without having to obtain the consent of Parliament.
</p><p>The head of the government, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">prime minister</a>, also has weekly meetings with the monarch, when she "has a right and a duty to express her views on Government matters...These meetings, as with all communications between The Queen and her Government, remain strictly confidential. Having expressed her views, The Queen abides by the advice of her ministers."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Royal Prerogative powers include, but are not limited to, the following:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic_powers">Domestic powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Domestic powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The power to appoint (and also, in theory, dismiss) a <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">prime minister</a>. This power is exercised by the monarch herself. By convention she appoints (and is expected to appoint) the individual most likely to be capable of commanding the confidence of a majority in the House of Commons.</li>
<li>The power to dismiss and appoint other <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Crown" title="Minister of the Crown">ministers</a>. This power is exercised by the monarch on the advice of the prime minister.</li>
<li>The power to assent to and enact laws by giving <a href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Assent">[Royal] Assent</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bill_(proposed_law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill (proposed law)">Bills</a> passed by both Houses of Parliament, which is required in order for a law to (from a passed Bill) <i>make it into the Statute Books</i> (i.e., to become a valid law) as an <i>Act</i> [of Parliament]. This is exercised by the monarch, who also theoretically has the power to refuse assent, although no monarch has refused assent to a bill passed by Parliament since <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Queen Anne</a> in 1708.</li>
<li>The power to give and to issue commissions to <a href="/wiki/Commissioned_officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Commissioned officer">commissioned officers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">Armed Forces</a>.</li>
<li>The power to command the <a href="/wiki/Armed_Forces_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed Forces of the United Kingdom">Armed Forces of the United Kingdom</a>. This power is exercised by the <a href="/wiki/Defence_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Defence Council of the United Kingdom">Defence Council</a> in the Queen's name.</li>
<li>The power to appoint members to <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Her Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council</a>.</li>
<li>The power to issue (and also to suspend, cancel, recall, impound, withdraw or revoke) British passports and the general power to provide (or deny) British passport facilities to British citizens and British nationals. This is exercised (in the United Kingdom, but not necessarily in the case of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> or the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a>) by the <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>.</li>
<li>The Royal <a href="/wiki/Prerogative_of_mercy" class="mw-redirect" title="Prerogative of mercy">Prerogative of mercy</a> (although capital punishment has been abolished (thereby removing the need to use this power to issue pardons to commute a death penalty imposed, usually substituted into life imprisonment in lieu), this power is still used under rare circumstances (e.g. to remedy errors in sentencing calculation).</li>
<li>The power to grant (and also to cancel and annul) <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">honours</a>.</li>
<li>The power to create corporations (including the status of being a city, with its own corporation) by <a href="/wiki/Royal_Charter" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Charter">Royal Charter</a>, and also to amend, replace and revoke existing charters.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_powers">Foreign powers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Foreign powers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The power to ratify and make <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a>.</li>
<li>The power to <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_war_by_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Declaration of war by the United Kingdom">declare war</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peace_treaty" title="Peace treaty">conclude peace</a> with other nations.</li>
<li>The power to deploy the Armed Forces overseas.</li>
<li>The power to <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_recognition" title="Diplomatic recognition">recognise states</a>.</li>
<li>The power to credit and receive diplomats.</li></ul>
<p>Even though the United Kingdom has no single constitutional document, the government published the above list in October 2003 to increase transparency, as some of the powers exercised in the name of the monarch are part of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prerogative" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Prerogative">Royal Prerogative</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, the complete extent of the Royal Prerogative powers has never been fully set out, as many of them originated in ancient custom and the period of <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a>, or were modified by later constitutional practice, 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_departments">Government departments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Government departments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_government_departments" title="British government departments">British government departments</a></div>
<p>Government ministers are supported by 560,000<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Civil Service">Civil Servants</a> and other staff working in the 24 <a href="/wiki/British_government_departments#Ministerial_departments" title="British government departments">Ministerial Departments</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and their <a href="/wiki/Executive_agency" title="Executive agency">executive agencies</a>. There are also an additional 26 <a href="/wiki/British_government_departments#Non-ministerial_departments" title="British government departments">non-Ministerial Departments</a> with a range of further responsibilities.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg/190px-2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg" width="190" height="109" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg/285px-2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg/380px-2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="457" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2010_Official_Downing_Street_pic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Main entrance of <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a>, the residence and offices of the First Lord of HM Treasury</div></div></div>
<p>The prime minister is based at <a href="/wiki/10_Downing_Street" title="10 Downing Street">10 Downing Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">Westminster</a>, London. Cabinet meetings also take place here. Most <a href="/wiki/Ministry_(government_department)" title="Ministry (government department)">government departments</a> have their headquarters nearby in <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Devolved_governments">Devolved governments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Devolved governments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">Devolution in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Since 1999, certain areas of central government have been devolved to accountable governments in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. These are not part of Her Majesty's Government, and are accountable to their own institutions, with their own authority under the Crown; in contrast, there is no devolved government in England.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg/220px-Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg/330px-Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg/440px-Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_Fire_Station_Oxford_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Refurbishment notice at Old Fire Station, <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, showing HM Government support.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">Local government in England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">Local government in Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Wales" title="Local government in Wales">Local government in Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Local government in Northern Ireland">Local government in Northern Ireland</a></div>
<p>Up to three layers of elected local authorities (such as County, District and <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">Parish Councils</a>) exist throughout all parts of the United Kingdom, in some places merged into <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">Unitary Authorities</a>. They have limited local tax-raising powers. Many other authorities and agencies also have statutory powers, generally subject to some central government supervision.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limits_of_government_power">Limits of government power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Limits of government power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament_(UK)" title="Act of Parliament (UK)">Act of Parliament (UK)</a></div>
<p>The government's powers include general executive and statutory powers, <a href="/wiki/Delegated_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Delegated legislation">delegated legislation</a>, and numerous powers of appointment and patronage. However, some powerful officials and bodies, (e.g. HM judges, <a href="/wiki/Local_authorities" class="mw-redirect" title="Local authorities">local authorities</a>, and the Charity Commissions) are legally more or less independent of the government, and government powers are legally limited to those retained by the Crown under <a href="/wiki/Common_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Law">Common Law</a> or granted and limited by Act of Parliament, and are subject to <a href="/wiki/European_Union_law" title="European Union law">European Union law</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_the_European_Union#Competencies" title="Politics of the European Union">competencies</a> that it defines. Both substantive and procedural limitations are enforceable in the Courts by <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a>.
</p><p>Nevertheless, magistrates and mayors can still be arrested for and put on trial for corruption, and the government has powers to insert commissioners into a local authority to oversee its work, and to issue directives that must be obeyed by the local authority, if the local authority is not abiding by its statutory obligations.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast, as in every other <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> (EU) member state, EU officials cannot be prosecuted for any actions carried out in pursuit of their official duties, and foreign country diplomats (though not their employees) and foreign <a href="/wiki/Members_of_the_European_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Members of the European Parliament">Members of the European Parliament</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> are immune from prosecution in the UK under any circumstance. As a consequence, neither EU bodies nor diplomats have to pay taxes, since it would not be possible to prosecute them for tax evasion. This caused a dispute in recent years when the US Ambassador to the UK claimed that London's <a href="/wiki/Congestion_charge" class="mw-redirect" title="Congestion charge">congestion charge</a> was a tax, and not a charge (despite the name), and therefore he did not have to pay it – a claim the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a> disputed.
</p><p>Similarly, the monarch is totally immune from criminal prosecution and may only be sued with her permission (this is known as <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_immunity" title="Sovereign immunity">sovereign immunity</a>). The monarch, by law, is not required to pay income tax, but <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> has voluntarily paid it since 1993, and also pays local rates voluntarily. However, the monarchy also receives a substantial grant from the government, the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Support_Grant" class="mw-redirect" title="Sovereign Support Grant">Sovereign Support Grant</a>, and Queen Elizabeth II's inheritance from her mother, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_The_Queen_Mother" title="Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother">Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother</a>, was exempt from <a href="/wiki/Inheritance_tax" title="Inheritance tax">inheritance tax</a>.
</p><p>In addition to legislative powers, HM Government has substantial influence over local authorities and other bodies set up by it, by financial powers and grants. Many functions carried out by local authorities, such as paying out housing benefit and council tax benefit, are funded or substantially part-funded by central government.
</p><p>Even though the <a href="/wiki/British_Broadcasting_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="British Broadcasting Corporation">British Broadcasting Corporation</a> (BBC) is supposed to be independent of the government on a day-to-day level and is supposed to be politically unbiased, some commentators<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (July 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> have argued that the prospects of the BBC having its funding cut or its charter changed in future charter renewals in practice cause the BBC to be subtly biased towards the government of the day (or the likely future government as an election approaches) at times.
</p><p>Neither the central government nor local authorities are permitted to sue anyone for <a href="/wiki/Defamation" title="Defamation">defamation</a>. Individual politicians are allowed to sue people for defamation in a personal capacity and without using government funds, but this is relatively rare (although <a href="/wiki/George_Galloway" title="George Galloway">George Galloway</a>, who was a backbench MP for a quarter of a century, has sued or threatened to sue for defamation a number of times). However, it is a criminal offence to make a false statement about any election candidate during an election, with the purpose of reducing the number of votes they receive (as with libel, opinions do not count).
</p>
