<p>The <b>Edwardian era</b> or <b>Edwardian period</b> of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of the United Kingdom">British history</a> covers the brief reign of <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Edward VII">King Edward VII</a>, 1901 to 1910, and is sometimes extended in both directions to capture long-term trends from the 1890s to the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>.  The death of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> in January 1901 marked the end of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>. The new king Edward VII was already the leader of a fashionable elite that set a style influenced by the art and fashions of continental Europe.  <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hynes" title="Samuel Hynes">Samuel Hynes</a> described the Edwardian era as a "leisurely time when women wore <a href="/wiki/Picture_hat" title="Picture hat">picture hats</a> and did not vote, when the rich were not ashamed to live conspicuously, and the sun really never set on the British flag'".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
The Liberals returned to power in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1906" title="United Kingdom general election, 1906">1906</a> and made <a href="/wiki/Liberal_welfare_reforms" title="Liberal welfare reforms">significant reforms</a>. Below the upper class, the era was marked by significant shifts in politics among sections of society that were largely excluded from wielding power in the past, such as common labourers, servants, the Irish and the industrial working class. Women became increasingly politicised.<sup id="cite_ref-Hattersley_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hattersley-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perceptions">Perceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Perceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Edwardian period is sometimes portrayed as a romantic golden age of long summer afternoons and garden parties, basking in a sun that never sets on the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. This perception was created in the 1920s and later by those who remembered the Edwardian age with nostalgia, looking back to their childhoods across the abyss of the <a href="/wiki/Great_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Great War">Great War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Edwardian age was also seen as a mediocre period of pleasure between the great achievements of the preceding Victorian age and the catastrophe of the following war.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Since 2000 historiographical  assessments emphasise the great differences between the wealthy and the poor during this period and describe the age as heralding great changes in political and social life.<sup id="cite_ref-Hattersley_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hattersley-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Historian <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_James" title="Lawrence James">Lawrence James</a> has argued that the leaders felt increasingly threatened by rival powers such as Germany, Russia, and the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-James_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-James-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  The sudden arrival of world war in summer 1914 was largely unexpected; only the Royal Navy was prepared for war.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There was a growing political awareness of the <a href="/wiki/Working-class" class="mw-redirect" title="Working-class">working-class</a>, leading to a rise in <a href="/wiki/Trade_unions" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade unions">trade unions</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Labour_movement" title="Labour movement">Labour movement</a> and demands for better working conditions. The aristocracy remained in control of top government offices.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservative_Party">Conservative Party</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conservative Party">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="History of the Conservative Party (UK)">History of the Conservative Party (UK)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservatives</a> – at the time called "Unionists" – were the dominant political party from the 1890s until <a href="/wiki/1906_UK_general_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1906 UK general election">1906</a>. The party had many strengths, appealing to voters supportive of <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a>, tariffs, the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, a powerful <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, and traditional hierarchical society.  There was a powerful leadership base in the landed aristocracy and landed gentry in rural England, plus strong support from the Church of England and military interests. Historians have used election returns to demonstrate  that Conservatives did surprisingly well in working-class districts.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  They had an appeal as well to the better-off element of traditional working-class Britons in the larger cities.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In rural areas, the national headquarters made highly effective use of paid traveling lecturers, with pamphlets, posters, and especially lantern slides, who were able to communicate effectively with rural voters – particularly the newly enfranchised agricultural workers.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  In the first years of the twentieth century, the Conservative government, with <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Balfour" title="Arthur Balfour">Arthur Balfour</a> as <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a>, had numerous successes in foreign policy, defence, and education, as well as solutions for the issues of alcohol licensing and land ownership for the peasants of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nevertheless, the weaknesses were accumulating, and proved so overwhelming in 1906 that they did not return to complete power until <a href="/wiki/1922_UK_general_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1922 UK general election">1922</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Conservative Party was losing its drive and enthusiasm, especially after the retirement of the charismatic <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">Joseph Chamberlain</a>. There was a bitter split on <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">"tariff reform"</a> (that is, imposing tariffs or taxes on all imports), that drove many of the free traders over to the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a> camp. Tariff reform was a losing issue that the Conservative leadership inexplicably clung to.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Support among the top tier of the working-class, and in lower middle-class weakened, and there was dissatisfaction among the intellectuals. The 1906 general election was a landslide victory for the Liberal Party, which saw its total vote share increase by 25%, while the Conservative total vote held steady.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liberal_Party">Liberal Party</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Liberal Party">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party (UK)</a></div>
<p>The Liberal Party lacked a unified ideological base in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It contained numerous contradictory and hostile factions, such as imperialists and supporters of the Boers;<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> near-socialists and laissez-faire classical liberals; suffragettes and opponents of women's suffrage;<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> antiwar elements and supporters of the military alliance with France.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Non-Conformist Dissenters – Protestants outside the Anglican fold – were a powerful element, dedicated to opposing the established church in terms of education and taxation.  However, the Dissenters were losing support and society at large and played a lesser and lesser role in party affairs after 1900.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Party, furthermore, also included <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholics" title="Irish Catholics">Irish Catholics</a>, and secularists from the labour movement. The middle-class business, professional and intellectual communities were generally strongholds, although some old aristocratic families played important roles as well. The working-class element was moving rapidly toward the newly emerging <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>.  One uniting element was widespread agreement on the use of politics and Parliament as a device to upgrade and improve society and to reform politics.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Labour_Party">Labour Party</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Labour Party">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Labour_Party_(UK)" title="History of the Labour Party (UK)">History of the Labour Party (UK)</a></div>
<p>The Labour Party was emerging from the rapidly growing trade union movement after 1890. In 1903 it entered the <a href="/wiki/Gladstone%E2%80%93MacDonald_pact" title="Gladstone–MacDonald pact">Gladstone–MacDonald pact</a> with the Liberals, allowing for cross-party support in elections, and the emergence of a small Labour contingent in Parliament. It was a temporary arrangement until the 1920s, when the Labour Party was strong enough to act on its own, and the Liberals were in an irreversible decline. Subtle social changes in the working-class were producing a younger generation that wanted to act independently. Michael Childs argues that the younger generation had reason to prefer Labour over Liberal political styles. Social factors included secularised elementary education (with a disappearing role for Dissenting schools that inculcated Liberal viewpoints); the "New Unionism" after 1890 brought unskilled workers into a movement previously dominated by the skilled workers;<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and new leisure activities, especially the <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> and sports, involved youth while repelling the older generation of Liberal voters.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Boer_War">Boer War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Boer War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_the_Second_Boer_War" title="Opposition to the Second Boer War">Opposition to the Second Boer War</a></div>
<p>The government entered the <a href="/wiki/Second_Boer_War" title="Second Boer War">Second Boer War</a> with great confidence, little expecting that the two small rural Boer republics in southern Africa with a combined White population less than London would hold off the concentrated power of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> for two and half years, and take 400,000 Imperial troops to secure victory.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  The war split the Liberal Party into anti- and pro-war factions. Great orators, such as  Liberal <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a>, who spoke against the war, became increasingly influential. Nevertheless, Liberal Unionist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">Joseph Chamberlain</a>, who was largely in charge of the war, maintained his hold on power. When <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Kitchener" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbert Kitchener">General Kitchener</a> took command in 1900, he initiated a scorched earth policy in order to foil Boer guerilla tactics. Captured Boer combatants were transported overseas to other British possessions as prisoners of war. However he relocated noncombatant Boers—mostly women and children—into heavily guarded internment camps. The internment camps were overcrowded with bad sanitation and meagre food rations. Contagious diseases such as measles, typhoid and dysentery were endemic. Many of the internees died. <a href="/wiki/Emily_Hobhouse" title="Emily Hobhouse">Emily Hobhouse</a> visited the camps and brought the conditions to the attention of the British public. Public outcry resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Millicent_Fawcett" title="Millicent Fawcett">Fawcett Commission</a> which corroborated Hobhouse's report and eventually led to improved conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  The Boers surrendered and the Boer Republics were annexed by the British  Empire. <a href="/wiki/Jan_Smuts" title="Jan Smuts">Jan Smuts</a>—a leading Boer general—became a senior official of the new government and even became a top British official in the World War.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1901, the six British self-governing colonies of Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, Tasmania, South Australia, and Western Australia united to form the Commonwealth of Australia, with almost complete control of its internal affairs, but with foreign policy and defence handled by London. <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Barton" title="Edmund Barton">Edmund Barton</a> was the first prime minister.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Liberal_reforms">The Liberal reforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Liberal reforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Liberal_welfare_reforms" title="Liberal welfare reforms">Liberal welfare reforms</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg/220px-Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg" width="220" height="321" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg/330px-Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg/440px-Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="1167" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ten_Years_of_Toryism.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Liberal poster c.1905–10</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> under <a href="/wiki/Henry_Campbell-Bannerman" title="Henry Campbell-Bannerman">Henry Campbell-Bannerman</a> rallied Liberals around the traditional platform of free trade and land reform and led them to <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1906" title="United Kingdom general election, 1906">the greatest electoral victory</a> in Liberal Party history.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> was overshadowed by his frontbench, most notably <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a> at the Exchequer, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Grey,_1st_Viscount_Grey_of_Fallodon" title="Edward Grey, 1st Viscount Grey of Fallodon">Edward Grey</a> at the Foreign Office, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burdon_Haldane,_1st_Viscount_Haldane" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Burdon Haldane, 1st Viscount Haldane">Richard Burdon Haldane</a> at the War Office and <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a>. Campbell-Bannerman retired in 1908 and was succeeded by Asquith. He stepped up the government's radicalism, especially in the "<a href="/wiki/People%27s_Budget" title="People&#39;s Budget">People's Budget</a>" of 1909 that proposed to fund expanded social welfare programmes with new taxes on land and high incomes. It was blocked by the Conservative-dominated <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, but eventually became law in April 1910.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Asquith_Q_42036.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Asquith_Q_42036.jpg/220px-Asquith_Q_42036.jpg" width="220" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Asquith_Q_42036.jpg/330px-Asquith_Q_42036.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Asquith_Q_42036.jpg/440px-Asquith_Q_42036.jpg 2x" data-file-width="632" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Asquith_Q_42036.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a></div></div></div> 
<p>Almost half of the Liberal MPs elected in 1906 were supportive of the '<a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">New Liberalism</a>', which advocated government action to improve people's lives.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Punch_cartoon_28_April_1909.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/Punch_cartoon_28_April_1909.jpg/280px-Punch_cartoon_28_April_1909.jpg" width="280" height="345" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cf/Punch_cartoon_28_April_1909.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="406" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Punch_cartoon_28_April_1909.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Wild Fare". Cartoonist <a href="/wiki/John_Bernard_Partridge" class="mw-redirect" title="John Bernard Partridge">John Bernard Partridge</a> depicts Lloyd George as a giant with a cudgel labelled "Budget" in reference to his <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Budget" title="People&#39;s Budget">People's Budget</a>; Asquith cowers beneath the table. <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i> 28 April 1909</div></div></div>
<p>Liberals in 1906–1911 passed major legislation designed to reform politics and society, such as the regulation of working hours, <a href="/wiki/National_Insurance" title="National Insurance">National Insurance</a> and the beginnings of the welfare state, as well as curtailing the power of the House of Lords.  Women's suffrage was not on the Liberal agenda.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> There were numerous major reforms helping labour, typified by the  <a href="/wiki/Trade_Boards_Act_1909" title="Trade Boards Act 1909">Trade Boards Act 1909</a> that set minimum wages in certain trades with the history of "sweated" or "sweatshop" rates of especially low wages, because of surplus of available workers, the presence of women workers, or the lack of skills. At first it applied to four industries: chain-making, ready-made tailoring, paper-box making, and the machine-made lace and finishing trade.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  It was later expanded to coal mining and then to other industries with preponderance of unskilled manual labour by the <a href="/wiki/Trade_Boards_Act_1918" title="Trade Boards Act 1918">Trade Boards Act 1918</a>. Under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> Liberals extended minimum wages to farm workers.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Conservative peers in the House of Lords tried to stop the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Budget" title="People&#39;s Budget">People's Budget</a>. The Liberals passed the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Act_1911" title="Parliament Act 1911">Parliament Act 1911</a> to sharply reduce the power of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> to block legislation. The cost was high, however, as the government was required by the king to call two general elections in 1910 to validate its position and ended up frittering away most of its large majority, with the balance of power held by <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Parliamentary_Party" title="Irish Parliamentary Party">Irish Parliamentary Party</a> members.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Foreign relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/International_relations_of_the_Great_Powers_(1814%E2%80%931919)" title="International relations of the Great Powers (1814–1919)">International relations of the Great Powers (1814–1919)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Causes_of_World_War_I" title="Causes of World War I">Causes of World War I</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_British_diplomatic_history" title="Timeline of British diplomatic history">Timeline of British diplomatic history</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomatic_ties_with_France_and_Russia_against_Germany">Diplomatic ties with France and Russia against Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Diplomatic ties with France and Russia against Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/France%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations" title="France–United Kingdom relations">France–United Kingdom relations</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany%E2%80%93United_Kingdom_relations" title="Germany–United Kingdom relations">Germany–United Kingdom relations</a></div>
<p>Germany's Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck" title="Otto von Bismarck">Otto von Bismarck</a> dominated European diplomacy from 1872-1890, with the determination to use the balance of power to keep the peace.  There were no wars.  Bismarck was removed by an aggressive young Kaiser Wilhelm in 1890, effectively decentralizing the Bismarckian Order that had been shrewdly managed and empowering French efforts to isolate Germany. With the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Entente" title="Triple Entente">Triple Entente</a>, Germany began to feel encircled: to the West lay France, with whom rivalry was awakening after a generation of dormancy following the Franco-Prussian War, to the East sat Russia, whose rapid industrialization worried leaders across the Continent, and in the seas, British primacy threatened a unified Germany's efforts to develop its ports and maintain its new global empire.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Chamberlain" title="Joseph Chamberlain">Joseph Chamberlain</a>, who played a major role in foreign policy in the late 1890s under the Salisbury government, repeatedly tried to open talks with Germany about some sort of an alliance.  Germany was not interested.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Meanwhile, Paris went to great pains to woo Russia and Great Britain. Key markers were the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Russian_Alliance" title="Franco-Russian Alliance">Franco-Russian Alliance</a> of 1894, the 1904 <a href="/wiki/Entente_Cordiale" title="Entente Cordiale">Entente Cordiale</a> linking France and Great Britain, and finally the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Russian_Entente" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Russian Entente">Anglo-Russian Entente</a> in 1907 which became the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Entente" title="Triple Entente">Triple Entente</a>. France thus had a formal alliance with Russia, and an informal alignment with Britain, against Germany and Austria.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  By 1903 good relations had been established with the United States and Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Britain abandoned the policy of holding aloof from the continental powers, so called "<a href="/wiki/Splendid_Isolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Splendid Isolation">Splendid Isolation</a>", in the 1900s after being isolated during the <a href="/wiki/Boer_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Boer War">Boer War</a>. Britain concluded agreements, limited to colonial affairs, with her two major colonial rivals: the <a href="/wiki/Entente_Cordiale" title="Entente Cordiale">Entente Cordiale</a> with France in 1904 and the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Russian_Entente" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Russian Entente">Anglo-Russian Entente</a> of 1907. Britain's alignment a reaction to an assertive German foreign policy and the buildup of its navy from 1898 which led to the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-German_naval_arms_race" title="Anglo-German naval arms race">Anglo-German naval arms race</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  British diplomat <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Nicolson,_1st_Baron_Carnock" title="Arthur Nicolson, 1st Baron Carnock">Arthur Nicolson</a> argued it was "far more disadvantageous to us to have an unfriendly France and Russia than an unfriendly Germany".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  The impact of the Triple Entente was to improve British relations with France and its ally Russia and to demote the importance to Britain of good relations with Germany.   After 1905, foreign policy was tightly controlled by the Liberal Foreign Secretary <a href="/wiki/Edward_Grey,_1st_Viscount_Grey_of_Fallodon" title="Edward Grey, 1st Viscount Grey of Fallodon">Edward Grey</a> (1862-1933), who seldom consulted with his party leadership.  Grey shared the strong Liberal policy against all wars and against military alliances that would force Britain to take a side in war. However, in the case of the Boer War, Grey held that the Boers had committed  an aggression that it was necessary to repulse. The Liberal party split on the issue, with a large faction strongly opposed to the war in Africa <sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The  Triple Entente between Britain, France and Russia is often compared to the <a href="/wiki/Triple_Alliance_(1882)" title="Triple Alliance (1882)">Triple Alliance</a> between Germany, Austria–Hungary and Italy, but historians caution against the comparison. The Entente, in contrast to the Triple Alliance or the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Russian_Alliance" title="Franco-Russian Alliance">Franco-Russian Alliance</a>, was not an alliance of mutual defence and Britain therefore felt free to make her own foreign policy decisions in 1914. The Liberals were highly moralistic, and by 1914 they have been increasingly convinced that German aggression violated international norms, and specifically that its invasion of neutral Belgium was completely unacceptable in terms of morality, Britain and Germany's obligations under the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_London_(1839)" title="Treaty of London (1839)">Treaty of London</a>, and in terms of British policy against any one power controlling the continent of Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until the last few weeks before it started in August 1914, almost no one saw a world war coming.  The expectation among the generals was that because of industrial advances any future war would produce a quick victory for the side that was better-prepared, better armed, and faster to move. No one saw that the innovations of recent decades—high explosives, long-range artillery and machine guns—were defensive weapons that practically guaranteed defeat of massed infantry attacks with very high casualties.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Naval_race_with_Germany">Naval race with Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Naval race with Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Anglo-German_naval_arms_race" title="Anglo-German naval arms race">Anglo-German naval arms race</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg/300px-HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg" width="300" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg/450px-HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg/600px-HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="558" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Dreadnought_1906_H61017.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The British <i>Dreadnought</i> (1906) made all battleships obsolete because it had ten long-range 12-inch big guns, mechanical computer-like range finders, high speed turbine engines that could make 21 knots, and armour plates 11 inches thick.</div></div></div>
<p>After 1805 the dominance of Britain's Royal Navy was unchallenged;  in the 1890s Germany decided to match it. Grand Admiral <a href="/wiki/Alfred_von_Tirpitz" title="Alfred von Tirpitz">Alfred von Tirpitz</a> (1849 – 1930) dominated German naval policy from 1897 until 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Before the Germany Empire formed in 1871, Prussia never had a real navy, nor did the other German states.  Tirpitz turned the modest little fleet into a world-class force that could threaten the British Royal Navy. The British responded with new technology typified by the <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dreadnought_(1906)" title="HMS Dreadnought (1906)">Dreadnought revolution</a>. It made every battleship obsolete and, supplemented by the global network of coaling stations and telegraph cables, enabled Britain to stay well in the lead in naval affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Apart from a determination to retain a strong naval advantage, the British lacked a military strategy or plans for a major war.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Edwardian era stands out as a time of peace and prosperity. There were no severe depressions, and prosperity was widespread. Britain's growth rate, manufacturing output and <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> (but not GDP per capita) fell behind its rivals, the United States and Germany, but the nation still led the world in trade, finance and shipping, and had strong bases in manufacturing and mining.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The industrial sector was slow to adjust to global changes, and there was a striking preference for leisure over entrepreneurship among the elite. However, major achievements should be underlined. <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> was the financial centre of the world—far more efficient and wide-ranging than New York, Paris or Berlin. Britain had built up a vast reserve of overseas credits in its formal Empire, as well as in its informal empire in Latin America and other nations. It had huge financial holdings in the United States, especially in railways. These assets proved vital in paying for supplies in the first years of the World War. The amenities, especially in urban life, were accumulating—prosperity was highly visible. The working classes were beginning to protest politically for a greater voice in government, but the level of industrial unrest on economic issues was not high until about 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_change_and_improved_health">Social change and improved health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Social change and improved health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the late-1880s, the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> had created new technologies that changed the way people lived. The growth of industry shifts in manufacturing factories, special-purpose machinery and technological innovations, which led to increased productivity. <a href="/wiki/Gender_roles" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender roles">Gender roles</a> shifted as women made use of the new technology to upgrade their lifestyle and their career opportunities.
</p><p>Mortality declined steadily in urban England and Wales 1870–1917. Robert Millward and Frances N. Bell looked statistically at those factors in the physical environment (especially population density and overcrowding) that raised death rates directly, as well as indirect factors such as price and income movements that affected expenditures on sewers, water supplies, food, and medical staff. The statistical data show that increases in the incomes of households and increases in town tax revenues helped cause the decline of mortality. The new money permitted higher spending on food, and also on a wide range of health-enhancing goods and services such as medical care. The major improvement in the physical environment was the quality of the housing stock, which rose faster than the population; its quality was increasingly regulated by central and local government.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">Infant mortality</a> fell faster in England and Wales than in Scotland. Clive Lee argues that one factor was the continued overcrowding in Scotland's housing.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>  During the First World War, infant mortality fell sharply across the country. J.M. Winter attributes this to the full employment and higher wages paid to war workers.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rising_status_of_women">Rising status of women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rising status of women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For housewives, sewing machines enabled the production of ready made clothing and made it easier for women to sew their own clothes; more generally, argues Barbara Burman, "home dressmaking was sustained as an important aid for women negotiating wider social shifts and tensions in their lives."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  Increased literacy in the <a href="/wiki/Middle-class" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle-class">middle-class</a> gave women wider access to information and ideas. Numerous new magazines appealed to her tastes and help define femininity.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The inventions of the typewriter, telephone, and new filing systems offered middle-class women increased employment opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>  So too did the rapid expansion of the school system,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> and the emergence of the new profession of nursing.  Education and status led to demands for female roles in the rapidly expanding world of sports.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Women were very active in church affairs, including attendance at services, Sunday school teaching, fund raising, pastoral care, social work and support for international missionary activities.  They quietly submitted to their almost complete exclusion from practically all leadership roles.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Women.27s_suffrage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women's_suffrage">Women's suffrage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Women&#039;s suffrage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Women&#39;s suffrage in the United Kingdom">Women's suffrage in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>As middle-class women rose in status they increasingly supported demands for a political voice.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>  There was significant support for woman suffrage in all the parties, but the Liberal Party was in control after 1906 and a handful of its leaders, especially <a href="/wiki/H._H._Asquith" title="H. H. Asquith">H. H. Asquith</a>, blocked it.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There were numerous organisations which did their work quietly.  After 1897 they were increasingly linked together by the <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Women%27s_Suffrage_Societies" title="National Union of Women&#39;s Suffrage Societies">National Union of Women's Suffrage Societies</a> (NUWSS) led by <a href="/wiki/Millicent_Fawcett" title="Millicent Fawcett">Millicent Fawcett</a>.  However front page publicity was seized by the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Social_and_Political_Union" title="Women&#39;s Social and Political Union">Women's Social and Political Union</a> (WSPU). Founded in 1903, it was tightly controlled by the three Pankhursts, <a href="/wiki/Emmeline_Pankhurst" title="Emmeline Pankhurst">Emmeline Pankhurst</a> (1858–1928), and her daughters <a href="/wiki/Christabel_Pankhurst" title="Christabel Pankhurst">Christabel Pankhurst</a> (1880–1958) and <a href="/wiki/Sylvia_Pankhurst" title="Sylvia Pankhurst">Sylvia Pankhurst</a> (1882–1960).<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>  It specialized in highly visible publicity campaigns such as large parades. This had the effect of energizing all dimensions of the suffrage movement. While there was a majority of support for suffrage in parliament, the ruling Liberal Party refused to allow a vote on the issue; the result of which was an escalation in the suffragette campaign. The WSPU, in dramatic contrast to its allies, embarked on a campaign of violence to publicize the issue, even to the detriment of its own aims.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_control">Birth control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Birth control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although abortion was illegal, it was nevertheless the most widespread form of birth control in use.<sup id="cite_ref-knight_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Used predominantly by <a href="/wiki/Working-class" class="mw-redirect" title="Working-class">working-class</a> women, the procedure was used not only as a means of terminating pregnancy, but also to prevent poverty and unemployment. Those who transported <a href="/wiki/Contraceptive" class="mw-redirect" title="Contraceptive">contraceptives</a> could be legally punished.  Contraceptives became more expensive over time and had a high failure rate. Unlike contraceptives, abortion did not need any prior planning and was less expensive. Newspaper advertisements were used to promote and sell <a href="/wiki/Abortifacient" title="Abortifacient">abortifacients</a> indirectly.<sup id="cite_ref-mclaren_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mclaren-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not all of society was accepting of contraceptives or abortion, and the opposition viewed both as part of one and the same sin. Abortion was much more common among the middle-classes than among those living in rural areas, where the procedure was not readily available. Women were often tricked into purchasing ineffective pills. In addition to fearing legal reprimands, many physicians did not condone abortion because they viewed it as an immoral procedure potentially endangering a woman's life.<sup id="cite_ref-mclaren_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mclaren-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Because abortion was illegal and physicians refused to perform the procedure, local women acted as abortionists, often using crochet hooks or similar instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-knight_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Feminists of the era focused on educating and finding jobs for women, leaving aside the controversial issues of contraceptives and abortion, which in popular opinion were often related to promiscuity and prostitution. The Church condemned abortion as immoral and a form of rebellion against the child-bearing role women were expected to assume. Many considered abortion to be a selfish act that allowed a woman to avoid personal responsibility, contributing to a decline in moral values.<sup id="cite_ref-knight_65-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knight-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Abortion was often a solution for women who already had children and did not want more. Consequently, the size of families decreased drastically.<sup id="cite_ref-mclaren_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mclaren-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty_among_working-class_women">Poverty among working-class women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Poverty among working-class women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1834_Poor_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="1834 Poor Law">1834 Poor Law</a> defined who could receive monetary relief. The act reflected and perpetuated prevailing gender conditions. In Edwardian society, men were the source of wealth. The law restricted relief for unemployed, able-bodied male workers, due to the prevailing view that they would find work in the absence of financial assistance. However, women were treated differently. After the Poor Law was passed, women and children received most of the aid. The law did not recognise single independent women, and lumped women and children into the same category.  If a man was physically disabled, his wife was also treated as disabled under the law. Unmarried mothers were sent to the workhouse, receiving unfair social treatment such as being restricted from attending church on Sundays.  During marriage disputes women often lost the rights to their children, even if their husbands were abusive.<sup id="cite_ref-thane_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thane-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the time, single mothers were the poorest sector in society, disadvantaged for at least four reasons. First, women lived longer, often leaving them widowed with children. Second, women had fewer opportunities to work, and when they did find it, their wages were lower than male workers' wages. Thirdly, women were often less likely to marry or remarry after being widowed, leaving them as the main providers for the remaining family members. Finally, poor women had deficient diets, because their husbands and children received disproportionately large shares of food. Many women were malnourished and had limited access to health care.<sup id="cite_ref-thane_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thane-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Female_servants">Female servants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Female servants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Edwardian Britain had large numbers of male and female <a href="/wiki/Domestic_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic servant">domestic servants</a>, in both urban and rural areas.<sup id="cite_ref-benson_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-benson-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Middle and upper-class women relied on servants to run their homes smoothly. Servants were provided with food, clothing, housing, and a small wage, and lived in a self-enclosed social system inside the mansion.<sup id="cite_ref-davidoff_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidoff-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> The number of domestic servants fell in the Edwardian era due to a declining number of young people willing to be employed in this area.<sup id="cite_ref-pooley_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pooley-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fashion">Fashion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Fashion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1900s_in_fashion" class="mw-redirect" title="1900s in fashion">1900s in fashion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Women_dress-1901-1911-Punch.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c7/Women_dress-1901-1911-Punch.jpg/300px-Women_dress-1901-1911-Punch.jpg" width="300" height="525" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/c7/Women_dress-1901-1911-Punch.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="416" data-file-height="728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Women_dress-1901-1911-Punch.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>cartoon in <i>Punch</i> (1911) compares changes in fashion between 1901 and 1911. "The dowdy voluminous clothes of the earlier date, making the grandmother an old lady and the mother seem plain, had been replaced by much simpler looser wear producing a sense of release for all three females."<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The upper-classes embraced leisure sports, which resulted in rapid developments in fashion, as more mobile and flexible clothing styles were needed.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>  During the Edwardian era, women wore a very tight <a href="/wiki/Corset" title="Corset">corset</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Bodice" title="Bodice">bodice</a>, and dressed in long skirts. The Edwardian era was the last time women wore corsets in everyday life. According to <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Marwick" title="Arthur Marwick">Arthur Marwick</a>, the most striking change of all the developments that occurred during the Great War was the modification in women's dress, "for, however far politicians were to put the clocks back in other steeples in the years after the war, no one ever put the lost inches back on the hems of women's skirts".<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Edwardians developed new styles in clothing design.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> While the Victorian Era is associated with large, ankle-length, heavy, dark velvet gowns and bustles for the classes that could afford them, the Edwardian era favored more tightly fitting skirts and dresses made of lightweight fabrics designed for a more active lifestyle.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The 2 pieces dress came into vogue. Skirts hung tight at the hips and flared at the hem, creating a trumpet of lily-like shape.</li>
<li>Skirts in 1901 had decorated hems with ruffles of fabric and lace.</li>
<li>Some dresses and skirts featured trains.</li>
<li>Tailored jackets, first introduced in 1880, increased in popularity and by 1900, tailored suits became popular.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>By 1904, skirts became fuller and less clingy.</li>
<li>In 1905, skirts fell in soft folds that curved in, then flared out near the hemlines.</li>
<li>From 1905 – 1907, waistlines rose.</li>
<li>In 1901, the <a href="/wiki/Hobble_skirt" title="Hobble skirt">hobble skirt</a> was introduced; a tight fitting skirt that restricted a woman's stride.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lingerie_dress" title="Lingerie dress">Lingerie dresses</a>, or tea gowns made of soft fabrics, festooned with ruffles and lace were worn indoors.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Around 1913 women's dresses acquired a lower and sometimes V-shaped neckline in opposition to the high collars a generation before.  This was considered a scandal and caused an outrage among the clergy throughout Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Gernsheim1963_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gernsheim1963-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers">Newspapers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Newspapers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The turn of the century saw the rise of popular journalism aimed at the lower middle class and tending to deemphasise highly detailed political and international news, which remain the focus of a handful of low-circulation prestige newspapers. They were family-owned and operated, and were primarily interested not in profits but in influence on the nation's elite by their control of the news and editorials on serious topics.  The new press they reached vastly larger audiences by emphasis on sports, crime, sensationalism, and gossip about famous personalities. Detailed accounts of major speeches and complex international events were not printed. <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Harmsworth,_1st_Viscount_Northcliffe" title="Alfred Harmsworth, 1st Viscount Northcliffe">Alfred Harmsworth, 1st Viscount Northcliffe</a> was the chief innovator.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>  He used his <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mirror" title="Daily Mirror">Daily Mirror</a></i> to transform the media along the American model of "<a href="/wiki/Yellow_Journalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Yellow Journalism">Yellow Journalism</a>".  <a href="/wiki/Max_Aitken,_1st_Baron_Beaverbrook" title="Max Aitken, 1st Baron Beaverbrook">Lord Beaverbrook</a> said he was "the greatest figure who ever strode down Fleet Street".<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>  Harmsworth made a great deal of money, but during the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> he also wanted political power.  For that he purchased the highest prestige newspaper, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>   P. P. Catterall and Colin Seymour-Ure conclude that: 
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>More than anyone [he] ... shaped the modern press. Developments he introduced or harnessed remain central: broad contents, exploitation of advertising revenue to subsidize prices, aggressive marketing, subordinate regional markets, independence from party control.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_arts">The arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: The arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Pan_(2733740964).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Peter_Pan_%282733740964%29.jpg/220px-Peter_Pan_%282733740964%29.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Peter_Pan_%282733740964%29.jpg/330px-Peter_Pan_%282733740964%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Peter_Pan_%282733740964%29.jpg/440px-Peter_Pan_%282733740964%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peter_Pan_(2733740964).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan_statue" title="Peter Pan statue">Peter Pan statue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gardens" title="Kensington Gardens">Kensington Gardens</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Edwardian era corresponds to the French <i><a href="/wiki/Belle_%C3%89poque" title="Belle Époque">Belle Époque</a></i>. Despite its brief pre-eminence, the period is characterised by its own unique architectural style, fashion, and lifestyle. <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a> had a particularly strong influence. Artists were influenced by the development of the automobile and electricity, and a greater awareness of human rights.
</p><p>In November 1910, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Fry" title="Roger Fry">Roger Fry</a> organised the exhibition <i>Manet and the Post-Impressionists</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Grafton_Galleries" title="Grafton Galleries">Grafton Galleries</a>, London. This exhibition was the first to prominently feature <a href="/wiki/Gauguin" class="mw-redirect" title="Gauguin">Gauguin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manet" class="mw-redirect" title="Manet">Manet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Matisse" class="mw-redirect" title="Matisse">Matisse</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Van_Gogh" class="mw-redirect" title="Van Gogh">Van Gogh</a> in England and brought their art to the public. He followed it up with the <i>Second Post-Impressionist Exhibition</i> in 1912.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/George_Frampton" title="George Frampton">George Frampton</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_Peter_Pan" class="mw-redirect" title="Statue of Peter Pan">statue of Peter Pan</a>, "erected in Hyde Park in 1912 ... immediately became a source of contention, sparking debate about the role of public statuary and its role in spaces of recreation."<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In fiction, some of the best-known names are <a href="/wiki/J._M._Barrie" title="J. M. Barrie">J. M. Barrie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Bennett" title="Arnold Bennett">Arnold Bennett</a>, <a href="/wiki/G._K._Chesterton" title="G. K. Chesterton">G. K. Chesterton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>, <a href="/wiki/E._M._Forster" title="E. M. Forster">E. M. Forster</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Galsworthy" title="John Galsworthy">John Galsworthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Grahame" title="Kenneth Grahame">Kenneth Grahame</a>, <a href="/wiki/M._R._James" title="M. R. James">M. R. James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>, <a href="/wiki/A.A._Milne" class="mw-redirect" title="A.A. Milne">A.A. Milne</a>, <a href="/wiki/D._H._Lawrence" title="D. H. Lawrence">D. H. Lawrence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edith_Nesbit" class="mw-redirect" title="Edith Nesbit">Edith Nesbit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_Potter" title="Beatrix Potter">Beatrix Potter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saki" title="Saki">Saki</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">H. G. Wells</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/P._G._Wodehouse" title="P. G. Wodehouse">P. G. Wodehouse</a>. Apart from these famous writers, this was a period when a great number of novels and short stories were being published, and a significant distinction between "<a href="/wiki/Highbrow" title="Highbrow">highbrow</a>" literature and <a href="/wiki/Popular_fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Popular fiction">popular fiction</a> emerged. Among the most famous works of literary criticism was <a href="/wiki/A._C._Bradley" title="A. C. Bradley">A. C. Bradley</a>'s <i>Shakespearean Tragedy</i> (1904).<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Live performances, both amateur and professional, were popular. <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wood" title="Henry Wood">Henry Wood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Elgar" title="Edward Elgar">Edward Elgar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Holst" title="Gustav Holst">Gustav Holst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Bax" title="Arnold Bax">Arnold Bax</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Butterworth" title="George Butterworth">George Butterworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Vaughan_Williams" title="Ralph Vaughan Williams">Ralph Vaughan Williams</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Beecham" title="Thomas Beecham">Thomas Beecham</a> were all active. Military and brass bands often played outside in parks during the summer.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>  The new technology of <a href="/wiki/Wax_cylinders" class="mw-redirect" title="Wax cylinders">wax cylinders</a> played on <a href="/wiki/Phonograph" title="Phonograph">phonographs</a>, made live performances permanently available for repetition at any time.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Performing_arts">Performing arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Performing arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cinema was primitive and audiences preferred live performances to picture shows. <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">Music hall</a> was very popular and widespread; influential performers included male impersonator <a href="/wiki/Vesta_Tilley" title="Vesta Tilley">Vesta Tilley</a> and comic <a href="/wiki/Little_Tich" title="Little Tich">Little Tich</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most successful playwright of the era was <a href="/wiki/W._Somerset_Maugham" title="W. Somerset Maugham">W. Somerset Maugham</a>. In 1908, he had four plays running simultaneously in London, and <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i> published a cartoon of Shakespeare biting his fingernails nervously as he looked at the billboards. Maugham's plays, like his novels, usually had a conventional plot structure, but the decade also saw the rise of the so-called New Drama, represented in plays by <a href="/wiki/George_Bernard_Shaw" title="George Bernard Shaw">George Bernard Shaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harley_Granville_Barker" class="mw-redirect" title="Harley Granville Barker">Harley Granville Barker</a>, and Continental imports by <a href="/wiki/Henrik_Ibsen" title="Henrik Ibsen">Henrik Ibsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerhardt_Hauptmann" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerhardt Hauptmann">Gerhardt Hauptmann</a>. The actor/manager system, as managed by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Henry_Irving" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Henry Irving">Sir Henry Irving</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Alexander_(actor)" title="George Alexander (actor)">Sir George Alexander</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sir_Herbert_Beerbohm_Tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Herbert Beerbohm Tree">Sir Herbert Beerbohm Tree</a>, was in decline.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_architecture" title="Edwardian architecture">Edwardian architecture</a></div>
<p>Notable architects included <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Lutyens" title="Edwin Lutyens">Edwin Lutyens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Rennie_Mackintosh" title="Charles Rennie Mackintosh">Charles Rennie Mackintosh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Giles_Gilbert_Scott" title="Giles Gilbert Scott">Giles Gilbert Scott</a>. In spite of the popularity of <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a> in Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_Baroque_architecture" title="Edwardian Baroque architecture">Edwardian Baroque</a> style of architecture was widely favoured for public structures and was a revival of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">Christopher Wren</a>–inspired designs of the late 17th and early 18th centuries. The change or reversal in taste from the Victorian eclectic styles corresponded with the historical revivals of the period, most prominently earlier <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neoclassical</a> styles of the late 18th and early 19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/White_City_Stadium" title="White City Stadium">White City Stadium</a> for the <a href="/wiki/1908_Summer_Olympics" title="1908 Summer Olympics">1908 Summer Olympics</a> was the first Olympic Stadium in the UK. Built on the site of the <a href="/wiki/Franco-British_Exhibition" class="mw-redirect" title="Franco-British Exhibition">Franco-British Exhibition</a>, it had a seating capacity of 68,000 was opened by King Edward VII on 27 April 1908. It was the largest structure of its type in the world, and was designed to be  awesome and enhance the love of large-scale spectacle that characterised Edwardian London.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Filmmakers <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_and_Kenyon" title="Mitchell and Kenyon">Mitchell and Kenyon</a> documented many scenes from Britain and Ireland from 1900–1907, sports, parades, factory exits, parks, city streets, boating and the like. Their films have survived in very good quality restored from the original negatives.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_and_technology">Science and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Science and technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The period featured many innovations. <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a> published his studies on <a href="/wiki/Radioactivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactivity">radioactivity</a>. The first transatlantic wireless signals were sent by <a href="/wiki/Guglielmo_Marconi" title="Guglielmo Marconi">Guglielmo Marconi</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Wright_brothers" title="Wright brothers">Wright brothers</a> flew for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the end of the era, <a href="/wiki/Louis_Bl%C3%A9riot" title="Louis Blériot">Louis Blériot</a> had crossed the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> by air; the largest ship in the world, <a href="/wiki/RMS_Olympic" title="RMS Olympic">RMS <i>Olympic</i></a>, had sailed on its maiden voyage and her larger sister <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a> was under construction; automobiles were common; and the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> was reached for the first time by <a href="/wiki/Roald_Amundsen" title="Roald Amundsen">Roald Amundsen</a>'s and then <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">Robert Falcon Scott</a>'s teams.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg/200px-London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg" width="200" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg/300px-London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg/400px-London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg 2x" data-file-width="695" data-file-height="474" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:London_1908_Steeplechasea.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1908 Summer Olympics in London: The water jump in the <a href="/wiki/Steeplechase_(athletics)" title="Steeplechase (athletics)">steeplechase</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1908_Summer_Olympics" title="1908 Summer Olympics">1908 Summer Olympic Games</a> were held in London. Popularity of sports tended to conform to class divisions, with tennis and yachting popular among the very wealthy and <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> favoured by the working class.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Edwardian_era&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Aston_Villa" class="mw-redirect" title="Aston Villa">Aston Villa</a> maintained their position as the pre-eminent football team of the era, winning the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> for the fourth time in 1904–05 and their sixth League title in 1909–10. The club colours of claret and sky blue were adopted by <a href="/wiki/Burnley_FC" class="mw-redirect" title="Burnley FC">Burnley</a> as a tribute to their success in 1910. Sunderland achieved their fourth league title in 1901–02. The era also saw <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a> (1900–01, 1905–06), <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_United" class="mw-redirect" title="Newcastle United">Newcastle United</a> (1904–05, 1906–07, 1908–09) and <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_F.C." title="Manchester United F.C.">Manchester United</a> (1907–08) winning their first league titles.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
