<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">JumpStation</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathon_Fletcher&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Jonathon Fletcher">Jonathon Fletcher</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox vcard" style="width:22em"><caption class="fn org">JumpStation</caption><tbody><tr><th scope="row"><div style="padding:0.1em 0;line-height:1.2em;">Type of site</div></th><td>
<a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">Web search engine</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Launched</th><td>
December&#160;12, 1993<span class="noprint">&#59;&#32;24 years ago</span><span style="display:none">&#160;(<span class="bday dtstart published updated">1993-12-12</span>)</span></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Current&#160;status</th><td class="category">
Closed</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>JumpStation</b> was the first <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">WWW search engine</a> that behaved, and appeared to the user, the way current web search engines do.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It started indexing on Sunday 12 December 1993<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and was announced on the <a href="/wiki/Mosaic_(web_browser)" title="Mosaic (web browser)">Mosaic</a> "What's New" webpage on 21 December 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It was hosted at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Stirling" title="University of Stirling">University of Stirling</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.
</p><p>It was written by Jonathon Fletcher, from <a href="/wiki/Scarborough,_North_Yorkshire" title="Scarborough, North Yorkshire">Scarborough, North Yorkshire</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> who graduated from the University with a first class honours degree in Computing Science in the summer of 1992<sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_on_Sunday_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_on_Sunday-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and has subsequently been named "father of the search engine".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was subsequently employed there as a systems administrator. JumpStation's development discontinued when he left the University in late 1994, having failed to get any investors, including the University of Stirling, to financially back his idea.<sup id="cite_ref-Scotland_on_Sunday_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scotland_on_Sunday-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At this point the database had 275,000 entries spanning 1,500 servers.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>JumpStation used document titles and headings to index the web pages found using a simple linear search, and did not provide any ranking of results.<sup id="cite_ref-matrix_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matrix-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, JumpStation had the same basic shape as <a href="/wiki/Google_search" class="mw-redirect" title="Google search">Google search</a> in that it used an index solely built by a <a href="/wiki/Web_robot" class="mw-redirect" title="Web robot">web robot</a>, searched this index using keyword queries entered by the user on a <a href="/wiki/Web_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Web form">web form</a> whose location was well-known,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and presented its results in the form of a list of URLs that matched those keywords.
</p><p>JumpStation was nominated for a "Best Of The Web" award in 1994<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and the story of its origin and development written up, using interviews with Fletcher, by Wishart and Bochsler.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
