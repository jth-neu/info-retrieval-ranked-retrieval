<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">National church</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about 'national churches' in the ethnic sense. For church organizations at a national level, see <a href="/wiki/State_church" class="mw-redirect" title="State church">state church</a>. For Catholic churches in Rome associated with various countries, see <a href="/wiki/National_churches_in_Rome" title="National churches in Rome">National churches in Rome</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg/250px-Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg" width="250" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg/375px-Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg/500px-Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2114" data-file-height="1560" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Holte_Kirke_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Denmark" title="Church of Denmark">Church of Denmark</a> parish church in <a href="/wiki/Holte" title="Holte">Holte</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Denmark" title="Flag of Denmark">Dannebrog</a> flying in its <a href="/wiki/Kirkyard" class="mw-redirect" title="Kirkyard">kirkyard</a></div></div></div>
<p>A <b>national church</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Christian_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian church">Christian church</a> associated with a specific <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation state</a>. The idea was notably discussed during the <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_the_19th_century" title="Christianity in the 19th century">19th century</a>, during the emergence of modern <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalism</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a>, in a draft discussing the question of <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_church_and_state" title="Separation of church and state">church and state</a> around 1828 wrote that 
</p>
<dl><dd>"a National Church might exist, and has existed, without [Christianity], because before the institution of the <i>Christian</i> Church - as [...] the Levitical Church in the Hebrew Constitution, [and] the <a href="/wiki/Druidical" class="mw-redirect" title="Druidical">Druidical</a> in the Celtic, would suffice to prove".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Wordsworth" title="John Wordsworth">John Wordsworth</a>, Bishop of Salisbury, wrote about the National Church of Sweden in 1911, interpreting the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Sweden" title="Church of Sweden">Church of Sweden</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> as national churches of the Swedish and the English peoples, respectively. Lake (1987) traces the development of <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterianism</a> in 16th-century England from the status of a "godly minority" which saw itself surrounded by the corrupt or hostile mass of the population, into a "genuine national church".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of a national church remains alive in the Protestantism of England and Scandinavia in particular. While, in a context of England, the national church remains a common denominator for the Church of England, some of the Lutheran "<b>folk churches</b>" of Scandinavia, characterized as national churches in the ethnic sense as opposed to the idea of a state church, emerged in the second half of the 19th century following the lead of <a href="/wiki/N._F._S._Grundtvig" title="N. F. S. Grundtvig">Grundtvig</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, in countries in which the state church (also known as the established church) has the following of the majority of citizens, the state church may also be the national church, and may be declared as such by the government e.g. <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Denmark" title="Church of Denmark">Church of Denmark</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Shadid1995_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shadid1995-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Greece" title="Church of Greece">Church of Greece</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-EnyediMadeley2004_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EnyediMadeley2004-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Iceland" title="Church of Iceland">Church of Iceland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
