<p><i><b>Vertigo</b></i> is a 1958 American <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a> <a href="/wiki/Psychological_thriller_film" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychological thriller film">psychological thriller film</a> directed and produced by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>. The story was based on the 1954 novel <i><a href="/wiki/D%27entre_les_morts" class="mw-redirect" title="D&#39;entre les morts">D'entre les morts</a></i> (<i>From Among the Dead</i>) by <a href="/wiki/Boileau-Narcejac" title="Boileau-Narcejac">Boileau-Narcejac</a>. The screenplay was written by <a href="/wiki/Alec_Coppel" title="Alec Coppel">Alec Coppel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_A._Taylor" title="Samuel A. Taylor">Samuel A. Taylor</a>.
</p><p>The film stars <a href="/wiki/James_Stewart" title="James Stewart">James Stewart</a> as former police <a href="/wiki/Detective" title="Detective">detective</a> John "Scottie" Ferguson. Scottie is forced into early retirement because an incident in the line of duty has caused him to develop <a href="/wiki/Acrophobia" title="Acrophobia">acrophobia</a> (an extreme fear of heights) and <a href="/wiki/Vertigo" title="Vertigo">vertigo</a> (a false sense of rotational movement). Scottie is hired by an acquaintance, Gavin Elster, as a <a href="/wiki/Private_investigator" title="Private investigator">private investigator</a> to follow Gavin's wife Madeleine (<a href="/wiki/Kim_Novak" title="Kim Novak">Kim Novak</a>), who is behaving strangely.
</p><p>The film was shot on location in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and at <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Paramount Studios">Paramount Studios</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>. It is the first film to use the <a href="/wiki/Dolly_zoom" title="Dolly zoom">dolly zoom</a>, an in-camera effect that distorts perspective to create disorientation, to convey Scottie's acrophobia. As a result of its use in this film, the effect is often referred to as "the <i>Vertigo</i> effect".
</p><p><i>Vertigo</i> received mixed reviews upon initial release, but is now often cited as a classic Hitchcock film and one of the defining works of his career. Attracting significant scholarly criticism, it replaced <i><a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">Citizen Kane</a></i> (1941) as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_considered_the_best" title="List of films considered the best">best film ever made</a> in the 2012 British Film Institute's <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> critics' poll.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1996, the film underwent a major restoration to create a new <a href="/wiki/70_mm_film" title="70 mm film">70&#160;mm print</a> and <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)" title="DTS (sound system)">DTS soundtrack</a>. It has appeared repeatedly in polls of the best films by the <a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> including a 2007 ranking as <a href="/wiki/AFI%27s_100_Years..._100_Movies" class="mw-redirect" title="AFI&#39;s 100 Years... 100 Movies">the ninth-greatest American movie of all time</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg/240px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg" width="240" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg/360px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg/480px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1002" data-file-height="566" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_Kim_Novak_at_Golden_Gate_Bridge_Fort_Point.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Madeleine" at Golden Gate Bridge, Fort Point, shortly before she jumps into the bay.</div></div></div>
<p>After a rooftop chase, where his <a href="/wiki/Acrophobia" title="Acrophobia">fear of heights</a> and vertigo result in the death of a policeman, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco</a> detective John "Scottie" Ferguson retires. Scottie tries to conquer his fear, but his friend and ex-fiancée Midge Wood says that another severe emotional shock may be the only cure.
</p><p>An acquaintance from college, Gavin Elster, asks Scottie to follow his wife, Madeleine, claiming that she is in some sort of danger. Scottie reluctantly agrees, and follows Madeleine to a florist where she buys a bouquet of flowers, to the <a href="/wiki/Mission_San_Francisco_de_As%C3%ADs" title="Mission San Francisco de Asís">Mission San Francisco de Asís</a> and the grave of one Carlotta Valdes (1831–1857), and to the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honor_(museum)" title="Legion of Honor (museum)">Legion of Honor</a> art museum where she gazes at the <i>Portrait of Carlotta</i>. He watches her enter the McKittrick Hotel, but on investigation she does not seem to be there.
</p><p>A local historian explains that Carlotta Valdes committed <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>: she had been the mistress of a wealthy married man and bore his child; the otherwise childless man kept the child and cast Carlotta aside. Gavin reveals that Carlotta (who he fears is <a href="/wiki/Spirit_possession" title="Spirit possession">possessing</a> Madeleine) is Madeleine's great-grandmother, although Madeleine has no knowledge of this, and does not remember the places she has visited. Scottie tails Madeleine to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Point,_San_Francisco" title="Fort Point, San Francisco">Fort Point</a> and, when she leaps into the bay, he rescues her.
</p><p>The next day Scottie follows Madeleine; they meet and spend the day together. They travel to <a href="/wiki/Muir_Woods" class="mw-redirect" title="Muir Woods">Muir Woods</a> and Cypress Point on <a href="/wiki/17-Mile_Drive" title="17-Mile Drive">17-Mile Drive</a>, where Madeleine runs down towards the ocean. Scottie grabs her and they embrace. Madeleine recounts a nightmare and Scottie identifies its setting as <a href="/wiki/Mission_San_Juan_Bautista" title="Mission San Juan Bautista">Mission San Juan Bautista</a>, childhood home of Carlotta. He drives her there and they express their love for each other. Madeleine suddenly runs into the church and up the bell tower. Scottie, halted on the steps by his acrophobia, sees Madeleine plunge to her death.
</p><p>The death is declared a suicide. Gavin does not fault Scottie, but Scottie <a href="/wiki/Mental_breakdown" title="Mental breakdown">breaks down</a>, becomes <a href="/wiki/Clinical_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinical depression">clinically depressed</a> and is in a <a href="/wiki/Sanatorium" title="Sanatorium">sanatorium</a>, almost <a href="/wiki/Catatonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catatonic">catatonic</a>. After release, Scottie frequents the places that Madeleine visited, often imagining that he sees her. One day, he notices a woman who reminds him of Madeleine, despite her different appearance. Scottie follows her and she identifies herself as Judy Barton, from <a href="/wiki/Salina,_Kansas" title="Salina, Kansas">Salina, Kansas</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(narrative)" title="Flashback (narrative)">flashback</a> reveals that Judy was the person Scottie knew as "Madeleine Elster"; she was impersonating Gavin's wife as part of a murder plot. Judy drafts a letter to Scottie explaining her involvement: Gavin had deliberately taken advantage of Scottie's acrophobia to substitute his wife's freshly killed body in the apparent "suicide jump". But Judy rips up the letter and continues the charade, because she loves Scottie.
</p><p>They begin seeing each other, but Scottie remains obsessed with "Madeleine", and asks Judy to change her clothes and hair so that she resembles Madeleine. After Judy complies, hoping that they may finally find happiness together, he notices her wearing the necklace portrayed in the painting of Carlotta, and realizes the truth, and that Judy had been Elster's mistress, before being cast aside just as Carlotta was. Scottie insists on driving Judy to the Mission.
</p><p>There, he tells her he must re-enact the event that led to his madness, admitting he now understands that "Madeleine" and Judy are the same person. Scottie forces her up the bell tower and makes her admit her deceit. Scottie reaches the top, finally conquering his acrophobia. Judy confesses that Gavin paid her to impersonate a "possessed" Madeleine; Gavin faked the suicide by throwing the body of his wife from the bell tower.
</p><p>Judy begs Scottie to forgive her, because she loves him. He embraces her, but a shadowed figure rises from the trapdoor of the tower, startling Judy, who steps backward and falls to her death. Scottie, bereaved again, stands on the ledge, while the figure, a nun investigating the noise, rings the mission bell.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Barr in his monograph dedicated to the study of <i>Vertigo</i> has stated that the central theme of the film is psychological obsession, concentrating in particular on Scottie as obsessed with the women in his life. As Barr states in his book, "This story of a man who develops a romantic obsession with the image of an enigmatic woman has commonly been seen, by his colleagues as well as by critics and biographers, as one that engaged Hitchcock in an especially profound way; and it has exerted a comparable fascination on many of its viewers. After first seeing it as a teenager in 1958, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Spoto" title="Donald Spoto">Donald Spoto</a> had gone back for 26 more viewings by the time he wrote <i>The Art of Alfred Hitchcock</i> in 1976. In a 1996 magazine article, Geoffrey O'Brien cites other cases of 'permanent fascination' with <i>Vertigo</i>, and then casually reveals that he himself, starting at age 15, has seen it 'at least thirty times'."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Critics have interpreted <i>Vertigo</i> variously as "a tale of male aggression and visual control; as a map of female Oedipal trajectory; as a deconstruction of the male construction of femininity and of masculinity itself; as a stripping bare of the mechanisms of directorial, Hollywood studio and colonial oppression; and as a place where textual meanings play out in an infinite regress of self-reflexivity."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Critic James F. Maxfield has suggested<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that <i>Vertigo</i> can be interpreted as a variant on the <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Bierce" title="Ambrose Bierce">Ambrose Bierce</a> short story "<a href="/wiki/An_Occurrence_at_Owl_Creek_Bridge" title="An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge">An Occurrence at Owl Creek Bridge</a>" (1890), and that the main narrative of the film is actually imagined by Scottie, whom we see  dangling from a building at the end of the opening rooftop chase.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg/260px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg" width="260" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg/390px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg/520px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg 2x" data-file-width="629" data-file-height="351" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_Novak.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kim Novak as "Madeleine", who has woken up naked in Scottie's bed after an apparent suicide attempt by drowning.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">screenplay</a> of <i>Vertigo</i> is an adaptation of the French novel <i><a href="/wiki/D%27entre_les_morts" class="mw-redirect" title="D&#39;entre les morts">D'entre les morts</a></i> (<i>From Among the Dead</i>) by <a href="/wiki/Boileau-Narcejac" title="Boileau-Narcejac">Pierre Boileau and Thomas Narcejac</a>. Hitchcock had attempted to buy the rights to the previous novel by the same authors, <i>Celle qui n'était plus</i>, but he failed, and it was made instead by <a href="/wiki/Henri-Georges_Clouzot" title="Henri-Georges Clouzot">Henri-Georges Clouzot</a> as <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Diaboliques_(film)" title="Les Diaboliques (film)">Les Diaboliques</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Although <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a> once suggested that <i>D'entre les morts</i> was specifically written for Hitchcock by Boileau and Narcejac,<sup id="cite_ref-truffaut_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-truffaut-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Narcejac subsequently denied that this was their intention.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> However, Hitchcock's interest in their work meant that <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> commissioned a synopsis of <i>D'entre les morts</i> in 1954, before it had even been translated into English.<sup id="cite_ref-auiler_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auiler-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg/260px-Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg" width="260" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg/390px-Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg/520px-Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2032" data-file-height="1524" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mission_San_Juan_Bautista.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The scenes with "Madeleine", and subsequently Judy, at <a href="/wiki/Mission_San_Juan_Bautista" title="Mission San Juan Bautista">Mission San Juan Bautista</a> used the real Mission location with a much higher bell tower as a <a href="/wiki/Special_effect" title="Special effect">special effect</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In the book, Judy's involvement in Madeleine's death was not revealed until the <a href="/wiki/Denoument" class="mw-redirect" title="Denoument">denouement</a>. At the script stage, Hitchcock suggested revealing the secret two-thirds of the way through the film, so that the audience would understand Judy's mental dilemma.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> After the first preview, Hitchcock was unsure whether to keep the "letter writing scene" or not. He decided to remove it. Herbert Coleman, <i>Vertigo'</i>s associate producer and a frequent collaborator with Hitchcock, felt the removal was a mistake. However, Hitchcock said, "Release it just like that." James Stewart, acting as mediator, said to Coleman, "Herbie, you shouldn't get so upset with Hitch. The picture's not that important." Hitchcock's decision was supported by <a href="/wiki/Joan_Harrison_(screenwriter)" title="Joan Harrison (screenwriter)">Joan Harrison</a>, another member of his circle, who felt that the film had been improved. Coleman reluctantly made the necessary edits. When he received news of this, Paramount head <a href="/wiki/Barney_Balaban" title="Barney Balaban">Barney Balaban</a> was very vocal about the edits and ordered Hitchcock to "Put the picture back the way it was." As a result, the "letter writing scene" remained in the final film.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were 3 screenwriters involved in the writing of <i>Vertigo</i>. Hitchcock originally hired playwright <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_Anderson" title="Maxwell Anderson">Maxwell Anderson</a> to write a screenplay, but rejected his work, which was titled <i>Darkling, I Listen,</i> a quotation from <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">Keats's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Ode_to_a_Nightingale" title="Ode to a Nightingale">Ode to a Nightingale</a>.</i> According to Charles Barr in his monograph dedicated to <i>Vertigo</i>, "Anderson was the oldest (at 68) [of the 3 writers involved], the most celebrated for his stage work and the least committed to cinema, though he had a joint script credit for Hitchcock's preceding film <i>The Wrong Man</i>. He worked on adapting the novel during Hitchcock's absence abroad, and submitted a treatment in September 1956."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A second version, written by <a href="/wiki/Alec_Coppel" title="Alec Coppel">Alec Coppel</a>, again left the director dissatisfied.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The final script was written by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_A._Taylor" title="Samuel A. Taylor">Samuel A. Taylor</a>—who was recommended to Hitchcock due to his knowledge of San Francisco—<sup id="cite_ref-auiler_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auiler-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> from notes by Hitchcock. Among Taylor's creations was the character of Midge.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Taylor attempted to take sole credit for the screenplay, but Coppel protested to the <a href="/wiki/Screen_Writers_Guild" title="Screen Writers Guild">Screen Writers Guild</a>, which determined that both writers were entitled to a credit and leave Anderson out of the film writing credits.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Casting">Casting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Casting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vera_Miles" title="Vera Miles">Vera Miles</a>, who was under personal contract to Hitchcock and had appeared on both his television show and in his film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wrong_Man_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Wrong Man (film)">The Wrong Man</a></i>, was originally scheduled to play Madeleine. She modeled for an early version of the painting featured in the film.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Following delays, including Hitchcock becoming ill with gallbladder problems, Miles became pregnant and so had to withdraw from the role.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The director declined to postpone shooting and cast <a href="/wiki/Kim_Novak" title="Kim Novak">Kim Novak</a> as the female lead. By the time Novak had tied up prior film commitments and a vacation promised by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>, the studio that held her contract, Miles had given birth and was available for the film. Hitchcock proceeded with Novak, nevertheless. Columbia head <a href="/wiki/Harry_Cohn" title="Harry Cohn">Harry Cohn</a> agreed to lend Novak to <i>Vertigo</i> if Stewart would agree to co-star with Novak in <i><a href="/wiki/Bell,_Book_and_Candle" title="Bell, Book and Candle">Bell, Book and Candle</a></i>, a Columbia production released in December 1958.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming">Filming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Filming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_on-site_principal_photography">Initial on-site principal photography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Initial on-site principal photography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>Vertigo</i> was filmed from September to December 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-auiler185_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auiler185-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Principal photography began on location in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> in September 1957 under the <a href="/wiki/Working_title" title="Working title">working title</a> <i>From Among the Dead</i> (the literal translation of <i>D'entre les morts</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The film uses extensive location footage of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a>, with its steep hills and tall, arching bridges. In the driving scenes shot in the city, the main characters' cars are almost always pictured heading <i>down</i> the city's steeply inclined streets.<sup id="cite_ref-auiler185_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auiler185-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In October 1996, the restored print of <i>Vertigo</i> debuted at the <a href="/wiki/Castro_Theatre" title="Castro Theatre">Castro Theatre</a> in San Francisco with a live on-stage introduction by surviving cast member Kim Novak, providing the city a chance to celebrate itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Kraft_2002_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft_2002-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Visiting the San Francisco film locations has something of a cult following as well as modest tourist appeal. Such a tour is featured in a subsection of <a href="/wiki/Chris_Marker" title="Chris Marker">Chris Marker</a>'s documentary montage <i><a href="/wiki/Sans_Soleil" title="Sans Soleil">Sans Soleil</a></i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg/260px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg" width="260" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg/390px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg/520px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="627" data-file-height="352" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_embrace_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>James Stewart as Scottie, and Kim Novak as Judy, in Scottie's apartment, with <a href="/wiki/Coit_Tower" title="Coit Tower">Coit Tower</a> visible through the window</div></div></div>
<p>The scene in which Madeleine falls from the tower was filmed at <a href="/wiki/Mission_San_Juan_Bautista" title="Mission San Juan Bautista">Mission San Juan Bautista</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_missions_in_California" title="Spanish missions in California">Spanish mission</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_Bautista,_California" title="San Juan Bautista, California">San Juan Bautista</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.  Associate producer Herbert Coleman's daughter Judy Lanini suggested the mission to Hitchcock as a filming location. A steeple, added sometime after the mission's original construction and secularization, had been demolished following a fire, so Hitchcock added a bell tower using scale models, <a href="/wiki/Matte_painting" title="Matte painting">matte paintings</a>, and trick photography at the Paramount studio in Los Angeles.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The original tower was much smaller and less dramatic than the film's version. The tower's staircase was later assembled inside a studio.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_shooting_locations">List of shooting locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: List of shooting locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul><li>Scottie's apartment (900 <a href="/wiki/Lombard_Street_(San_Francisco)" title="Lombard Street (San Francisco)">Lombard Street</a>) is one block downhill from the "crookedest street in the world". The facade of the building remained mostly intact until 2012, when the owner of the property erected a wall enclosing the entrance area on the Lombard side of the building.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kraft_2002_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft_2002-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mission_San_Juan_Bautista" title="Mission San Juan Bautista">Mission San Juan Bautista</a>, where Madeleine falls from the tower, is a real place, but the tower had to be <a href="/wiki/Matte_(filmmaking)" title="Matte (filmmaking)">matted</a> in with a painting using studio effects; Hitchcock had first visited the mission before the tower was torn down due to dry rot, and was reportedly displeased to find it missing when he returned to film his scenes. The original tower was much smaller and less dramatic than the film's version.</li>
<li>The Carlotta Valdes headstone featured in the film (created by the props department) was left at <a href="/wiki/Mission_Dolores" class="mw-redirect" title="Mission Dolores">Mission Dolores</a>. Eventually, the headstone was removed as the mission considered it disrespectful to the dead to house a tourist attraction grave for a fictional person. All other cemeteries in San Francisco were evicted from city limits in 1912, so the screenwriters had no other option but to locate the grave at Mission Dolores.</li>
<li>Madeleine jumps into the sea at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Point,_San_Francisco" title="Fort Point, San Francisco">Fort Point</a>, underneath the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">Golden Gate Bridge</a>.</li>
<li>The gallery where Carlotta's painting appears is the <a href="/wiki/California_Palace_of_the_Legion_of_Honor" class="mw-redirect" title="California Palace of the Legion of Honor">California Palace of the Legion of Honor</a> in San Francisco. The Carlotta Valdes portrait was lost after being removed from the gallery, but many of the other paintings in the background of the portrait scenes are still on view.</li>
<li>What purports to be <a href="/wiki/Muir_Woods_National_Monument" title="Muir Woods National Monument">Muir Woods National Monument</a> in the film is in fact <a href="/wiki/Big_Basin_Redwoods_State_Park" title="Big Basin Redwoods State Park">Big Basin Redwoods State Park</a>; however, the cutaway of the redwood tree showing its age was copied from one that can still be found at Muir Woods.</li>
<li>The coastal region where Scottie and Madeleine first kiss is Cypress Point, along the <a href="/wiki/17_Mile_Drive" class="mw-redirect" title="17 Mile Drive">17 Mile Drive</a> near <a href="/wiki/Pebble_Beach,_California" title="Pebble Beach, California">Pebble Beach</a>. However, the lone tree they kiss next to was a prop brought specially to the location.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The domed building Scottie and Judy walk past is the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Fine_Arts" title="Palace of Fine Arts">Palace of Fine Arts</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coit_Tower" title="Coit Tower">Coit Tower</a> appears in many background shots. Hitchcock once said that he included it as a <a href="/wiki/Phallic_symbol" class="mw-redirect" title="Phallic symbol">phallic symbol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Also prominent in the background is the tower of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Ferry_Building" title="San Francisco Ferry Building">San Francisco Ferry Building</a>.</li>
<li>The exterior of the sanatorium where Scottie is treated was a real sanatorium, St. Joseph's Hospital, located at 355 Buena Vista East, across from <a href="/wiki/Buena_Vista_Park" title="Buena Vista Park">Buena Vista Park</a>. The complex has been converted into condominiums and the building, built in 1928, is on the National Register of Historic Places.</li>
<li>Gavin and Madeleine's apartment building is "The Brocklebank" at 1000 Mason Street on <a href="/wiki/Nob_Hill" class="mw-redirect" title="Nob Hill">Nob Hill</a>, which still looks essentially the same. It is across the street from the Fairmont Hotel, where Hitchcock usually stayed when he visited and where many of the cast and crew stayed during filming. Shots of the surrounding neighborhood feature the <a href="/wiki/Flood_Mansion" class="mw-redirect" title="Flood Mansion">Flood Mansion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grace_Cathedral,_San_Francisco" title="Grace Cathedral, San Francisco">Grace Cathedral</a>. Barely visible is the Mark Hopkins hotel, mentioned in an early scene in the movie.</li>
<li>The "McKittrick Hotel" was a privately owned Victorian mansion from the 1880s at Gough and Eddy Streets. It was torn down in 1959 and is now an athletic practice field for <a href="/wiki/Sacred_Heart_Cathedral_Preparatory" title="Sacred Heart Cathedral Preparatory">Sacred Heart Cathedral Preparatory</a> School. The St. Paulus Lutheran Church, seen across from the mansion, was destroyed in a fire in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Podesta_Baldocchi" title="Podesta Baldocchi">Podesta Baldocchi</a> is the flower shop Madeleine visits as she is being followed by Scottie. The shop's location at the time of filming was 224 Grant Avenue. The Podesta Baldocchi flower shop now does business from a location at 410 Harriet Street.<sup id="cite_ref-podesta_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-podesta-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Empire Hotel is a real place, called the York Hotel, and now (as of January 2009) the Hotel Vertigo at 940 Sutter Street. Judy's room was created, but the green neon of the "Hotel Empire" sign outside is based on the actual hotel's sign (it was replaced when the hotel was renamed).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Ernie's Restaurant (847 Montgomery St.) was a real place in <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Square,_San_Francisco" title="Jackson Square, San Francisco">Jackson Square</a>, one mile<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> from Scottie's apartment. It is no longer operating.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>One short scene shows <a href="/wiki/Union_Square,_San_Francisco" title="Union Square, San Francisco">Union Square</a> at dawn, with old-fashioned "semaphore" traffic lights. Pop Leibel's bookstore, the Argosy, was not a real location, but one recreated on the Paramount lot in imitation of the real-life Argonaut Book Store, which still exists near Sutter and Jones.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Elster's fictitious <a href="/wiki/Dogpatch,_San_Francisco" title="Dogpatch, San Francisco">Dogpatch</a> shipyard office.  Filmed at the real (or simulated with mattes) <a href="/wiki/Union_Iron_Works" title="Union Iron Works">Union Iron Works shipyard</a>, by then the post-WW2 Bethlehem Steel shipyard.  Elster's office has a MIssion telephone exchange (MI or 64) prefix, regarding which Scotty says "Why, that's Skid Row", probably because the city's southern MIssion exchange served all of the south-of-slot (SoMa today) and southern (Mission District) phones, and this shipyard area of course met the description of a <a href="/wiki/Skid_row" title="Skid row">Skid Row</a>.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_studio_shooting">Subsequent studio shooting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Subsequent studio shooting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following 16 days of location shooting, the production moved to Paramount's studios in Hollywood for two months of filming.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Hitchcock preferred to film in studios as he was able to control the environment. Once sufficient location footage had been obtained, interior sets were designed and constructed in the studio.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hitchcock popularized the <a href="/wiki/Dolly_zoom" title="Dolly zoom">dolly zoom</a> in this film, leading to the technique's sobriquet, amongst several others, "the <i>Vertigo</i> effect". This "dolly-out/zoom-in" method involves the camera physically moving away from a subject whilst simultaneously zooming in<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> (a similar effect can be achieved in reverse), so that the subject retains its size in the frame, but the background's perspective changes.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Hitchcock used the effect to look down the tower shaft to emphasise its height and Scottie's disorientation.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Following difficulties filming the shot on a full-sized set, a model of the tower shaft was constructed, and the dolly zoom was filmed horizontally.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The "special sequence" (Scottie's nightmare sequence) was designed by artist <a href="/wiki/John_Ferren" title="John Ferren">John Ferren</a>, who also created the painting of Carlotta used in the film.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rotating patterns in the title sequence were done by <a href="/wiki/John_Whitney_(animator)" title="John Whitney (animator)">John Whitney</a>, who used a <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_computer" title="Mechanical computer">mechanical computer</a> called the <a href="/wiki/3-inch_Gun_M5" title="3-inch Gun M5">M5 gun</a> <a href="/wiki/Director_(military)" title="Director (military)">director</a>, <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/AKA" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:AKA">AKA</a> the <a href="/wiki/Kerrison_Predictor" title="Kerrison Predictor">Kerrison Predictor</a>, which was used during World War II to aim anti-aircraft cannons at moving targets. This made it possible to produce an animated version of shapes (known as <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_curve" title="Lissajous curve">Lissajous curves</a>) based on graphs of parametric equations by mathematician <a href="/wiki/Jules_Antoine_Lissajous" title="Jules Antoine Lissajous">Jules Lissajous</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In March 1997, the cultural French magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Inrockuptibles" title="Les Inrockuptibles">Les Inrockuptibles</a></i> published a special issue titled <i>Vertigo's</i> about the film locations in San Francisco, <i>Dans le décor</i>, which lists and describes all actual locations.<sup id="cite_ref-inrockuptibles1997_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inrockuptibles1997-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costume_design">Costume design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Costume design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hitchcock and costume designer <a href="/wiki/Edith_Head" title="Edith Head">Edith Head</a> used color to heighten emotion.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Grey was chosen for Madeleine's suit because it is not usually a blonde's colour, so was psychologically jarring.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, Novak's character wore a white coat when she visited Scottie's apartment, which Head and Hitchcock considered more natural for a blonde to wear.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternate_ending">Alternate ending</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Alternate ending">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A coda to the film was shot that showed Midge at her apartment, listening to a radio report (voiced by San Francisco TV reporter <a href="/wiki/Dave_McElhatton" title="Dave McElhatton">Dave McElhatton</a>) describing the pursuit of Gavin Elster across Europe. Midge switches the radio off when Scottie enters the room. They then share a drink and look out of the window in silence. Contrary to reports that this scene was filmed to meet foreign censorship needs,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> this tag ending had originally been demanded by Geoffrey Shurlock of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Production_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Production Code">Production Code Administration</a>, who had noted: "It will, of course, be most important that the indication that Elster will be brought back for trial is sufficiently emphasized."
</p><p>Hitchcock finally succeeded in fending off most of Shurlock's demands (which included toning down erotic allusions) and had the alternative ending dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-auiler_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auiler-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The footage was discovered in Los Angeles in May 1993, and was added as an alternative ending on the <a href="/wiki/Laserdisc" class="mw-redirect" title="Laserdisc">laserdisc</a> release, and later on <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> releases.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vertigo_(film_score)" title="Vertigo (film score)">Vertigo (film score)</a></div>
<p>The score was written by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Herrmann" title="Bernard Herrmann">Bernard Herrmann</a>. It was conducted by <a href="/wiki/Muir_Mathieson" title="Muir Mathieson">Muir Mathieson</a> and recorded in Europe because there was a musicians' strike in the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-ScorseseMusic_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScorseseMusic-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2004 special issue of the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s (BFI) magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i>, director <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> described the qualities of Herrmann's famous score:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Hitchcock's film is about obsession, which means that it's about circling back to the same moment, again and again&#160;... And the music is also built around spirals and circles, fulfilment and despair. Herrmann really understood what Hitchcock was going for&#160;— he wanted to penetrate to the heart of obsession.<sup id="cite_ref-ScorseseMusic_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ScorseseMusic-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Graphic_design">Graphic design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Graphic design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Graphic designer <a href="/wiki/Saul_Bass" title="Saul Bass">Saul Bass</a> used <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_curve" title="Lissajous curve">spiral</a> motifs in both the title sequence and the movie poster, emphasizing what the documentary <i>Obsessed with Vertigo</i> calls, "<i>Vertigo</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s psychological vortex".<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Vertigo</i> premiered in San Francisco on May 9, 1958, at the Stage Door Theater at Mason and Geary (now the <a href="/wiki/Ruby_Skye" title="Ruby Skye">Ruby Skye</a> nightclub).<sup id="cite_ref-Auiler_2000_174_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Auiler_2000_174-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> While <i>Vertigo</i> did break even upon its original release,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> earning $2.8 million in <a href="/wiki/Gross_rental" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross rental">gross rental</a> in the United States alone against its $2,479,000 cost,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> it earned significantly less than other Hitchcock productions.<sup id="cite_ref-Auiler_2000_174_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Auiler_2000_174-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cinematic_restoration_release">Cinematic restoration release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Cinematic restoration release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg/260px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg" width="260" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg/390px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg/520px-Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg 2x" data-file-width="630" data-file-height="354" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vertigo_1958_trailer_Stewart_on_a_laddder.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/James_Stewart" title="James Stewart">James Stewart</a> as Johnny "Scottie" Ferguson in Midge's apartment, standing on a stepladder trying to overcome his acrophobia</div></div></div>
<p>In October 1983, <i><a href="/wiki/Rear_Window" title="Rear Window">Rear Window</a></i> and <i>Vertigo</i> were the first two films re-released by Hitchcock's estate after his death. These two films and three others&#160;– <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Knew_Too_Much_(1956_film)" title="The Man Who Knew Too Much (1956 film)">The Man Who Knew Too Much</a></i> (1956), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trouble_with_Harry" title="The Trouble with Harry">The Trouble with Harry</a></i> (1955), and <i><a href="/wiki/Rope_(film)" title="Rope (film)">Rope</a></i> (1948)&#160;– had been kept out of distribution by Hitchcock since 1968. Cleaning and restoration were performed on each film when new 35mm prints were struck.
</p><p>In 1996, the film was given a lengthy and controversial restoration by <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Harris" title="Robert A. Harris">Robert A. Harris</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_C._Katz" title="James C. Katz">James C. Katz</a> and re-released to theaters. The new print featured restored color and newly created audio, using modern sound effects mixed in <a href="/wiki/Digital_Theater_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Theater System">DTS</a> digital surround sound. In October 1996, the restored <i>Vertigo</i> premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Castro_Theatre" title="Castro Theatre">Castro Theatre</a> in San Francisco, with Kim Novak and <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Hitchcock" class="mw-redirect" title="Patricia Hitchcock">Patricia Hitchcock</a> in person. At this screening, the film was exhibited for the first time in DTS and <a href="/wiki/70_mm_film" title="70 mm film">70mm</a>, a format with a similar frame size to the <a href="/wiki/VistaVision" title="VistaVision">VistaVision</a> system in which it was originally shot. When restoring the sound, Harris and Katz wanted to stay as close as possible to the original, and had access to the original music recordings that had been stored in the vaults at Paramount. However, as the project demanded a new <a href="/wiki/DTS_(sound_system)" title="DTS (sound system)">6-channel DTS stereo soundtrack</a>, it was necessary to re-record some sound effects using the <a href="/wiki/Foley_artist" class="mw-redirect" title="Foley artist">Foley</a> process.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The soundtrack was remixed at the Alfred Hitchcock Theatre at <a href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Studios">Universal Studios</a>. Aware that the film had a considerable following, the restoration team knew that they were under particular pressure to restore the film as accurately as possible. To achieve this, they used Hitchcock's original dubbing notes for guidance of how the director wanted the film to sound in 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Harris and Katz sometimes added extra sound effects to camouflage defects in the old soundtrack ("hisses, pops, and bangs"); in particular they added extra seagull cries and a foghorn to the scene at <a href="/wiki/The_Lone_Cypress" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lone Cypress">Cypress Point</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The new mix has also been accused of putting too much emphasis on the score at the expense of the sound effects.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_media">Home media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Home media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1996, director Harrison Engle produced a documentary about the making of Hitchcock's classic, <i>Obsessed with Vertigo</i>. Narrated by <a href="/wiki/Roddy_McDowell" class="mw-redirect" title="Roddy McDowell">Roddy McDowell</a>, the film played on <a href="/wiki/American_Movie_Classics" class="mw-redirect" title="American Movie Classics">American Movie Classics</a>, and has since been included with DVD versions of <i>Vertigo</i>. Surviving members of the cast and crew participated, along with noted filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> and Alfred's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Hitchcock" class="mw-redirect" title="Patricia Hitchcock">Patricia Hitchcock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Engle first visited the <i>Vertigo</i> shooting locations in the summer of 1958, just months after completion of the film.
</p><p><i>Vertigo</i> was first released on DVD in March 1998. It was subsequently released on Blu-ray in October 2012 as part of <i>Alfred Hitchcock: The Masterpiece Collection</i>, in June 2013 as part of <i>Alfred Hitchcock: The Essentials Collection</i>, and finally in May 2014 as a stand-alone Blu-ray edition. Some of the home video releases also carry the original mono audio track.
</p><p>The 2005 <i>Hitchcock Masterpiece Collection</i> DVD contains the original mono track as an option. Significant color correction was necessary because of the fading of original <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">Technicolor</a> negatives. In some cases a new negative was created from the silver separation masters, but in many instances this was impossible because of differential separation shrinkage, and because the 1958 separations were poorly made. Separations used three individual films: one for each of the primary colors. In the case of <i>Vertigo</i>, these had shrunk in different and erratic proportions, making re-alignment impossible.<sup id="cite_ref-obsessed_13-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obsessed-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> As such, significant amounts of computer assisted coloration were necessary. Although the results are not noticeable on viewing the film, some elements were as many as eight generations away from the original negative, in particular the entire "Judy's Apartment" sequence, which is perhaps the most pivotal sequence in the entire film. When such large portions of re-creation become necessary, then the danger of artistic license by the restorers becomes an issue, and the restorers received some criticism for their re-creation of colors that allegedly did not honor the director's and cinematographer's intentions. The restoration team argued that they did research on the colors used in the original locations, cars, wardrobe, and skin tones. One breakthrough moment came when the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> supplied a well-preserved green paint sample for a car used in the film. As the use of the color green in the film has artistic importance, matching a shade of green was a stroke of luck for restoration and provided a reference shade.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2014, a new <a href="/wiki/4K_resolution" title="4K resolution">4K restoration</a> was presented at the <a href="/wiki/Castro_Theatre" title="Castro Theatre">Castro Theatre</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>. This version gives credit to Harris and Katz at the end of the film, and thanks them for providing some previously unknown stereo soundtracks. This version, however, removes some of the "excessive" Foley sound that was added in the 1996 restoration.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporaneous_reception">Contemporaneous reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Contemporaneous reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The initial reception expressed in film reviews for <i>Vertigo</i> were mixed. <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> said the film showed Hitchcock's "mastery", but was too long and slow for "what is basically only a psychological murder mystery".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> admired the scenery, but found the plot "too long" and felt it "bogs down" in "a maze of detail"; scholar Dan Auiler says that this review "sounded the tone that most popular critics would take with the film".<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> However, the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Examiner" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles Examiner">Los Angeles Examiner</a></i> loved it, admiring the "excitement, action, romance, glamor and [the] crazy, off-beat love story".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> film critic <a href="/wiki/Bosley_Crowther" title="Bosley Crowther">Bosley Crowther</a> also gave <i>Vertigo</i> a positive review by explaining that "[the] secret [of the film] is so clever, even though it is devilishly far-fetched."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_L._Coe" title="Richard L. Coe">Richard L. Coe</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> praised the film as a "wonderful weirdie," writing that "Hitchcock has even more fun than usual with trick angles, floor shots and striking use of color. More than once he gives us critical scenes in long shots establishing how he's going to get away with a couple of story tricks."<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_McCarten" title="John McCarten">John McCarten</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> panned the film, writing that Hitchcock "has never before indulged in such farfetched nonsense."<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Contemporaneous response in England was summarized by Charles Barr in his monograph on <i>Vertigo</i> stating: "In England, the reception was if anything rather less friendly. Of the 28 newspaper and magazine reviews that I have looked at, six are, with reservations, favourable, nine are very mixed, and 13 almost wholly negative. Common to all of these reviews is a lack of sympathy with the basic structure and drive of the picture. Even the friendlier ones single out for praise elements that seem, from today's perspective, to be marginal virtues and incidental pleasures – the 'vitality' of the supporting performances (Dilys Powell in <i>The Sunday Times</i>), the slickness with which the car sequences are put together (Isobel Quibley in <i>The Spectator</i>)".<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additional reasons for the mixed response initially were that Hitchcock fans were not pleased with his departure from the romantic-thriller territory of earlier films and that the mystery was solved with one-third of the film left to go.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a> disliked the film, telling his friend, director <a href="/wiki/Henry_Jaglom" title="Henry Jaglom">Henry Jaglom</a>, that the movie was "worse" than <i><a href="/wiki/Rear_Window" title="Rear Window">Rear Window</a></i>, another film that Welles disliked.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> In an interview with <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a>, Hitchcock stated that <i>Vertigo</i> was one of his favourite films, with some reservations.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Hitchcock blamed the film's failure on the 49-year-old Stewart looking too old to play a convincing love interest for the 24-year-old Kim Novak.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> At the time of the film's release on May 9, 1958, Novak had turned 25 the previous February, and Stewart would turn 50 some 11 days later, on May 20, 1958.
</p><p>Hitchcock and Stewart received awards at the <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_International_Film_Festival" title="San Sebastián International Film Festival">San Sebastián International Film Festival</a>, including a Silver Seashell for Best Director (tied with <a href="/wiki/Mario_Monicelli" title="Mario Monicelli">Mario Monicelli</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Big_Deal_on_Madonna_Street" title="Big Deal on Madonna Street">Big Deal on Madonna Street</a></i> (aka <i>Persons Unknown</i>) and Best Actor (also tied, with <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Douglas" title="Kirk Douglas">Kirk Douglas</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Vikings_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Vikings (film)">The Vikings</a></i>). The film was nominated for two <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>, in the technical categories<sup id="cite_ref-NY_Times_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Art_Direction" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award for Best Art Direction">Best Art Direction – Black-and-White or Color</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hal_Pereira" title="Hal Pereira">Hal Pereira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Bumstead" title="Henry Bumstead">Henry Bumstead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_M._Comer" title="Samuel M. Comer">Samuel M. Comer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_McKelvy" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank McKelvy">Frank McKelvy</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Sound" class="mw-redirect" title="Academy Award for Sound">Best Sound</a> (<a href="/wiki/George_Dutton" title="George Dutton">George Dutton</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Oscars1959_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oscars1959-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-evaluation">Re-evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertigo_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Re-evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 21st century and starting with the 2002 <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> polls, <i>Vertigo</i> was ranked just behind <i><a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">Citizen Kane</a></i> (1941) as the best film ever made; ten years later, in the same magazine, it was voted by critics as the best film ever made.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Already in the 1960s, the French <i><a href="/wiki/Cahiers_du_cin%C3%A9ma" title="Cahiers du cinéma">Cahiers du cinéma</a></i> critics began re-evaluating Hitchcock as a serious artist rather than just a populist showman. However, even <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Truffaut" title="François Truffaut">François Truffaut</a>'s important 1962 book of interviews with Hitchcock (not published in English until 1967) devotes only a few pages to <i>Vertigo</i>. Dan Auiler has suggested that the real beginning of <i>Vertigo</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s rise in adulation was the British-Canadian scholar <a href="/wiki/Robin_Wood_(critic)" title="Robin Wood (critic)">Robin Wood</a>'s <i>Hitchcock's Films</i> (1968), which calls the film "Hitchcock's masterpiece to date and one of the four or five most profound and beautiful films the cinema has yet given us".<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Adding to its mystique was the fact that <i>Vertigo</i> was one of five Hitchcock-owned films removed from circulation in 1973. When <i>Vertigo</i> was re-released in theaters in October 1983, and then on home video in October 1984, it achieved an impressive commercial success and laudatory reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Similarly adulatory reviews were written for the October 1996 showing of a restored print in <a href="/wiki/70mm" class="mw-redirect" title="70mm">70mm</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_Theater_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Theater System">DTS</a> sound at the <a href="/wiki/Castro_Theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Castro Theater">Castro Theater</a> in San Francisco.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1989, <i>Vertigo</i> was recognized as a "culturally, historically and aesthetically significant" film by the United States <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> and selected for preservation in the <a href="/wiki/National_Film_Registry" title="National Film Registry">National Film Registry</a> in the first year of the registry's voting.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Among international film critics, the film has experienced a similar re-evaluation. Every ten years since 1952, the <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s film magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> has asked the world's leading film critics to compile a list of the 10 best films of all time. Not until 1982 did <i>Vertigo</i> enter the list, and then in 7th place.<sup id="cite_ref-BFI1982_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI1982-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> By 1992 it had advanced to 4th place,<sup id="cite_ref-BFI1992_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI1992-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> by 2002 to 2nd.<sup id="cite_ref-BFI2002_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI2002-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> <i>Vertigo</i> was voted in first place in <i>Sight &amp; Sound</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s 2012 poll of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_films_considered_the_best" title="List of films considered the best">greatest films of all time</a>, both in the crime genre and in general, displacing <a href="/wiki/Orson_Welles" title="Orson Welles">Orson Welles</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Citizen_Kane" title="Citizen Kane">Citizen Kane</a></i> from the position it had occupied since 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BFI2012_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI2012-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Commenting upon the 2012 results, the magazine's editor Nick James said that <i>Vertigo</i> was "the ultimate critics' film. It is a dream-like film about people who are not sure who they are but who are busy reconstructing themselves and each other to fit a kind of cinema ideal of the ideal soul-mate."<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In recent years, critics have noted that the casting of <a href="/wiki/James_Stewart" title="James Stewart">James Stewart</a> as a character who becomes disturbed and obsessive ultimately enhances the film's unconventionality and effectiveness as suspense, since Stewart had previously been known as an actor of warmhearted roles.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A small minority of critics have expressed dissenting opinions. In his 2004 book <i><a href="/wiki/Blockbuster_(2004_book)" class="mw-redirect" title="Blockbuster (2004 book)">Blockbuster</a></i>, British film critic <a href="/wiki/Tom_Shone" title="Tom Shone">Tom Shone</a> suggested that <i>Vertigo</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s critical re-evaluation has led to excessive praise, and argued for a more measured response. Faulting <i><a href="/wiki/Sight_%26_Sound" title="Sight &amp; Sound">Sight &amp; Sound</a></i> for "perennially" putting the film on the list of best-ever films, he wrote, "Hitchcock is a director who delights in getting his plot mechanisms buffed up to a nice humming shine, and so the <i>Sight and Sound</i> team praise the one film of his in which this is not the case&#160;– it's all loose ends and lopsided angles, its plumbing out on display for the critic to pick over at his leisure."<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, poet and critic <a href="/wiki/Dan_Schneider_(writer)" title="Dan Schneider (writer)">Dan Schneider</a> criticized the ending of <i>Vertigo</i> as <a href="/wiki/Melodrama" title="Melodrama">melodramatic</a> and argued that a close examination of the film's plot reveals numerous implausibilities, such as Elster allowing someone who knew his murder plot to remain living and thus possibly reveal the plan, or the police officers at the crime scene not inspecting the tower for evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, <i>Vertigo</i> came in second (to <i><a href="/wiki/Goodfellas" title="Goodfellas">Goodfellas</a></i>) in British magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Film" title="Total Film">Total Film</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;s</span> book <i>100 Greatest Movies of All Time</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> In 2008, an <i><a href="/wiki/Empire_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Empire (magazine)">Empire</a></i> poll of readers, actors, and critics named it the 40th greatest movie ever made.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/American_Film_Institute" title="American Film Institute">American Film Institute</a> recognition</b>

<p>The San Francisco locations have become celebrated amongst the film's fans, with organised tours across the area.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> In March 1997, the cultural French magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Les_Inrockuptibles" title="Les Inrockuptibles">Les Inrockuptibles</a></i> published a special issue about <i>Vertigo's</i> locations in San Francisco, <i>Dans le décor</i>, which lists and describes all actual locations.<sup id="cite_ref-inrockuptibles1997_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inrockuptibles1997-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In October 1996, the restored print of <i>Vertigo</i> debuted at the <a href="/wiki/Castro_Theatre" title="Castro Theatre">Castro Theatre</a> in San Francisco with a live on-stage introduction by Kim Novak, providing the city a chance to celebrate itself.<sup id="cite_ref-Kraft_2002_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kraft_2002-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Director <a href="/wiki/Martin_Scorsese" title="Martin Scorsese">Martin Scorsese</a> has listed <i>Vertigo</i> as one of his favorite films of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
