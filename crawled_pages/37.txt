<p>The process of <b>weighting</b> involves emphasizing the contribution of some aspects of a phenomenon (or of a set of <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>) to a final effect or result, giving them more weight in the <a href="/wiki/Analysis" title="Analysis">analysis</a>. That is, rather than each variable in the data contributing equally to the final result, some data are adjusted to contribute more than others. It is analogous to the practice of adding extra <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> to one side of a pair of scales to favour a buyer or seller.
</p><p>While weighting may be applied to a set of data, such as epidemiological data, it is more commonly applied to measurements of light, heat, sound, <a href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Gamma radiation">gamma radiation</a>, in fact any stimulus that is spread over a <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">spectrum</a> of frequencies.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weighting_and_loudness">Weighting and loudness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Weighting and loudness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the measurement of <a href="/wiki/Loudness" title="Loudness">loudness</a>, for example, a <a href="/wiki/Weighting_filter" title="Weighting filter">weighting filter</a> is commonly used to emphasise frequencies around 3 to 6&#160;kHz where the human ear is most sensitive, while attenuating very high and very low frequencies to which the ear is insensitive. A commonly used weighting is the <a href="/wiki/A-weighting" title="A-weighting">A-weighting</a> curve, which results in units of dBA sound pressure level. Because the frequency response of human hearing varies with loudness, the A-weighting curve is correct only at a level of 40-<a href="/wiki/Phon" title="Phon">phon</a> and other curves known as B, C and D weighting are also used, the latter being particularly intended for the measurement of aircraft noise.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weighting_in_audio_measurement">Weighting in audio measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Weighting in audio measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In broadcasting and audio equipment measurements <a href="/wiki/468-weighting" class="mw-redirect" title="468-weighting">468-weighting</a> is the preferred weighting to use because it was specifically devised to allow subjectively valid measurements on noise, rather than pure tones. It is often not realised that equal loudness curves, and hence A-weighting, really apply only to tones, as tests with <a href="/wiki/Noise_band" class="mw-redirect" title="Noise band">noise bands</a> show increased sensitivity in the 5 to 7&#160;kHz region on noise compared to tones.
</p><p>Other weighting curves are used in <a href="/wiki/Rumble_measurement" class="mw-redirect" title="Rumble measurement">rumble measurement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flutter_measurement" class="mw-redirect" title="Flutter measurement">flutter measurement</a> to properly assess subjective effect.
</p><p>In each field of measurement, special units are used to indicate a weighted measurement as opposed to a basic physical measurement of energy level. For sound, the unit is the <a href="/wiki/Phon" title="Phon">phon</a> (1 <a href="/wiki/KHz" class="mw-redirect" title="KHz">kHz</a> equivalent level).
</p><p>In the fields of acoustics and audio engineering, it is common to use a standard curve referred to as <a href="/wiki/A-weighting" title="A-weighting">A-weighting</a>, one of a set that are said to be derived from <a href="/wiki/Equal-loudness_contours" class="mw-redirect" title="Equal-loudness contours">equal-loudness contours</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weighting_and_gamma_rays">Weighting and gamma rays</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weighting and gamma rays">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the measurement of gamma rays or other ionising radiation, a radiation monitor or dosimeter will commonly use a filter to attenuate those energy levels or wavelengths that cause the least damage to the human body but letting through those that do the most damage, so any source of radiation may be measured in terms of its true danger rather than just its strength. The resulting unit is the <a href="/wiki/Sievert" title="Sievert">sievert</a> or microsievert.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weighting_and_television_colour_components">Weighting and television colour components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Weighting and television colour components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Another use of weighting is in television, in which the red, green and blue components of the signal are weighted according to their perceived brightness. This ensures compatibility with black and white receivers and also benefits noise performance and allows separation into meaningful <a href="/wiki/Luminance_(video)" class="mw-redirect" title="Luminance (video)">luminance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chrominance" title="Chrominance">chrominance</a> signals for transmission.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weighting_and_UV_factor_derivation_for_sun_exposure">Weighting and UV factor derivation for sun exposure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Weighting and UV factor derivation for sun exposure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Skin damage due to sun exposure is very wavelength dependent over the UV range 295 to 325&#160;nm, with power at the shorter wavelength causing around 30 times as much damage as the longer one. In the calculation of <a href="/wiki/UV_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="UV Index">UV Index</a>, a weighting curve is used which is known as the McKinlay-Diffey Erythema action spectrum.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://serc.si.edu/labs/photobiology/UVIndex_calculation.aspx">[1]</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Weighting_in_3D_modeling_and_animation">Weighting in 3D modeling and animation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Weighting in 3D modeling and animation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Weighting in the context of 3D modeling and animation refers to how closely the components of a soft body follow their "target", "guide", "goal", or "controller". Components (usually vertices) with higher weights follow (or "conform" to) their guide quite closely, but those with lower weights do not. Here are some examples:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skeleton_structure">Skeleton structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Skeleton structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Often, when an object is rigged with a skeleton, weights are applied to the vertices near the joints. The vertices closer to the joint will usually have a lower weight assigned; the reason is so that during deformation, the geometry of the skin does not fold in on itself. Weighting in this situation will most of the time be done automatically using skinning techniques but is often done by hand fine tune the skeleton's deformation effects.
</p><p>Another example of using weights with a skeleton structure would be to actually apply weights to vertices that are not part of a character's skin. An appropriate situation for this method would be the case when an elephant's trunk dangles freely as it walks. The elephant's trunk is rigged with a skeleton and inverse kinematic (IK) curve. Then, a goal curve is created so that the IK curve has something to "aim for" and thus the trunk has a certain shape to which it conforms.
</p><p>Closer to the end of the trunk, the vertices of the IK curve are given lower weights. Thus, for example, if the goal curve was parented to the elephant and a gravity field was in effect, if the elephant stopped walking, the end of the IK curve and, thus, the end of the trunk would continue to move, dangle back and forth, ultimately coming to a rest in the position determined by the goal curve.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloth">Cloth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weighting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cloth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Weights are used with cloth as well. For instance, if a character was being outfitted with a dress, around the waist, the cloth should stay attached to the character, but farther down, its movements should be governed more by effects directly induced by the neighbouring cloth vertices than by the hip movements of the character.
</p><p>Higher weights are then applied to the cloth vertices close to the hip so that they hug the character quite closely compared to the cloth farther down the dress. To make the dress more realistic, it is possible to assign cloth weights of even 0 to much of the dress because springs hold the cloth together; one must keep in mind, however, that doing this for the entire dress would effectively cancel the attachment of the dress to the character.
</p>
