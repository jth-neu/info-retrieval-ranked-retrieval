<p>The <b>Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</b> (sometimes colloquially referred to by the <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a> <i><b>SCOTUK</b></i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme court</a> in all matters under <a href="/wiki/English_and_Welsh_law" class="mw-redirect" title="English and Welsh law">English and Welsh law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Irish_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Irish law">Northern Irish law</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scots_law" title="Scots law">Scottish</a> <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(common_law)" title="Civil law (common law)">civil law</a>. It is the court of last resort and the highest <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">appellate court</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, although the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a> remains the court of last resort for <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal law</a> in Scotland (except in respect of devolution and human rights issues). The Supreme Court also has <a href="#Jurisdiction_and_powers">jurisdiction</a> to resolve disputes relating to <a href="/wiki/Devolution_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Devolution in the United Kingdom">devolution in the United Kingdom</a> and concerning the legal powers of the three devolved governments (in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland) or laws made by the devolved legislatures.
</p><p>The Supreme Court was established by Part 3 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a> and started work on 1 October 2009. It assumed the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">judicial functions of the House of Lords</a>, which had been exercised by the <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" title="Lords of Appeal in Ordinary">Lords of Appeal in Ordinary</a> (commonly called "Law Lords"), the 12 judges appointed as members of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> to carry out its judicial business. Its jurisdiction over devolution matters had previously been <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council#Domestic_jurisdiction" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">exercised</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>.
</p><p>The United Kingdom has a doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">parliamentary sovereignty</a>, so the Supreme Court is much more limited in its powers of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a> than the constitutional or supreme courts of some other countries. It cannot overturn any <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>. However, it can overturn <a href="/wiki/Secondary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary legislation">secondary legislation</a> if, for example, that legislation is found to be <i><a href="/wiki/Ultra_vires" title="Ultra vires">ultra vires</a></i> to the powers in primary legislation allowing it to be made. Further, under section 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a>, the Supreme Court, like some other courts in the United Kingdom, may make a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_incompatibility" title="Declaration of incompatibility">declaration of incompatibility</a>, indicating that it believes that the legislation subject to the declaration is incompatible with one of the rights in the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>. Such a declaration can apply to primary or secondary legislation. The legislation is not overturned by the declaration, and neither Parliament nor the government is required to agree with any such declaration. However, if they do accept a declaration, ministers can exercise powers under <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998#Structure_of_the_Act" title="Human Rights Act 1998">section 10 of the act</a> to amend the legislation by statutory instrument to remove the incompatibility or ask Parliament to amend the legislation.
</p><p>The current President of the Supreme Court is <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Hale,_Baroness_Hale_of_Richmond" title="Brenda Hale, Baroness Hale of Richmond">Baroness Hale of Richmond</a>, and its Deputy President is <a href="/wiki/Robert_Reed,_Lord_Reed" title="Robert Reed, Lord Reed">Lord Reed</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The creation of a Supreme Court for the United Kingdom was first mooted in a consultation paper published by the Department of Constitutional Affairs in July 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-DCA_paper_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCA_paper-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Although the paper noted that there had been no criticism of the then-current Law Lords or any indication of an actual bias, it argued that the separation of the <a href="/wiki/Judicial" class="mw-redirect" title="Judicial">judicial</a> functions of the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Judicial Committee of the House of Lords">Judicial Committee of the House of Lords</a> from the legislative functions of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> should be made explicit. The paper noted the following concerns:
</p>
<ol><li>Whether there was any longer sufficient transparency of independence from the executive and the legislature to give assurance of the independence of the judiciary.<sup id="cite_ref-DCA_paper_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCA_paper-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The requirement for the appearance of impartiality and independence limited the ability of the Law Lords to contribute to the work of the House itself, thus reducing the value to both them and the House of their membership.<sup id="cite_ref-DCA_paper_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCA_paper-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>It was not always understood by the public that judicial decisions of "the House of Lords" were in fact taken by the Judicial Committee and that non-judicial members were never involved in the judgments. Conversely, it was felt that the extent to which the Law Lords themselves had decided to refrain from getting involved in political issues in relation to legislation on which they might later have had to adjudicate was not always appreciated.<sup id="cite_ref-DCA_paper_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCA_paper-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The new President of the Court, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Phillips,_Baron_Phillips_of_Worth_Matravers" title="Nick Phillips, Baron Phillips of Worth Matravers">Lord Phillips of Worth Matravers</a>, has claimed that the old system had confused people and that with the Supreme Court there would for the first time be a clear <a href="/wiki/Separation_of_powers" title="Separation of powers">separation of powers</a> among the judiciary, the legislature and the executive.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Space within the House of Lords was at a constant premium and a separate supreme court would ease the pressure on the Palace of Westminster.<sup id="cite_ref-DCA_paper_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DCA_paper-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>The main argument against a new Supreme Court was that the previous system had worked well and kept costs down.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
Reformers expressed concern that this second main example of a mixture of the legislative, judicial and executive might conflict with professed values under the <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a>. Officials who make or execute laws have an interest in court cases that put those laws to the test. When the state invests judicial authority in those officials or even their day-to-day colleagues, it puts the independence and impartiality of the courts at risk. Consequently, it was hypothesised closely connected decisions of the Law Lords to debates had by friends or on which the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> had expressed a view might be challenged on Human Rights grounds on the basis that they had not constituted a fair trial.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Neuberger,_Baron_Neuberger_of_Abbotsbury" title="David Neuberger, Baron Neuberger of Abbotsbury">Lord Neuberger of Abbotsbury</a>, later President of the Supreme Court, expressed fear that the new court could make itself more powerful than the House of Lords committee it succeeded, saying that there is a real risk of "judges arrogating to themselves greater power than they have at the moment". <a href="/wiki/Nick_Phillips,_Baron_Phillips_of_Worth_Matravers" title="Nick Phillips, Baron Phillips of Worth Matravers">Lord Phillips</a> said such an outcome was "a possibility", but was "unlikely".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The reforms were controversial and were brought forward with little consultation but were subsequently extensively debated in Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> During 2004, a select committee of the House of Lords scrutinised the arguments for and against setting up a new court.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Government estimated the set-up cost of the Supreme Court at  £56.9&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Supreme Court was established by Part 3 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a> and started work on 1 October 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-Enactment_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enactment-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Commencement_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Commencement-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It assumed the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">judicial functions of the House of Lords</a>, which had been exercised by the <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" title="Lords of Appeal in Ordinary">Lords of Appeal in Ordinary</a> (commonly called "Law Lords"), the 12 professional judges appointed as members of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> to carry out its judicial business. Its jurisdiction over devolution matters had previously been exercised by the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>.
</p><p>The first case heard by the Supreme Court was <i><a href="/wiki/HM_Treasury_v_Ahmed" title="HM Treasury v Ahmed">HM Treasury v Ahmed</a></i>, which concerned "the separation of powers", according to Phillips, its inaugural President. At issue was the extent to which Parliament has, by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Act_1946" title="United Nations Act 1946">United Nations Act 1946</a>, delegated to the executive the power to legislate. Resolution of this issue depended upon the approach properly to be adopted by the court in interpreting legislation which may affect fundamental rights at common law or under the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>.
</p><p>Because of the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_sovereignty" title="Parliamentary sovereignty">parliamentary sovereignty</a>, the Supreme Court is much more limited in its powers of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a> than the constitutional or supreme courts of some other countries. It cannot overturn any <a href="/wiki/Primary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary legislation">primary legislation</a> made by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, it can overturn <a href="/wiki/Secondary_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secondary legislation">secondary legislation</a> if, for example, that legislation is found to be <i><a href="/wiki/Ultra_vires" title="Ultra vires">ultra vires</a></i> to the powers in primary legislation allowing it to be made. Further, under section 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a>, the Supreme Court, like some other courts in the United Kingdom, may make a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_incompatibility" title="Declaration of incompatibility">declaration of incompatibility</a>, indicating that it believes that the legislation subject to the declaration is incompatible with one of the rights in the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Such a declaration can apply to primary or secondary legislation. The legislation is not overturned by the declaration, and neither Parliament nor the government is required to agree with any such declaration. However, if they do accept a declaration, ministers can exercise powers under <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998#Structure_of_the_Act" title="Human Rights Act 1998">section 10 of the act</a> to amend the legislation by statutory instrument to remove the incompatibility or ask Parliament to amend the legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The current President of the Supreme Court is <a href="/wiki/Brenda_Hale,_Baroness_Hale_of_Richmond" title="Brenda Hale, Baroness Hale of Richmond">Baroness Hale of Richmond</a>, and its Deputy President is <a href="/wiki/Robert_Reed,_Lord_Reed" title="Robert Reed, Lord Reed">Lord Reed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The main role of the Supreme Court is to hear appeals from the courts in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a> and also from the courts in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and also appeals from the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a> in Scotland regarding civil (but not criminal-law) cases or matters.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For Scottish civil cases decided prior to September 2015, permission to appeal from the Court of Session was not required and any such case can proceed to the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom if two <a href="/wiki/Faculty_of_Advocates" title="Faculty of Advocates">Advocates</a> certify that an appeal is suitable.  The entry into force of the Courts Reform (Scotland) Act 2014 has essentially brought the procedure for current and future Scottish civil cases into line with England, Wales and Northern Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> where permission to appeal is required, either from the Court of Appeal or from a <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Justice of the Supreme Court</a> itself.
</p><p>The Supreme Court's focus is on cases that raise points of law of general public importance. As with the former <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">Appellate Committee of the House of Lords</a>, appeals from many fields of law are likely to be selected for hearing, including commercial disputes, family matters, judicial review claims against public authorities and issues under the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a>. The Supreme Court also hears some criminal appeals, but not from Scotland, as there is no general right of appeal from the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a>, Scotland's highest criminal court, other than with respect to devolution issues.
</p><p>The Supreme Court also determines "devolution issues" (as defined by the <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Act_1998" title="Scotland Act 1998">Scotland Act 1998</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Act_1998" title="Northern Ireland Act 1998">Northern Ireland Act 1998</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a>). These are legal proceedings about the powers of the three <a href="/wiki/Devolved" class="mw-redirect" title="Devolved">devolved</a> administrations—the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Government" title="Welsh Government">Welsh Government</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>. Devolution issues were previously heard by the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council and most are about compliance with rights under the <a href="/wiki/European_Convention_on_Human_Rights" title="European Convention on Human Rights">European Convention on Human Rights</a>, brought into national law by the Devolution Acts and the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a>.
</p><p>Ordinarily, all twelve justices do not all hear every case. Typically a case is heard by a panel of five justices, though sometimes the panel may consist of three, seven or nine members. The justices are also members of the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> and spend some of their time in that capacity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Supreme Court has a separate administration from the other courts of the United Kingdom, with its administration is under a Chief Executive who is appointed by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="President of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">President of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_supreme_courts_in_the_United_Kingdom">Other supreme courts in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other supreme courts in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Accountant_of_Court" title="Office of the Accountant of Court">Office of the Accountant of Court</a> comprise the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Justice" title="College of Justice">College of Justice</a>, and are known as the Supreme Courts of Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before 1 October 2009 there were two other courts known as supreme court, namely the Supreme Court of England and Wales (known as the Supreme Court of Judicature until 1981), which was created in the 1870s under the <a href="/wiki/Judicature_Acts" title="Judicature Acts">Judicature Acts</a>, and the Supreme Court of Judicature in Northern Ireland, each of which consisted of a <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a>, a <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a>. When the provisions of the Constitutional Reform Act 2005 came into force these became known as the <a href="/wiki/Senior_Courts_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior Courts of England and Wales">Senior Courts of England and Wales</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Judicature_of_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Supreme Court of Judicature of Northern Ireland">Court of Judicature of Northern Ireland</a> respectively.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> also retains <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council#Jurisdiction" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">jurisdiction</a> over certain matters.  The <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">judicial functions of the House of Lords</a> have all been abolished, other than the trial of <a href="/wiki/Impeachment" title="Impeachment">impeachments</a>, a procedure which has been obsolete for 200 years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Justices">Justices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Justices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>The court is composed of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="President of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">President</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deputy_President_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Deputy President of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Deputy President</a> and ten <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Justices of the Supreme Court</a>. They are not subject to term limits but may be removed from office on the address of Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Like all British judges, Supreme Court justices are obliged to retire at age 70 if first appointed to a judicial office after 31 March 1995 or at age 75 otherwise.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The President and Deputy President of the court are separately appointed to those roles.
</p><p>Ten <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord of Appeal in Ordinary">Lords of Appeal in Ordinary</a> (Law Lords) holding office on 1 October 2009 became the first <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">justices</a> of the 12-member Supreme Court.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The 11th place on the Supreme Court was filled by <a href="/wiki/Tony_Clarke,_Baron_Clarke_of_Stone-cum-Ebony" title="Tony Clarke, Baron Clarke of Stone-cum-Ebony">Lord Clarke of Stone-cum-Ebony</a> (formerly the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Rolls" title="Master of the Rolls">Master of the Rolls</a>), who was the first Justice to be appointed directly to the Supreme Court.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> One of the former Law Lords, <a href="/wiki/David_Neuberger,_Baron_Neuberger_of_Abbotsbury" title="David Neuberger, Baron Neuberger of Abbotsbury">Lord Neuberger of Abbotsbury</a>, was appointed to replace Clarke as Master of the Rolls,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> and so did not move to the new court. <a href="/wiki/John_Dyson,_Lord_Dyson" title="John Dyson, Lord Dyson">Lord (formerly Sir John) Dyson</a> became the 12th and final justice of the Supreme Court on 13 April 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> granted Justices who are not peers use of the title Lord or Lady, by <a href="/wiki/Royal_warrant_(document)" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal warrant (document)">warrant</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Royal_sign-manual" title="Royal sign-manual">royal sign-manual</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Gazette_Lord_Warrant_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gazette_Lord_Warrant-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Senior Law Lord on 1 October 2009, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Phillips,_Baron_Phillips_of_Worth_Matravers" title="Nick Phillips, Baron Phillips of Worth Matravers">Lord Phillips of Worth Matravers</a>, became the Supreme Court's first President,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and the Second Senior Law Lord, <a href="/wiki/David_Hope,_Baron_Hope_of_Craighead" title="David Hope, Baron Hope of Craighead">Lord Hope of Craighead</a>, became the first Deputy President.
</p><p>On 30 September 2010 <a href="/wiki/Mark_Saville,_Baron_Saville_of_Newdigate" title="Mark Saville, Baron Saville of Newdigate">Lord Saville of Newdigate</a> became the first Justice to retire,<sup id="cite_ref-sav-ret_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sav-ret-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> followed by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Collins,_Baron_Collins_of_Mapesbury" title="Lawrence Collins, Baron Collins of Mapesbury">Lord Collins of Mapesbury</a> on 7 May 2011, although the latter remained as an acting Justice until the end of July 2011.
</p><p>In June 2011 <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rodger,_Baron_Rodger_of_Earlsferry" title="Alan Rodger, Baron Rodger of Earlsferry">Lord Rodger of Earlsferry</a> became the first Justice to die in office after a short illness.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_judges">Acting judges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Acting judges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the ten permanent <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Justices</a>, the President may request other senior judges drawn from two groups to sit as "acting judges" of the Supreme Court.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The first group are those judges who hold 'office as a senior territorial judge': judges of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal of England and Wales</a>, judges of the <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Courts of Northern Ireland">Court of Appeal of Northern Ireland</a> and judges of the First or Second Division of the <a href="/wiki/Inner_House" title="Inner House">Inner House</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a> in Scotland.</li>
<li>The second group are known as the 'supplementary panel'. The President may approve in writing retired senior judges' membership of this panel if they are under 75 years of age (a system similar to <a href="/wiki/Senior_status" title="Senior status">senior status</a> in the United States <a href="/wiki/United_States_courts_of_appeal" class="mw-redirect" title="United States courts of appeal">Federal Courts of Appeal</a>).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Appointment_process">Appointment process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Appointment process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a> makes provision for a new appointment process for Justices of the Supreme Court. A selection commission is to be formed when vacancies arise. This is to be composed of the President and Deputy President of the Supreme Court and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Appointments_Commission" title="Judicial Appointments Commission">Judicial Appointments Commission</a> of England and Wales, the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Appointments_Board_for_Scotland" title="Judicial Appointments Board for Scotland">Judicial Appointments Board for Scotland</a> and the Northern Ireland Judicial Appointments Commission. In October 2007, the Ministry of Justice announced that this appointment process would be adopted on a voluntary basis for appointments of <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" title="Lords of Appeal in Ordinary">Lords of Appeal in Ordinary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The commission selects one person for the vacancy and notifies the Lord Chancellor of its choice.  The Lord Chancellor then either
</p>
<ul><li>approves the commission's selection</li>
<li>rejects the commission's selection, or</li>
<li>asks the commission to reconsider its selection.</li></ul>
<p>If the Lord Chancellor approves the person selected by the commission, the Prime Minister must then recommend that person to the Monarch for appointment.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>New judges appointed to the Supreme Court after its creation will not necessarily receive peerages; however, they are given the <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_titles_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Courtesy titles in the United Kingdom">courtesy title</a> of Lord or Lady upon appointment.<sup id="cite_ref-Gazette_Lord_Warrant_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gazette_Lord_Warrant-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The President and Deputy President are appointed to those roles rather than being the most senior by tenure in office.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building">Building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_Guildhall" title="Middlesex Guildhall">Middlesex Guildhall</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom,_Court_1_Interior,_London,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom%2C_Court_1_Interior%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/220px-Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom%2C_Court_1_Interior%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom%2C_Court_1_Interior%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/330px-Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom%2C_Court_1_Interior%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom%2C_Court_1_Interior%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/440px-Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom%2C_Court_1_Interior%2C_London%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7289" data-file-height="5137" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom,_Court_1_Interior,_London,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Court 1 in the Supreme Court building</div></div></div>
<p>The court is housed in <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_Guildhall" title="Middlesex Guildhall">Middlesex Guildhall</a> – which it shares with the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a> – in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a> gave time for a suitable building to be found and fitted out before the Law Lords moved out of the <a href="/wiki/Houses_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Houses of Parliament">Houses of Parliament</a>, where they had previously used a series of rooms in the Palace of Westminster.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a lengthy survey of suitable sites, including <a href="/wiki/Somerset_House" title="Somerset House">Somerset House</a>, the Government announced that the new court would be at the <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_Guildhall" title="Middlesex Guildhall">Middlesex Guildhall</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Square" title="Parliament Square">Parliament Square</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">Westminster</a>. That decision was examined by the Constitutional Affairs Committee,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and the grant of planning permission by <a href="/wiki/Westminster_City_Council" title="Westminster City Council">Westminster City Council</a> for refurbishment works was challenged in a <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review_in_English_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Judicial review in English Law">judicial review</a> by the conservation group <a href="/wiki/SAVE_Britain%27s_Heritage" title="SAVE Britain&#39;s Heritage">SAVE Britain's Heritage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> It was also reported that <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> had been put under great pressure to approve the alterations.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Feilden + Mawson, supported by Foster &amp; Partners, were the appointed architects.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The building had been used as the Middlesex <a href="/wiki/Quarter_Sessions" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter Sessions">Quarter Sessions</a> House, adding later its <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County_Council" title="Middlesex County Council">County Council</a> chamber, and lastly as a <a href="/wiki/Crown_Court" title="Crown Court">Crown Court</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Badge">Badge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Badge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg/170px-UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg.png" width="170" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg/255px-UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg/340px-UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Supreme_Court_badge_2.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The emblem with stylised depictions of the four floral emblems.</div></div></div>
<p>The official badge of the Supreme Court was granted by the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Arms" title="College of Arms">College of Arms</a> in October 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-collegeofarms_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collegeofarms-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> It comprises both the Greek letter <a href="/wiki/Omega" title="Omega">omega</a> (representing finality) and the symbol of <a href="/wiki/Libra_(astrology)" title="Libra (astrology)">Libra</a> (symbolising the scales of justice), in addition to the four <a href="/wiki/Floral_emblem#United_Kingdom" title="Floral emblem">floral emblems of the United Kingdom</a>: a <a href="/wiki/Tudor_rose" title="Tudor rose">Tudor rose</a>, representing England, conjoined with the leaves of a <a href="/wiki/Leek" title="Leek">leek</a>, representing Wales; a <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> blossom for Northern Ireland; and a <a href="/wiki/Thistle" title="Thistle">thistle</a>, representing Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two adapted versions of its official badge are used by the Supreme Court. One features the words "The Supreme Court" and the letter omega in black (in the official badge granted by the College of Arms, the interior of the Latin and Greek letters are gold and white, respectively), and displays a simplified version of the crown (also in black) and larger, stylised versions of the floral emblems; this modified version of the badge is featured on the new Supreme Court website,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> as well as in the forms that will be used by the Supreme Court.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> A further variant omits the crown entirely and is featured prominently throughout the building.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another emblem is formed from a more abstract set of depictions of the four floral emblems and is used in the carpets of the Middlesex Guildhall designed by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Sir Peter Blake</a>, creator of such works as the cover of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' 1967 album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sgt._Pepper%27s_Lonely_Hearts_Club_Band" title="Sgt. Pepper&#39;s Lonely Hearts Club Band">Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
