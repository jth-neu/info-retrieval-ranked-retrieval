<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">The Crown</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the term referring to the state in some nations. For the Netflix television series, see <a href="/wiki/The_Crown_(TV_series)" title="The Crown (TV series)">The Crown (TV series)</a>. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Crown_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Crown (disambiguation)">Crown</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg/220px-Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg/330px-Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg/440px-Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Queen_Elizabeth_II_of_New_Zealand.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> is the living embodiment of the Crown in each of her realms</div></div></div>
<p><b>The Crown</b> is the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> in all its aspects within the <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">jurisprudence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a> and their sub-divisions (such as <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada#Provinces" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">provinces</a>, or states). Depending on the context, the term may refer to the state<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> or to the reigning monarch.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Corporation_sole" title="Corporation sole">corporation sole</a>, the Crown is the legal embodiment of executive, <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislative</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Judiciary" title="Judiciary">judicial</a> governance in the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">monarchy</a> of each country. These monarchies are united by the <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal union</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a>, but they are independent states. The concept of the Crown developed first in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> as a separation of the literal crown and property of the <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation state</a> from the person and personal property of the monarch. It spread through English and later <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British colonisation</a> and is now rooted in the legal lexicon of the United Kingdom, its Crown dependencies, and the other 15 independent realms. It is not to be confused with any physical crown, such as those of the <a href="/wiki/British_regalia" class="mw-redirect" title="British regalia">British regalia</a>.
</p><p>The term is also found in expressions such as "<a href="/wiki/Crown_land" title="Crown land">Crown land</a>", which some countries refer to as "public land" or "state land"; as well as in some offices, such as <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_the_Crown" title="Minister of the Crown">Minister of the Crown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crown_Attorney" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Attorney">Crown Attorney</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prosecutor" title="Crown Prosecutor">Crown Prosecutor</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of the Crown took form under the <a href="/wiki/Feudal_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal system">feudal system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Though not used this way in all countries that had this system, in England, all rights and privileges were ultimately bestowed by the ruler. Land, for instance, was granted by the Crown to lords in exchange for feudal services and they, in turn, granted the land to lesser lords. One exception to this was common <a href="/wiki/Socage" title="Socage">socage</a>—owners of land held as socage held it subject only to the Crown.  When such lands become owner-less they are said to <a href="/wiki/Escheat" title="Escheat">escheat</a>; i.e., return to direct ownership of the Crown (Crown lands). <i><a href="/wiki/Bona_vacantia" title="Bona vacantia">Bona vacantia</a></i> is the royal prerogative by which unowned property (primarily unclaimed inheritances) become the property of the Crown (except in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, where it becomes the property of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duke of Cornwall</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> where it becomes the property of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duke of Lancaster</a>).
</p><p>The monarch is the living embodiment of the Crown and,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as such, is regarded as the <a href="/wiki/Personification" class="mw-redirect" title="Personification">personification</a> of the state.<sup id="cite_ref-Pers1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pers1-5">&#91;n 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Compendium_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Compendium-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The body of the reigning sovereign thus holds two distinct personas in constant coexistence: that of a natural-born human being and that of the state as accorded to him or her through law; the Crown and the monarch are "conceptually divisible but legally indivisible&#160;... [t]he office cannot exist without the office-holder".<sup id="cite_ref-Boyce_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boyce-13">&#91;n 2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The terms <i>the state</i>, <i>the Crown</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <i>the Crown in Right of [jurisdiction]</i>, <i>Her Majesty the Queen in Right of [jurisdiction]</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and similar are all synonymous and the monarch's <a href="/wiki/Legal_personality" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal personality">legal personality</a> is sometimes referred to simply as the relevant jurisdiction's name.<sup id="cite_ref-Compendium_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Compendium-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> (In countries using systems of government derived from Roman <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a>, the State is the equivalent concept to the Crown.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson20_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson20-18">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>As such, the king or queen is the employer of all government officials and staff (including the viceroys, judges, members of the armed forces, police officers, and parliamentarians),<sup id="cite_ref-Employ_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Employ-19">&#91;n 3&#93;</a></sup> the guardian of foster children (<i><a href="/wiki/Crown_ward" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown ward">Crown wards</a></i>), as well as the owner of all state lands (<i><a href="/wiki/Crown_land" title="Crown land">Crown land</a></i>), buildings and equipment (<i>Crown held property</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> state owned companies (<i><a href="/wiki/Crown_corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown corporation">Crown corporations</a></i>), and the <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> for government publications (<i><a href="/wiki/Crown_copyright" title="Crown copyright">Crown copyright</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This is all in his or her position as sovereign, and not as an individual; all such property is held by the Crown in perpetuity and cannot be sold by the sovereign without the proper advice and consent of his or her relevant ministers.
</p><p>The Crown also represents the legal embodiment of executive, <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislative</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Judiciary" title="Judiciary">judicial</a> governance. While the Crown's legal personality is usually regarded as a <a href="/wiki/Corporation_sole" title="Corporation sole">corporation sole</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> it can, at least for some purposes, be described as a <a href="/wiki/Corporation_aggregate" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporation aggregate">corporation aggregate</a> headed by the monarch.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisibility_of_the_Crown">Divisibility of the Crown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Divisibility of the Crown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, the Crown was considered to be indivisible. Two judgments—<i>Ex parte</i> Indian Association of Alberta (<a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">EWCA</a>, 1982) and <i>Ex parte</i> Quark (<a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" title="Lords of Appeal in Ordinary">House of Lords</a>, 2005)—challenged that view. Today, the Crown is considered separate in every country, province, state, or territory, regardless of its degree of independence, that has the shared monarch as part of the local government, though limitations on the power of the monarch in right of each territory vary according to relevant laws, thus making the difference between full sovereignty, semi-sovereignty, dependency, etc. The Lords of Appeal wrote: "The Queen is as much the Queen of New South Wales and Mauritius and other territories acknowledging her as head of state as she is of England and Wales, Scotland, Northern Ireland or the United Kingdom."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commonwealth_realms">Commonwealth realms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commonwealth realms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Crown in each of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a> is a similar, but separate, legal concept. To distinguish the institution's role in one jurisdiction from its place in another, Commonwealth law employs the expression <i>the Crown in right of [place]</i>; for example, the Crown in right of the United Kingdom,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> the Crown in right of Canada, the Crown in right of the Commonwealth of Australia, etc. Because both Canada and Australia are federations, there are also crowns in right of each <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">Canadian province</a> and each <a href="/wiki/States_and_territories_of_Australia" title="States and territories of Australia">Australian state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Crown's powers are exercised either by the monarch personally or by his or her representative in each jurisdiction, on the <a href="/wiki/Advice_(constitutional)" title="Advice (constitutional)">advice</a> of the appropriate local <a href="/wiki/Ministers_of_the_Crown" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministers of the Crown">ministers</a>, legislature, or judges, none of which may advise the Crown on any matter pertinent to another of the Crown's jurisdictions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_dependencies">Crown dependencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Crown dependencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a>, statements by the <a href="/wiki/Law_Officers_of_the_Crown" title="Law Officers of the Crown">Law Officers of the Crown</a> define the Crown's operation in that jurisdiction as <i>the Crown in right of Jersey</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> with all Crown land in the Bailiwick of Jersey belonging to the Crown in right of Jersey and not to the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Estate" title="Crown Estate">Crown Estate</a> of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The Succession to the Crown (Jersey) Law 2013 defined the Crown, for the purposes of implementing the <a href="/wiki/Perth_Agreement" title="Perth Agreement">Perth Agreement</a> in Jersey law, as the <i>Crown in right of the Bailiwick of Jersey</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Legislation in the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> also defines the Crown in right of the Isle of Man as being separate from the Crown in right of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a>, legislation refers to <i>the Crown in right of the Bailiwick</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and the Law Officers of the Crown of Guernsey submitted that "[t]he Crown in this context ordinarily means the Crown in right of the <i>république</i> of the Bailiwick of Guernsey"<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and that this comprises "the collective governmental and civic institutions, established by and under the authority of the Monarch, for the governance of these Islands, including the States of Guernsey and legislatures in the other Islands, the Royal Court and other courts, the Lieutenant Governor, Parish authorities, and the Crown acting in and through the Privy Council".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> This constitutional concept is also worded as <i>the Crown in right of the Bailiwick of Guernsey</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Overseas_Territories">British Overseas Territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: British Overseas Territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the Lords' decision in <i>Ex parte</i> Quark, 2005, it is held that the Queen in exercising her authority over <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a> does not act on the advice of the government of the UK, but in her role as Queen of each territory, with the exception of fulfilling the UK's international responsibilities for its territories.  The reserve powers of the Crown for each territory are no longer considered to be exercisable on the advice of the UK government.  To comply with the court's decision, the territorial governors now act on the advice of each territory's executive and the UK government can no longer disallow legislation passed by territorial legislatures.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_courts">In the courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In the courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Criminal_law" title="Criminal law">criminal proceedings</a>, the state is the prosecuting party and is usually designated on the title or name of a case as "R v" – where <i>R</i> can stand for either <i>Rex</i> (if the current monarch is male) or <i>Regina</i> (if the monarch is female) versus the <a href="/wiki/Defendant" title="Defendant">defendant</a>; for example, a criminal case against Smith might be referred to as <i>R v Smith</i>, and verbally read as "the Crown against Smith". On the indictment notice, it may state "The Queen - v - <i>Defendant</i>" as well as "R v <i>Defendant</i>".
</p><p>Often cases are brought by the Crown according to the complaint of a <a href="/wiki/Plaintiff" title="Plaintiff">claimant</a>. The titles of these case now follow the pattern of "R (on the application of X) v Y", notated as "R (X) v Y" for short. Thus <i><a href="/wiki/R_(Miller)_v_Secretary_of_State_for_Exiting_the_European_Union" title="R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union">R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union</a></i> is <i>R (on the application of Miller and other) v Secretary of State for Exiting the European Union</i>, where "Miller" is <a href="/wiki/Gina_Miller" title="Gina Miller">Gina Miller</a>, a citizen. Until the end of the twentieth century, such case titles used the pattern <i>R v Secretary of State of State for Exiting the European Union, <a href="/wiki/Ex_parte" title="Ex parte">ex parte</a> Miller</i>.
</p><p>In Scotland, criminal prosecutions are undertaken by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Advocate" title="Lord Advocate">Lord Advocate</a> (or the relevant <a href="/wiki/Procurator_Fiscal" class="mw-redirect" title="Procurator Fiscal">Procurator Fiscal</a>) in the name of the Crown. Accordingly, the abbreviation HMA is used in the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a> for "His/Her Majesty's Advocate" in place of <i>Rex</i> or <i>Regina</i>, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Flight_103_bombing_trial" title="Pan Am Flight 103 bombing trial">HMA v Al Megrahi and Fahima</a></i>.
</p><p>In Australia, each state uses <i>R</i> in the title of criminal cases and <i>The Queen</i> (or <i>The King</i>) in criminal appeal cases (i.e., the case name at trial would be <i>R v Smith</i>; if appealed, the case name would be <i>Smith v The Queen</i>). Judges usually refer to the prosecuting party as simply "the prosecution" in the text of judgments (only rarely is <i>The Crown</i> used in the text, and never <i>R</i>). In civil cases where the Crown is a party, it is a customary to list the appropriate government Minister as the party instead. When a case is announced in court, the Clerk or Bailiff refers to the crown orally as "Our Sovereign Lady the Queen" (or "Our Sovereign Lord the King").
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> court reporting, news reports will refer to the prosecuting lawyer (often called a <i>Crown prosecutor</i>, as in Canada and the United Kingdom) as representing the Crown, usages such as "For the Crown, Joe Bloggs argued..." being common.
</p><p>This practice of using the seat of <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> as the injured party is analogous with criminal cases in the United States, where the format is "the People" or "the State v. [defendant]" (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/People_v._LaValle" title="People v. LaValle">People of the State of New York v. LaValle</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Pennsylvania_v._Brady" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth of Pennsylvania v. Brady">Commonwealth of Pennsylvania v. Brady</a></i>) under the doctrine of <a href="/wiki/Popular_sovereignty" title="Popular sovereignty">popular sovereignty</a>. In Federal criminal cases, it is "United States v. [defendant]," as in <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Nixon" title="United States v. Nixon">United States v. Nixon</a></i>.
</p><p>The Crown can also be a plaintiff or defendant in civil actions to which the government of the Commonwealth realm in question is a party. Such <a href="/wiki/Crown_proceedings" title="Crown proceedings">Crown proceedings</a> are often subject to specific rules and limitations, such as the enforcement of judgments against the Crown.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Qui_tam" title="Qui tam">Qui tam</a></i> lawsuits on behalf of the Crown were once common but have been unusual since the <a href="/wiki/Common_Informers_Act_1951" title="Common Informers Act 1951">Common Informers Act 1951</a> ended the practice of allowing such suits by common informers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crown_forces">Crown forces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Crown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Crown forces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Crown_forces"></span>
The term <i>crown forces</i> has been applied by <a href="/wiki/Physical_force_Irish_republicanism" title="Physical force Irish republicanism">militant Irish republicans</a> to British-authorised <a href="/wiki/Security_forces" title="Security forces">security forces</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a> and armed police such as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a>, which are seen as <a href="/wiki/Enemy_combatant" title="Enemy combatant">enemy combatants</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Military_occupation" title="Military occupation">occupation</a> force.<sup id="cite_ref-Morrison_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morrison-41">&#91;n 4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish nationalist">Irish nationalist</a> historical narrative may apply <i>crown forces</i> to earlier forces raised by the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Castle_administration" title="Dublin Castle administration">Dublin Castle administration</a> at intervals since the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Tudor conquest of Ireland">Tudor conquest of Ireland</a> to suppress <a href="/wiki/List_of_Irish_uprisings" title="List of Irish uprisings">various Irish uprisings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ferriter2012_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferriter2012-45">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
