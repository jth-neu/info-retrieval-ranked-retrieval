<p><b>Hurling</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">iománaíocht</i>, <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">iomáint</i></i>) is an outdoor <a href="/wiki/Team_sport" title="Team sport">team game</a> of ancient <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic culture">Gaelic</a> and Irish origin. It is administered by the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Athletic_Association" title="Gaelic Athletic Association">Gaelic Athletic Association</a> (GAA). The game has <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric</a> origins, and has been played for 4,000 years. One of Ireland's native <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_games" title="Gaelic games">Gaelic games</a>, it shares a number of features with <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_football" title="Gaelic football">Gaelic football</a>, such as the field and goals, the number of players, and much <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_Gaelic_games_terms" title="Glossary of Gaelic games terms">terminology</a>. There is a similar game for women called <a href="/wiki/Camogie" title="Camogie">camogie</a> (<i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">camógaíocht</i></i>). It shares a common Gaelic root with the sport of <a href="/wiki/Shinty" title="Shinty">shinty</a> (<i><i lang="gd" title="Scottish Gaelic language text">camanachd</i></i>), which is played predominantly in Scotland.
</p><p>The objective of the game is for players to use a wooden (ash) stick called a <a href="/wiki/Hurley_(stick)" title="Hurley (stick)">hurley</a> (in Irish a <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">camán</i></i>, pronounced <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɔː/: &#39;au&#39; in &#39;fraud&#39;">ɔː</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>) to hit a small ball called a <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text"><a href="/wiki/Sliotar" title="Sliotar">sliotar</a></i></i> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ʃ/: &#39;sh&#39; in &#39;shy&#39;">ʃ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span> between the opponents' goalposts either over the crossbar for one point, or under the crossbar into a net guarded by a <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_football_and_Hurling_positions#Goalkeeper" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaelic football and Hurling positions">goalkeeper</a> for one goal, which is equivalent to three points. The <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">sliotar</i></i> can be caught in the hand and carried for not more than four steps, struck in the air, or struck on the ground with the hurley. It can be kicked, or slapped with an open hand (the hand pass) for short-range passing. A player who wants to carry the ball for more than four steps has to bounce or balance the <i><i lang="ga" title="Irish language text">sliotar</i></i> on the end of the stick, and the ball can only be handled twice while in his possession.
</p><p>Provided that a player has at least one foot on the ground, a player may make a shoulder to shoulder charge on an opponent:
</p>
<ul><li>who is in possession of the ball</li>
<li>who is playing the ball</li>
<li>when both players are moving in the direction of the ball to play it</li></ul>
<p>No protective padding is worn by players. A plastic protective helmet with a faceguard is mandatory for all age groups, including senior level, as of 2010. The game has been described as "a bastion of humility", with player names absent from jerseys and a player's number decided by his position on the field.<sup id="cite_ref-forbespitchman_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbespitchman-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hurling is played throughout the world, and is popular among members of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_diaspora" title="Irish diaspora">Irish diaspora</a> in North America, Europe, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, Argentina, and South Korea. In many parts of Ireland, however, hurling is a fixture of life.<sup id="cite_ref-forbespitchman_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbespitchman-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It has featured regularly in art forms such as <a href="/wiki/Hurling_in_popular_culture" title="Hurling in popular culture">film, music and literature</a>. The final of the <a href="/wiki/All-Ireland_Senior_Hurling_Championship" title="All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship">All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship</a> was listed in second place by <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> in its "10 sporting events you have to see live", after the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> and ahead of both the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a> and <a href="/wiki/UEFA_European_Football_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="UEFA European Football Championship">UEFA European Football Championship</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> After covering the <a href="/wiki/1959_All-Ireland_Senior_Hurling_Championship_Final" title="1959 All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship Final">1959 All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship Final</a> between <a href="/wiki/Kilkenny_GAA" title="Kilkenny GAA">Kilkenny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waterford_GAA" title="Waterford GAA">Waterford</a> for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Television" title="BBC Television">BBC Television</a>, English commentator <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Wolstenholme" title="Kenneth Wolstenholme">Kenneth Wolstenholme</a> was moved to describe hurling as his second favourite sport in the world after his first love, football.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i> magazine described the media attention and population multiplication of <a href="/wiki/Thurles" title="Thurles">Thurles</a> town ahead of one of the game's annual provincial hurling finals as being "the rough equivalent of 30 million Americans watching a regional <a href="/wiki/Lacrosse" title="Lacrosse">lacrosse</a> game".<sup id="cite_ref-forbespitchman_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbespitchman-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">Statistics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statistics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>A team comprises 15 players, or "hurlers"</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hurley_(stick)" title="Hurley (stick)">hurley</a> is generally 24 to 36 inches (61 to 91&#160;cm) in length</li>
<li>The ball, known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Sliotar" title="Sliotar">sliotar</a></i>, has a cork centre and a leather cover; it is between 69 and 72&#160;mm (2.7 and 2.8&#160;in) in diameter, and weighs between 110 and 120&#160;g (3.9 and 4.2&#160;oz)</li>
<li>The goalkeeper's hurley usually has a <i>bas</i> (the flattened, curved end) twice the size of other players' hurleys to provide some advantage against the fast moving <i>sliotar</i></li>
<li>A good strike with a hurley can propel the ball over 150&#160;km/h (93&#160;mph) in speed and 110 metres (361&#160;ft) in distance<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A ball hit over the bar is worth one point. A ball that is hit under the bar is called a <i>goal</i> and is worth three points.</li>
<li>As of 2010, all players must wear helmets</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules">Rules</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rules">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_field">Playing field</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Playing field">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg/220px-Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg.png" width="220" height="320" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg/330px-Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg/440px-Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="301" data-file-height="438" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaelic_football_pitch_diagram.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A standard hurling pitch</div></div></div>
<p>A hurling pitch is similar in some respects to a <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a> pitch but larger. The grass pitch is rectangular, stretching 130–145 metres (140–160 yards) long and 80–90&#160;m (90–100&#160;yd) wide. There are <i>H</i>-shaped goalposts at each end, formed by two posts, which are usually 6–7 metres (20–23 feet) high, set 6.5&#160;m (21&#160;ft) apart, and connected 2.5&#160;m (8.2&#160;ft) above the ground by a crossbar. A net extending behind the goal is attached to the crossbar and lower goal posts. The same pitch is used for <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_football" title="Gaelic football">Gaelic football</a>; the GAA, which organizes both sports, decided this to facilitate dual usage. Lines are marked at distances of 14 yards, 21 yards and 65 yards (45 yards for Gaelic football) from each end-line. Shorter pitches and smaller goals are used by youth teams.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Teams">Teams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Teams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_football,_hurling_and_camogie_positions" title="Gaelic football, hurling and camogie positions">Gaelic football, hurling and camogie positions</a></div>
<p>Teams consist of fifteen players: a goalkeeper, three full backs, three half backs, two midfielders, three half forwards and three full forwards (see diagram). The panel is made up of 24–30 players and five substitutions are allowed per game. An exception can now be made in the case of a blood substitute being necessary.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Helmets">Helmets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Helmets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:167px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg/165px-Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg" width="165" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg/248px-Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg/330px-Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurling_helmet_000_0200.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A standard hurling helmet</div></div></div>
<p>From 1 January 2010, the wearing of helmets with faceguards became compulsory for hurlers at all levels. This saw senior players follow the regulations already introduced in 2009 at minor and under 21 grades. The GAA hopes to significantly reduce the number of injuries by introducing the compulsory wearing of helmets with full faceguards, both in training and matches. Hurlers of all ages, including those at nursery clubs when holding a hurley in their hand, must wear a helmet and faceguard at all times. Match officials will be obliged to stop play if any player at any level appears on the field of play without the necessary standard of equipment.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timekeeping">Timekeeping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Timekeeping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaelic_Pitch.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Gaelic_Pitch.png/220px-Gaelic_Pitch.png" width="220" height="335" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Gaelic_Pitch.png/330px-Gaelic_Pitch.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Gaelic_Pitch.png/440px-Gaelic_Pitch.png 2x" data-file-width="1823" data-file-height="2779" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaelic_Pitch.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Standard hurling positions</div></div></div>
<p>Senior inter-county matches last 70 minutes (35 minutes per half). All other matches last 60 minutes (30 minutes per half). For teams under-13 and lower, games may be shortened to 50 minutes. Timekeeping is at the discretion of the referee who adds on stoppage time at the end of each half.
</p><p>If a knockout game finishes in a draw, a replay is staged. If a replay finishes in a draw, 20 minutes of extra time are played (10 minutes per half). If the game is still tied, another replay is staged.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In club competitions, replays are increasingly not used due to the fixture backlogs caused. Instead, extra time is played after a draw, and if the game is still level after that it will go to a replay.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_fouls">Technical fouls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Technical fouls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following are considered technical fouls ("fouling the ball"):
</p>
<ul><li>Picking the ball directly off the ground (instead it must be flicked up with the hurley)</li>
<li>Throwing the ball (instead it must be "hand-passed": slapped with the open hand)</li>
<li>Going more than four steps with the ball in the hand (it may be carried indefinitely on the hurley)</li>
<li>Catching the ball three times in a row without it touching the ground (touching the hurley does not count)</li>
<li>Putting the ball from one hand to the other</li>
<li>Hand-passing a goal</li>
<li>"Chopping" slashing downwards on another player's hurl.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scoring">Scoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scoring is achieved by sending the <i>sliotar</i> between the opposition's goal posts. The posts, which are at each end of the field, are <i>H</i> posts as in <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby football</a> but with a net under the crossbar as in football. The posts are 6.4 m apart and the crossbar is 2.44 m above the ground.
</p><p>If the ball goes over the crossbar, a <i>point</i> is scored and a white flag is raised by an umpire. If the ball goes below the crossbar, a <i>goal</i>, worth three points, is scored, and a green flag is raised by an umpire. A goal must be scored by either a striking motion or by directly soloing the ball into the net. The goal is guarded by a goalkeeper. Scores are recorded in the format {goal total} – {point total}. For example, the 1997 <a href="/wiki/All-Ireland_Senior_Hurling_Championship" title="All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship">All-Ireland</a> final finished: <a href="/wiki/Clare_GAA" title="Clare GAA">Clare</a> 0–20 <a href="/wiki/Tipperary_GAA" title="Tipperary GAA">Tipperary</a> 2–13. Thus Clare won by "twenty points to nineteen" (20 to 19). 2–0 would be referred to as "two goals", never "two zero". 0–0 is said "no score".
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg/220px-Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg/330px-Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg/440px-Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg 2x" data-file-width="599" data-file-height="337" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaa_scoreboard_smaple.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of a typical GAA scoreboard</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tackling">Tackling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tackling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Players may be tackled but not struck by a one handed slash of the stick; exceptions are two handed jabs and strikes. Jersey-pulling, wrestling, pushing and tripping are all forbidden. There are several forms of acceptable tackling, the most popular being:
</p>
<ul><li>the "block", where one player attempts to smother an opposing player's strike by trapping the ball between his hurley and the opponent's swinging hurl</li>
<li>the "hook", where a player approaches another player from a rear angle and attempts to catch the opponent's hurley with his own at the top of the swing</li>
<li>the "side pull", where two players running together for the <i>sliotar</i> will collide at the shoulders and swing together to win the tackle and "pull" (name given to swing the hurley) with extreme force</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restarting_play">Restarting play</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Restarting play">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The match begins with the referee throwing the <i>sliotar</i> in between the four midfielders on the halfway line</li>
<li>After an attacker has scored or put the ball wide of the goals, the goalkeeper may take a "puckout" from the hand at the edge of the small square. All players must be beyond the 20 m line.</li>
<li>After a defender has put the ball wide of the goals, an attacker may take a "65" from the 65 m line level with where the ball went wide. It must be taken by lifting and striking. However, the ball must not be taken into the hand but struck whilst the ball is lifted.</li>
<li>After a player has put the ball over the sideline, the other team may take a 'sideline cut' at the point where the ball left the pitch. It must be taken from the ground.</li>
<li>After a player has committed a foul, the other team may take a 'free' at the point where the foul was committed. It must be taken by lifting and striking in the same style as the "65".</li>
<li>After a defender has committed a foul inside the square (large rectangle), the other team may take a "penalty" from the ground from behind the 20 m line. Only the goalkeeper may guard the goals. It must be taken by lifting and striking and the <i>sliotar</i> must be struck on or behind the 20m line (The penalty rule was amended in 2015 due to safety concerns. Before this the ball merely had to start at the 20m line but could be struck beyond it. To balance this advantage the two additional defenders previously allowed on the line have been removed).</li>
<li>If many players are struggling for the ball and no side is able to capitalize or gain control of it the referee may choose to throw the ball in between two opposing players. This is also known as a "clash".</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Officials">Officials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Officials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A hurling match is watched over by eight officials:
</p>
<ul><li>The referee (on field)</li>
<li>Two linesmen (sideline)</li>
<li>Sideline official/standby linesman (inter-county games only)</li>
<li>Four umpires (two at each end)</li></ul>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:128px;max-width:128px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:62px;max-width:62px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Yellow_card.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Yellow_card.svg/60px-Yellow_card.svg.png" width="60" height="78" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Yellow_card.svg/90px-Yellow_card.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Yellow_card.svg/120px-Yellow_card.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="260" /></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:62px;max-width:62px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Red_card.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Red_card.svg/60px-Red_card.svg.png" width="60" height="78" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Red_card.svg/90px-Red_card.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Red_card.svg/120px-Red_card.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="260" /></a></div></div><div style="clear:left"></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">Players are cautioned with a yellow card, and dismissed from the game with a red card</div></div></div>
<p>The referee is responsible for starting and stopping play, recording the score, awarding frees, noting infractions, and issuing yellow (caution) and red (order off) <a href="/wiki/Penalty_card" title="Penalty card">penalty cards</a> to players after offences. A second yellow card at the same game leads to a red card, and therefore to a dismissal.
</p><p>Linesmen are responsible for indicating the direction of line balls to the referee and also for conferring with the referee. The fourth official is responsible for overseeing substitutions, and also indicating the amount of stoppage time (signalled to him by the referee) and the players substituted using an electronic board. The umpires are responsible for judging the scoring. They indicate to the referee whether a shot was: wide (spread both arms), a 65 m puck (raise one arm), a point (wave white flag), or a goal (wave green flag).
</p><p>Contrary to popular belief within the association, all officials are not obliged to indicate "any misdemeanours" to the referee, but are in fact only permitted to inform the referee of violent conduct they have witnessed which has occurred without the referee's knowledge. A linesman or umpire is not permitted to inform the referee of technical fouls such as a "third time in the hand", where a player catches the ball for a third time in succession after soloing or an illegal pick up of the ball. Such decisions can only be made at the discretion of the referee.
</p><p><big><b>Risks</b></big>
</p><p>Blunt injury to the larynx is an infrequent consequence of contact sports despite protective equipment and stringent rules. Hurling, one of the two national sporting games of Ireland, is seen as one of the fastest field sports on earth and only played with a facemask and helmet as protection, making injury an unavoidable feature of the game.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurling_Scoring.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Hurling_Scoring.png/220px-Hurling_Scoring.png" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Hurling_Scoring.png/330px-Hurling_Scoring.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Hurling_Scoring.png/440px-Hurling_Scoring.png 2x" data-file-width="1389" data-file-height="895" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurling_Scoring.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A graph demonstrating hurling scoring since 1910</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_hurling" title="History of hurling">History of hurling</a></div>
<p>Hurling is older than the recorded history of Ireland. It is thought to predate Christianity, having come to Ireland with the Celts.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The earliest written references to the sport in <a href="/wiki/Brehon_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Brehon law">Brehon law</a> date from the fifth century.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian.co.uk_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian.co.uk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Seamus King's book <i>A History of Hurling</i> references oral history going back as far as 1200 <a href="/wiki/BCE" class="mw-redirect" title="BCE">BCE</a> of the game being played in <a href="/wiki/Hill_of_Tara" title="Hill of Tara">Tara</a>, County Meath. Hurling is related to the games of <a href="/wiki/Shinty" title="Shinty">shinty</a> that is played primarily in Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Cammag" title="Cammag">cammag</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bando_(sport)" title="Bando (sport)">bando</a> which was played formerly in England and Wales. The tale of the <i><a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1in_B%C3%B3_Cuailgne" class="mw-redirect" title="Táin Bó Cuailgne">Táin Bó Cuailgne</a></i> (drawing on earlier legends) describes the hero <a href="/wiki/C%C3%BAchulainn" class="mw-redirect" title="Cúchulainn">Cúchulainn</a> playing hurling at <a href="/wiki/Emain_Macha" class="mw-redirect" title="Emain Macha">Emain Macha</a>. Similar tales are told about <a href="/wiki/Fionn_Mac_Cumhail" class="mw-redirect" title="Fionn Mac Cumhail">Fionn Mac Cumhail</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fianna" title="Fianna">Fianna</a>, his legendary warrior band. Recorded references to hurling appear in many places such as the fourteenth century <a href="/wiki/Statutes_of_Kilkenny" title="Statutes of Kilkenny">Statutes of Kilkenny</a> and a fifteenth-century grave slab survives in <a href="/wiki/Inishowen" title="Inishowen">Inishowen</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Donegal" title="County Donegal">County Donegal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hurling was said to be played in ancient times by teams representing neighbouring villages. Villages would play games involving hundreds of players, which would last several hours or even days.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Injuries were rife in the early form of the game, but this changed dramatically in the mid 1800s with the introduction of the ball, which was due in no small measure to the tireless lobbying of Sir William Arthur Clement, and led soon after to the flattening of the hurley stick, and removal of the notorious 'Claddagh' spikes.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg/220px-Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg/330px-Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg/440px-Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3683" data-file-height="2368" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Enda_Kenny_presents_Barack_Obama_with_a_hurley.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>U.S. president <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> accepting a <a href="/wiki/Hurley_(stick)" title="Hurley (stick)">hurley</a> from <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/Enda_Kenny" title="Enda Kenny">Enda Kenny</a></div></div></div>
<p>The eighteenth century is frequently referred to as "The Golden Age of Hurling". This was when members of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Irish">Anglo-Irish</a> landed gentry kept teams of players on their estates and challenged each other's teams to matches for the amusement of their tenants.
</p><p>One of the first modern attempts to standardise the game with a formal, written set of rules came with the foundation of the Irish Hurling Union at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College_Dublin" title="Trinity College Dublin">Trinity College Dublin</a> in 1879. It aimed "to draw up a code of rules for all clubs in the union and to foster that manly and noble game of hurling in this, its native country".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png/220px-Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png/330px-Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png/440px-Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png 2x" data-file-width="1186" data-file-height="894" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurling_and_Gaelic_Football_ratio_of_points_to_goals_1910_to_2015.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graph of hurling and gaelic football ratio of points to goals from 1910 to 2015</div></div></div>
<p>The founding of the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Athletic_Association" title="Gaelic Athletic Association">Gaelic Athletic Association</a> (GAA) in 1884 in Hayes Hotel, Thurles, Co Tipperary, ended decline by organising the game around a common set of written rules. In 1891 the first All-Ireland hurling final was played with <a href="/wiki/Kerry_GAA" title="Kerry GAA">Kerry</a> winning the championship. However, the twentieth century saw <a href="/wiki/Cork_GAA" title="Cork GAA">Cork</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kilkenny_GAA" title="Kilkenny GAA">Kilkenny</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Tipperary_GAA" title="Tipperary GAA">Tipperary</a> dominate hurling with each of these counties winning more than 20 All-Ireland titles each. <a href="/wiki/Wexford_GAA" title="Wexford GAA">Wexford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waterford_GAA" title="Waterford GAA">Waterford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clare_GAA" title="Clare GAA">Clare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limerick_GAA" title="Limerick GAA">Limerick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Offaly_GAA" title="Offaly GAA">Offaly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antrim_GAA" title="Antrim GAA">Antrim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin_GAA" title="Dublin GAA">Dublin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Galway_GAA" title="Galway GAA">Galway</a> were also strong hurling counties during the twentieth century.
</p><p>As hurling entered the new millennium, it has remained Ireland's second most popular sport. An extended qualifier system resulted in a longer <a href="/wiki/All-Ireland_Senior_Hurling_Championship" title="All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship">All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship</a>. Pay-for-play remains controversial and the <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Players_Association" title="Gaelic Players Association">Gaelic Players Association</a> continues to grow in strength. The inauguration of the <a href="/wiki/Christy_Ring_Cup" title="Christy Ring Cup">Christy Ring Cup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicky_Rackard_Cup" title="Nicky Rackard Cup">Nicky Rackard Cup</a> gave new championships and an opportunity to play in <a href="/wiki/Croke_Park" title="Croke Park">Croke Park</a> to the weaker county teams. Further dissemination of the championship structure was completed in 2009 with the addition of the <a href="/wiki/Lory_Meagher_Cup" title="Lory Meagher Cup">Lory Meagher Cup</a> to make it a four tier championship.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurling_at_the_Olympic_Games">Hurling at the Olympic Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hurling at the Olympic Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hurling was an unofficial sport at the <a href="/wiki/1904_Summer_Olympics" title="1904 Summer Olympics">1904 Summer Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, in the United States. In the final, Fenian F.C. (Chicago) USA beat Innisfails (St. Louis). This was the only time hurling was in the Olympics.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Hurling_outside_Ireland" title="Hurling outside Ireland">Hurling outside Ireland</a></div>
<p>Although many hurling clubs exist worldwide, only Ireland has a national team (although it includes only players from weaker counties in order to ensure matches are competitive). It and the Scotland <a href="/wiki/Shinty" title="Shinty">shinty</a> team have played for many years with modified match rules (as with <a href="/wiki/International_Rules_Football" class="mw-redirect" title="International Rules Football">International Rules Football</a>). The match is the only such international competition. However, competition at club level has been going on around the world since the late nineteenth century thanks to emigration from Ireland, and the strength of the game has ebbed and flowed along with emigration trends. Nowadays, growth in hurling is noted in Continental Europe, Australia, and North America.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Irish immigrants began arriving in Argentina in the nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest reference to hurling in Argentina dates from the late 1880s in <a href="/wiki/Mercedes,_Buenos_Aires" title="Mercedes, Buenos Aires">Mercedes, Buenos Aires</a>. However, the game was not actively promoted until 1900, when it came to the attention of author and newspaperman <a href="/wiki/William_Bulfin" title="William Bulfin">William Bulfin</a>. Under Bulfin's patronage, the <a href="/wiki/Argentine_Hurling_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Argentine Hurling Club">Argentine Hurling Club</a> was formed on 15 July 1900, leading to teams being established in different neighborhoods of Buenos Aires and the surrounding farming communities.
</p><p>Games of hurling were played every weekend until 1914 and received frequent coverage from Argentina's Spanish language newspapers, such as <i>La Nación</i>. After the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, it became almost impossible to obtain hurleys from Ireland. An attempt was made to use native Argentine mountain ash, but it proved too heavy and lacking in pliability. Although the game was revived after the end of the war, the golden age of Argentine hurling had passed. <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> finally brought the era to its close.
</p><p>In the aftermath of the Second World War, immigration from Ireland slowed to a trickle. In addition, native born Irish-Argentines assimilated into the local community. The last time that hurling was played in Argentina was in 1980, when the Aer Lingus Hurling Club conducted a three-week tour of the country and played matches at several locations.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Since 2009, with the realization of several Summers Camps and the visit of the All Stars in December, hurling returned to be a frequent activity at the Hurling Club, where many boys and young men have since been trained and taught to play. Even the Hurling Club are invited to participate Hurling Festival is organized within The Gathering events organized by Aer Lingus. This team will be present in September 2013 in the city of Galway. The team consists of 21 players from Hockey and Rugby teams. Many have contributed to the return of hurling as an activity in the Club. As an example we can name Alejandro Yoyo Wade, Johnny Wade, Barbie, Cecilia and Irene Scally, David Ganly, Dickie Mac Allister, Eduardo Cabrera Punter, Hernan Magrini Scally. Several Irish have participated in many opportunities to work with the skills and education: Jonathan Lynch, Kevin O'Connors and Michael Connery, who currently works with the team's training to participate in the Aer Lingus International Hurling Festival.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Australasia_GAA" title="Australasia GAA">Australasia GAA</a></div>
<p>The earliest reference to hurling in Australia is related in the book "Sketches of Garryowen." On 12 July 1844, a match took place at Batman's Hill in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> as a counterpoint to a march by the <a href="/wiki/Orange_Order" title="Orange Order">Orange Order</a>. Reportedly, the hurling match attracted a crowd of five hundred Irish immigrants, while the Orange march shivered out of existence.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several hurling clubs existed in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> in the 1870s including Melbourne, Collingwood, Upper Yarra, Richmond and Geelong.
</p><p>In 1885, a game between two Sydney based teams took place before a crowd of over ten thousand spectators. Reportedly, the contest was greatly enjoyed despite the fact that one newspaper dubbed the game "Two Degrees Safer Than War."<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Arden_Street_Oval" title="Arden Street Oval">Arden Street Oval</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Melbourne" class="mw-redirect" title="North Melbourne">North Melbourne</a> was used by Irish immigrants during the 1920s.
The game in Australasia is administered by <a href="/wiki/Australasia_GAA" title="Australasia GAA">Australasia GAA</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Hurling was brought to Great Britain in the 19th century. The game is administered by <a href="/wiki/British_GAA" title="British GAA">British GAA</a>. <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire_GAA" title="Warwickshire GAA">Warwickshire</a> and Lancashire compete at inter-county level in the <a href="/wiki/Lory_Meagher_Cup" title="Lory Meagher Cup">Lory Meagher Cup</a>, competing against other counties in Ireland. <a href="/wiki/London_GAA" title="London GAA">London</a> is the only non-Irish team to have won the <a href="/wiki/All-Ireland_Senior_Hurling_Championship" title="All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship">All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship</a> (having captured the title in <a href="/wiki/1901_All-Ireland_Senior_Hurling_Championship" title="1901 All-Ireland Senior Hurling Championship">1901</a>), and after winning the <a href="/wiki/2012_Christy_Ring_Cup" title="2012 Christy Ring Cup">2012 Christy Ring Cup</a> gained the right to contest the Liam MacCarthy Cup in 2013.
</p><p>The first ever hurling game played in the Scottish Highlands was played at Easter 2012 between CLG Micheal Breathnach and Fir Uladh, an Ulster select of Gaeiligoiri, as part of the Iomain Cholmcille festival, na Breathnaich coming out victorious.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soldiers who served in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_commandos" title="Irish commandos">Irish Brigade</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Boer_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Boer War">Anglo-Boer War</a> are believed to have played the game on the <a href="/wiki/Veld" title="Veld">veldt</a>. Immigrants from <a href="/wiki/County_Wicklow" title="County Wicklow">County Wicklow</a> who had arrived to work in the explosives factory in <a href="/wiki/Umbogintwini" title="Umbogintwini">Umbogintwini</a>, <a href="/wiki/KwaZulu-Natal" title="KwaZulu-Natal">KwaZulu-Natal</a> formed a team c. 1915–16. A major burst of immigration in the 1920s led to the foundation of the <i>Transvaal Hurling Association</i> in <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a> in 1928. Games were traditionally played in a pitch on the site of the modern day Johannesburg Central Railway Station every <a href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" class="mw-redirect" title="Easter Sunday">Easter Sunday</a> after <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">Mass</a>.
</p><p>In 1932, a South African hurling team sailed to Ireland to compete in the <a href="/wiki/Tailteann_Games_(Irish_Free_State)" title="Tailteann Games (Irish Free State)">Tailteann Games</a>, where they carried a banner donated by a convent of Irish nuns in <a href="/wiki/Cape_Town" title="Cape Town">Cape Town</a>. On their arrival, they were personally received by the <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> (Prime Minister) at the time, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="Éamon de Valera">Éamon de Valera</a>.
</p><p>South African hurling continued to prosper until the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, which caused immigration from Ireland to cease and made it impossible to import equipment. Games of hurling and <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_football" title="Gaelic football">Gaelic football</a> were occasionally sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Christian_Brothers_schools" title="List of Christian Brothers schools">Christian Brothers schools</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boksburg" title="Boksburg">Boksburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pretoria" title="Pretoria">Pretoria</a> well into the 1950s. Both games have all but ceased to be played.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_GAA" title="Canadian GAA">Canadian GAA</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_GAA" title="New York GAA">New York GAA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_American_GAA" title="North American GAA">North American GAA</a></div>
<p>References to hurling on the North American continent date from the 1780s in modern-day Canada concerning immigrants from <a href="/wiki/County_Waterford" title="County Waterford">County Waterford</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Kilkenny" title="County Kilkenny">County Kilkenny</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and also, in New York City. After the end of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, references to hurling cease in American newspapers until the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Great Famine</a> when Irish people moved to America in huge numbers, bringing the game with them.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Newspaper reports from the 1850s refer to occasional matches played in San Francisco, <a href="/wiki/Hoboken,_New_Jersey" title="Hoboken, New Jersey">Hoboken</a> and New York City. The first game of hurling played under GAA rules outside Ireland was played on <a href="/wiki/Boston_Common_(park)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Common (park)">Boston Common</a> in June 1886.
</p><p>In 1888, there was an American tour by fifty Gaelic athletes from Ireland, known as the 'American Invasion'. This created enough interest among Irish Americans to lay the groundwork for the <a href="/wiki/North_American_GAA" title="North American GAA">North American GAA</a>. By the end of 1889, almost a dozen GAA clubs existed in America, many of them in and around New York City, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> and Chicago. Later, clubs were formed in Boston, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, and many other centers of Irish America. <a href="/wiki/Concord,_New_Hampshire" title="Concord, New Hampshire">Concord, New Hampshire</a> has its state's only hurling team, sponsored by The Barley House Pub.
</p><p>In 1910, twenty-two hurlers, composed of an equal number from Chicago and New York, conducted a tour of Ireland, where they played against the County teams from <a href="/wiki/County_Kilkenny" title="County Kilkenny">Kilkenny</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Tipperary" title="County Tipperary">Tipperary</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Limerick" title="County Limerick">Limerick</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Dublin" title="County Dublin">Dublin</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">Wexford</a>.
</p><p>Traditionally, hurling was a game played by Irish immigrants and discarded by their children. Many American hurling teams took to raising money to import players directly from Ireland. In recent years, this has changed considerably with the advent of the Internet and increased travel. The Barley House Wolves hurling team from New Hampshire was formed when U.S. soldiers returning from Iraq saw a hurling game on the television in Shannon Airport as their plane refuelled.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Outside of the traditional North American GAA cities of New York, Boston, Chicago and San Francisco, clubs are springing up in other places where they consist of predominantly American-born players who bring a new dimension to the game and actively seek to promote it as a mainstream sport, especially Joe Maher, a leading expert at the sport in Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Currently, the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Hurling_Club" title="Milwaukee Hurling Club">Milwaukee Hurling Club</a>, with 300 members, is the largest Hurling club in the world outside Ireland, which is made of all Americans and very few Irish immigrants. The St. Louis Gaelic Athletic Club was established in 2002 and has expanded its organization to an eight team hurling league in the spring and six team <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_football" title="Gaelic football">Gaelic football</a> league in the fall. They also have a 30-member camogie league. Saint Louis has won two National Championships in Jr C Hurling (2004 and 2011), as well as two National Championships in Jr D Gaelic Football (2005, and 2013). The Indianapolis Hurling Club began in 2002, then reformed in 2005. In 2008, the Indy Hurling Club won the Junior C National Championship. In 2011, Indy had 7 club teams and sent a Junior B, Junior C and Camogie team to nationals. Hurling continues to grow in popularity with teams now in <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_SC" class="mw-redirect" title="Charleston, SC">Charleston, SC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_FL" class="mw-redirect" title="Orlando, FL">Orlando, FL</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tampa,_FL" class="mw-redirect" title="Tampa, FL">Tampa, FL</a>, <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_GA" class="mw-redirect" title="Augusta, GA">Augusta, GA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_SC" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenville, SC">Greenville, SC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis, IN</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worcester,_Massachusetts" title="Worcester, Massachusetts">Worcester, MA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corvallis,_OR" class="mw-redirect" title="Corvallis, OR">Corvallis, OR</a>, Raleigh, NC, <a href="/wiki/Concord,_NH" class="mw-redirect" title="Concord, NH">Concord, NH</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland, Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Twin_Cities,_MN" class="mw-redirect" title="Twin Cities, MN">Providence, RI, Twin Cities, MN</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madison,_WI" class="mw-redirect" title="Madison, WI">Madison, WI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads,_VA" class="mw-redirect" title="Hampton Roads, VA">Hampton Roads, VA</a>, Rochester NY, <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_TN" class="mw-redirect" title="Nashville, TN">Nashville, TN</a>, Richmond, VA, and <a href="/wiki/Hartford,_Connecticut" title="Hartford, Connecticut">Hartford, Connecticut</a>.
</p><p>The GAA have also begun to invest in American college students with university teams springing up at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Connecticut" title="University of Connecticut">University of Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">UC Berkeley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Purdue_University" title="Purdue University">Purdue University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University" title="Indiana University">Indiana University</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Montana" title="University of Montana">University of Montana</a> and other schools. On 31 January 2009, the first ever US collegiate hurling match was held between UC Berkeley and Stanford University, organized by the newly formed California Collegiate Gaelic Athletic Association. UC Berkeley won the match by 1-point, as well as the most recent best-of-three College Cup, 2 matches to 1. On Memorial Day Weekend of 2011, the first ever National Collegiate GAA championship was played. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Indiana_University_Hurling_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Indiana University Hurling Club (page does not exist)">Indiana University Hurling Club</a> won all matches of the tournament, and won by four points in the championship final to be crowned the first ever <a href="/wiki/National_U.S._Collegiate_Hurling_Champions" title="National U.S. Collegiate Hurling Champions">U.S. National Collegiate Champions</a>.
</p>
