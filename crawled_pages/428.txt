<p>The <b>subdivisions of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a></b> constitute a hierarchy of <a href="/wiki/Administrative_division" title="Administrative division">administrative divisions</a> and non-administrative ceremonial areas.
</p><p>Overall, England is divided into <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">nine regions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">48 ceremonial counties</a>, although these have only a limited role in public policy. For the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">local government</a>, the country is divided into <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">counties</a>, <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">districts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes of England">parishes</a>. In some areas, counties and districts form a two-tier administrative structure, while in others they are combined under a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authority" title="Unitary authority">unitary authority</a>. Parishes cover only part of England.
</p><p>The current system is the result of incremental reform which has its origins in legislation enacted in 1965 and <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">1972</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regions">Regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_England" title="Regions of England">Regions of England</a></div>
<p>At the highest level, all of England is divided into nine regions that are each made up of a number of counties and districts. These 'government office regions' were created in 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-Atkinson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atkinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and since the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1999_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="European Parliament election, 1999 (UK)">1999 Euro-elections</a>, they have been used as the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_constituencies_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="European Parliament constituencies in the United Kingdom">European Parliament constituencies in the United Kingdom</a> and in England's <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> constituencies. 
</p><p>The regions vary greatly in their areas covered, populations and contributions to the national economy.<sup id="cite_ref-Atkinson_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atkinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> All have the same status, except <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">London</a> which has substantive <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">devolved powers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guide_London_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guide_London-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was a failed attempt to create <a href="/wiki/Northern_England_devolution_referendums,_2004" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern England devolution referendums, 2004">elected regional assemblies</a> outside London in 2004 and since then the structures of regional governance (<a href="/wiki/Regional_assembly_(England)" title="Regional assembly (England)">regional assemblies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regional_development_agency" title="Regional development agency">regional development agencies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Local_authority_leaders%27_board" title="Local authority leaders&#39; board">local authority leaders' boards</a>) have been subject to review.  Following the change of government in 2010, these have been scheduled for abolition by 2012.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (March 2014)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counties_and_districts">Counties and districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Counties and districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">Counties of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Districts_of_England" title="Districts of England">Districts of England</a></div>
<p>For non-administrative purposes, England is wholly divided into 48 counties,<sup id="cite_ref-LA_1997_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA_1997-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> commonly known, but not named in statute, as <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial counties</a>. These counties are used for the purposes of appointing <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lieutenant">Lords Lieutenant</a><sup id="cite_ref-LA_1997_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA_1997-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> who are historically the Crown's representatives in those areas. Ceremonial counties are often different from the metropolitan and non-metropolitan counties used for local government as they include the areas covered by unitary authorities. They are taken into consideration when drawing up Parliamentary constituency boundaries.
</p><p>For local government, England is divided into areas with a two-tier structure of counties and districts governed by two local authorities, and unitary authority areas where there is one local authority. The arrangement varies in different parts of the country and there are four main configurations: non-metropolitan two-tier 'shire' areas, six metropolitan counties, unitary authorities, and Greater London.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two-tier_non-metropolitan_counties">Two-tier non-metropolitan counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Two-tier non-metropolitan counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the geographical area of England is within a two-tier non-metropolitan arrangement. In 27 of these areas the <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> provide the majority of services, including education and social services, and the 201 district councils have a more limited role.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Non-metropolitan districts can additionally have the status of borough or city, although this has no effect on their powers or functions. All two-tier non-metropolitan counties are also ceremonial counties.<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_county_3"><a href="#endnote_county_3">C3</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> is an anomaly in this arrangement whereby its districts are unitary authorities, but the non-metropolitan county was not formally abolished<sup id="cite_ref-Berks_1996_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berks_1996-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and it is also a ceremonial county.<sup id="cite_ref-LA_1997_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LA_1997-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire" title="Bedfordshire">Bedfordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> are two former non-metropolitan counties that continue to exist only as ceremonial counties.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_counties">Metropolitan counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metropolitan counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg/300px-English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg.png" width="300" height="364" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg/450px-English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg/600px-English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1108" data-file-height="1345" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:English_regions_and_counties_by_type_2009.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="legend-line"><span style="display: inline-block; <p>Six large conurbations of England correspond to <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan counties</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Each metropolitan county had a county council providing limited strategic services, such as public transport and planning, from 1974 to 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Despite no longer having county councils the metropolitan counties still legally exist, and are each a ceremonial county. County-level functions, such as public transport, are exercised by joint-boards and other arrangements organised by the district councils. In the metropolitan counties, the 36 district councils operate effectively as unitary authorities and provide the majority of services, including education and social services.<sup id="cite_ref-Atkinson_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Atkinson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> All metropolitan districts additionally have the status of borough, and some are cities, although this has no effect on their powers or functions. From April 2011 there has been a formal upper-tier structure in Greater Manchester with the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester_Combined_Authority" title="Greater Manchester Combined Authority">Greater Manchester Combined Authority</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GMCA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GMCA-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="London">London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> administrative area created in 1965 corresponds to the London region. Through incremental change, culminating in 2000, the upper-tier authority is the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a>, comprising an elected <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">Mayor of London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Travers_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Travers-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Greater London is divided into 32 <a href="/wiki/London_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="London borough">London boroughs</a>, also dating from 1965, each governed by a London borough council. The ancient <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> forms a 33rd division and is governed by the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London_Corporation" title="City of London Corporation">City of London Corporation</a>, a <i>sui generis</i> authority unlike any other in England<sup id="cite_ref-Travers_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Travers-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> that has largely avoided any of the reforms of local government in the 19th and 20th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-Hebbert_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hebbert-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The City of London, and the rest of Greater London, each form a ceremonial county. The London borough councils and the City of London Corporation provide the majority of services, for example they are education authorities and co-ordinate waste management, whereas the Greater London Authority is responsible for the key strategic services of public transport, the police, economic development and emergency planning.<sup id="cite_ref-Travers_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Travers-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unitary_authorities">Unitary authorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unitary authorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">Unitary authorities of England</a></div>
<p>Outside London and the metropolitan counties, some parts of England are governed by a single council, commonly called (but not named in statute) as a unitary authority.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Unitary authorities are a combined non-metropolitan county and non-metropolitan district, undertaking the functions of both.<sup id="cite_ref-1992_Act_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1992_Act-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Unitary authorities can additionally have the status of <a href="/wiki/Borough_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Borough status in the United Kingdom">borough</a> or <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">city</a>, although this has no effect on their powers or functions.
</p><p>46 unitary authorities were <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1992)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1992)">created between 1995 and 1998</a> and nine more were <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">created in 2009</a>. They were formed either by non-metropolitan districts taking on county-level functions, or by counties taking on district-level functions. In some cases, borders were altered or districts were combined during this reorganisation. <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> is an anomaly in this arrangement whereby its districts became unitary authorities, but the non-metropolitan county was not formally abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-Berks_1996_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Berks_1996-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For ceremonial purposes, unitary authorities are considered to be part of the county to which they formerly belonged.  Politically, however, they are fully independent entities, unaffiliated with the council of their former county.  For instance, the unitary authority of <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a> is traditionally considered part of <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, though politically it is not part of the county.  In the case of <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, there is no county council, as all of its former territory is now covered by six unitary authorities, unaffiliated with each other politically.  Although there is no county council in Berkshire, these six unitary authorities comprise the ceremonial county of Berkshire.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isles_of_Scilly">Isles of Scilly</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Isles of Scilly">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> are governed by a <i>sui generis</i> local authority called the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_Isles_of_Scilly" title="Council of the Isles of Scilly">Council of the Isles of Scilly</a>. The authority was established in 1890 as the Isles of Scilly Rural District Council. It was renamed but otherwise unreformed by the changes in local government that occurred in 1974 in the rest of England outside Greater London.<sup id="cite_ref-1972_Act_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1972_Act-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Although effectively a unitary authority, for example it is an education authority,<sup id="cite_ref-Scilly_Education_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scilly_Education-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the Isles of Scilly are part of the <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> ceremonial county and combine with <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Council" title="Cornwall Council">Cornwall Council</a> for services such as health<sup id="cite_ref-Cornwall_PCT_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornwall_PCT-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and economic development.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornwall_Partner_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornwall_Partner-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_parishes">Civil parishes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Subdivisions_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Civil parishes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">Civil parishes in England</a></div>
<p>The civil parish is the most local unit of government in England.<sup id="cite_ref-Jones_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A parish is governed by a <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish council</a> or <a href="/wiki/Parish_meeting" title="Parish meeting">parish meeting</a>, which exercises a limited number of functions that would otherwise be delivered by the local authority. There is one civil parish in Greater London (<a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Park,_London" title="Queen&#39;s Park, London">Queen's Park</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Westminster" title="City of Westminster">City of Westminster</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and not all of the rest of England is parished. The number of parishes and total area parished is growing.
</p>
