<p><i><b>The Life and Strange Surprizing Adventures of Robinson Crusoe, Of York, Mariner: Who lived Eight and Twenty Years, all alone in an un-inhabited Island on the Coast of America, near the Mouth of the Great River of Oroonoque; Having been cast on Shore by Shipwreck, wherein all the Men perished but himself. With An Account how he was at last as strangely deliver'd by Pyrates</b></i>, commonly known as <i><b>Robinson Crusoe,</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a>, first published on 25 April 1719. The first edition credited the work's protagonist Robinson Crusoe as its author, leading many readers to believe he was a real person and the book a <a href="/wiki/Travelogue_(literature)" class="mw-redirect" title="Travelogue (literature)">travelogue</a> of true incidents.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Epistolary_novel" title="Epistolary novel">Epistolary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Confessional_writing" title="Confessional writing">confessional</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Didacticism" title="Didacticism">didactic</a> in form, the book is presented as an autobiography of the title character (whose birth name is Robinson Kreutznaer)—a <a href="/wiki/Castaway" title="Castaway">castaway</a> who spends 28 years on a remote tropical <a href="/wiki/Desert_island" title="Desert island">desert island</a> near <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, encountering <a href="/wiki/Cannibals" class="mw-redirect" title="Cannibals">cannibals</a>, captives, and mutineers, before ultimately being rescued. The story has been thought to be based on the life of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Selkirk" title="Alexander Selkirk">Alexander Selkirk</a>, a Scottish castaway who lived for four years on a Pacific island called "Más a Tierra", now part of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe_Island" title="Robinson Crusoe Island">Robinson Crusoe Island</a> in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-Severin_pp._23_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Severin_pp._23-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite its simple narrative style, <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> was well received in the literary world and is often credited as marking the beginning of realistic fiction as a literary genre. It is generally seen as a contender for the first <a href="/wiki/English_novel" title="English novel">English novel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Before the end of 1719, the book had already run through four editions, and it has gone on to become one of the most widely published books in history, spawning so many imitations, not only in literature but also in film, television and radio, that its name is used to define a genre, the <a href="/wiki/Robinsonade" title="Robinsonade">Robinsonade</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Crusoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg/200px-Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg" width="200" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg/300px-Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg/400px-Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg 2x" data-file-width="648" data-file-height="434" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robinson.Crusoe.island.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pictorial map of Crusoe's island, the "Island of Despair", showing incidents from the book</div></div></div>
<p>Crusoe (the family name corrupted from the German name "Kreutznaer") set sail from <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Kingston upon Hull</a> on a sea voyage in August 1651, against the wishes of his parents, who wanted him to pursue a career in law.  After a tumultuous journey where his ship is wrecked in a storm, his lust for the sea remains so strong that he sets out to sea again. This journey, too, ends in disaster, as the ship is taken over by <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9" title="Salé">Salé</a> <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">pirates</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9_Rovers" title="Salé Rovers">Salé Rovers</a>) and Crusoe is enslaved by a <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moor</a>.  Two years later, he escapes in a boat with a boy named Xury; a captain of a <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> ship off the west coast of Africa rescues him.  The ship is <i>en route</i> to <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">Brazil</a>. Crusoe sells Xury to the captain. With the captain's help, Crusoe procures a <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantation</a>.
</p><p>Years later, Crusoe joins an expedition to <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">bring slaves from Africa</a>, but he is shipwrecked in a storm about forty miles out to sea on an island (which he calls the Island of Despair) near the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> river on 30 September 1659.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  He observes the latitude as 9 degrees and 22 minutes north.  He sees <a href="/wiki/Penguins" class="mw-redirect" title="Penguins">penguins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinniped" title="Pinniped">seals</a> on his island.  As for his arrival there, only he and three animals, the captain's dog and two cats, survive the shipwreck.  Overcoming his despair, he fetches arms, tools and other supplies from the ship before it breaks apart and sinks.  He builds a fenced-in habitat near a cave which he excavates.  By making marks in a wooden cross, he creates a calendar.  By using tools salvaged from the ship, and some which he makes himself, he hunts, grows barley and rice, dries grapes to make raisins, learns to make pottery and raises goats.  He also adopts a small parrot.  He reads the Bible and becomes religious, thanking God for his fate in which nothing is missing but human society.
</p><p>More years pass and Crusoe discovers native <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism" title="Cannibalism">cannibals</a>, who occasionally visit the island to kill and eat prisoners.  At first he plans to kill them for committing an abomination but later realizes he has no right to do so, as the cannibals do not knowingly commit a crime.  He dreams of obtaining one or two servants by freeing some prisoners; when a prisoner escapes, Crusoe helps him, naming his new companion "<a href="/wiki/Friday_(Robinson_Crusoe)" title="Friday (Robinson Crusoe)">Friday</a>" after the day of the week he appeared. Crusoe then teaches him English and converts him to Christianity.
</p><p>After more natives arrive to partake in a cannibal feast, Crusoe and Friday kill most of the natives and save two prisoners.  One is Friday's father and the other is a Spaniard, who informs Crusoe about other Spaniards shipwrecked on the mainland.  A plan is devised wherein the Spaniard would return to the mainland with Friday's father and bring back the others, build a ship, and sail to a Spanish port.
</p><p>Before the Spaniards return, an English ship appears; mutineers have commandeered the vessel and intend to maroon their captain on the island.  Crusoe and the ship's captain strike a deal in which Crusoe helps the captain and the loyal sailors retake the ship and leave the worst mutineers on the island.  Before embarking for England, Crusoe shows the mutineers how he survived on the island and states that there will be more men coming.  Crusoe leaves the island 19 December 1686 and arrives in England on 11 June 1687.  He learns that his family believed him dead; as a result, he was left nothing in his father's will.  Crusoe departs for Lisbon to reclaim the profits of his estate in Brazil, which has granted him much wealth.  In conclusion, he transports his wealth overland to England from Portugal to avoid travelling by sea.  Friday accompanies him and, <i>en route</i>, they endure one last adventure together as they fight off famished wolves while crossing the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>.
</p>

<p>There were many stories of real-life castaways in Defoe's time, most famous, Defoe's suspected inspiration for <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> is thought to be from Scottish sailor, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Selkirk" title="Alexander Selkirk">Alexander Selkirk</a>, who spent four years on the uninhabited island of <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe_Island" title="Robinson Crusoe Island">Más a Tierra</a> (renamed Robinson Crusoe Island in 1966)<sup id="cite_ref-Severin_pp._23_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Severin_pp._23-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Islands" title="Juan Fernández Islands">Juan Fernández Islands</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean coast</a>. Selkirk was rescued in 1709 by <a href="/wiki/Woodes_Rogers" title="Woodes Rogers">Woodes Rogers</a> during an English expedition that led to the publication of Selkirk's adventures in both <a href="/wiki/A_Voyage_to_the_South_Sea,_and_Round_the_World" title="A Voyage to the South Sea, and Round the World">A Voyage to the South Sea, and Round the World</a> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Cruising_Voyage_Around_the_World&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="A Cruising Voyage Around the World (page does not exist)">A Cruising Voyage Around the World</a></i> in 1712. According to Tim Severin, "Daniel Defoe, a secretive man, neither confirmed or denied that Selkirk was the model for the hero of the his book. Apparently written in six months or less, <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> was a publishing phenomenon.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The author of <i>Crusoe's Island,</i> Andrew Lambert states, "the ideas that a single, real Crusoe is a "false premise" because Crusoe's story is a complex compound of all the other buccaneer survival stories." <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> is far from a copy of Rogers' account: Becky Little argues three events that distinguish the two stories. Robinson Crusoe was shipwrecked while Selkirk decided to leave his ship thus marooning himself; the island Crusoe was shipwrecked on had already been inhabited, unlike the solidarity of Selkirk's adventures. The last and most crucial difference between the two stories is Selkirk is a pirate, looting and raiding coastal cities. Andrew Lambert states, “The economic and dynamic thrust of the book is completely alien to what the buccaneers are doing,” Lambert says. “The buccaneers just want to capture some loot and come home and drink it all, and Crusoe isn’t doing that at all. He’s an economic imperialist. He’s creating a world of trade and profit.”
</p><p>Other possible sources for the narrative include <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Tufail" title="Ibn Tufail">Ibn Tufail</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hayy_ibn_Yaqdhan" title="Hayy ibn Yaqdhan">Hayy ibn Yaqdhan</a></i>, and Spanish sixteenth century sailor <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Serrano_(sailor)" title="Pedro Serrano (sailor)">Pedro_Serrano_(sailor)</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Tufail" title="Ibn Tufail">Ibn Tufail</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hayy_ibn_Yaqdhan" title="Hayy ibn Yaqdhan">Hayy ibn Yaqdhan</a></i>is a twelfth-century philosophical novel also set on a <a href="/wiki/Desert_island" title="Desert island">desert island</a> and translated into Latin and English a number of times in the half-century preceding Defoe's novel.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Amber_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amber-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wainwright_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pedro_Serrano_(sailor)" title="Pedro Serrano (sailor)">Pedro Luis Serrano</a> was a Spanish sailor who was marooned for seven or eight years in the sixteenth century on a small desert island after shipwrecking on a small island in the Caribbean off the coast of Nicaragua in 1520s. He had no access to fresh water and lived off the blood and flesh of sea turtles and birds. He was quite a celebrity when he returned to Europe and before passing away, he recorded the hardships suffered in documents that show, the endless anguish and suffering, the product of the most absolute abandonment to his fate and that can be found now in the Archivo General de Indias, in Seville. It's very likely that Defoe heard his story, 200 years old by then but still very popular, in one of his visits to Spain before becoming a writer.
</p><p>Yet another source for Defoe's novel may have been the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Knox_(sailor)" title="Robert Knox (sailor)">Robert Knox</a> account of his abduction by the King of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Ceylon</a> in 1659 in <i><a href="/wiki/An_Historical_Relation_of_the_Island_Ceylon" title="An Historical Relation of the Island Ceylon">An Historical Relation of the Island Ceylon</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Filreis_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filreis-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tim_Severin" title="Tim Severin">Tim Severin</a>'s book <i>Seeking Robinson Crusoe</i> (2002) unravels a much wider and more plausible range of potential sources of inspiration, and concludes by identifying castaway surgeon Henry Pitman as the most likely. An employee of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Monmouth" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Monmouth">Duke of Monmouth</a>, Pitman played a part in the <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_Rebellion" title="Monmouth Rebellion">Monmouth Rebellion</a>. His short book about his desperate escape from a Caribbean penal colony, followed by his shipwrecking and subsequent desert island misadventures, was published by <a href="/wiki/J._Taylor" class="mw-redirect" title="J. Taylor">J. Taylor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Paternoster_Row" title="Paternoster Row">Paternoster Row</a>, London, whose son William Taylor later published Defoe's novel. Severin argues that since Pitman appears to have lived in the lodgings above the father's publishing house and that Defoe himself was a mercer in the area at the time, Defoe may have met Pitman in person and learned of his experiences first-hand, or possibly through submission of a draft.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Severin also discusses another publicised case of a marooned man named only as <a href="/wiki/Will_(Indian)" title="Will (Indian)">Will</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Miskito_people" title="Miskito people">Miskito</a> people of Central America, who may have led to the depiction of <a href="/wiki/Man_Friday" class="mw-redirect" title="Man Friday">Man Friday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Wellesley_Secord&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arthur Wellesley Secord (page does not exist)">Arthur Wellesley Secord</a> in his <i>Studies in the narrative method of Defoe</i> (1963: 21–111) analyses the composition of <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> and gives a list of possible sources of the story, rejecting the common theory that the story of Selkirk is Defoe's only source.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_and_sequels">Reception and sequels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Crusoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception and sequels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG/220px-SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG" width="220" height="319" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG/330px-SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG/440px-SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2434" data-file-height="3525" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SFEC_HULL_CRUSOE1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plaque in <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Gardens,_Hull" title="Queen&#39;s Gardens, Hull">Queen's Gardens, Hull</a>, showing him on his island</div></div></div>
<p>The book was published on 25 April 1719. Before the end of the year, this first volume had run through four editions.
</p><p>By the end of the nineteenth century, no book in the history of <a href="/wiki/Western_literature" title="Western literature">Western literature</a> had more editions, spin-offs and translations (even into languages such as <a href="/wiki/Inuktitut" title="Inuktitut">Inuktitut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">Maltese</a>) than <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>, with more than 700 such alternative versions, including children's versions with pictures and no text.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Robinsonade" title="Robinsonade">Robinsonade</a>" was coined to describe the genre of stories similar to <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>.
</p><p>Defoe went on to write a lesser-known sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Farther_Adventures_of_Robinson_Crusoe" title="The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe">The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe</a></i> (1719). It was intended to be the last part of his stories, according to the original title page of the sequel's first edition, but a third book, '<a href="/w/index.php?title=%27Serious_Reflections_During_the_Life_%26_Surprising_Adventures_of_Robinson_Crusoe:_With_His_Vision_of_the_Angelic_World&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="&#39;Serious Reflections During the Life &amp; Surprising Adventures of Robinson Crusoe: With His Vision of the Angelic World (page does not exist)">'Serious Reflections During the Life &amp; Surprising Adventures of Robinson Crusoe: With His Vision of the Angelic World</a><i> (1720), was written.</i>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">Interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Crusoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robinson_Crusoe_and_Man_Friday_Offterdinger.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Robinson_Crusoe_and_Man_Friday_Offterdinger.jpg/200px-Robinson_Crusoe_and_Man_Friday_Offterdinger.jpg" width="200" height="310" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Robinson_Crusoe_and_Man_Friday_Offterdinger.jpg/300px-Robinson_Crusoe_and_Man_Friday_Offterdinger.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e0/Robinson_Crusoe_and_Man_Friday_Offterdinger.jpg 2x" data-file-width="332" data-file-height="515" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robinson_Crusoe_and_Man_Friday_Offterdinger.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crusoe standing over <a href="/wiki/Friday_(Robinson_Crusoe)" title="Friday (Robinson Crusoe)">Friday</a> after he frees him from the cannibals</div></div></div>
<p>Novelist <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a> noted that the true symbol of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> is Robinson Crusoe, to whom he ascribed stereotypical and somewhat hostile English racial characteristics: "He is the true prototype of the British colonist.&#160;... The whole Anglo-Saxon spirit in Crusoe: the manly independence, the unconscious cruelty, the persistence, the slow yet efficient intelligence, the sexual apathy, the calculating taciturnity."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In a sense Crusoe attempts to replicate his society on the island.  This is achieved through the use of European technology, agriculture and even a rudimentary political hierarchy. Several times in the novel Crusoe refers to himself as the "king" of the island, whilst the captain describes him as the "governor" to the mutineers. At the very end of the novel the island is explicitly referred to as a "colony". The idealised master-servant relationship Defoe depicts between Crusoe and Friday can also be seen in terms of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_imperialism" title="Cultural imperialism">cultural imperialism</a>. Crusoe represents the "enlightened" European whilst Friday is the "savage" who can only be redeemed from his barbarous way of life through assimilation into Crusoe's culture. Nonetheless Defoe also takes the opportunity to criticise the historic Spanish conquest of South America.
</p><p>According to J. P. Hunter, Robinson is not a hero but an <a href="/wiki/Everyman" title="Everyman">everyman</a>. He begins as a wanderer, aimless on a sea he does not understand, and ends as a <a href="/wiki/Pilgrim" title="Pilgrim">pilgrim</a>, crossing a final mountain to enter the <a href="/wiki/Promised_land" class="mw-redirect" title="Promised land">promised land</a>. The book tells the story of how Robinson becomes closer to God, not through listening to <a href="/wiki/Sermon" title="Sermon">sermons</a> in a church but through spending time alone amongst <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> with only a Bible to read.
</p><p>Conversely, cultural critic and literary scholar Michael Gurnow views the novel from a <a href="/wiki/Rousseau" class="mw-redirect" title="Rousseau">Rousseauian</a> perspective. In "'The Folly of Beginning a Work Before We Count the Cost': Anarcho-Primitivism in Daniel Defoe's Robinson Crusoe", the central character's movement from a primitive state to a more civilized one is interpreted as Crusoe's denial of humanity's <a href="/wiki/State_of_nature" title="State of nature">state of nature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Robinson Crusoe</i> is filled with religious aspects. Defoe was a <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> moralist and normally worked in the guide tradition, writing books on how to be a good Puritan Christian, such as <i>The New Family Instructor</i> (1727) and <i>Religious Courtship</i> (1722). While <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> is far more than a guide, it shares many of the themes and theological and moral points of view. "Crusoe" may have been taken from Timothy Cruso, a classmate of Defoe's who had written guide books, including <i>God the Guide of Youth</i> (1695), before dying at an early age – just eight years before Defoe wrote <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>. Cruso would have been remembered by contemporaries and the association with guide books is clear. It has even been speculated that <i>God the Guide of Youth</i> inspired <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> because of a number of passages in that work that are closely tied to the novel.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A leitmotif of the novel is the Christian notion of <a href="/wiki/Divine_providence" title="Divine providence">Providence</a>, penitence and redemption.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Crusoe comes to repent of the follies of his youth. Defoe also foregrounds this theme by arranging highly significant events in the novel to occur on Crusoe's birthday. The denouement culminates not only in Crusoe's deliverance from the island, but his spiritual deliverance, his acceptance of Christian doctrine, and in his intuition of his own salvation.
</p><p>When confronted with the cannibals, Crusoe wrestles with the problem of <a href="/wiki/Cultural_relativism" title="Cultural relativism">cultural relativism</a>. Despite his disgust, he feels unjustified in holding the natives morally responsible for a practice so deeply ingrained in their culture. Nevertheless, he retains his belief in an absolute standard of morality; he regards cannibalism as a "national crime" and forbids Friday from practising it.
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe_economy" title="Robinson Crusoe economy">Robinson Crusoe economy</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_economics" title="Classical economics">classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">neoclassical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austrian_economics" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian economics">Austrian economics</a>, Crusoe is regularly used to illustrate the theory of production and choice in the absence of trade, money and prices.<sup id="cite_ref-isbn0-393-95924-4_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isbn0-393-95924-4-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Crusoe must allocate effort between production and leisure and must choose between alternative production possibilities to meet his needs. The arrival of Friday is then used to illustrate the possibility of trade and the gains that result.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tim_Severin" title="Tim Severin">Tim Severin</a>'s book <i>Seeking Robinson Crusoe</i> (2002) unravels a much wider range of potential sources of inspiration.  Severin concludes his investigations by stating that the real Robinson Crusoe figure was Henry Pitman, a castaway who had been surgeon to the <a href="/wiki/James_Scott,_1st_Duke_of_Monmouth" title="James Scott, 1st Duke of Monmouth">Duke of Monmouth</a>.  Pitman's short book about his desperate escape from a Caribbean penal colony for his part in the <a href="/wiki/Monmouth_Rebellion" title="Monmouth Rebellion">Monmouth Rebellion</a>, his shipwrecking and subsequent desert island misadventures was published by J. Taylor of <a href="/wiki/Paternoster_Row" title="Paternoster Row">Paternoster Street</a>, London, whose son William Taylor later published Defoe's novel.  Severin argues that since Pitman appears to have lived in the lodgings above the father's publishing house and since Defoe was a <a href="/wiki/Mercery" title="Mercery">mercer</a> in the area at the time, Defoe may have met Pitman and learned of his experiences as a castaway.  If he did not meet Pitman, Severin points out that Defoe, upon submitting even a draft of a novel about a castaway to his publisher, would undoubtedly have learned about Pitman's book published by his father, especially since the interesting castaway had previously lodged with them at their former premises.
</p><p>Severin also provides evidence in his book that another publicised case<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> of a real-life marooned <a href="/wiki/Miskito_people" title="Miskito people">Miskito</a> Central American man named only as Will may have caught Defoe's attention, inspiring the depiction of <a href="/wiki/Friday_(Robinson_Crusoe)" title="Friday (Robinson Crusoe)">Man Friday</a> in his novel.
</p>

<p>The novel has been variously read as an allegory for the development of civilisation, as a manifesto of economic individualism and as an expression of European colonial desires but it also shows the importance of repentance and illustrates the strength of Defoe's religious convictions.  Critics such as Maximillian E. Novak support the connection between the religious and economic themes within <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>, citing Defoe's religious ideology as the influence for his portrayal of Crusoe's economic ideals and his support of the individual.  Within Novak's article, "Robinson Crusoe's 'Original Sin'", Novak cites <a href="/wiki/Ian_Watt" title="Ian Watt">Ian Watt</a>'s extensive research in Watt's book, <i>Myths of Modern Individualism: Faust, Don Quixote, Don Juan, Robinson Crusoe</i>, in which Watt explores the impact that several Romantic Era novels had against economic individualism, and the reversal of those ideals that takes place within <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  In Tess Lewis' review, "The Heroes We Deserve", of Ian Watt's article, she furthers Watt’s argument with a development on Defoe's intention as an author, "to use individualism to signify nonconformity in religion and the admirable qualities of self-reliance", (Lewis 678).&#160;&#160;This further supports the belief that Defoe used aspects of a spiritual biography in order to introduce the benefits of individualism to a not entirely convinced ideological community.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  It is also considered by many to be the <a href="/wiki/First_novel_in_English" class="mw-redirect" title="First novel in English">first novel written in English</a>.  Early critics, such as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a>, admired it, saying that the footprint scene in <i>Crusoe</i> was one of the four greatest in English literature and most unforgettable; more prosaically, Dr. Wesley Vernon has seen the origins of <a href="/wiki/Forensic_podiatry" title="Forensic podiatry">forensic podiatry</a> in this episode.<sup id="cite_ref-west_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-west-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  It has inspired a new genre, the <a href="/wiki/Robinsonade" title="Robinsonade">Robinsonade</a>, as works like <a href="/wiki/Johann_David_Wyss" title="Johann David Wyss">Johann David Wyss</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Swiss_Family_Robinson" title="The Swiss Family Robinson">The Swiss Family Robinson</a></i> (1812) adapt its premise and has provoked modern <a href="/wiki/Postcolonialism" title="Postcolonialism">postcolonial</a> responses, including <a href="/wiki/J._M._Coetzee" title="J. M. Coetzee">J. M. Coetzee</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Foe_(novel)" title="Foe (novel)">Foe</a></i> (1986) and <a href="/wiki/Michel_Tournier" title="Michel Tournier">Michel Tournier</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Vendredi_ou_les_Limbes_du_Pacifique" class="mw-redirect" title="Vendredi ou les Limbes du Pacifique">Vendredi ou les Limbes du Pacifique</a></i> (in English, <i>Friday, or, The Other Island</i>) (1967).  Two sequels followed, Defoe's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Farther_Adventures_of_Robinson_Crusoe" title="The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe">The Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe</a></i> (1719) and his <i>Serious reflections during the life and surprising adventures of Robinson Crusoe: with his Vision of the angelick world</i> (1720).  <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels" title="Gulliver&#39;s Travels">Gulliver's Travels</a></i> (1726) in part parodies Defoe's adventure novel.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Crusoe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Robinson_Crusoe&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(September 2010)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The book proved so popular that the names of the two main protagonists have entered the language. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, people who decided to stay and hide in the <a href="/wiki/Planned_destruction_of_Warsaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Planned destruction of Warsaw">ruins of the German-occupied city</a> of <a href="/wiki/Warsaw" title="Warsaw">Warsaw</a> for a period of three winter months, from October to January 1945, when they were rescued by the <a href="/wiki/Red_Army" title="Red Army">Red Army</a>, were later called <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoes_of_Warsaw" title="Robinson Crusoes of Warsaw">Robinson Crusoes of Warsaw</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Robinson Crusoe usually referred to his servant as "my man Friday", from which the term "<a href="/wiki/Man_Friday" class="mw-redirect" title="Man Friday">Man Friday</a>" (or "Girl Friday") originated.
</p><p><i>Robinson Crusoe</i> marked the beginning of realistic fiction as a literary genre.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Its success led to many imitators, and castaway novels, written by Ambrose Evans, <a href="/wiki/Penelope_Aubin" title="Penelope Aubin">Penelope Aubin</a>, and others, became quite popular in Europe in the 18th and early 19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Most of these have fallen into obscurity, but some became established, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Swiss_Family_Robinson" title="The Swiss Family Robinson">The Swiss Family Robinson</a></i>, which borrowed Crusoe's first name for its title.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Swift" title="Jonathan Swift">Jonathan Swift</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels" title="Gulliver&#39;s Travels">Gulliver's Travels</a></i>, published seven years after <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>, may be read as a systematic rebuttal of Defoe's optimistic account of human capability. In <i>The Unthinkable Swift: The Spontaneous Philosophy of a Church of England Man</i>, <a href="/wiki/Warren_Montag" title="Warren Montag">Warren Montag</a> argues that Swift was concerned about refuting the notion that the individual precedes society, as Defoe's novel seems to suggest. In <i><a href="/wiki/Treasure_Island" title="Treasure Island">Treasure Island</a></i>, author <a href="/wiki/Robert_Louis_Stevenson" title="Robert Louis Stevenson">Robert Louis Stevenson</a> parodies Crusoe with the character of <a href="/wiki/Ben_Gunn_(Treasure_Island)" title="Ben Gunn (Treasure Island)">Ben Gunn</a>, a friendly castaway who was marooned for many years, has a wild appearance, dresses entirely in goat skin and constantly talks about providence.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a>'s treatise on education, <i><a href="/wiki/Emile,_or_On_Education" title="Emile, or On Education">Emile, or On Education</a></i>, the one book the protagonist is allowed to read before the age of twelve is <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>. Rousseau wants Emile to identify himself as Crusoe so he can rely upon himself for all of his needs. In Rousseau's view, Emile needs to imitate Crusoe's experience, allowing necessity to determine what is to be learned and accomplished. This is one of the main themes of Rousseau's educational model.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG/220px-Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG/330px-Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG/440px-Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2773" data-file-height="2275" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robinson_crusoe_bookstore.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robinson Crusoe Bookstore on <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0stiklal_Avenue" title="İstiklal Avenue">İstiklal Avenue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_Little_Pig_Robinson" title="The Tale of Little Pig Robinson">The Tale of Little Pig Robinson</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Beatrix_Potter" title="Beatrix Potter">Beatrix Potter</a> directs the reader to <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> for a detailed description of the island (the land of the Bong tree) to which her eponymous hero moves. In <a href="/wiki/Wilkie_Collins" title="Wilkie Collins">Wilkie Collins</a>' most popular novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Moonstone" title="The Moonstone">The Moonstone</a></i>, one of the chief characters and narrators, Gabriel Betteredge, has faith in all that Robinson Crusoe says and uses the book for a sort of <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>. He considers <i>The Adventures of Robinson Crusoe</i> the finest book ever written, reads it over and over again, and considers a man but poorly read if he had happened not to read the book.
</p><p>French novelist <a href="/wiki/Michel_Tournier" title="Michel Tournier">Michel Tournier</a> published <i><a href="/wiki/Friday,_or,_The_Other_Island" title="Friday, or, The Other Island">Friday, or, The Other Island</a></i> (French <i>Vendredi ou les Limbes du Pacifique</i>) in 1967. His novel explores themes including civilization versus nature, the psychology of solitude, as well as death and sexuality in a retelling of Defoe's <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> story. Tournier's Robinson chooses to remain on the island, rejecting civilization when offered the chance to escape 28 years after being shipwrecked. Likewise, in 1963, <a href="/wiki/J._M._G._Le_Cl%C3%A9zio" title="J. M. G. Le Clézio">J. M. G. Le Clézio</a>, winner of the 2008 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize" title="Nobel Prize">Nobel Prize</a> in literature, published the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Le_Proces-Verbal" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Proces-Verbal">Le Proces-Verbal</a></i>. The book's <a href="/wiki/Epigraph_(literature)" title="Epigraph (literature)">epigraph</a> is a quote from <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>, and like Crusoe, Adam Pollo suffers long periods of loneliness.
</p><p>"Crusoe in England", a 183-line poem by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Bishop" title="Elizabeth Bishop">Elizabeth Bishop</a>, imagines Crusoe near the end of his life, recalling his time of exile with a mixture of bemusement and regret.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/J._M._Coetzee" title="J. M. Coetzee">J. M. Coetzee</a>'s 1986 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Foe_(novel)" title="Foe (novel)">Foe</a></i> recounts the tale of Robinson Crusoe from the perspective of a woman named Susan Barton.
</p><p>The story was also illustrated and published in comic book form by <i><a href="/wiki/Classics_Illustrated" title="Classics Illustrated">Classics Illustrated</a></i> in 1943 and 1957. The much improved 1957 version was inked/penciled by Sam Citron, who is most well known for his contributions to the earlier issues of <i><a href="/wiki/Superman_(comic_book)" title="Superman (comic book)">Superman</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomime</a> version of <i>Robinson Crusoe</i> was staged at the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Royal,_Drury_Lane" title="Theatre Royal, Drury Lane">Theatre Royal, Drury Lane</a> in 1796, with <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Grimaldi" title="Joseph Grimaldi">Joseph Grimaldi</a> as <a href="/wiki/Harlequinade#Pierrot" title="Harlequinade">Pierrot</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Harlequinade" title="Harlequinade">harlequinade</a>. The piece was produced again in 1798, this time starring Grimaldi as <a href="/wiki/Harlequinade#Pierrot" title="Harlequinade">Clown</a>. In 1815, Grimaldi played Friday in another version of <i>Robinson Crusoe</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jacques_Offenbach" title="Jacques Offenbach">Jacques Offenbach</a> wrote an <a href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ra_comique" title="Opéra comique">opéra comique</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Cruso%C3%A9" title="Robinson Crusoé">Robinson Crusoé</a></i>, which was first performed at the <a href="/wiki/Op%C3%A9ra-Comique" title="Opéra-Comique">Opéra-Comique</a> in Paris on 23 November 1867. This was based on the British pantomime version rather than the novel itself. The libretto was by Eugène Cormon and <a href="/wiki/Hector-Jonathan_Cr%C3%A9mieux" title="Hector-Jonathan Crémieux">Hector-Jonathan Crémieux</a>.
</p><p>There is a 1927 silent film titled <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe_(1927_film)" title="Robinson Crusoe (1927 film)">Robinson Crusoe</a></i>. The Soviet 3D film <i><a href="/wiki/Robinzon_Kruzo" class="mw-redirect" title="Robinzon Kruzo">Robinzon Kruzo</a></i> was produced in 1946. <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">Luis Buñuel</a> directed <i><a href="/wiki/Adventures_of_Robinson_Crusoe" class="mw-redirect" title="Adventures of Robinson Crusoe">Adventures of Robinson Crusoe</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Dan_O%27Herlihy" title="Dan O&#39;Herlihy">Dan O'Herlihy</a>, released in 1954. <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a> later comedicized the novel with <i><a href="/wiki/Lt._Robin_Crusoe,_U.S.N." title="Lt. Robin Crusoe, U.S.N.">Lt. Robin Crusoe, U.S.N.</a></i>, featuring <a href="/wiki/Dick_Van_Dyke" title="Dick Van Dyke">Dick Van Dyke</a>. In this version, Friday became a beautiful woman, but named 'Wednesday' instead. <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Toole" title="Peter O&#39;Toole">Peter O'Toole</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Roundtree" title="Richard Roundtree">Richard Roundtree</a> co-starred in a 1975 film <i><a href="/wiki/Man_Friday_(1975_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Man Friday (1975 film)">Man Friday</a></i> which sardonically portrayed Crusoe as incapable of seeing his dark-skinned companion as anything but an inferior creature, while Friday is more enlightened and sympathetic. In 1988, <a href="/wiki/Aidan_Quinn" title="Aidan Quinn">Aidan Quinn</a> portrayed Robinson Crusoe in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Crusoe_(film)" title="Crusoe (film)">Crusoe</a></i>. A 1997 movie entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe_(1997_film)" title="Robinson Crusoe (1997 film)">Robinson Crusoe</a></i> starred <a href="/wiki/Pierce_Brosnan" title="Pierce Brosnan">Pierce Brosnan</a> and received limited commercial success. Variations on the theme include the 1954 <i><a href="/wiki/Miss_Robin_Crusoe" title="Miss Robin Crusoe">Miss Robin Crusoe</a></i>, with a female castaway, played by <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Blake" title="Amanda Blake">Amanda Blake</a>, and a female Friday, and the 1964 film <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe_on_Mars" title="Robinson Crusoe on Mars">Robinson Crusoe on Mars</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mantee" title="Paul Mantee">Paul Mantee</a>, with an alien Friday portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Victor_Lundin" title="Victor Lundin">Victor Lundin</a> and an added character played by <a href="/wiki/Adam_West" title="Adam West">Adam West</a>. The 2000 film <i><a href="/wiki/Cast_Away" title="Cast Away">Cast Away</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a> as a FedEx employee stranded on an Island for many years, also borrows much from the Robinson Crusoe story.
</p><p>In 1964 a French film production crew made a 13-part serial of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Robinson_Crusoe_(TV_series)" title="The Adventures of Robinson Crusoe (TV series)">The Adventures of Robinson Crusoe</a></i>. It starred <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hoffmann" title="Robert Hoffmann">Robert Hoffmann</a>. The black and white series was dubbed into English and German. In the UK, the BBC broadcast it on numerous occasions between 1965 and 1977. In 1981 <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakian</a> director and animator <a href="/wiki/Stanislav_L%C3%A1tal" title="Stanislav Látal">Stanislav Látal</a> made a version of the story under the name <i><a href="/wiki/Adventures_of_Robinson_Crusoe,_a_Sailor_from_York" title="Adventures of Robinson Crusoe, a Sailor from York">Dobrodružství Robinsona Crusoe, námořníka z Yorku</a></i> (The Adventures of Robinson Crusoe, a sailor from York) combining traditional and stop-motion animation. The movie was coproduced by regional West Germany broadcaster Sudwestfunk Baden-Baden.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dean_(South_Korean_singer)" title="Dean (South Korean singer)">Dean</a> briefly mentions Crusoe in one of his music videos. In the official music video for Instagram, there is a part when viewers hear Dean's distorted voice; "Sometimes, I feel alone . . . I feel like I'm Robinson Crusoe . . ."
</p>
