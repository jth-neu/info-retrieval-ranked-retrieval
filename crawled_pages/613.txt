<p>The <b>Union of the Crowns</b> (<a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <i lang="gd">Aonadh nan Crùintean</i>; <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>: <i lang="sco">Union o the Crouns</i>) was the <a href="/wiki/Coronation" title="Coronation">accession</a> of <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a> to the thrones of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a>, and the consequential unification for some purposes (such as overseas diplomacy) of the three <a href="/wiki/Realms" class="mw-redirect" title="Realms">realms</a> under a single monarch on 24 March 1603. The Union of Crowns followed the death of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>, the last monarch of the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor dynasty</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> who was James's unmarried and childless <a href="/wiki/Cousin#1stTwiceRem" title="Cousin">first cousin twice removed</a>.
</p><p>The Union was a <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">personal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dynastic_union" title="Dynastic union">dynastic</a> union, with the <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">Crown</a> of Scotland remaining both distinct and separate—despite James's best efforts to create a new "imperial" throne of "Great Britain".  <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> continued as autonomous states sharing a monarch with Ireland (with an <a href="/wiki/Interregnum_(1649%E2%80%931660)" title="Interregnum (1649–1660)">interregnum</a> in the 1650s during the <a href="/wiki/Tender_of_Union" title="Tender of Union">republican unitary state</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth (England)">Commonwealth</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">the Protectorate</a>), until the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Acts of Union</a> of 1707 during the reign of the last <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">Stuart monarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Queen_of_Great_Britain" title="Anne, Queen of Great Britain">Anne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_unification">Early unification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Union_of_the_Crowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early unification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Greenwich" title="Treaty of Greenwich">Treaty of Greenwich</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Margaret_Tudor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Margaret_Tudor.jpg/220px-Margaret_Tudor.jpg" width="220" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Margaret_Tudor.jpg/330px-Margaret_Tudor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8f/Margaret_Tudor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="384" data-file-height="460" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Margaret_Tudor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Tudor" title="Margaret Tudor">Margaret Tudor</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In August 1503, <a href="/wiki/James_IV_of_Scotland" title="James IV of Scotland">James IV of Scotland</a> married <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Tudor" title="Margaret Tudor">Margaret</a>, eldest daughter of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII of England</a>, and the spirit of the new age was celebrated by the poet <a href="/wiki/William_Dunbar" title="William Dunbar">William Dunbar</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thrissil_and_the_Rois" title="The Thrissil and the Rois">The Thrissil and the Rois</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The marriage was the outcome of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Perpetual_Peace_(1502)" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Perpetual Peace (1502)">Treaty of Perpetual Peace</a>, concluded the previous year, which, in theory, ended centuries of Anglo-Scottish rivalry. The marriage brought Scotland's <a href="/wiki/House_of_Stuart" title="House of Stuart">Stuarts</a> into England's <a href="/wiki/Tudor_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor dynasty">Tudor</a> <a href="/wiki/Line_of_succession" class="mw-redirect" title="Line of succession">line of succession</a>, despite the improbability of a Scottish prince acceding the English throne at the time. However, many on the English side were concerned by the dynastic implications of matrimony, including some <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_England" title="Privy Council of England">Privy Councillors</a>. In countering these fears Henry VII is reputed to have said:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>our realme wald receive na damage thair thorow, for in that caise Ingland wald not accress unto Scotland, bot Scotland wald acress unto Ingland, as to the most noble heid of the hole yle...evin as quhan Normandy came in the power of Inglis men our forbearis.
</p></blockquote>
<p>The peace did not last in "perpetuity"; it was disturbed in 1513 when <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a>, who had succeeded his father four years before, declared war on <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">France</a>. In response France invoked the terms of the <a href="/wiki/Auld_Alliance" title="Auld Alliance">Auld Alliance</a>, her ancient bond with Scotland. James duly invaded <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a> leading to the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Flodden" title="Battle of Flodden">Battle of Flodden</a>.
</p><p>In the decades that followed, <a href="/wiki/Category:England%E2%80%93Scotland_relations" title="Category:England–Scotland relations">England's relations with Scotland</a> were turbulent.  By the middle of Henry's reign, the problems of the royal succession, which seemed so unimportant in 1503, acquired ever bigger dimensions, when the question of Tudor fertility – or the lack thereof – entered directly into the political arena.  Margaret's line was excluded from the English succession, though, during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth I">Elizabeth I</a> concerns were once again raised.  In the last decade of her reign it was clear to all that <a href="/wiki/James_VI_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="James VI of Scotland">James VI of Scotland</a>, great-grandson of James IV and Margaret, was the only generally acceptable heir.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accession_of_James_VI">Accession of James VI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Union_of_the_Crowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Accession of James VI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JamesIofEngland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/15/JamesIofEngland.jpg" width="180" height="247" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="180" data-file-height="247" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JamesIofEngland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>James VI of Scotland.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_(1).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_%281%29.JPG/220px-0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_%281%29.JPG" width="220" height="295" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_%281%29.JPG/330px-0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_%281%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_%281%29.JPG/440px-0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_%281%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2738" data-file-height="3676" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:0_L%27Angleterre_et_l%27%C3%89cosse_avec_Minerve_et_l%27Amour_-_P.P._Rubens_(1).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"England and Scotland with Minerva and Love" Allegorical work of the Union of the Crowns by <a href="/wiki/Peter_Paul_Rubens" title="Peter Paul Rubens">Peter Paul Rubens</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>From 1601, in the last years of Elizabeth I's life, certain English politicians, notably her chief minister <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cecil,_1st_Earl_of_Salisbury" title="Robert Cecil, 1st Earl of Salisbury">Sir Robert Cecil</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> maintained a <a href="/wiki/Secret_correspondence_of_James_VI" title="Secret correspondence of James VI">secret correspondence with James</a> to prepare in advance for a smooth succession. Cecil advised James not to press the matter of the succession upon the queen but simply to treat her with kindness and respect.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The approach proved effective: "I trust that you will not doubt," Elizabeth wrote to James, "but that your last letters are so acceptably taken as my thanks cannot be lacking for the same, but yield them you in grateful sort."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In March 1603, with the queen clearly dying, Cecil sent James a draft proclamation of his accession to the English throne. Strategic fortresses were put on alert, and London placed under guard. Elizabeth died in the early hours of 24 March. Within eight hours, James was proclaimed king in London, the news received without protest or disturbance.<sup id="cite_ref-Croftp49_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Croftp49-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 5 April 1603, James left <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> for London, promising to return every three years (a promise he failed to keep, returning only once, in 1617, fourteen years after his initial departure),<sup id="cite_ref-Croftp49_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Croftp49-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  and progressed slowly from town to town, in  order to arrive in the capital after Elizabeth's funeral.<sup id="cite_ref-Croftp49_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Croftp49-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Local lords received James with lavish hospitality along the route; and James's new subjects flocked to see him, relieved above all that the succession had triggered neither unrest nor invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As James entered London, he was mobbed. The crowds of people, one observer reported, were so great that "they covered the beauty of the fields; and so greedy were they to behold the King that they injured and hurt one another."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> James's English coronation took place on 25 July, with elaborate allegories provided by dramatic poets such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dekker_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Dekker (poet)">Thomas Dekker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>, though the festivities had to be restricted because of an outbreak of the plague.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, all London turned out for the occasion: "The streets seemed paved with men," wrote Dekker. "Stalls instead of rich wares were set out with children, open casements filled up with women".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whatever residual fears many in England may have felt at the prospect of being ruled by a Scot, James's arrival aroused a mood of high expectation. The twilight years of Elizabeth had been a disappointment; and for a nation troubled for so many years by the question of succession, the new king was a family man who already had male heirs in the wing. But James's honeymoon was of very short duration; and his initial political actions were to do much to create the rather negative tone which was to turn a successful Scottish king into a disappointing English one. The greatest and most obvious of these was the question of his exact status and title. James intended to be King of Great Britain and Ireland. His first obstacle along this imperial road was the attitude of the English Parliament.
</p><p>In his first speech to his southern assembly on 19 March 1604 James gave a clear statement of the royal manifesto:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>What God hath conjoined let no man separate. I am the husband and the whole isle is my lawful wife; I am the head and it is my body; I am the shepherd and it is my flock. I hope therefore that no man will think that I, a Christian King under the Gospel, should be a polygamist and husband to two wives; that I being the head should have a divided or monstrous body or that being the shepherd to so fair a flock should have my flock parted in two.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Parliament may very well have rejected polygamy; but the marriage, if marriage it was, between the realms of England and Scotland was to be at best morganatic. James's ambitions were greeted with very little enthusiasm, as one by one MPs rushed to defend the ancient name and realm of England. All sorts of legal objections were raised: all laws would have to be renewed and all treaties renegotiated. For James, whose experience of parliaments was limited to the stage-managed and semi-feudal Scottish variety, the self-assurance — and obduracy — of the English version, which had long experience of upsetting monarchs, was an obvious shock. He decided to side-step the whole issue by unilaterally assuming the title of King of Great Britain by a <i>Proclamation concerning the Kings Majesties Stile</i> on 20 October 1604 announcing that he did "assume to Our selfe by the cleerenesse of our Right, The Name and Stile of KING OF GREAT BRITTAINE, FRANCE AND IRELAND, DEFENDER OF THE FAITH, &amp;c." .<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This only deepened the offence. Even in Scotland there was little real enthusiasm for the project, though the two parliaments were eventually prodded into taking the whole matter 'under consideration'.  Consider it they did for several years, never drawing the desired conclusion.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_the_union">Opposition to the union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Union_of_the_Crowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Opposition to the union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_debate_on_the_Union" title="Jacobean debate on the Union">Jacobean debate on the Union</a></div>
<p>In Scotland there were early signs that many saw the risk of the "lesser being drawn by the greater", as Henry VII once predicted. An example before Scottish eyes was the case of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Ireland</a>, a kingdom in name, but – since 1601 – a subject nation in practice. John Russell, lawyer and writer, an initial enthusiast for "the happie and blissed Unioun betuixt the tua ancienne realmes of Scotland and Ingland" was later to warn James:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Lett it not begyne vith ane comedie, and end in ane tragedie; to be ane verball unioun in disparitie nor reall in conformity…thairby, to advance the ane kingdome, to great honor and beccome forȝetfull of the uther, sua to mak the samyn altogidder solitat and desoltat qhilk cannot stand vith your Majestie's honor. As god hes heichlie advanceit your Majestie lett Scotland qhilk is ȝour auldest impyir be partakeris of ȝour blissings.
</p></blockquote>
<p>These fears were echoed by the Scottish Parliament. Its members were telling the king that they were "confident" that his plans for an incorporating union would not prejudice the ancient <a href="/wiki/Laws_and_liberties_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws and liberties of Scotland">laws and liberties of Scotland</a>; for any such hurt would mean that "it culd no more be a frie monarchie". James attempted to reassure his new English subjects that the new union would be much like that between England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and that if Scotland should refuse "he would compel their assents, having a stronger party there than the opposite party of the mutineers". In June 1604 the two national parliaments passed acts appointing commissioners to explore the possibility of "a more perfect union". James closed the final session of his first parliament with a rebuke to his opponents in the House of Commons — "Here all things suspected...He merits to be buried in the bottom of the sea that shall but think of separation, where God had made such a Union".
</p><p>The Union Commission made some limited progress, on discrete issues such as hostile border laws, trade and citizenship: the borders were to become the "middle shires". <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">Free trade</a> proved contentious, as did the issue of equal rights before the law. Fears were openly expressed in the Westminster Parliament that English jobs would be threatened by all the poor people of the realm of Scotland, who will "draw near to the Sonn, and flocking hither in such Multitudes, that death and dearth is very probable to ensue". The exact status of the <i>post nati</i>, those born after the Union of March 1603, was not decided by Parliament, but in the courts by <i><a href="/wiki/Calvin%27s_Case" title="Calvin&#39;s Case">Calvin's Case</a></i> (1608), which extended property rights to all the King's subjects in <a href="/wiki/English_common_law" class="mw-redirect" title="English common law">English common law</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_animosity">National animosity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Union_of_the_Crowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: National animosity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scottish aristocrats and other place seekers made their way to London, to compete for high positions in government. Several years later Sir Anthony Weldon was to write that
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Scotland was too guid for those that inhabit it, and too bad for others to be at the charge of conquering it. The ayre might be wholesome, but for the stinking people that inhabit it...Thair beastis be generallie small (women excepted) of which sort there are no greater in the world.
</p></blockquote>
<p>A wounding observation came in the comedy <i>Eastward Ho,</i> a collaboration between <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Chapman" title="George Chapman">George Chapman</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Marston_(poet)" title="John Marston (poet)">John Marston</a>. In enthusing over the good life to be had in the colony of <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_Virginia" title="Colony of Virginia">Virginia</a> it is observed:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>And then you shal live freely there, without Sergeants, or Courtiers, or Lawyers, or Intelligencers – onely a few industrious Scots perhaps, who indeed are disperst over the face of the whole earth. But as for them, there are no greater friends of Englishmen and England, when they are out an't, in the world, then they are. And for my part, I would a hundred thousand of them were there, for wee are all one Countrymen now, yee know; and wee shoulde finde ten times more comfort of them there, then wee do here.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Anti-English satires proliferated, and in 1609 the king had an act passed, promising the direst penalties against the writers of "pasquillis, libellis, rymis, cockalanis, comedies and sicklyk occasiones whereby they slander and maligne and revile the estait and countrey of England..."
</p><p>In October 1605 the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Venetian</a> ambassador in London noted "the question of the Union will, I am assured, be dropped; for His Majesty is now well aware that nothing can be effected, both sides displaying such obstinacy that an accommodation is impossible; and so his Majesty is resolved to abandon the question for the present, in hope that time may consume the ill-humours."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbols_of_the_union">Symbols of the union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Union_of_the_Crowns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Symbols of the union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>King James devised new coats of arms and a uniform coinage. The creation of a national flag proved contentious, designs acceptable to one side typically offending the other. James finally proclaimed the new Union Flag on 12 April 1606: Scots who saw in it a <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_England" title="Flag of England">St. George's Cross</a> superimposed upon a <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Scotland" title="Flag of Scotland">St. Andrew's Saltire</a> sought to create their own 'Scotch' design which saw the reverse superimposition take place. (This design was used in Scotland until 1707.) For years afterwards vessels of the two nations continued to fly their respective "flags", the royal proclamation notwithstanding. The <a href="/wiki/Union_Flag" class="mw-redirect" title="Union Flag">Union Flag</a> only entered into common use under Cromwell's <a href="/wiki/The_Protectorate" title="The Protectorate">Protectorate</a>.
</p>
