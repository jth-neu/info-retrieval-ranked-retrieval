<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Internet bot</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>An <b>internet bot</b>, also known as <b>web robot</b>, <b>WWW robot</b> or simply <b>bot</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Software_application" class="mw-redirect" title="Software application">software application</a> that runs automated tasks (scripts) over the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Typically, bots perform tasks that are both simple and structurally repetitive, at a much higher rate than would be possible for a human alone. The largest use of bots is in <a href="/wiki/Web_crawler" title="Web crawler">web spidering</a> (<i>web crawler</i>), in which an automated script fetches, analyzes and files information from web servers at many times the speed of a human. More than half of all web traffic is made up of bots.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeifman_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeifman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Efforts by servers hosting websites to counteract bots vary. Servers may choose to outline rules on the behaviour of internet bots by implementing a  <code><a href="/wiki/Robots.txt" class="mw-redirect" title="Robots.txt">robots.txt</a></code> file: this file is simply text stating the rules governing a bot's behaviour on that server. Any bot interacting with (or 'spidering') any server that does not follow these rules should, in theory, be denied access to, or removed from, the affected website. If the only rule implementation by a server is a posted text file with no associated program/software/app, then adhering to those rules is entirely voluntary – in reality there is no way to enforce those rules, or even to ensure that a bot's creator or implementer acknowledges, or even reads, the robots.txt file contents. Some bots are "good" – e.g. search engine spiders – while others can be used to launch malicious and harsh attacks, most notably, in political campaigns.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeifman_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeifman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="IM_and_IRC">IM and IRC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: IM and IRC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some bots communicate with other users of Internet-based services, via <a href="/wiki/Instant_messaging" title="Instant messaging">instant messaging</a> (IM), <a href="/wiki/Internet_Relay_Chat" title="Internet Relay Chat">Internet Relay Chat</a> (IRC), or another web interface such as <a href="/wiki/Facebook_Bots" class="mw-redirect" title="Facebook Bots">Facebook Bots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twitterbot" class="mw-redirect" title="Twitterbot">Twitterbots</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Chatterbot" class="mw-redirect" title="Chatterbot">chatterbots</a> may allow people to ask questions in plain English and then formulate a proper response. These bots can often handle many tasks, including reporting weather, zip-code information, sports scores, converting currency or other units, etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Others are used for entertainment, such as <a href="/wiki/SmarterChild" title="SmarterChild">SmarterChild</a> on <a href="/wiki/AOL_Instant_Messenger" class="mw-redirect" title="AOL Instant Messenger">AOL Instant Messenger</a> and <a href="/wiki/MSN_Messenger" class="mw-redirect" title="MSN Messenger">MSN Messenger</a>.
</p><p>An additional role of <a href="/wiki/IRC_bot" title="IRC bot">IRC bots</a> may be to lurk in the background of a conversation channel, commenting on certain phrases uttered by the participants (based on <a href="/wiki/Pattern_matching" title="Pattern matching">pattern matching</a>). This is sometimes used as a help service for new users, or for censorship of <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_bots">Social bots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Social bots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Social_bot" title="Social bot">Social bot</a></div>
<p>Social networking bots are sets of algorithms that take on the duties of repetitive sets of instructions in order to establish a service or connection among social networking users. Various designs of networking bots vary from <a href="/wiki/Chat_bot" class="mw-redirect" title="Chat bot">chat bots</a>, algorithms designed to converse with a human user, to social bots, algorithms designed to mimic human behaviors to converse with behavioral patterns similar to that of a human user. The history of social botting can be traced back to <a href="/wiki/Alan_Turing" title="Alan Turing">Alan Turing</a> in the 1950s and his vision of designing sets of instructional code that passes the <a href="/wiki/Turing_test" title="Turing test">Turing test</a>. From 1964 to 1966, <a href="/wiki/ELIZA" title="ELIZA">ELIZA</a>, a natural language processing computer program created by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Weizenbaum" title="Joseph Weizenbaum">Joseph Weizenbaum</a>, is an early indicator of artificial intelligence algorithms that inspired computer programmers to design tasked programs that can match behavior patterns to their sets of instruction. As a result, natural language processing has become an influencing factor to the development of artificial intelligence and social bots as innovative technological advancements are made alongside the progression of the mass spreading of information and thought on social media websites.
</p><p>Reports of political interferences in recent elections<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Relative_time_references" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="Clarify the applicable timeframe so that it is unambiguous when read at a later date.">timeframe?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> have set the notion of botting being more prevalent because of the ethics that is challenged between the bot’s design and the bot’s designer. According to Emilio Ferrara, a computer scientist from the University of Southern California reporting on Communications of the ACM<sup id="cite_ref-RiseOfSocialBots_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RiseOfSocialBots-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>, the lack of resources available to implement fact-checking and information verification results in the large volumes of false reports and claims made on these bots in social media platforms. In the case of Twitter, most of these bots are programmed with searching filter capabilities that target key words and phrases that reflect in favor and against political agendas and retweet them.
While the attention of bots is programmed to spread unverified information throughout the social media platform,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> it is a challenge that programmers face in the wake of a hostile political climate. Binary functions are designated to the programs and using an Application Program interface embedded in the social media website executes the functions tasked. The Bot Effect is what Ferrera reports as when the socialization of bots and human users creates a vulnerability to the leaking of personal information and polarizing influences outside the ethics of the bot’s code. According to Guillory Kramer in his study, he observes the behavior of emotionally volatile users and the impact the bots have on the users, altering the perception of reality.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_bots">Commercial bots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commercial bots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(August 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>There has been a great deal of controversy about the use of bots in an automated trading function. Auction website <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> has been to court in an attempt to suppress a third-party company from using bots to traverse their site looking for bargains; this approach backfired on eBay and attracted the attention of further bots. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Bet_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Bet exchange">bet exchange</a> <a href="/wiki/Betfair" title="Betfair">Betfair</a> saw such a large amount of traffic coming from bots they launched a WebService API aimed at bot programmers through which Betfair can actively manage bot interactions.
</p><p>Bot farms are known to be used in online app stores, like the <a href="/wiki/Apple_App_Store" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple App Store">Apple App Store</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Play" title="Google Play">Google Play</a>, to manipulate positions<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> or to increase positive ratings/reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A rapidly growing, benign, form of internet bot is the <a href="/wiki/Chatbot" title="Chatbot">chatbot</a>. From 2016, when Facebook Messenger allowed developers to place chatbots on their platform there has been an exponential growth of their use on that forum alone. 30,000 bots were created for Messenger in the first six months, rising to 100,000 by September 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Avi Ben Ezra, CTO of SnatchBot, told Forbes that evidence from the use of their chatbot building platform pointed to a near future saving of millions of hours of human labour as 'live chat' on websites was replaced with bots.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Companies use internet bots to increase online engagement and streamline communication. Companies often use bots to cut down on cost, instead of employing people to communicate with consumers, companies have developed new ways to be efficient. These chatbots are used to answer customers' questions. For example, Dominos has developed a chatbot that can take orders via Facebook messenger. Chatbots allow companies to allocate their employees' time to more important things.<sup id="cite_ref-MarTech_Today_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MarTech_Today-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Malicious_bots">Malicious bots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Malicious bots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A malicious use of bots is the coordination and operation of an <a href="/wiki/Automated_threat" title="Automated threat">automated attack</a> on networked computers, such as a <a href="/wiki/Denial-of-service_attack" title="Denial-of-service attack">denial-of-service attack</a> by a <a href="/wiki/Botnet" title="Botnet">botnet</a>. Internet bots can also be used to commit <a href="/wiki/Click_fraud" title="Click fraud">click fraud</a> and more recently have seen usage around <a href="/wiki/MMORPG" class="mw-redirect" title="MMORPG">MMORPG</a> games as <a href="/wiki/Computer_game_bot" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer game bot">computer game bots</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A <a href="/wiki/Spambot" title="Spambot">spambot</a>  is an internet bot that attempts to <a href="/wiki/Spam_(electronic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spam (electronic)">spam</a> large amounts of content on the Internet, usually adding advertising links. More than 94.2% of websites have experienced a bot attack.<sup id="cite_ref-Zeifman_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeifman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>There are malicious bots (and <a href="/wiki/Botnets" class="mw-redirect" title="Botnets">botnets</a>) of the following types:</li></ul>
<ol><li>Spambots that harvest email addresses from contact or guestbook pages</li>
<li>Downloader programs that suck <a href="/wiki/Bandwidth_(computing)" title="Bandwidth (computing)">bandwidth</a> by downloading entire websites</li>
<li>Website scrapers that grab the content of websites and re-use it without permission on automatically generated doorway pages</li>
<li>Viruses and worms</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/DDoS" class="mw-redirect" title="DDoS">DDoS</a> attacks</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Botnets" class="mw-redirect" title="Botnets">Botnets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zombie_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zombie (computer science)">zombie computers</a>, etc.</li></ol>
<ul><li>Bots are also used to buy up good seats for concerts, particularly by ticket brokers who resell the tickets.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Bots are employed against entertainment event-ticketing sites. The bots are used by ticket brokers to unfairly obtain the best seats for themselves while depriving the general public of also having a chance to obtain the good seats. The bot runs through the purchase process and obtains better seats by pulling as many seats back as it can.</li>
<li>Bots are often used in <a href="/wiki/MMORPG" class="mw-redirect" title="MMORPG">Massively Multiplayer Online Roleplaying Games</a> to farm for resources that would otherwise take significant time or effort to obtain; this is a concern for most online in-game economies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>Bots are also used to increase views for <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> videos.</li>
<li>Bots are used to increase traffic counts on analytics reporting to extract money from advertisers. A study by comScore found that 54 percent of display ads shown in thousands of campaigns between May 2012 and February 2013 never appeared in front of a human being.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>in 2012, reporter Percy von Lipinski reported that he discovered millions of bot or botted or pinged views at CNN iReport. CNN iReport quietly removed millions of views from the account of so-called superstar iReporter Chris Morrow.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is not known if the ad revenue received by CNN from the fake views was ever returned to the advertisers.</li>
<li>Bots may be used on internet forums to automatically post inflammatory or nonsensical posts to <a href="/wiki/Internet_troll" title="Internet troll">disrupt the forum and anger users</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The most widely used anti-bot technique is the use of <a href="/wiki/CAPTCHA" title="CAPTCHA">CAPTCHA</a>, which is a form of <a href="/wiki/Turing_test" title="Turing test">Turing test</a> used to distinguish between a human user and a less-sophisticated AI-powered bot, by the use of graphically-encoded human-readable text. Examples of providers include <a href="/wiki/Recaptcha" class="mw-redirect" title="Recaptcha">Recaptcha</a>, and commercial companies such as Minteye, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solve_Media&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Solve Media (page does not exist)">Solve Media</a>, and <a href="/wiki/NuCaptcha" title="NuCaptcha">NuCaptcha</a>. Captchas, however, are not foolproof in preventing bots as they can often be <a href="/wiki/CAPTCHA#Circumvention" title="CAPTCHA">circumvented</a> by computer character recognition, security holes, and even by outsourcing captcha solving to cheap laborers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Helpful_bots">Helpful bots</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Internet_bot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Helpful bots">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Companies and customers can benefit from internet bots. Internet bots are allowing customers to communicate with companies without having to communicate with a person. <a href="/wiki/KLM_Royal_Dutch_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="KLM Royal Dutch Airlines">KLM Royal Dutch Airlines</a> has produced a chatbot that allows customers to receive boarding passes, check in reminders, and other information that is needed for a flight.<sup id="cite_ref-MarTech_Today_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MarTech_Today-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Companies have made chatbots that can benefit customers. Customer engagement has grown since these chatbots have been developed.
</p><p>Chat bots are used on a daily basis. <a href="/wiki/Google_Assistant" title="Google Assistant">Google Assistant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siri" title="Siri">Siri</a> are considered forms of chat bots. Google Assistant and Siri allow people to ask questions and get a response using an AI system. These technological advances are positively benefiting people's daily lives.
</p>
