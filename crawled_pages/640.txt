<p><br />
<b>T</b> (<a href="/wiki/English_alphabet#Letter_names" title="English alphabet">named</a> <i>tee</i> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is the 20th <a href="/wiki/Letter_(alphabet)" title="Letter (alphabet)">letter</a> in the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">modern English</a> <a href="/wiki/English_alphabet" title="English alphabet">alphabet</a> and the <a href="/wiki/ISO_basic_Latin_alphabet" title="ISO basic Latin alphabet">ISO basic Latin alphabet</a>. It is the most commonly used <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonant</a> and the second most common letter in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-language</a> texts.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><i><a href="/wiki/Taw" title="Taw">Taw</a></i> was the last letter of the Western <a href="/wiki/Semitic_alphabets" class="mw-redirect" title="Semitic alphabets">Semitic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">Hebrew alphabets</a>.  The sound value of Semitic <i>Taw</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">Greek alphabet</a> Tαυ (<i>Tau</i>), <a href="/wiki/Old_Italic_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Italic alphabet">Old Italic</a> and Latin T has remained fairly constant, representing <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Alveolar" title="Voiceless dental and alveolar stops">t</a>]</span> in each of these; and it has also kept its original basic shape in most of these alphabets.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_writing_systems">Use in writing systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use in writing systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In English, &#x27e8;t&#x27e9; usually denotes the <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless alveolar plosive">voiceless alveolar plosive</a> (<a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> and <a href="/wiki/X-SAMPA" title="X-SAMPA">X-SAMPA</a>: <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Alveolar" title="Voiceless dental and alveolar stops">t</a>/</span>), as in <i>tart</i>, <i>tee</i>, or <i>ties</i>, often with <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">aspiration</a> at the beginnings of words or before <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">stressed</a> vowels.
</p><p>The digraph &#x27e8;ti&#x27e9; often corresponds to the sound <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʃ/</span> (a <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_sibilant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar sibilant">voiceless palato-alveolar sibilant</a>) word-medially when followed by a vowel, as in <i>nation</i>, <i>ratio</i>, <i>negotiation,</i> and <i>Croatia</i>.
</p><p>The letter &#x27e8;t&#x27e9; corresponds to the affricate <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t͡ʃ/</span> in some words as a result of <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_English_consonant_clusters#Yod-coalescence" title="Phonological history of English consonant clusters">yod-coalescence</a> (for example, in words ending in "-ture", such as <i>future</i>).
</p><p>A common <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">digraph</a> is &#x27e8;th&#x27e9;, which usually represents a <a href="/wiki/Dental_fricative" title="Dental fricative">dental fricative</a>, but occasionally represents <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span> (as in <i>Thomas</i> and <i>thyme</i>.)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">Other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Orthographies" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthographies">orthographies</a> of other languages, &#x27e8;t&#x27e9; is often used for <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span>, the <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless dental plosive">voiceless dental plosive</a> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t̪/</span> or similar sounds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">Other systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=T&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a>, &#x27e8;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">t</span>&#x27e9; denotes the <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless alveolar plosive">voiceless alveolar plosive</a>.
</p>
